THE COLONIAL FANTASY AND THE RED STAFF

First Contact and the Logic of Misrecognition — 1498–1517

"If this is a devil, I adore the true God."

— João de Sá, kneeling before a Hindu deity in Calicut, 1498

6.1 — THE COMEDY OF ERRORS

Later European historians would frame the Portuguese arrival in the Indian Ocean as a grand unveiling of an unknown world. Fernão Lopes de Castanheda's sixteenth-century chronicle would be rendered into English as The First Booke of the Historie of the Discoverie and Conquest of the East Indias, Enterprised by the Portingales. Manuel de Faria y Sousa's monumental Asia Portuguesa became the History of the Discovery and Conquest of India by the Portuguese. The premise embedded in these titles is as revealing as it is historically absurd: they proceed as if India — a subcontinent that had been anchoring the global economy, dominating maritime trade, and hosting complex cosmopolitan societies for millennia — required 'discovery' by the Portuguese. Western Europe was the last actor to arrive on a stage that had been bustling with trans-oceanic traffic since antiquity. 1

What Vasco da Gama's fleet encountered when it dropped anchor off Calicut in May 1498 was not a lost continent but an intricate political and commercial ecology that had been functioning quite well without European participation. And the first thing the Portuguese did upon entering it was to kneel and pray — in the wrong building, to the wrong god, in the wrong religion.

Shortly after landing, da Gama's party was escorted by locals to what the Roteiro — the anonymous chronicle of da Gama's first voyage — describes as a place of worship. The Brahmin priests wore sacred threads, which the visitors mistook for vestments. In the sanctuary — dimly lit, accessible only to the temple attendants — an image stood which the attendants identified by calling out a name the Portuguese heard as 'Holy Mary.' It was almost certainly a manifestation of a local goddess. Da Gama fell to his knees. His men followed. But one man hesitated. João de Sá, staring at the multi-armed figures on the temple walls, at the animal gods and the fierce iconography that bore no resemblance to any Christian art he had ever seen, knelt and prayed with a cautious caveat: 'If this is a devil, I adore the true God.' Da Gama, hearing it, smiled. 2

The moment captures, in a single kneeling figure, the entire cognitive apparatus the Portuguese had carried around the Cape of Good Hope: the binary worldview that divided all humanity into Christians and infidels; the centuries-old conviction that Eastern Christians must exist beyond the Islamic barrier; the sheer inability to conceive of a situation in which Christianity and Islam could coexist. There was no third option. Sá's prayer was not entirely foolish.

For the duration of the first voyage, the Portuguese operated under the confused belief that the Zamorin and his Hindu subjects were some kind of exotic, schismatic sect of Christians. King Manuel I's letters, written after da Gama's return, bear testimony to the fact that the Portuguese came home convinced they had found the Eastern Christians they had sought. 3

6.2 — THE THREE FOUNDATIONS: SCEPTRE, FORT, AND CRUSADE

The first voyage ended in mutual hostility at Calicut. Muslim merchants — both local Mappilas and Arab traders connected to the Red Sea network — recognised the Portuguese as dangerous competitors within an established commercial system and acted to undermine them. The Zamorin, operating within the traditional commercially integrative port-polity system of Kerala, initially tried to incorporate the Portuguese into his system. He granted da Gama an audience. He offered trading terms. But the deal was marked by tension from the outset. Da Gama's gifts — striped cloth, strings of coral, wash basins — were laughable by the standards of a court accustomed to receiving Arab and Chinese merchants. The Zamorin's officials pointed out, with barely concealed contempt, that even the poorest Arab merchant arriving from Mecca brought more valuable goods. Entrenched Muslim merchant networks, central to the Zamorin's commercial order, resisted the Portuguese presence, further complicating any attempt at accommodation. 4

Vasco da Gama returned to Portugal a year later with frustrated expectations and enduring misconceptions about the region. He would return to the Malabar Coast twice more — first in 1502 at the head of a heavily armed expedition that abandoned any pretence of accommodation, and finally in 1524 as Viceroy of the Estado da Índia. He died that same year at Kochi, in the very land that later European historians would claim had been 'discovered' and conquered by him.

Cabral's Carracks

When Pedro Álvares Cabral arrived with the second, 13 ship strong fleet of heavy cargo ships called carracks in 1500, hostility with the Muslim merchants and the Zamorin intensified. A Portuguese factory was established at Calicut, then attacked by Muslim merchants; Cabral retaliated by bombarding the city. The Zamorin turned decisively against the Portuguese. It was this hostility that pushed them southward — to Cochin, the one court on the Malabar coast that had every reason to welcome them.

What the Portuguese did next, in rapid succession, established the three foundations of their Indian enterprise. Each must be understood within the seven-hundred-year crucible of the Reconquista — the formative framework the Portuguese possessed for engaging with the world beyond Christendom.

The Sceptre. On 19 November 1502, during Vasco da Gama's second voyage — this time a military expedition, not a trading mission — a delegation of Thomas Christians arrived at Cochin from Kodungalloor and from many other places in the interior. They brought presents of fowls and fruits, and they tendered to the admiral a pointed staff, red in colour, both ends plated in silver, with three silver bells at one end. Together with it they brought a letter from the lord of their community, said to number some 30,000 adults. According to later Portuguese narratives, the delegation accepted the king of Portugal as their king and yielded their fidelity and homage, declaring that from that day onwards they would not administer justice except in his name, and requesting that a fortress be built in their country. 5

If this event occurred as described — it is only briefly and imperfectly attested in the Portuguese chronicles — its meaning is accessible only through Portuguese interpretation. The episode was read through the lenses of the Christian king tradition and European feudal categories. The Portuguese concluded that the Thomas Christians were surrendering the sovereignty of a 'Christian king,' his dynasty recorded in corrupted forms such as billert and later interpreted as Villarvattom, to the Portuguese Crown. What may have been a pragmatic request for alliance and protection was thus codified in Lisbon as the submission of a vassal polity. 6 From this interpretive framework, the later Villarvattom dynasty narrative would emerge.

The Fort. In 1503, the Portuguese built Fort Manuel at Kochi — the first European fortress in India. This was not merely a trading post; it was a militarised beachhead. By anchoring their artillery within the territory of the king of Cochin, they began to transform a sovereign Hindu raja into a dependent client. The king who had invited them in to counter the Zamorin increasingly found himself reliant on their guns. The fort drew the St. Thomas Christians — whose commercial networks were closely tied to Cochin's pepper trade — into a zone shaped by Portuguese military power. 7

The Crusade. The Portuguese inflicted heavy damage on Muslim shipping and coastal networks. Vasco da Gama announced his intention of attacking Muslim vessels, seizing their goods, and killing or enslaving their crews. In 1502, he intercepted the Miri, a vessel returning from Mecca carrying several hundred pilgrims — men, women, and children — and burned it. For the Portuguese, this was the same warfare they had waged against the Moors of North Africa after Ceuta, and that their fathers and grandfathers had waged in the Algarve. The violence deployed in the Indian Ocean drew on familiar precedents, even as it was applied in a radically different commercial and political environment. The result was the disruption of the existing Malabar system. 8

6.3 — THE PLURALISM BLIND SPOT

The deepest source of Portuguese confusion was not religious but sociological. They could not separate the Christians from the surrounding population.

The Jornada — the account of Archbishop Aleixo de Menezes's later journey through Kerala — records what the Portuguese had observed from the very beginning: the Thomas Christians 'in their dress they are not different from the Nairs.' They wore long hair bound at the top of the head with coloured silk ribbon, to which many Christians added prayer beads or a cross of gold or silver. 'Thus although in their dress they are not different from the Nairs... one can distinguish a Christian' — but only faintly, and by small devotional markers. Their priests often dressed like the laity and offered Mass in everyday attire. Their ears were pierced and adorned with gold jewellery, as among other Malabar communities. They trained in the same kalaris as the Nairs, fought in the same armies, and served the same Hindu rulers. When certain Nairs attacked the Thomas Christians in response to Menezes's disparaging remarks about Hindu gods, other Nair soldiers reportedly intervened, calling the Christians 'brothers' and halting the attack. 9

For the Portuguese, this integration was difficult to comprehend — and potentially threatening. They had arrived expecting a clearly bounded Christian ally: a community distinguishable from 'infidel' or 'idolatrous' populations, capable of being aligned within a broader crusading framework. What they encountered instead was a community so deeply embedded in the local social fabric that such binary distinctions blurred in practice.

A comparable bewilderment appears in some external Muslim accounts. Foreign observers from the Islamic world, operating within juridical frameworks that emphasised political and social distinction, noted that the Muslims of the Malabar coast lived under Hindu rulers and were closely integrated into local society. The Mappilas served in the Zamorin's forces alongside Nairs, adopted local dress and language, and participated in the region's commercial networks. Just as the Portuguese were frustrated that the Christians did not align against Muslims, some external Muslim observers found it striking that local Muslims did not fight jihad against infiedels.

In this context, religion on the Malabar coast functioned less as a boundary of conflict than as one element within a broader, stratified social and economic order. Different communities tended to specialise in particular roles — Christians prominently in inland production and pepper trade networks, Muslims in maritime commerce, Nairs in military and administrative service — within a system ultimately overseen by Hindu rulers. The result was not an absence of tension, but a pattern of coexistence structured by interdependence rather than confessional warfare. 10

This was the reality that the Synod of Diamper would eventually attempt to destroy — by forcing 'distinction of the faithful from unbelievers' through changes to dress, ornament, diet, and custom. But the impulse to separate the Thomas Christians from their Hindu and Muslim neighbours, to make them visibly and culturally distinct, was present from the very first Portuguese generation. It was not an afterthought of the 1590s. It was structurally embedded in the crusading binary the Portuguese carried with them.

6.4 — JOSEPH THE INDIAN: THE VOICE THAT WENT UNHEARD

Before the hostility at Calicut had hardened into permanent war, Cabral's fleet had already made contact with Malabar Christians. At Kodungalloor — the ancient metropolitan seat — his men had visited the Thomas Christian community in their own town. Two Christians from Kodungalloor boarded Cabral's ships: a man named Mathias, and Joseph, who had returned from his 1490 mission to Mesopotamia as an ordained priest and was now rector of the principal church. Mathias died during the voyage or shortly after reaching Portugal. Joseph survived. 11

Joseph was received by King Manuel I, to whom he described, among other things, the tomb of the Apostle Thomas at Mylapore. The king was greatly interested — here was a connection to apostolic tradition that placed Portuguese India within sacred history, alongside Spain's Santiago de Compostela. Manuel ordered all Portuguese going to India to inquire closely into the affairs of these Christians.

After visiting Rome — where he may have had an audience with Pope Alexander VI — Joseph arrived in Venice in June 1502. There, his testimony was recorded by an anonymous interviewer. He described the Thomas Christians' ecclesiastical structure, their liturgy, their commerce. He described a pluralistic Calicut with four distinct audience chambers — for Gentiles, Moors, Jews, and Christians — coexisting under the Zamorin's rule. And critically, he spoke of the Pope with reverence, describing Rome as the seat of the Apostle Peter, the head of the universal Church. 12

This is the detail that later Portuguese policy would render tragically ironic. Joseph did not reject Rome. The Thomas Christians, as a community, had never rejected the papacy. Their understanding of ecclesiology was broadly this: both the Portuguese and they belonged to the universal Church of Christ. In practice, this belonging was expressed through obedience to their own pastors — the East Syrian bishops — and through willingness to maintain fraternal relations with the Portuguese Christians. Roman communion and Latin jurisdiction were, in their minds, never identical. They were perfectly willing to acknowledge the Pope as the successor of Peter and the head of the universal Church. What they were not willing to do — and what they would never willingly do — was to replace their ancient Eastern hierarchy with Portuguese Latin clergy appointed by the king of Portugal under the Padroado. 13

Joseph's testimony, circulated in Italian and Latin, was the earliest detailed firsthand account of Indian Christian life to reach Europe. It described exactly the pluralist, self-governing, apostolically rooted community that the Portuguese would spend the next century trying to dismantle. But the information, once again, was siloed. Not from Venice this time — from Lisbon's own ideological commitments. The script was already written. Joseph's counter-narrative was incompatible with the programme, and so it was set aside.

6.5 — THE BISHOPS' LETTER: THE HONEYMOON DOCUMENT

While the Portuguese were building forts and burning Muslim ships, the ecclesiastical connection between the Thomas Christians and Mesopotamia was playing out its final act.

As we saw in the previous chapter, the delegation of 1490 had returned with bishops. Mar John stayed in India; Mar Thomas returned to the Catholicos carrying 'first-fruits and offerings.' When the Catholicos Mar Simeon died in 1497 and was succeeded by Elias V, the new patriarch consecrated three monks from the monastery of St. Eugene at Gazarta — Rabban David (ordained Mar Jabballaha), Rabban George (ordained Mar Denha), and Rabban Masud (ordained Mar Jacob) — and sent them to India with the returning Mar Thomas in 1503. 14

The bishops' arrival coincided precisely with the crystallisation of the Portuguese-Nazrani alliance. They made first contact with the Portuguese at Kannur, then travelled south to Cochin — where the Portuguese headquarters, the Nazrani leadership, and political safety from the Zamorin were concentrated. The bishops' letter of 1504, written in Syriac from Cochin and addressed to Patriarch Elias V, is one of the most significant and most heartbreaking documents in the history of Indian Christianity.

The letter radiates ecclesiological optimism. The bishops describe the Portuguese — 'the Franks called the Portuguese,' estimated at about four hundred men — not as foreign conquerors but as Christian brethren sent by God. 'The fear and dread of them fell on all infidels and Ismaelites of these countries,' they report, interpreting Portuguese naval supremacy not as European imperialism but as divine deliverance. The Franciscan chaplains of the Portuguese fleet had welcomed the East Syrian bishops, admired their piety, and shared the altar with them. At this moment, the formal theological lines drawn by the councils of Ephesus and Chalcedon did not matter on the ground in India. A Christian was a Christian. The bishops wrote to their Patriarch completely unaware that the Latin West viewed the Church of the East as 'Nestorian' heretics. 15

Mar Jacob, who would guide the destinies of the Thomas Christians for the next half-century, noted in a calendar colophon that 'in the above mentioned year (1504) we arrived in these Indian countries at the town called Kannur in which we found our Christian brethren in truth, the Franks called the Portuguese.'

Read with the benefit of hindsight, the letter is a Greek tragedy. The bishops were celebrating brotherhood with men whose institution was already drafting plans to subjugate them. While the East Syrian bishops were writing to Babylon about the beauty of sharing the Eucharist with 'our brethren the Franks,' the Portuguese official apparatus was recording the sceptre incident as proof of feudal submission and sending reports to Lisbon about 'heretics' who needed to be brought under control. The 1504 letter is the high-water mark of what Indian Christianity's encounter with the Portuguese might have been — a genuine communion between East and West — before the Padroado decided that unity could only be achieved through Latin conformity. 16

6.6 — THE EMPIRE HARDENS

For the first seven years after da Gama's landing, the Portuguese presence on the Malabar coast had been fluid and seasonal — visible principally during the annual arrival of the fleet from Lisbon, then fading into the factory at Cochin and the garrison at Kannur until the next monsoon brought fresh ships. That changed in 1505, when Dom Francisco de Almeida arrived as the first viceroy of India.

Almeida's mandate was to give permanence to the power that had so far moved about in fluid form. The fortified headquarters at Cochin became the seat of the Estado da Índia. Armed fortresses rose at Kannur, Anjediva, and Kilwa in East Africa. The standing fleet that da Gama had stationed under Vicente Sodré was systematised into a permanent naval patrol: every ship plying the Indian Ocean was now required to carry a cartaz — a clearance certificate issued by the Portuguese — or face seizure. Certificates were issued only for ships of Kannur, Cochin, and Quilon. The Indian Ocean, which had been an open commercial commons for millennia, was being enclosed by a single European power operating on a crusading mandate. 17

The response was swift and multinational. The Portuguese monopoly threatened not only the Muslim merchants of the Malabar coast but the entire financial architecture of the eastern Mediterranean. Venice — whose wealth depended on the Cairo–Alexandria spice route — watched with alarm as Portuguese ships began diverting pepper around the Cape directly to Lisbon. The Venetian Signoria dispatched secret agents to Egypt and proposed an extraordinary alliance: the Republic of St. Mark would fund and supply a Mamluk fleet to destroy the Portuguese in the Indian Ocean. The Mamluks, who had never maintained a significant naval presence in the Arabian Sea, agreed. Timber was shipped from Venice to Suez; Venetian gunners and shipwrights supervised the

construction of a war fleet in the Red Sea. The Zamorin of Calicut joined the coalition, as did elements of the Gujarat Sultanate. For a brief moment, the commercial interests of three continents — European Venice, Mamluk Egypt, and the Hindu-Muslim Malabar coast — converged against the Portuguese intrusion. The same Venice that had recorded Joseph the Indian's testimony in 1502 was now funding an armada to destroy the fleet that had brought Joseph to Europe. 18

In 1509, Almeida's fleet joined battle with this combined Mamluk-Gujarati-Zamorin armada off Diu. The Portuguese won decisively. The old world-system — the Calicut–Red Sea–Cairo–Venice route that had sustained the Mamluk and Venetian economies for centuries — received a blow from which it would not recover.

The Shift to Land Power

When Afonso de Albuquerque succeeded Almeida as governor in 1510, the sea-oriented policy gave way to a land-oriented imperial programme. Albuquerque understood that controlling trade routes was not enough; the Portuguese needed territory — places that could sustain a population, generate revenue, and anchor the archipelago of fortresses into something resembling a state. Goa was conquered in 1510 and made the new capital. Malacca fell in 1511 — the door to Southeast Asia. Hormuz in 1515 — the gateway to the Persian Gulf. These three conquests, together with Cochin and Kannur on the Malabar coast, defined the territorial skeleton of the Estado da Índia: a political archipelago, its scattered nodes connected by shipping routes, its power exercised through fortress walls and cannon, its economy driven by the spice monopoly. 19

Albuquerque was also the first to confront the demographic problem that would haunt Portuguese India throughout its existence. There were simply not enough Portuguese to sustain an empire. His solution was pragmatic: he encouraged Portuguese soldiers to marry Indian women, creating a generation of mestiço subjects loyal to the Crown. He allotted these married settlers — the casados — petty trades to sustain them, and permitted them to set up shops and manufacturing units. The casado became the basic demographic unit of Portuguese India, and the private trade these settlers would later conduct in the Bay of Bengal and Southeast Asia would eventually rival the Crown's own commerce. 20

For the Thomas Christians, this imperial hardening had immediate and paradoxical consequences. In 1503, Albuquerque's cousin Francisco had already established a working relationship with the community at Quilon — one that was pragmatic and, initially, respectful. The Thomas Christians of Quilon had held the power of civil jurisdiction in their town, and the elder Albuquerque was careful to preserve it: the Portuguese factor was instructed to exercise authority 'according to the counsel and advice of the Indian Christians.' 21

Meanwhile, the institutional spine of the community was being quietly renewed from Mesopotamia. Metropolitan Mar John, who had arrived with the 1490 delegation, is said to have appointed George as archdeacon in 1502 — the office that was, in practice, the administrative head of the entire community. George was succeeded in turn by Jacob, Alexander, and finally by another George — the archdeacon who would later become known as George of the Cross, the figure who would confront Archbishop Menezes at the end of the century and whose community's oath at the Coonan Cross would fracture Indian Christianity permanently. 22

▣ THE ARCHDEACON AND THE PAKALOMATTAM QUESTION

The archdeaconate — Jathikkukarthaviyan, 'the one responsible for the community' — was the most powerful native office in Thomas Christian life. Its holder governed the community during the bishops' absences, fixed the financial contributions each village owed for the maintenance of foreign prelates, adjudicated disputes, maintained churches, and could mobilise the community's martial resources.

A tradition recorded by the scholar Wilhelm Germann in 1877, drawing on a padiyola (palm-leaf document) held by the Pakalomattam family, states that the archdeaconate was made hereditary in the Pakalomattam family by formal agreement — the highest dignity in the Church of India after the episcopate. Kollaparambil, a leading modern scholar of the office, accepts this tradition and identifies an early George (often associated with c. 1502) as 'Archdeacon Ittikuriath' mentioned in the same padiyola. 23

However, several cautions are necessary. The padiyola itself has been lost — Mundadan records that 'on inquiry I found that this padiyola had been lent by the family to some curious reader and now there is no trace of it.' The succession list George → Jacob → Alexander → George derives not from contemporary documentation but from George Kurian's The Syrian Church of Malankara (1908) — a community history written four centuries after the events. No sixteenth-century Portuguese source explicitly identifies any of these archdeacons by the family name 'Pakalomattam.'

The link between the archdeaconate and a specific family becomes clearer only in the seventeenth century — and then it points to a different family. Bishop Sebastiani, reporting to Rome in 1681, stated that the archdeaconate was 'conferred always on persons from the family of Campo' — the Portuguese rendering of the Malayalam name Parambil (also Parampil). It was Archdeacon Thomas de Campo (Thomas Parambil) who took the Coonan Cross Oath in 1653, and his successors who governed the community through the turbulent decades that followed.

Whether the archdeaconate passed from the Pakalomattam family to the Parambil family at some point during the sixteenth century is unknown. It is equally possible that the two names represent different branches of the same kinship network, or that the Pakalomattam attribution is itself a later construction projected backward onto an earlier period. These questions remain unresolved. 24

What is beyond doubt is the office itself: its antiquity, its immense practical authority, and its role as the institutional centre of gravity of Thomas Christian life across centuries of episcopal absence. It was the archdeacon, not the foreign bishop, whom the Portuguese would have to break if they wished to bring the community under the Padroado. And it was the archdeaconal leadership that would ultimately resist their control.

But in the early years — the years of Almeida and Albuquerque — the contradiction between Portuguese imperial ambitions and the community's autonomy could still be managed. Both sides needed each other. Mar Jacob directed pepper to the Portuguese factory. The Portuguese offered military protection against the Zamorin. The Franciscans at Cochin maintained warm personal relations with the bishop. The arrangement held — not because the contradiction had been resolved, but because neither side had yet been forced to choose between alliance and submission.

The man who forced that choice was a priest named Alvaro Penteado.

6.7 — THE FORGOTTEN ARCHITECT: FATHER ALVARO PENTEADO

Nearly eighty years before Archbishop Menezes convened the Synod of Diamper, Penteado carried the exact same blueprint to Kerala. History remembers Menezes because he succeeded. It has forgotten Penteado because he failed. But Penteado's failure is more revealing than Menezes's success, because it shows both the Portuguese intent and the community's strength at their rawest and most unadorned.

Penteado came to India of his own accord, arriving in Goa in 1511. He was not, initially, an official agent of the Crown — he was a freelance zealot, travelling without state subsidy in the ship of João Serrão. In Goa, the vicar general put him to work in ordinary pastoral care. He accompanied Afonso de Albuquerque to Hormuz and back. But Penteado was not a man who could be satisfied with routine. As soon as he heard about the Christians of Kodungalloor, he was, as the Portuguese archives record, 'fired with zeal' to work among them. 25

The Usurpation at Kodungalloor

When Governor Lopo Soares arrived in Cochin in early 1516, Penteado secured an official appointment as spiritual caretaker of the Kodungalloor church, which the governor designated 'the head and primate' of the Thomas Christian communities. He went straight to Kodungalloor. The chief priest was conveniently absent, on pilgrimage to the tomb of St. Thomas at Mylapore. During this absence, Penteado installed himself in the principal church and began ministering — without any regard for the rights of the local clergy and hierarchy. It was, as the sources acknowledge, a sort of usurpation, which had the approval of the Portuguese authorities. Those authorities treated the Thomas Christians as if they were already subjects of the king of Portugal, invoking the sceptre incident of 1502 as their legal basis. But the Thomas Christians had never intended that incident to be used in that way. 26

The reckoning came when the chief priest of Kodungalloor returned from Mylapore. The chief priest — who may well have been Joseph the Indian himself, now aged and returned from his European journey — found Penteado established in his church, ministering to his people without permission. He acted swiftly. He asked Penteado what exactly he intended to do and whether he planned to stay. Penteado's answer was unsatisfactory. The priest saw to it that the people stopped attending Penteado's Mass. The children who had been going to him for instruction were withdrawn. He forbade Penteado to say Mass in the principal church. The boycott was total. 27

Penteado, finding his position untenable, arranged a meeting between the priest and Governor Lopo Soares. The governor's message to the Christians was blunt: he was surprised that while Muslim and Hindu kings had sent messengers to him, the Christians — although they were Christians — had not approached him. They should conform their conduct to the Portuguese, following the customs and teachings of the Holy Roman Church. They should consider that Portuguese Christians throughout the world followed a uniform set of customs, and that it would be to their advantage to do the same. 28

The response of the Thomas Christians — conveyed through their priest — was courteous but immovable. Penteado retired to write his letter to King Manuel I — a long, detailed report describing the community's origin, history, government, faith, worship, sacraments, and customs. In it, he described how the Christians had 'Fathers' sent from 'Armenia,' who were obeyed with more reverence than the Latins showed the Pope. He attributed the community's deficiencies to the avarice of these foreign Fathers. And he laid before the king a programme: the favour and liberality of the king would contribute to their reform; they should be united under a superior; and thus, 'they would come under the obedience and teaching of Holy Mother the Church.' Penteado compared the king of Portugal to St. Peter himself, applying to Manuel the words of Christ: 'Peter, when you turn back to me you must strengthen your brothers.' 29

King Manuel nominated Penteado officially as spiritual caretaker of the Thomas Christians and administrator of the house of St. Thomas at Mylapore. It was probably at Penteado's inducement that Manuel wrote a letter to Mar Jacob — attempting to make the bishop financially dependent on the Crown through a state subsidy, and requesting that Mar Jacob allow Penteado and other Portuguese priests to baptise his Christians.

Mar Jacob's Defiance

Mar Jacob's reply — preserved in the national archives of Lisbon — is a masterpiece of diplomatic firmness. He thanked the king for the annuity. As for the request to let Penteado baptise his Christians: if the king had been given false reports about the validity of his baptism, Father Master João Caro (Domincan frair 1512-22 stationed in Cochin, become a trusted confidant to Mar Jacob and a vocal defender of the St. Thomas Christians' theological orthodoxy) could inform the king that he had been deceived. Although he did not know the usages of the Popes and of Rome, he was 'not so silly and ignorant as not to know the Holy Scriptures, both Old and New, as far as the matter of baptism was concerned.' 30

No Portuguese except one man — a certain Carcere — had a kind word for Penteado. The Franciscans at Cochin — who had maintained warm relations with Mar Jacob and understood that the military alliance with the Nazranies was more important than liturgical purity — watched Penteado's blundering with dismay. Mar Jacob himself would later write that 'the Christians of this country will not accept him... because he is very hard and does not know how to win them.' 31

Penteado failed. History would forget him. But the blueprint he articulated — 'come under the obedience and teaching of Holy Mother the Church' — would not be forgotten. It would be carried forward by the Jesuits, refined by successive governors, and finally executed, with the full machinery of the Portuguese military state behind it, by Archbishop Aleixo de Menezes at the Synod of Diamper in 1599. Everything that happened at Diamper — the burning of the Syriac books, the imposition of clerical celibacy, the anathematising of the Patriarch of Babylon, the forced changes to dress and diet and liturgy — was already contained in Penteado's letter to King Manuel.

And everything that happened at the Coonan Cross — the community's explosive refusal, the archdeacon's oath, the fracture that persists to the present day — was already contained in the chief priest's response: the locked church door, the withdrawn children, the courteous but absolute refusal to submit.

Between Penteado's failure in 1517 and Menezes's arrival in 1599 lie the decades that will test both sides: Mar Jacob's careful diplomacy, the arrival of the Jesuits, the deepening contest over pepper and liturgy, bishops arrested and deported, manuscripts hidden and burned. On one side, the Padroado — armed with papal bulls, pepper monopolies, fortress walls, and eventually the Inquisition — would move inexorably toward total Latinisation. On the other, the archdeaconate — backed by the palli yogams, sustained by its connection to Mesopotamia, undergirded by the community's martial and commercial indispensability — would resist. The succession of archdeacons — from George through Jacob, Alexander, and finally George of the Cross — would carry the office through the entire century of confrontation.

In 1624, George of the Cross would write to the Jesuit general a single sentence that distils the community's position across a hundred and twenty years of resistance:

'Errors, perhaps, there might have been; but heresies, which have to be confirmed with pertinacity, no.'

That is the story of the chapters that follow.

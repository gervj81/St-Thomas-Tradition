# Chapter 4: THE LEGEND, THE WITNESSES, AND THE COLLISION

## 1037 CE – 1498 CE

> *"We came to seek Christians and spices."*
>
> **— Portuguese emissary at Calicut, May 1498**

---

The previous chapter closed with Alfred the Great's envoys setting out for the tomb of St. Thomas in 883 CE — the tradition's farthest reach across a darkening world. Within a century and a half, the window through which those emissaries might have passed would be closed altogether. What followed was not a single story but two: a legend developing in the West, and a documentary record accumulating in the East. They ran in parallel for four centuries, each ignorant of the other. They converged on the deck of a Portuguese vessel at Calicut in 1498 — and the collision between what the legend had promised and what the documents attested would reshape the Thomas Christian community's history irrevocably.

---

## 4.1 — THE CURTAIN AND THE LEGEND

### The Seljuk Closure (1037–1099)

The consolidation of Seljuk Turkish power in Persia and Mesopotamia from 1037 CE onward accomplished what the Arab conquests four centuries earlier had not: the effective severance of the remaining channels connecting the Church of the East's Indian province to its institutional centre at Baghdad.

The Arab caliphates had disrupted the Alexandrian vector and transformed the Persian Gulf corridor, but the Church of the East had survived — indeed flourished — within the caliphal system. Patriarch Timothy I had operated from Baghdad itself, negotiating with caliphs and administering bishoprics from the Euphrates to the Malabar coast. The Seljuks shattered this arrangement. Tughril Beg's capture of Baghdad in 1055, though it left the Abbasid caliphate nominally intact, placed real military power in the hands of Turkic warlords whose interest in maintaining the Persian Gulf's ecclesiastical networks was negligible. The overland routes along which episcopal appointments, liturgical texts, and theological correspondence had flowed were attenuated or cut.

In 1071, the Seljuk sultan Alp Arslan defeated the Byzantine emperor Romanos IV Diogenes at Manzikert, opening Anatolia to Turkic settlement. In the same year, Seljuk forces captured Jerusalem from the relatively tolerant Fatimid caliphate. For the Thomas Christians of the Malabar coast, the Seljuk ascendancy meant increasing isolation from the patriarchal authority that had governed them for centuries. For Latin Christendom, it meant something else: the political conditions for the Crusades — and with the Crusades, a new and intensely distorted awareness of the eastern Christian world.[EN1]

### Patriarch John in Rome (1122): The Seed

It was precisely into this atmosphere of disrupted communication and heightened western curiosity that a remarkable episode occurred.

In 1122 CE, a man identifying himself as a patriarch from India arrived in Rome. Two independent texts — an anonymous narrative called the *De Adventu Patriarchae Indorum* and a letter by Odo of Rheims, who claimed to have been present — record that this John was granted an audience with Pope Calixtus II. Before the Pope, he described his homeland: a city with the largest walls in the world, where one of the rivers of paradise deposited gold and gemstones. And — critically — he reported that a church outside the city housed the body of the apostle Thomas. Every year, John declared, the red-haired body of Thomas awoke to bestow blessings upon the faithful.

The two accounts differ in numerous specifics, but their independence from each other suggests that someone did visit Rome and tell such tales. The visitor was almost certainly not from India. He was probably a native of Edessa — the city steeped in the Thomas relic tradition, whose possession of the apostle's bones had been celebrated since the fourth century. But whether impostor or genuine pilgrim, the effect was the same: it deposited into the Latin European consciousness the image of a distant eastern Christian kingdom associated with the apostle Thomas and his miraculous body. The story was repeated as fact in chronicles for three centuries.[EN2]

### The Birth of Prester John (1145)

Two decades later, the rumour acquired a name.

In 1145, Bishop Hugh of Jabala — a prelate from the Latin Crusader States — made a diplomatic embassy to Pope Eugenius III to report on the fall of Edessa to Zengi in 1144. In the course of his report, Hugh mentioned that a powerful Christian ruler named John, governing a kingdom beyond Persia and Armenia, had recently defeated Muslim forces in a great battle. This John professed Christianity 'of the Nestorian persuasion' and was 'descended from the Magi.'

The German chronicler Otto of Freising, who was present, recorded the account in his *De Duabus Civitatibus* — the first written mention of *Presbiter Johannes*, Prester John. Otto introduces the name with a revealing clause: 'as indeed they are accustomed to call him,' indicating that the designation already had oral currency. He closes with the scathing words 'but enough of this,' suggesting he regarded the story with suspicion. The pope took no action to contact this eastern ruler.[EN3]

The historical nucleus behind Hugh's report is now understood. The 'great battle' corresponds to the Battle of Qatwan (9 September 1141), fought between the Seljuk Sultan Sanjar and Yēlǜ Dàshí, leader of the Kara-Khitai — a Buddhist East Asian people, not Christians at all. The defeat was so crushing that a later Arab historian wrote: 'in the history of Islam, there was no battle greater than this.' The shock of a powerful Muslim sultan being overthrown by an unknown eastern force sent reverberations to the Crusader States. By the time the news reached Hugh of Jabala, the steppe warrior had become a Nestorian Christian king.[EN4]

### The Letter of Prester John (c. 1165–1170)

The legend might have faded as a minor chronicle entry had it not been for a document of extraordinary ambition: the *Letter of Prester John*, a fabricated epistle purporting to have been written by Prester John himself to the Byzantine Emperor Manuel I Komnenos.

The Letter survives in at least 469 manuscripts. No Greek-language version has ever been found; it bears all the hallmarks of a Latin forgery. In it, Prester John describes his kingdom — the 'Three Indias' — as a land of marvels, monsters, and moral perfection, where the apostle Thomas rests, where a fountain of youth makes anyone who drinks from it perpetually thirty-two years old, and where there are no thieves, liars, or vice whatsoever. The writer combines the traditions of the Marvels of the East — a genre stretching back to Alexander the Great — with the same apostolic association that 'Patriarch John' had deposited in Rome in 1122: the body of St. Thomas, in a marvellous kingdom in India.

The Letter's genesis has been convincingly linked to the conflict between Pope Alexander III and Emperor Frederick Barbarossa — a piece of Hohenstaufen propaganda designed to demonstrate that a ruler who combined secular and religious authority could produce a more harmonious Christian society than one governed by papal supremacy. It was probably produced in the circle of Rainald of Dassel, Archbishop of Cologne. But whatever its original political purpose, the Letter took on a life of its own. Five interpolations added successive layers of detail. Translations appeared across European languages. It became less a single text than a genre — a framework through which Latin Europe processed its fragmentary knowledge of the East.[EN5]

At the Letter's centre, embedded within the marvels and monsters, was this: the body of St. Thomas lay in Prester John's kingdom, in the Three Indias. The tradition that Gregory of Tours had recorded in 590 CE, that Alfred's envoys had pursued in 883, that the Syriac chain had maintained from Jesuab through Bar Hebraeus — this tradition now entered the Latin European imagination in a form simultaneously garbled and extraordinarily influential.

### Meanwhile, in India: The Tomb Chain Continues

While the Prester John legend was crystallising in the West, the Syriac documentary tradition continued to record the same tomb — without any awareness that Latin Europe was fantasising about it.

The chain runs unbroken. Jesuab of Nisibis (seventh century) had used the toponym 'Calamina.' By the twelfth and thirteenth centuries, the identification becomes more specific. Ishoyahb of Nisibis (bishop, 1187–1222 CE) attests that the body of Thomas remains in India. Solomon of Basra, writing in the thirteenth century, specifies the site as 'Mayluph' — unmistakably Mylapore on the Coromandel coast. And Bar Hebraeus (d. 1286), the great Syriac polymath, equates Mylapore with Calamina, completing the identification chain that runs across six centuries: Calamina = Mylapore. The tomb site that was generating legend in Europe was being documented with increasing precision in the Syriac world — and neither tradition knew of the other.[EN6]

### The Mongol Catastrophe and Its Paradox (1219–1258)

Then the world changed.

The Mongol conquests of the thirteenth century constituted the greatest single catastrophe in the history of the Church of the East. The devastation of Central Asia under Genghis Khan (1219 CE onward) destroyed the network of metropolitan sees, monasteries, and trade routes that had sustained the church's eastern expansion. In 1258, Hulagu Khan sacked Baghdad itself — the caliphal capital, and the city from which the Patriarch of the Church of the East had administered his vast communion for five centuries. The last Abbasid Caliph, al-Musta'sim, was executed. The institutional infrastructure that had connected Mesopotamia to the Thomas Christians of the Malabar coast was shattered.

Yet the Mongol period contained a paradox. Several Mongol khans had Christian wives or mothers — a product of the Church of the East's earlier missionary success on the Mongolian steppe. Hulagu's own wife, Dokuz Khatun, was a Nestorian Christian; she protected the Patriarch even as Hulagu's armies destroyed the caliphate. Under the Ilkhanate (1256–1335 CE), this gave the church a brief measure of imperial protection. And — crucially for the Thomas Christian story — the Mongol conquest reopened routes between Europe and Asia that had been effectively closed since the Seljuk consolidation. The *Pax Mongolica*, enforced by military terror across the largest contiguous empire in history, allowed missionaries, merchants, and diplomats to travel from the Mediterranean to China for the first time in centuries.[EN7]

It was through this briefly reopened corridor that the most remarkable cluster of independent witnesses converged on the Indian coast.

### The Witnesses at the Shrine (1291–1348)

In 1291–1292, two men travelling in opposite directions reached the same shrine on the Coromandel coast. Marco Polo, the Venetian merchant, was sailing westward from China. John of Montecorvino, a Franciscan papal legate bound for the Mongol court, was sailing eastward. Both stopped at Mylapore. Polo described the tomb, its Nestorian and Muslim guardians, the miraculous earth that cured fevers. Montecorvino spent thirteen months at the shrine, buried his companion there, and baptised around a hundred converts. Independently, around 1321–1324, another Franciscan — Odoric of Pordenone — also visited the Mylapore shrine and corroborated Polo's account.

A fourth witness, the Dominican Jordanus Catalani of Séverac (c. 1321), took a different path. He visited Christian communities on the western and Konkan coast — at Thana, Salsette, Sopara, Broach, and Quilon — but did not go to Mylapore. His report to Pope John XXII led to the establishment of the Diocese of Quilon in 1329 — the first papally recognised Latin Catholic diocese in India.

The pattern revealed by these four witnesses is telling. All four engaged the western coast as their primary point of contact with Indian Christianity — the coast where the living community was commercially active and institutionally embedded. Three of the four then visited Mylapore from the west, as a secondary pilgrimage to the tomb. Jordanus did not go to Mylapore at all. By the late thirteenth century, the western coast was where the living community was found; Mylapore was the tomb site, visited secondarily. The documentary centre of gravity had shifted — and the witnesses' own behaviour made the shift visible.[EN8]

### Vatican Syriac MS 22: The Community Names Itself (1301)

In 1301 CE — between Polo's visit and Jordanus's report — a fourteen-year-old deacon named Zakarya bar Joseph, working at Kodungallur on the Malabar coast, copied a Pauline Lectionary in Syriac Estrangelo script. His colophon, preserved in what is now Vatican Syriac MS 22, contains three clauses of extraordinary significance. He dated his work to 1612 of the Seleucid Era. He identified his distant Patriarch as Mar Yahballaha III — the Mongol-born patriarch elected in 1281, who was at that moment administering the Church of the East from Maragha in the Ilkhanate. And he recorded that his bishop presided over the 'Apostolic See of St. Thomas.'

In three sentences, this colophon establishes: that a functioning episcopal see at Kodungallur in 1301 understood itself as the Apostolic See of St. Thomas; that this see was in communion with the Church of the East Patriarch operating under Mongol-Ilkhanate conditions in Azerbaijan; and that the community was sufficiently literate, organised, and self-aware to produce a formally dated liturgical manuscript. The community was naming itself — and the name it chose was apostolic.[EN9]

The manuscript's survival is its own story. It was brought to Rome in the 1560s by a Chaldean Catholic Metropolitan of India who was deported by the Portuguese Inquisition and sent to prove his orthodoxy. He brought ancient manuscripts as evidence that his community was not heretical but apostolic. Vatican Syriac MS 22 survived in the Vatican Library through the very period — after the Synod of Diamper in 1599 — when the Portuguese were burning Syriac texts across Kerala. It was saved from the Portuguese by the Portuguese.

### The Consolidation of the Western Coast (1329–1450)

In 1341 CE, an exceptional flooding of the Periyar river silted up or destroyed the ancient harbour at Kodungallur. The port through which Roman amphorae and Sangam-era pepper consignments had once moved was eliminated by a single geological event. The Thomas Christian community, centred on Cranganore since the tradition's earliest documented layer, shifted southward. The community consolidated at Quilon, sharpening the structural division in Kerala's port economy: Calicut under the Zamorin oriented toward the Arab-dominated Red Sea trade; Quilon under the Venad Swaroopam maintaining its connections with Persian Christian and Chinese merchant networks.

John of Marignolli, the Franciscan papal legate who visited Quilon in 1348–1349 on his return from the Mongol court, left the clearest documentary statement of the community's economic position. He described the Thomas Christians as 'masters of the pepper gardens' — the dominant commercial class controlling the spice trade of the Venad hinterland. This is not a community self-description. It is an external Latin ecclesiastical observer, with no interest in flattering the Thomas Christians (whom he elsewhere regarded as schismatic), recording what he saw. A community that controlled the pepper gardens was not a tolerated minority. It was a structurally essential component of the Indian Ocean's most commercially significant economy.[EN10]

By the mid-fifteenth century, the Thomas Christian community was sufficiently known in European geographic consciousness to appear on the most authoritative world map of the pre-Portuguese era. Fra Mauro's Mappa Mundi (c. 1450), the great Venetian cartographic synthesis, explicitly labels 'Christiani di San Thomaxo' on the Malabar coast. This is not a missionary document. It is a cartographic record, produced in Venice for commercial purposes, noting the Thomas Christians as established geographic fact — known in Venice without any Portuguese intermediary, nearly half a century before da Gama's landfall. Niccolò dei Conti, the Venetian traveller who visited the Malabar coast around 1440–1445, provided the last independent European witness to the community in full autonomy before the Portuguese arrival.[EN11]

### From Legend to Strategy: Dom João II and the Twin Expeditions (1487)

Meanwhile, in the West, the Prester John legend had completed its migration from Asia to Africa. By the fourteenth century, the figure was increasingly identified with the Christian kingdom that actually existed in East Africa: Ethiopia, whose Solomonic dynasty ruled a Coptic Christian state. Ethiopian embassies had reached Venice in 1402 and 1408. By the 1450s, the Genoese merchant-explorer Antoniotto Usodimare, operating off the West African coast on behalf of the Portuguese, speculated that he was no more than three hundred leagues from the kingdom of Prester John. The legend, originally rooted in genuine intelligence about central Asian and Indian Christians, had been geographically displaced — but the underlying strategic logic remained unchanged. Somewhere beyond Islam, a Christian kingdom existed. If it could be contacted, Islam could be outflanked.[EN12]

It was this logic — hardened by three centuries of legend, sharpened by the commercial imperative of bypassing the Arab-Venetian monopoly on the spice trade — that produced the Portuguese strategy of the 1480s.

In August 1487, Dom João II launched a twin expedition. By sea, Bartolomeu Dias sailed southward with three vessels, rounding the Cape of Good Hope by early 1488. By land, two Arabic-speaking members of the royal household, Péro da Covilhã and Afonso de Paiva, travelled overland via Spain, Italy, and Alexandria, tracing the spice trade to its source. Paiva died en route. Covilhã reached Cannanore and Calicut on the Malabar coast — the very ports where the Thomas Christian community was commercially dominant — before sending word back to Lisbon through a Jewish intermediary and then continuing to Ethiopia, where he remained for the rest of his life in the fabled lands of the Prester.[EN13]

The two missions were complementary halves of a single strategic vision: find the sea route to the spice trade and make contact with the eastern Christians. These were not separate goals pursued simultaneously. They were the same goal, fused together by three centuries of legend. The Letter of Prester John had placed the apostle Thomas's body in the same kingdom that produced gold, gemstones, and spice. The Portuguese were looking for both at once — because the legend had taught them that both were found in the same place.

### 'Christians and Spices' (1498)

On 20 May 1498, Vasco da Gama's fleet reached Calicut. When an Arabic-speaking intermediary asked the Portuguese what had brought them so far, the answer was immediate: 'We came to seek Christians and spices.'

The phrase is the most economical summary of the Prester John tradition's effect on European action. It is the end product of a causal chain that runs from the Seljuk closure (1037), through the garbled intelligence reaching the Crusader States (1141–1145), through the fabricated Letter with its marvellous eastern kingdom containing the body of St. Thomas (c. 1165–1170), through the legend's migration from Asia to Africa, through Dom João II's twin expeditions of 1487, to the deck of a Portuguese vessel off the Malabar coast.

What the Portuguese found was not what the legend had prepared them for. There was no Prester John. There was no kingdom of marvels. But there were Christians — the Thomas Christians of the Malabar coast, commercially dominant, institutionally embedded in the Cēra polity, ecclesiastically governed by bishops from the Church of the East, and calling their own seat the 'Apostolic See of St. Thomas.' And there were spices — pepper above all, controlled by the same community. The legend had been right at the fundamental level, even as every specific detail was wrong. There really were Christians in India. They really did control the spice trade. And they really did trace their origins to the apostle Thomas.

The collision between what the Portuguese expected and what they found — between a Latin church that assumed all legitimate Christianity must be papal, and an Indian church that had governed itself for a millennium under a Mesopotamian patriarch — would be the subject of the next phase of the Thomas Christian story. The Portuguese arrived to find allies. What they found instead was a community that needed no saving and desired no correction. The consequences of this misrecognition would unfold across the next century, through the Synod of Diamper, the burning of the Syriac manuscripts, and the schism that fractured the community into the confessional divisions that persist to the present day.

But that is a story for a later chapter.

5.1 — THE LETTER AND THE WORLD IT COULD NOT SEE

Sometime between 1431 and 1447, Pope Eugenius IV composed a letter addressed to his 'beloved Son in Christ, Thomas, the illustrious emperor of the Indians.' He was recommending his legate, the Franciscan Albert de Sartiano, whom he was dispatching somewhere eastward. The letter almost certainly never reached any Indian. But its address line is a document in itself.1

A century earlier, the papacy had written very differently. In 1330, Pope John XXII entrusted a bundle of letters to Jordanus Catalani, the newly consecrated bishop of Quilon, each addressed to a specific ruler or community in India — the Sultan of Delhi, the king of 'Lesser India,' the rulers of Quilon and Ethiopia, and, critically, the dominus Nascarinorum: the 'lord of the Nazaranis,' the leader of the Thomas Christian community at Quilon. This man appeared in Chinese documents of the same period as a sort of ambassador of the local Hindu monarch. The letter did not imagine a Christian emperor. It addressed a 'merchant-prince' embedded in a Hindu kingdom — a figure whose authority derived from copper-plate grants and guild headship, not from a crown. The Prester John legend was already in wide circulation — it had been for two centuries — but the papacy in 1330 still possessed something that held it in check: direct institutional contact. Jordanus had been there. He had baptised converts, buried colleagues, reported back in person. Against that kind of first-hand knowledge, a pope could still distinguish between imagination and the actual structure of Indian Christian life.2

By the time Eugenius wrote, that counterweight was gone. The mendicant networks that had once supplied Rome with living informants had been destroyed. The Latin diocese of Quilon had vanished; its last known bishop, one James, flickers in Avignon records for 1363–64 and then disappears. What had destroyed this infrastructure was not negligence but a sequence of catastrophes that amounted to the near-total annihilation of Eastern Christianity as a functioning institutional presence. Tamerlane's conquests in the second half of the fourteenth century obliterated the whole of Central Asian Christianity and the eastern provinces of the Persian Church. The Church of the East — which had once administered bishoprics from the Euphrates to the Chinese coast — was reduced to a remnant in the mountains of Kurdistan. In China, the Ming Dynasty overthrew the Mongols in 1368 and began persecuting the Church founded by John of Montecorvino; the last Catholic bishop of Zaytun was martyred the following year. Christians were expelled from Beijing. The Ming briefly projected enormous naval power under Zheng He, whose treasure fleets dwarfed anything Portugal would later launch — and then abruptly burned those fleets and sealed the coast, creating a vast power vacuum across the Indian Ocean. Meanwhile, the Ottoman advance swallowed Constantinople in 1453 — the second Rome, the seat of Eastern Christendom for a millennium. The ancient patriarchates of Antioch, Alexandria, and Jerusalem lay under Islamic sovereignty. The Crusader states were dust. From the Bosphorus to the South China Sea, the institutional architecture through which Rome had once maintained contact with Eastern Christians had been systematically dismantled — by the earlier Mongol cataclysm, the Timurid annihilation of Persian Christianity, Ming isolationism, and finally Ottoman expansion into the Mediterranean itself.3

No friar had returned from India within living memory. Every decade brought worse news from the East — ancient Christian centres falling, patriarchates submerged, the Ottoman frontier advancing toward Europe itself. In that atmosphere, the hope for a powerful Christian ally beyond the Islamic barrier did not need to be invented. It only needed to go uncorrected. The Catalan map of 1375 had already placed a 'King Stephen' in Greater India. Eugenius's 'illustrious emperor of the Indians' was the next step in the same drift — not a new fabrication, but what remained when the people who knew better were no longer alive to say so. This same drift — real but limited authority quietly inflating into imagined sovereignty across distance and silence — would repeat itself when the Portuguese arrived on the Malabar coast and received a red staff from the Nazranies. They would hear 'Christian king' because the legend had taught them to hear nothing else. The community's own traditions, rooted in the genuine memory of copper-plate privileges and merchant-prince leadership, would over the coming centuries absorb and amplify this European expectation — producing the full-blown Villarvattom dynastic narrative. But in 1498, the distortion was still Europe's, not India's.

And yet, at the very moment Eugenius was writing to a phantom sovereign, better information existed — just not in Rome. A Venetian merchant named Niccolò de' Conti was returning to Italy after a quarter-century in the East, having visited Mylapore and the Malabar coast and observed the Thomas Christians directly — comparing them to 'the Jews in Europe,' dispersed and commercially powerful but sovereign over nothing. His testimony was recorded by the papal secretary around 1450; within a few years, Fra Mauro's Mappa Mundi in Venice would label 'Christiani di San Thomaxo' on the Malabar coast as cartographic fact. But the information was siloed. Venice guarded its intelligence on the Indian Ocean as jealously as it guarded its spice contracts. The papacy possessed fragments — the memory of Jordanus's diocese, the echo of Conti's debriefing, the ghost of the Catalan map — but lacked the institutional continuity to assemble them into a coherent picture.4

For the papacy, this was not merely a strategic setback. It was an existential crisis of ecclesiastical geography. Yet Eugenius's letter to the phantom 'emperor of the Indians' was not, in fairness, an isolated act of fantasy. It belonged to the Council of Florence (1431–1449) — arguably the Western Church's most ambitious and genuine diplomatic attempt to heal the fractures of Christendom through theology rather than force. The council achieved, on paper, what centuries of schism had made unthinkable. In 1439, the decree Laetentur Caeli proclaimed union with the Greek Orthodox Church; the Byzantine Emperor John VIII Palaiologos himself attended the sessions and signed the agreement. Unions with the Armenian Church followed in the same year, with the Copts in 1442, with select Syriac and Mesopotamian communities shortly after. Eugenius's legate to 'Thomas' was an extension of this outreach — an attempt to draw even the distant Indian Christians into the Florentine reconciliation, alongside Greeks, Armenians, and Copts, within a restored universal Church.5

The attempt failed — comprehensively and consequentially. The Greek union was repudiated almost before the ink dried; the Byzantine clergy and populace rejected it as a capitulation extracted under duress, and the famous declaration attributed to the Grand Duke Loukas Notaras — that he would rather see the Sultan's turban in Constantinople than the cardinal's hat — captured the depth of Eastern bitterness toward Rome. The roots of that bitterness ran deep: the Great Schism of 1054 had hardened the institutional divide, but it was the catastrophic sack of Constantinople by Latin Crusaders in 1204 — the Fourth Crusade turned upon fellow Christians — that had made the wound unhealable. Constantinople fell to the Ottomans just fourteen years after Laetentur Caeli, and the union died with it. The Armenian and Coptic agreements produced no lasting institutional change. The Mesopotamian contacts — the very channels through which Rome might have reached the Thomas Christians' own patriarchate — dissolved into the chaos of Timurid and Ottoman conquest. Florence had offered reconciliation. The East, for reasons compounded of theological pride, justified resentment, and sheer political impossibility, had refused or been unable to accept.

It was the failure of Florence that made the Padroado thinkable. If theology could not reunite Christendom, commerce and cannon might — and the instrument was Portugal.

In 1452, watching the impending fall of Constantinople and the collapse of the last Christian bulwark in the eastern Mediterranean before the Ottoman advance, Nicholas V issued Dum Diversas. It was a papal bull granting King Afonso V authority to 'wage war against Saracens, infidels, unbelievers and all other enemies of Christ,' to 'invade, occupy, seize and subdue their kingdoms,' and to take possession of their belongings 'for the greater glory of the Divine Name.' By the time Romanus Pontifex consolidated these sweeping powers in 1455, Constantinople had already fallen. Nevertheless, the bull expressed the hope that the sea might become 'navigable as far as to the Indians who are said to worship the name of Christ,' so that the king might 'enter into relation with them, and incite them to aid the Christians against the Saracens and other such enemies of the faith.' In 1456, Callistus III extended the spiritual jurisdiction of the Order of Christ — of which the Portuguese Crown was grand master — over all regions conquered or to be conquered, from Cape Bojador to the Indies. By 1534, when Paul III erected the Diocese of Goa and formally defined the Padroado — the right of the Portuguese Crown to present candidates for all bishoprics, canonicates, and benefices in its Eastern territories — the juridical architecture was complete. The Portuguese did not merely claim to trade in the Indian Ocean. They claimed to govern its churches.6

These bulls fused two imperatives that had until now run in parallel: the Portuguese Crown's ambition to break the Venetian-Mamluk spice monopoly by reaching the Indian Ocean directly, and the papacy's desperate need to rebuild an ecclesiastical presence in the East after a century of institutional annihilation. Portugal would provide the ships, the soldiers, and the commercial infrastructure. The papacy would provide the legitimacy — the divine mandate to conquer, convert, and administer. The spice trade would fund the crusade; the crusade would justify the monopoly. And at the point where these two imperatives converged stood the phantom figure: the 'Indians who are said to worship the name of Christ,' who would be 'incited to aid' the war against Islam.

The phrase deserves to be read against the world the bull-writers actually inhabited. For four centuries, the Mediterranean experience of Christian-Muslim relations had been binary and existential. You either lived under the Cross or you lived as a dhimmi under the Crescent — a tolerated subject, taxed for your faith, barred from authority, reminded daily of your subordination. The Iberian Reconquista, completed in the very decade of Gama's voyage, had been a seven-hundred-year war to reverse precisely this condition. The Ottoman advance into southeastern Europe was producing the same binary in real time. The Portuguese and papal vocabulary reflected this lived reality with perfect consistency: the world contained Christians and it contained 'Saracens' and 'Moors' — the terms their documents would carry to India and apply, indiscriminately, to every Muslim they encountered on the Malabar coast, whether an Al-Karimi trader from Cairo or a Mappila merchant born in Calicut. What neither the papal bulls nor the Portuguese Crown could conceive of — because nothing in their experience had prepared them for it — was a place where Christians and Moors cooperated and competed as equals under the suzerainty of a Hindu king. Yet that is precisely what the Malabar coast was.

On the eve of the Portuguese arrival, the polity of Kerala was in the midst of a twofold transformation. Along the coast, maritime state units were forming around the major exchange centres — Calicut, Cochin, Cannanore, Quilon — each empowered by a spice-producing hinterland and increasingly taking precedence over the landlocked chiefdoms of the interior. The old Kulasekhara political edifice had long since fragmented into dozens of petty kingdoms and chiefdoms, and the debris of that fragmentation was being reassembled, piecemeal, by whichever ruler could command the revenues of maritime trade. Among these competing centres, Calicut had emerged as the dominant force — the only polity approaching a pan-Kerala dimension, its expansion carried out through weapons of war and tools of culture alike.7

The Zamorin's state-building was inseparable from his alliance with the Muslim mercantile communities — the very 'Moors' of Portuguese documents. He patronised their interests so thoroughly that he encouraged a male member from each Hindu family in his domains to be brought up according to the tenets of Islam. The Arab and Al-Karimi traders who had settled in Calicut following the Mamluk redirection of the spice trade through Cairo were not merely commercial partners; they were the financial and logistical backbone of the Zamorin's territorial expansion. As this Muslim-backed commerce pushed southward, the older merchant guilds — the Jews and the St. Thomas Christians who had operated in the commercial zones of south India for centuries — lost their prominence and were displaced. When the Zamorin attacked Cochin towards the end of the fifteenth century, the Nazranies who had traded there were stripped of their commercial rights, and those privileges were transferred to the Muslims. By the time the Portuguese arrived, Quilon was the only port where the Thomas Christian traders still retained some commercial predominance — and even that would not last.8

Into this volatile landscape the Portuguese would insert themselves — not as a neutral force but as a new variable in an existing rivalry. The king of Cochin would ally with the newcomers for reasons that had nothing to do with Prester John or the defence of Christendom. The Zamorin had already deprived him of many stately rights — including the right to strike coins and to roof his palace with tiles — and the Portuguese offered the military counterweight he needed. The commercial and political logic was entirely local: Cochin against Calicut, Perumpadappu Swarupam against Nediyiruppu Swarupam. The Portuguese were to be used as much as they were using. Their first vessels would come for trade only, but they carried the machineries of a mercantile state in which the government itself functioned as a trader. The site for their factory, obtained from the king of Cochin in 1500, would be the base for their initial economic operations — and a feeble nucleus for the power that would soon reshape the coast.9

It was with this enormously consequential yet historically necessitated blind spot that the Portuguese, under Vasco da Gama, sailed around the Cape of Good Hope toward the fabled lands of India — carrying the Padroado in one hand and the Prester John legend in the other, equipped with legal instruments that presumed jurisdiction over churches they had never seen, and a mental map in which the only two categories were ally and infidel. What happened when this apparatus collided with the actual world of the Nazranies — their temples mistaken for churches, their caste privileges mistaken for sovereignty, their merchant-princes mistaken for crusading kings — is the story of what follows.

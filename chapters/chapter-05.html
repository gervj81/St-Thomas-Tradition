THE ENCOUNTER THAT NEVER WAS

Portuguese Arrival and the Architecture of Misrecognition — 1498–1502

"You mistook one thing for another."

— The Zamorin's courtiers to the Portuguese, Calicut, 1498

Timeline: Prelude to the Portuguese
c. 1340 CE
The Rise of Calicut
The Zamorin shifts his seat from inland Ernad to the maritime centre of Calicut. With Al-Karimi and Arab merchant capital, he conquers neighbouring principalities, seizes the Mamankam festival at Tirunavai, and begins building the only state on the Malabar coast approaching a pan-Kerala dimension. The Muslim merchants, whom the Portuguese will later call 'Moors,' become the commercial backbone of this expanding state.
1249–1492 CE
The Diverging Crusades
While Spain remains locked in a terrestrial holy war against the Moors until the fall of Granada in 1492, Portugal completes its territorial Reconquista much earlier, in 1249. This crucial chronological gap pushes Portuguese crusading momentum off the land and onto the open ocean, forging the absolute "ally or infidel" worldview they will eventually carry around the Cape of Good Hope to the Malabar coast.
1453 CE
Fall of Constantinople to Ottomans
Constantinople falls to the Ottoman Turks, and the Byzantine Empire is extinguished. The gradual conquest that began with the Seljuk Turks culminates with the Ottomans. Anatolia, home to some of the earliest Christian churches, falls firmly under Islamic rule in the region that will later become known as Türkiye. This severs Europe's traditional access to the East.
1452–1456 CE
The Padroado Bulls
Dum Diversas (1452), Romanus Pontifex (1455), and Inter Caetera (1456) grant Portugal the authority to conquer, trade, and govern churches from West Africa to the Indies.
July 1497 CE
The Royal Commission
Building on the earlier 1487 scouting missions of Dom João II, the newly crowned King Manuel I of Portugal officially sanctions Vasco da Gama's historic voyage. Armed with the divine and legal mandates of the Padroado, Manuel commissions da Gama to command a fleet of four ships with a dual objective: to finally outflank the Islamic maritime monopoly by reaching the Indian Ocean, and to forge a crusading alliance with the fabled Eastern Christians.

5.1 — THE LETTER AND THE WORLD IT COULD NOT SEE

Sometime between 1431 and 1447, Pope Eugenius IV composed a letter addressed to his 'beloved Son in Christ, Thomas, the illustrious emperor of the Indians.' He was recommending his legate, the Franciscan Albert de Sartiano, whom he was dispatching somewhere eastward. The letter almost certainly never reached any Indian. But its address line is a document in itself.1

A century earlier, the papacy had written very differently. In 1330, Pope John XXII entrusted a bundle of letters to Jordanus Catalani, the newly consecrated bishop of Quilon, each addressed to a specific ruler or community in India — the Sultan of Delhi, the king of 'Lesser India,' the rulers of Quilon and Ethiopia, and, critically, the dominus Nascarinorum: the 'lord of the Nazaranis,' the leader of the Thomas Christian community at Quilon. This man appeared in Chinese documents of the same period as a sort of ambassador of the local Hindu monarch. The letter did not imagine a Christian emperor. It addressed a 'merchant-prince' embedded in a Hindu kingdom — a figure whose authority derived from copper-plate grants and guild headship, not from a crown.

The Prester John legend was already in wide circulation — it had been for two centuries — but the papacy in 1330 still possessed something that held it in check: direct institutional contact. Jordanus had been there. He had baptised converts, buried colleagues, reported back in person. Against that kind of first-hand knowledge, a pope could still distinguish between imagination and the actual structure of Indian Christian life.2

The Severed Connection

By the time Eugenius drafted his letter a century later, this direct institutional counterweight had vanished. The mendicant networks that had once supplied Rome with living informants had been destroyed. The Latin diocese of Quilon had vanished; its last known bishop, one James, flickers in the Avignon records for 1363–64 and then disappears. What had destroyed this infrastructure was not negligence, but a sequence of catastrophes that amounted to the near-total annihilation of Eastern Christianity as a functioning institutional presence.

Tamerlane's conquests in the second half of the fourteenth century obliterated the whole of Central Asian Christianity and the eastern provinces of the Persian Church. The Church of the East—which had once administered bishoprics from the Euphrates to the Chinese coast—was reduced to a remnant in the mountains of Kurdistan. In China, the Ming Dynasty overthrew the Mongols in 1368 and began persecuting the Church founded by John of Montecorvino; the last Catholic bishop of Zaytun was martyred the following year, and Christians were expelled from Beijing.

The ancient patriarchates of Antioch, Alexandria, and Jerusalem already lay under Islamic sovereignty. The Crusader states were dust. From the Bosphorus to the South China Sea, the institutional architecture through which Rome had once maintained contact with Eastern Christians had been systematically dismantled by the Mamluk consolidation of Egypt and the Levant, the Timurid annihilation of Persian Christianity in Central Asia, and Ming isolationism in China.3

No friar had returned from India within living memory. Every decade brought worse news from the East—ancient Christian centres falling, patriarchates submerged, and, most terrifyingly, the relentless advance of the Ottoman Turks. Unlike the Mamluks of Egypt, who controlled the southern spice transit but remained geographically contained, the Ottomans—a rapidly expanding Islamic military superpower surging out of Anatolia—were actively consuming the remnants of the Byzantine Empire. They were absorbing the Balkans and pushing a violent frontier directly toward the heart of Latin Europe itself. In that atmosphere of encroaching dread, the hope for a powerful Christian ally beyond the Islamic barrier did not need to be invented. It only needed to go uncorrected.

The Catalan map of 1375 had already placed a 'King Stephen' in Greater India. Eugenius's 'illustrious emperor of the Indians' was the next step in the same drift—not a new fabrication, but what remained when the people who knew better were no longer alive to say so. This same drift—real but limited authority quietly inflating into imagined sovereignty across distance and silence—would repeat itself when the Portuguese arrived on the Malabar coast and received a red staff from the Nazranies. They would hear 'Christian king' because the legend had taught them to hear nothing else. The community's own traditions, rooted in the genuine memory of copper-plate privileges and merchant-prince leadership, would over the coming centuries absorb and amplify this European expectation, producing the full-blown Villarvattom dynastic narrative. But in 1498, the distortion was still Europe's, not India's.

And yet, at the very moment Eugenius was writing to a phantom sovereign, better information existed—just not in Rome. The Venetian merchant we encountered in the previous chapter, Niccolò de' Conti, was returning to Italy with his Indian family after a quarter-century in the East. His testimony was recorded by the papal secretary around 1450. But the information was siloed. Venice guarded its intelligence on the Indian Ocean as jealously as it guarded its spice contracts. The papacy possessed fragments—the memory of Jordanus's diocese, the echo of Conti's debriefing, the ghost of the Catalan map—but lacked the institutional continuity to assemble them into a coherent picture.4

The Failure of Florence

For Rome, the loss of reliable eastern intelligence was not merely a strategic setback; it was an existential crisis of ecclesiastical geography. Yet Eugenius's letter to the phantom 'emperor of the Indians' was not, in fairness, an isolated act of fantasy. It belonged to the Council of Florence (1431–1449) — arguably the Western Church's most ambitious and genuine diplomatic attempt to heal the fractures of Christendom through theology rather than force. The council achieved, on paper, what centuries of schism had made unthinkable. In 1439, the decree Laetentur Caeli proclaimed union with the Greek Orthodox Church; the Byzantine Emperor John VIII Palaiologos himself attended the sessions and signed the agreement. Unions with the Armenian Church followed in the same year, with the Copts in 1442, with select Syriac and Mesopotamian communities shortly after. Eugenius's legate to 'Thomas' was an extension of this outreach — an attempt to draw even the distant Indian Christians into the Florentine reconciliation, alongside Greeks, Armenians, and Copts, within a restored universal Church.5

The attempt failed — comprehensively and consequentially. The Greek union was repudiated almost before the ink dried; the Byzantine clergy and populace rejected it as a capitulation extracted under duress, and the famous declaration attributed to the Grand Duke Loukas Notaras — that he would rather see the Sultan's turban in Constantinople than the cardinal's hat — captured the depth of Eastern bitterness toward Rome. The roots of that bitterness ran deep: the Great Schism of 1054 had hardened the institutional divide, but it was the catastrophic sack of Constantinople by Latin Crusaders in 1204 — the Fourth Crusade turned upon fellow Christians — that had made the wound unhealable. Constantinople fell to the Ottomans just fourteen years after Laetentur Caeli, and the union died with it. The Armenian and Coptic agreements produced no lasting institutional change. The Mesopotamian contacts — the very channels through which Rome might have reached the Thomas Christians' own patriarchate — dissolved into the chaos of Timurid and Ottoman conquest. Florence had offered reconciliation. The East, for reasons compounded of theological pride, justified resentment, and sheer political impossibility, had refused or been unable to accept.

It was the failure of Florence that made the Padroado thinkable. If theology could not reunite Christendom, commerce and cannon might — and the instrument were Portugal and Spain.

The papal response to this closing world was swift and legally sweeping. In 1452, watching the impending fall of Constantinople and the collapse of the last Christian bulwark in the eastern Mediterranean before the Ottoman advance, Pope Nicholas V issued Dum Diversas. It was a papal bull granting King Afonso V authority to 'wage war against Saracens, infidels, unbelievers and all other enemies of Christ,' to 'invade, occupy, seize and subdue their kingdoms,' and to take possession of their belongings 'for the greater glory of the Divine Name.' By the time Romanus Pontifex consolidated these sweeping powers in 1455, Constantinople had already fallen. Nevertheless, the bull expressed the hope that the sea might become 'navigable as far as to the Indians who are said to worship the name of Christ,' so that the king might 'enter into relation with them, and incite them to aid the Christians against the Saracens and other such enemies of the faith.' In 1456, Callistus III extended the spiritual jurisdiction of the Order of Christ — of which the Portuguese Crown was grand master — over all regions conquered or to be conquered, from Cape Bojador to the Indies. By 1534, when Paul III erected the Diocese of Goa and formally defined the Padroado — the right of the Portuguese Crown to present candidates for all bishoprics, canonicates, and benefices in its Eastern territories — the juridical architecture was complete. The Portuguese did not merely claim to trade in the Indian Ocean. They claimed to govern its churches.6

These bulls fused two imperatives that had until now run in parallel: the Portuguese Crown's ambition to break the Venetian-Mamluk spice monopoly by reaching the Indian Ocean directly, and the papacy's desperate need to rebuild an ecclesiastical presence in the East after a century of institutional annihilation. Portugal would provide the ships, the soldiers, and the commercial infrastructure. The papacy would provide the legitimacy — the divine mandate to conquer, convert, and administer. The spice trade would fund the crusade; the crusade would justify the monopoly. And at the point where these two imperatives converged stood the phantom figure: the 'Indians who are said to worship the name of Christ,' who would be 'incited to aid' the war against Islam.

The phrase deserves to be read against the world the bull-writers actually inhabited. For four centuries, the Mediterranean experience of Christian-Muslim relations had been binary and existential. You either lived under the Cross or you lived as a dhimmi under the Crescent — a tolerated subject, taxed for your faith, barred from authority, reminded daily of your subordination.

This worldview was forged during the Iberian Reconquista — the longest sustained war in European history against Islamic rule on European soil. Portugal had completed its own reconquest early, driving the Moors from the Algarve by 1249, but the crusading momentum did not stop at the water's edge. In 1415, King John I and his sons — including the prince who would become known as Henry the Navigator — crossed the Strait of Gibraltar and seized the North African stronghold of Ceuta, carrying the war against Islam onto the continent from which it had reached Europe. Ceuta was the hinge: it turned the Iberian crusade into an oceanic enterprise. Over the following decades, the Portuguese pushed steadily down the West African coast — Madeira, the Azores, Cape Bojador, the Gold Coast. Spain's trajectory converged from the opposite direction: Granada, the last Muslim emirate in Iberia, fell in January 1492; Columbus sailed in August of the same year. Both crowns were Atlantic-facing, shut out of the Mediterranean spice system controlled by Venice and Genoa, and both possessed crusading military orders — Portugal's Order of Christ, Spain's Order of Santiago — whose institutional resources were repurposed for oceanic expansion. When the two crowns began competing over newly discovered territories, the papacy intervened to divide the world itself. The Treaty of Tordesillas (1494), ratifying an earlier papal bull of Alexander VI, drew a meridian through the Atlantic: everything east of it — Africa, Asia, the Indian Ocean — belonged to Portugal and its Padroado Real; everything west — the Americas — belonged to Spain and its Patronato Real.

The symmetry was theological as much as geographical: the same binary that had structured the Reconquista — Christendom against Islam, the Cross against the Crescent — was now projected onto the globe. The Portuguese and papal vocabulary reflected this lived reality with perfect consistency: the world contained Christians and it contained 'Saracens' and 'Moors' — the terms their documents would carry to India and apply, indiscriminately, to every Muslim they encountered on the Malabar coast, whether an Al-Karimi trader from Cairo or a Mappila merchant born in Calicut. What neither the papal bulls nor the Portuguese Crown could conceive of — because nothing in their experience had prepared them for it — was a place where Christians and Muslims cooperated and competed as equals under the suzerainty of a Hindu king. Yet that is precisely what the Malabar coast was.

5.2 — THE FRAGMENTED COAST AND THE WAITING CHURCH

Meanwhile the polity of Kerala was in the midst of a twofold transformation. Along the coast, maritime state units were forming around the major exchange centres — Calicut, Cochin, Cannanore, Quilon — each empowered by a spice-producing hinterland and increasingly taking precedence over the landlocked chiefdoms of the interior. The old Kulasekhara political edifice had long since fragmented into dozens of petty kingdoms and chiefdoms, and the debris of that fragmentation was being reassembled, piecemeal, by whichever ruler could command the revenues of maritime trade. Among these competing centres, Calicut had emerged as the dominant force — the only polity approaching a pan-Kerala dimension, its expansion carried out through weapons of war and tools of culture alike.7

The Zamorin's state-building was inseparable from his alliance with the Muslim mercantile communities — the very 'Moors' of Portuguese documents. He patronised their interests so thoroughly that he encouraged a male member from each Hindu family in his domains to be brought up according to the tenets of Islam. The Arab and Al-Karimi traders who had settled in Calicut following the Mamluk redirection of the spice trade through Cairo were not merely commercial partners; they were the financial and logistical backbone of the Zamorin's territorial expansion. As this Muslim-backed commerce pushed southward, the older merchant guilds — the Jews and the St. Thomas Christians who had operated in the commercial zones of south India for centuries — lost their prominence and were displaced. When the Zamorin attacked Cochin towards the end of the fifteenth century, the Nazranies who had traded there were stripped of their commercial rights, and those privileges were transferred to the Muslims. By the time the Portuguese arrived, Quilon was the only port where the Thomas Christian traders still retained some commercial predominance — and even that would not last.8

Dispersed across this fragmented coast — through some sixty towns and villages in the territories of perhaps twenty rajas and a multitude of petty lords, in the upland region the Portuguese would come to call the Serra — the Thomas Christians were living a life that no European document had accurately described for over a century.9

The Two Pillars: Pepper and the Sword

The socio-economic foundation of the Thomas Christians rested on two pillars, and both were coming under mounting pressure. They were the dominant cultivators of pepper in the upland regions of central Kerala — the spice on which the entire Indian Ocean trade turned. Portuguese documents would later confirm that 'all the pepper was in the hands of the St. Thomas Christians' and that the majority of pepper exported to Portugal was sold by them. They had expanded this cultivation steadily through forest clearance along the fertile riverbeds, establishing new settlements and churches as went. But their older mercantile role in the coastal ports was contracting. The community that had once brokered the pepper that filled Zheng He's treasure fleets was being squeezed between its still-formidable agrarian base in the hinterland and its diminishing commercial position on the coast.10

Their military standing, however, remained formidable. Most Christian settlements maintained their own kalaris — schools for training in martial arts and fencing — run by Christian panikkars. Where no Christian kalari existed, young men trained alongside Nairs. Some Christian panikkars commanded eight to nine thousand disciples, both Christians and Nairs, receiving from their students the same deference as to a king. The rulers of Vadakkenkur (the inland principality centred on modern Thodupuzha-Muvattupuzha), Thekkankur (the inland principality centred on modern Kottayam) and Cochin depended heavily on the Christian fighting force for wars of defence and expansion. This martial capacity was not incidental to the community's social position; it was the guarantor of it. The copper-plate privileges — the elephants, the palanquins, the carpets, the toll-collection rights — were ultimately underwritten by the ability to field soldiers. A community that controlled the pepper gardens and could raise an army was not a tolerated minority. It was a structural necessity for any Kerala ruler who wished to hold territory and generate revenue.11

The depth of this integration was inscribed in the documentary record itself. The copper-plate tradition — the Tharisapally grants of 849 CE has been discussed in earlier chapters. But one grant in particular illuminates the position of the community on the eve of Portuguese contact and connects directly to a document we have already encountered. The Iravi Korthan plates (c. 1225 CE) record the elevation of a Christian merchant to the headship of the Manigramam guild at Cranganore — effectively making him 'Lord of the City,' with jurisdiction over the commercial apparatus of what was then still one of Kerala's premier ports. This was not an honorary title. Guild headship in the Kerala system carried quasi-governmental authority: control over trade disputes, market standards, and the right to represent the mercantile community before the ruler. It was, in all likelihood, this figure — or one very like him — that reports filtering back to Rome described, and that Pope John XXII addressed in 1330 as the dominus Nascarinorum, the 'lord of the Nazaranis,' when he sent his bundle of letters with Jordanus Catalani. The pope's Latin title was a precise translation of a real position: not a king, not a sovereign, but a guild lord with quasi-governmental commercial authority embedded in a Hindu political order. A century later, in Eugenius IV's letter, that same figure had inflated into 'the illustrious emperor of the Indians.' The drift from dominus to emperor — from guild lord to phantom sovereign — was complete.12

By the thirteenth century, the Manigramam of Kerala had evolved into a distinctly Christian commercial institution, linking the angadi trade of the interior — the market settlements clustered around churches along the riverine transport routes — to the wider channels of overseas commerce emanating from the ports. The Thazhekkad inscription of 1024 CE, found at the foot of the open-air cross before the Catholic church at Irinjalakuda, records King Rajasimhan conferring similar privileges on the Christian traders Chathan Vadukan and Iravi Chathan, both members of the Manigramam guild. What the copper plates reveal, taken together, is a community whose position rested not on royal favour alone but on a constitutional relationship between merchant guilds, local rulers, and religious institutions — formalised in metal, witnessed by signatories in Pahlavi, Kufic Arabic, and Hebrew, and enforced by the guilds themselves. The Portuguese, arriving with European feudal categories in which legitimacy flowed downward from a sovereign, would have no framework for understanding a system in which commercial corporations held quasi-governmental authority by contractual grant.13

The Anguish of Disconnection

Yet, while their commercial and martial integration within Kerala remained strong, ecclesiastically the community was in a condition of acute isolation — and this isolation would prove decisive in the encounter to come.

The prelates of the St. Thomas Christians had come from 'Babylon' — the patriarchal seat of the Church of the East in Mesopotamia — for as long as anyone could remember. Tradition was unanimous on this point, and documents confirmed it. But the supply of bishops had always been precarious, dependent on the immense distances of the Indian Ocean, the political stability of the patriarchate, and the willingness of the Catholicos to consecrate and dispatch men to a province at the far edge of his communion. By the late fifteenth century, all three conditions had deteriorated catastrophically. The Timurid and Ottoman conquests had devastated the Church of the East's heartland. The patriarchate itself had retreated into isolation, surviving only by turning the office into a hereditary mountain fiefdom. The sea routes were contested. The community had endured extended periods without bishops — and without bishops, the supply of ordained priests dwindled, sacraments went unadministered, and the liturgical and doctrinal life of the church slowly frayed at its edges.13

Around 1490, the community took matters into its own hands. A delegation of three men — including a decon named Joseph from Cranganore, set out from Kerala for Mesopotamia to petition the Catholicos, Mar Simeon, for bishops. One of the delegates died en route. The surviving two reached Mar Simeon at Gazarta (the present Cizre, between Mosul and Diyarbakir), in the church of St. Eugene. The Catholicos consecrated bishops for the Indian church and ordained Joseph as a priest. The delegation returned with at least two, and possibly as many as four or five, new bishops — Mar Thomas, Mar John, Mar Jabballaha, and Mar Denha among them. It was the last episcopal appointment the Church of the East would make for its Indian province before the Portuguese intervened to sever the connection.14

Joseph himself — the priest of Cranganore who had escorted the delegation — would later travel to Europe with Cabral's fleet in 1501 and provide, in Venice, the earliest detailed firsthand account of Indian Christian life to reach the West. But that is a story for the next chapter. What matters here is the picture his testimony and the bishops' own letter of 1504 paint of the community on the eve of contact: a church that understood itself as the 'Apostolic See of St. Thomas,' in communion with the Catholicos of the East, possessing its own liturgy in Syriac, its own calendar, its own fasting traditions, its own architectural conventions for church-building — and governing itself, in the prolonged absences of its foreign bishops, through the institution that had become the true centre of gravity of Thomas Christian life: the archdeaconate.15

▣ THE ARCHDEACON OF ALL INDIA
  • Title: Jathikkukarthaviyan — literally, 'the one responsible for the community' (also Archidiaconus Indiae Totius, 'Archdeacon of All India,' in Latin sources)
  • Origin: The office derived from the Church of the East, where the archdeacon held uniquely elevated status — a priest by ordination, but with authority exceeding that of any other cleric below the bishop. The archdeacon could examine a newly arrived bishop's competence and withhold permission to exercise episcopal powers if the bishop could not recite the canons.
  • Hereditary Succession: In India, the office passed within a single family — the Pakalomattam — and was conferred for life. Metropolitan John of India appointed Geevarghese (George) Pakalomattam as archdeacon in 1502.
  • Powers: The archdeacon was the administrative head of the entire community. He fixed the financial contributions each village owed for the maintenance of the foreign bishops. He governed the community during the bishops' absences — which were frequent and prolonged. He adjudicated disputes, maintained churches, collected funds for orphans and the poor, and ensured orthodoxy. His decisions were conditioned by the palli yogams — parish assemblies restricted to aristocratic families — whose veto was practically absolute.
  • Civic Standing: Reports to Rome consistently describe the archdeacon as 'the prince and head of all the Christians of St. Thomas,' greatly esteemed by the kings and princes of Malabar. Between 1576 and 1581, Pope Gregory XIII wrote five briefs directly to the archdeacon. The archdeacon himself wrote to the Jesuit visitor that 'the whole Christian community of Malabar rested on his shoulders, both in spiritual and temporal matters.'
  • Military Authority: The archdeacon could mobilise the community's martial resources. When the Portuguese attempted to arrest bishops, the archdeacon's men formed chaverpada — suicide squads — and the Panikkars placed themselves with drawn swords at the archbishop's elbows. The local kings, who depended on these same Christian soldiers, invariably backed the archdeacon against the Portuguese.
  • Significance: The archdeacon was a figure for which Portuguese ecclesiology had no category. He was not a bishop, not a king, not a feudal lord — yet he combined sacral, judicial, military, and administrative functions in a single office. It was this figure, not the foreign bishops who came and went from Mesopotamia, who held the community together across centuries of isolation. It was this figure whom the Portuguese would have to break if they wished to bring the Thomas Christians under the Padroado.16

The community the Portuguese would encounter, then, was not a flock waiting for a shepherd. It was a sophisticated, self-governing commonwealth — economically rooted in the pepper trade, militarily integrated into the Kerala feudal structure, ecclesiastically connected (however tenuously) to the ancient patriarchate of the East, and administratively governed by an archdeacon whose practical authority exceeded that of any bishop. It possessed its own copper-plate constitutional documents, its own liturgical language, its own martial infrastructure, its own representative assemblies, and its own understanding of its apostolic origins. It had maintained its Christian identity for over a millennium not through isolation or sovereignty but through strategic integration into a pluralist political ecology in which Christians, Muslims, Jews, and Hindus coexisted under shared commercial and political frameworks.

A Collision of Two Worlds

It was into this complex, pluralist landscape that the Portuguese finally arrived. Carrying an enormously consequential yet historically necessitated blind spot, they sailed around the Cape of Good Hope toward the fabled lands of India under Vasco da Gama. They arrived with the Padroado in one hand and the Prester John legend in the other, equipped with legal instruments that presumed jurisdiction over churches they had never seen, and a mental map in which the only two categories were ally and infidel.

But the Portuguese would not insert themselves as a sweeping crusading force; rather, they became a new, heavily armed variable in a pre-existing local rivalry. The king of Cochin allied with the newcomers for reasons that had absolutely nothing to do with Prester John or the defence of Christendom. The Zamorin had already deprived him of many sovereign rights—including the right to strike coins and to roof his palace with tiles—and the Portuguese offered the exact military counterweight he desperately needed. The commercial and political logic was entirely local: Cochin against Calicut, Perumpadappu Swarupam against Nediyiruppu Swarupam. The Portuguese were to be used just as much as they were using.

While their first vessels came ostensibly for trade, they carried the machinery of a militarized mercantile state in which the government itself functioned as a trader. The site for their factory, obtained from the king of Cochin in 1500, would be the base for their initial economic operations—and a formidable nucleus for the power that would soon reshape the coast.

What happened when this European apparatus collided with the actual world of the Nazranies—their temple-like architecture mistaken for Latin churches, their caste privileges mistaken for political sovereignty, and their merchant-princes mistaken for crusading kings—is the story of what follows.17

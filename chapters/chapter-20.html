Critical analysis of the royal, Knanaya, and Ramban Pattu traditions

The Rhythms of Reconstruction

"The traditions of the Malabar Church are not mere fables; they represent the social contract of a community that has navigated the changing tides of Indian history for two thousand years."

— A. Mathias Mundadan, History of Christianity in India

The long century of printing and litigation had transformed the St. Thomas Christians' historical heritage into a contested arsenal. Yet, beneath the polemical claims of the twentieth-century apologists lay a deeper, older rhythm of memory. To understand the modern identities of the Nazranis, we must turn back to the core traditions that survived the centuries: the royal charters of the ancient kings, the legendary migration of Knai Thoma, the sharp divide between Northists and Southists, and the historical ballads like the Ramban Pattu. By analyzing these traditions through the lens of modern critical scholarship, we can separate the historical core from the layers of apologetic reconstruction, returning to the balanced perspectives of A. Mathias Mundadan and István Perczel. 1

The Royal Tradition and the Plates

The primary anchors of Syrian civic status in Kerala were the copper-plate grants (cheppeds) issued by local Hindu rulers (perumals). The most famous of these were the Tarisappalli plates of Kollam (dated to AD 825) and the Iravi Korttan plates. These plates were not merely historical records; they were legal charters that granted the Christian traders royal privileges, tax exemptions, and high social standing, positioning them as protectors of local commerce. In the social order of medieval Malabar, these plates functioned as visible proof of caste precedence, giving the cattanars and their community the right to use royal ornaments, ride on elephants, and carry a red staff. The loss of several of these physical plates under Portuguese rule created a vacuum, forcing the community to reconstruct their contents from memory and translations, which became a vital part of the twentieth-century apologetics. 2

The Knai Thoma Tradition

Closely bound up with these royal grants was the Knanaya migration tradition, centered on the figure of Knai Thoma (Thomas of Kana). The traditional account dated this migration to AD 345, claiming that Thomas, a wealthy merchant from Jerusalem or Edessa, arrived in Malabar with a group of seventy-two families, a bishop, and several priests, obtaining seventy-two royal privileges from Cheraman Perumal. However, twentieth-century philological criticism by T. K. Joseph resolved a major demographic misunderstanding. Joseph demonstrated that the famous phrase "seventy-two families," long held to represent the Knanaya demographic base, was a misreading of the Malayalam phrase elupattirandu vituperu, which actually referred to "seventy-two royal privileges." Further, historical analysis by István Perczel and other scholars challenged the fourth-century chronology, suggesting that the migration details were later copied into the Tarisappalli plates of Kollam (825), collapsing the fourth-century date and revealing how the community backdated its origins to align with early ecclesiastical histories. 3

Northist-Southist Division

The Knai Thoma tradition served as the foundational narrative for the social and ethnic division between the Southists (Knanaya) and the Northists (non-Knanaya). The Southists claimed direct, pure descent from the original Mesopotamian immigrants who landed with Thomas of Kana, asserting that they had settled in the southern street of Kodungallur and maintained strict endogamy to preserve their racial purity. The Northists, on the other hand, represented the indigenous converts or those who settled in the northern street. This division, which was mapped onto spatial and ritual distinctions in Kerala, generated centuries of caste precedence disputes. The Southists constructed a history of aristocratic descent and royal favor, while the Northists countered by asserting their own local seniority and casting doubt on the Knanaya claims of pure descent. In the modern era, these divisions led to separate institutional structures, culminating in the personal diocese of Kottayam in 1911. 4

The Scholarly Consensus

Modern historical scholarship has moved away from both the literal acceptance of these traditions and their complete dismissal as fabrications, seeking a balanced middle ground. The church historian A. Mathias Mundadan pioneered a balanced stance, arguing that while these traditions cannot be treated as literal history, they contain a core historical memory: the arrival of Middle Eastern Christian traders in successive waves, the integration of these traders into the high-caste Hindu order, and the division of the community. Similarly, István Perczel has analyzed the pre-modern apologetic histories to show that these narratives were strategic documents. Rather than being simple fabrications, they were carefully constructed to defend the community's legal and ritual rights against Portuguese and Dutch encroachments, transforming oral traditions into a legal shield in canonical and temporal disputes. 5

The Ramban Pattu Analysis

Finally, the Ramban Pattu (the Song of Thomas Ramban) represents a crucial synthesis of family prestige, liturgical memory, and ecclesiastical defense. Supposedly written in 1601 by a cattanar of the Pakalomattom family, the song served to legitimize the family's hereditary claim to the archdeaconate and the leadership of the Serra. Critical analysis of the song reveals that its structured itinerary—an arrival in AD 52, the establishment of the seven churches, and the martyrdom in AD 72—was a late compilation. The song was updated and edited in the nineteenth and twentieth centuries to respond to the Western skeptics who denied the apostolic origin of the Malabar church. The Ramban Pattu was not an ancient document preserved unchanged in amber, but a living tradition that was reshaped to serve as a print weapon in the war for the past, illustrating how the rhythms of memory were constantly reconstructed to meet the demands of the present. 6

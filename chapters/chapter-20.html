T. K. Joseph, the community's charter, and the traditions under the lamp, c. 1900–1935

The Rhythms of Reconstruction

"The traditions of the Malabar Church are not mere fables; they represent the social contract of a community that has navigated the changing tides of Indian history for two thousand years."

— A. Mathias Mundadan, History of Christianity in India

Sometime in the early nineteen-twenties, a schoolmaster of the St. Thomas Christians sat down with his people's oldest songs and began, line by patient line, to take them apart. T. K. Joseph was a Nazrani himself, and no enemy of the community; but he had the philologist's cold eye, and what he found in the ballads that the community had just begun to print as the charter of its origin was not the first century. He found late language, borrowed words, muddled dates, and a saint's itinerary that would not hold still. He said so, in the learned journals, at the precise moment his community least wished to hear it. For in those same decades the Nazranis' antiquity was bearing real weight—their dignity among their neighbours rested upon it, and so, in the secular courts, did their claim to their own inheritance—and a heritage held up to the philologist's lamp is a hard thing to rest a living claim upon. This chapter follows Joseph's quarrel with the traditions, and the traditions' quarrel with him; and in doing so it returns, by the community's own memory, to the question with which this history began. 1

The Uses of the Past

To see why the quarrel cut so deep, one must recall what the preceding chapters have shown being done to the Nazrani past—not by the community alone, but to it. Long before Joseph, its antiquity had become a thing to be anxiously defended, under three pressures that Chapters 17 and 18 traced. The first was scholarly. The Travancore State Manual recorded four contested histories for the Christians and declined to adjudicate between them, opening an atmosphere of learned dispute in which skeptics doubted the tradition and defenders answered them. It was the controversy, not the hung verdict, that stung. The community met it, as Bernard of St. Thomas bitterly recorded, by hunting out old documents to turn remembered antiquity into documented history. The second pressure was confessional, and it fell on both communities alike, each in its own idiom. On the Catholic Syrians it pressed through the Latin account inherited from Diamper, which had cast them as Nestorian heretics rescued from error by the Portuguese, a weak flock that had survived only under Latin tutelage; and although Rome had at last begun to give them back to themselves—native vicars in 1896, a hierarchy of their own rite in 1923—that very dependence was the wound. On the Malankara Syrians it pressed from the other Western church: the Anglican missionaries who had come to reform them held, in effect, that the ancient church could not stand unaided and had to be shored up under the protection of the Church of England. Two rescues were on offer, one Latin and one Protestant, and both carried the same sting—the assumption that the Nazranis were a broken remnant who owed their survival to a Western patron. To answer the charge, to prove an orthodoxy older and less broken than the men who claimed to have saved them, the Pazhayakūr needed Nazrani antiquity as sorely as the Puthankūr did. 2

The third pressure was secular and institutional at once. The nineteenth century had turned the Nazrani past into evidence, and the early twentieth turned evidence into property. The great church cases—the Seminary suit that split off the Mar Thoma reformers in 1889, the vaṭṭippaṇam litigation that followed the schism of 1912—were fought, in the end, over questions a secular judge could answer: which was the ancient church, who was its lawful heir, and therefore who held its funds and its parishes. Under all of this at once, antiquity was no longer only a devotion. It had become a defence, and it had become a deed.

The same years hardened identity in other ways. The Catholicate raised at Niranam in 1912 hardened the claim to an indigenous church—native and self-standing, owing allegiance neither to Rome, nor to Chaldea, nor to Antioch. Its charter was the Apostle Thomas who had come to Kerala itself, the Thomas of oral tradition, asserted before the foreign Thomas of the Acts of Judas Thomas. The personal Vicariate of Kottayam, erected in 1911, hardened purity of descent, writing the Knanaya's claim of pure, endogamous blood into canon law and giving a marriage-boundary the force of a diocese. The tradition itself, meanwhile, passed in these same years from the palm-leaf and the tongue into print: E. M. Philip drew on them for the Malankara cause in 1908, P. U. Lukas gathered the ancient songs in 1910, and Bernard of St. Thomas printed the whole of the Ramban Pattu in his Mar Thoma Kristyanikal in 1916. What had been fluid became fixed; what was sung became a book; and a book could be cited. 3

None of this happened in a quiet room. The landscape of late nineteenth- and early twentieth-century Travancore was defined by an unprecedented wave of communal self-assertion, whose roots Chapter 17 traced back to the breakdown of old martial feudalism and the rise of the modern bureaucratic state. Where standing had once been guaranteed by royal patronage and traditional caste rank, the expansion of English education and government service turned identity into a political arena. In 1891, the Malayali Memorial had brought Syrian Christians, Nairs, and Ezhavas into a joint petition against the Tamil Brahmin monopoly in public office. Over the next three decades, that broad front fragmented into distinct, fiercely organized communal bodies. Driven by Dr. Palpu and inspired by Sree Narayana Guru, the Ezhavas mobilized under the SNDP Yogam (1903) to challenge civil exclusion; Mannathu Padmanabhan gathered the Nairs into the Nair Service Society (1914) to consolidate their social and economic standing; and the Thomas Christians, divided in rite but united in ambition, built their own press, schools, and political associations. By the nineteen-twenties and thirties, this communal awakening had reached its climax in the Vaikom temple-entry satyagraha (1924–25) and the Abstention (Nivarttana) agitation (1932–33), in which Christians, Ezhavas, and Muslims united to demand proportional representation in the state legislature against upper-caste dominance. In a state where numerical representation and social rank were being renegotiated line by line, the songs and copper plates were not antiquarian curiosities. They were the community's charter of precedence, its title-deeds held up before the court of public life. It was into this boiling cauldron that T. K. Joseph dropped his philology. 4

Action and Reaction

The reaction began, as such things often do, with a book meant as a defence. Bernard of St. Thomas TOCD had already printed the four hundred and forty-eight lines of the Ramban Pattu in the first volume of his Malayalam history Mar Thoma Kristyanikal (1916), setting the sung tradition into type where a manuscript in a sacristy could never go: into the court of scholarship. In 1924 he carried the case into English. His Brief Sketch of the History of the St. Thomas Christians opened by promising to point out "a few of the numerous local evidences attesting the preaching of St. Thomas in South India," and then marshalled them like exhibits: the Kodungallur landing in AD 52, the Dukhrana feast fixing the martyrdom at Mylapore on the third of July, AD 72, the baptism of Brahmin families and establishment of the canonical seven churches in the Thoma Parvam, and the Knanaya and St. Thomas Christian oral tradition—epically preserved in the Purātana Pāṭṭukaḷ—documenting the arrival of Thomas of Cana in AD 345. Here was the whole apologetic method in miniature: a liturgy and living folk-songs read as primary historical evidence for first-century events, and firm calendar dates lifted straight from them. What had been sung was now evidence, entered and citable. 5

Against this newly printed apologetic, T. K. Joseph turned his scalpel. A schoolmaster and Bachelor of Arts with a Licentiate in Teaching, he read a document the way the new historical scholarship taught him to: a text was to be dated by its own language, weighed against its other copies, and credited only so far as it could be empirically verified. He would give the better part of fifty years to the St. Thomas Christians and their records, until even the community's own writers—who had least cause to love his conclusions—came to call him the most engaged Malabar historian of his day. 6

Joseph's campaign against the apostolic origins of the Nazrani tradition unfolded in three distinct phases, each pitched a degree higher than the last. His was the temper of the age, and not Malabar's alone: across the Anglosphere the study of everything—history, language, philosophy—was turning empirical and analytical, learning to trust only what could be shown and to doubt whatever could not. Joseph was no philosopher, but his history breathed that air. The strength of the method was real, and it gave him his lasting corrections. Its hidden flaw was equally real, and it would betray him: a discipline that credits only what can be demonstrated slides, almost without noticing, into treating whatever cannot be demonstrated as false—the very fallacy this book named in its first part. Joseph's rigour and Joseph's overreach were two faces of one coin. 7

Phase One: The Song and the Scalpel (c. 1920–1928)

In his first phase, Joseph focused his scrutiny upon the community's sacred ballad, the Ramban Pattu. Held to the evidentiary standard of his generation, the song failed at the first test and at every test after. Its Malayalam was late and Sanskritised, its manuscripts disagreed with one another, and its dates wandered from copy to copy—and this was being offered as a record of the first century. What galled the philologist most was the song's own account of itself. It claimed to be a faithful transcription made in 1601 by one Thomas Ramban, forty-eighth in descent from a priest the apostle had ordained with his own hands, merely copying down a composition of the apostolic age. Joseph, with the Jesuit historian Henri Hosten—who shared his method and who printed Joseph's translation of the Song of Thomas Ramban in 1928—set out to prove that this was the legend within the legend. The surviving song was not an ancient original preserved through a copyist's hand; it was a late redaction (dating to c. 1601 or later), its language and its furniture centuries younger than the century it purported to describe. Where the traditionalists said the text had merely been copied, the philologist answered that it had been composed—and composed late. 8

Look at what the song actually narrates, and it is not the straight, simple itinerary the popular apologists make it out to be. In their précis the apostle lands in Malabar, converts the noble families, founds his churches, and passes east to Mylapore, where a lance makes him a martyr. The song itself is far less orderly. In places the Ramban Pattu is quite precise, bringing Thomas ashore in the west at Maliankara, dating the landing to the year 50, and keeping him there eight days. But it will not hold to that one landfall. Within eight days it sends him "in great hurry" across the peninsula to Mylapore, in the Chōla east, and it is there—not in Malabar—that the first sustained preaching unfolds; from Mylapore it even dispatches him to China and back within a few months, a round voyage no first-century traveller could have made in the time, though the eastward reach itself echoes an older tradition of a Thomas mission to China. 9 On his return the Chōla king commissions him to build a palace. Only on a second arrival at Maliankara, a year later, in 51, does the Malabar mission proper begin and the Kerala churches get founded. So much of the song's weight falls on the eastern, Chōla side that in some stanzas the apostle seems to enter the Chōla kingdom first and to reach Malabar only afterwards. Nor is this the song's confusion alone: the Leiden manuscript, the community's own oldest church-history (its making described in Chapter 14 above), is more emphatic still, bringing the apostle to Mylapore on the Coromandel coast first and only afterwards west to Māliyamkara, the Cēra king's country—the very reverse of the popular western landing. The apostle oscillates between the two coasts; the western landfall later fixed as Kodungallur the song leaves doubled and loose, dated now to 50, now to 51, with the familiar "52" supplied by later reckoning. 10

The canonical seven are no firmer, and not even fixed as churches: at each foundation the song plants a slīva, a cross, and a beth slīva, a cross-house, so that the seven are told now as churches and now as crosses, and counted now as seven, now as the popular "seven-and-a-half" (ezharappaḷḷikaḷ). That same Leiden church-history counts only six, and the notion of a settled seven cannot be found in any written source before the eighteenth century, the very place-names shifting from telling to telling. 11

It must be said plainly that the modern defenders of the song as a historical record, learned men, mistake this fluidity for depth. The maximalist among them reads the ballad as preserving a memory of the South Indian apostolate older than the North Indian memory of the Acts of Thomas. He rests the claim on a pair of speculative Syriac etymologies he calls "enough to prove" a now-lost second-century document—a South Indian Acts, perhaps, from which the extant Acts of Judas Thomas was itself redacted: a great weight on a slender thread. Others present the songs as genuine first-century primary sources while conceding, in the same breath, that their common source is the Acts; another reifies one late and variable list of seven churches into first-century fact; and yet another finds it a "strange coincidence" that the seven churches stand near the old Jewish colonies, and lets the coincidence stand as proof. The modest premise they share—that a late text may still carry old memory—is sound. The immodest conclusion they draw from it—that the memory therefore fixes dates, coasts, and numbers—is the overreach. 12

To show the song late, however, is to establish something real and something narrow: how the memory took its written shape. It is not to establish that no apostle came. The song proves neither the apostle nor his absence; it proves a community that held the belief early and deeply enough to sing it. That Joseph himself would fail to keep this distinction is the burden of his third phase. 13

Phase Two: The Charter in Copper and the Two Peoples (c. 1928–1935)

In his second phase, Joseph turned from the song to the charters, the copper plates (cheppeds) on which the community's civic memory rested; and here his work was at its most surgical, and its most lasting. In Malabar Christians and Their Ancient Documents (1929) he went first at the sacred founding date of the community. The AD 345 of the Thomas of Cana migration, he showed, was engraved on no plate at all. It was a calculation, made by Bishop Francis Roz in his Relação sobre a Serra of 1604, who reckoned some twelve hundred and fifty years back from his own day and arrived at the middle of the fourth century. The earliest Portuguese witnesses had in fact placed the migration far later, in the ninth century, alongside the founding of Kollam; and modern scholarship inclines the same way, reading the lost Cana plates as belonging to the same age, and very likely the same issuing world, as the Tarisāppaḷḷi plates of the 840s. 14

His scalpel went deeper into the same plates. The community claimed that seventy-two families had come with Thomas of Cana; Joseph showed the claim to rest on a misreading. The engraved phrase was eḻupattiraṇṭu viṭupēṟu, "seventy-two privileges"—viṭu, a privilege, confused with vīṭu, a house or family—a standard grant of civic honours of the kind conferred on the Añcuvaṇṇam and Maṇigramam trade guilds. At a stroke the great colony of founding households shrank to a body of merchant-settlers receiving the customary honours; and with the demographic base went much of the scaffolding of the Northist–Southist story, which had leaned on those seventy-two "pure" families for its arithmetic of descent. 15

For that story, too, will not stand as it is usually told. Precision matters here, because the tradition is commonly misstated. The Southists—the Knanaya—are a genuinely endogamous body, holding themselves the pure posterity of the colonists who came with Thomas of Cana, and guarding that descent by a strict rule of marriage. The Northists are not a matching caste of equal definition; they are the general mass of the St. Thomas Christians, the community at large from which the Knanaya keep themselves apart. There is one bounded descent-group and one body it distinguishes itself from, not two symmetrical races.

The received forms of the division are later constructions. Bishop Francis Roz, gathering scattered local memory into one ordered account in 1604, made it a matter of two origins: the Southists he traced to the colonists who had come with Thomas of Cana, the Northists to the apostle's older native converts and their noble houses. Popular tradition told it more luridly, as a matter of two wives of Thomas of Cana: the Southists born of his lawful Syrian wife, and so claiming pure Semitic blood, the Northists of a local Nair wife and the mingling that came of it. But the Leiden manuscript knows nothing of two wives and little of purity. As Emy Joy has shown, it describes only what the popular story omits—a settlement at Kodungallur whose shops and houses stood in two facing rows, one ranged to the south and one to the north (Tekkumbhāgam and Vaṭakkumbhāgam)—and it records, against the later claim of unmixed blood, that the incomers married among the natives. What the tradition dramatises as blood was in truth a marriage rule, kept on one side and not the other. 16

He did the like to the relics. The Pahlavi-inscribed stone crosses of Kottayam, Muttuchira, and Kadamattom were, he argued, medieval monuments of the Persian Christian migration rather than first-century relics, modelled on the great cross at Mylapore. This was source-criticism of a high order, and its core verdict has stood: the crosses belong to a seventh-to-ninth-century Persian horizon, not the apostolic age, and Perczel, Mundadan, and modern epigraphers keep the correction. The dating of one specimen was later contested—a Pahlavi cross recovered at Alangad in 1931 was proposed by the Parsi epigrapher B. T. Anklesaria as a fourth-century prototype for the rest—but that early date has not been accepted, and it leaves Joseph's main point standing. Had he laid down his pen here, he would be remembered simply as the father of critical Nazrani history. 17

Phase Three: The Six St. Thomases (c. 1935–1955)

But in his last phase the auditor turned prosecutor. To show a text late is one thing; to declare the event behind it never happened is another, and this Joseph now did. In Six St. Thomases of South India (1955) he affirmed, on the coins of Gondophares and the Acts, the northern, Parthian mission of the apostle—and denied the southern, Malabar one altogether. No record in Malayalam or Syriac from before the sixteenth century, he insisted, could be produced to prove that Thomas had landed in Malabar in AD 52 or founded the seven churches; all such claims rested on late oral songs and Portuguese-era back-calculations. The "St. Thomas of Malabar," he argued, was a composite stitched together from six different men named Thomas across the centuries; and the tomb at Mylapore, holiest of the community's sites, was not the apostle's at all but a Muslim's—one Thawwama, "born twin" in Arabic, a hunting-accident victim whom the Portuguese, after 1517, had mistaken for the saint.

The thesis was not spun from nothing: Joseph leaned on the two earliest lay witnesses to the tomb; but neither bears the weight he put on it. The inference outran the evidence, for the belief in a martyrdom in India was no invention of 1517 but a thousand years old. What he could fairly establish was narrower, and worth keeping: that the manner of the apostle's death was told in more than one way, and that whether Mylapore is the exact place of it the evidence cannot decisively settle. That is a real caution, not the unmaking of the apostle. 18

It was a rigorous, corrosive, and unprovable thesis, and it broke his own rule. The absence of early evidence, which his sober work had rightly treated as absence of proof, he now treated as proof of absence—the very error Part One had warned against. And here the fork that the first phase had hidden came at last into the open. Joseph's collaborator on the song, Henri Hosten, had given years to the very antiquities Joseph would dissolve. His Antiquities from San Thomé and Mylapore, printed posthumously in 1936, catalogued the churches, crosses, statues, and hundreds of tombstone inscriptions of Mylapore and the two Mounts to uphold the traditional site of the apostle's martyrdom and tomb; he worked the same materials as Joseph, the songs and the crosses and the local tradition, with a mastery of detail his own editor thought unsurpassed, and drew the opposite conclusion. Yet the defence proved no surer than the attack. Even Hosten, that editor conceded, "had not the time to collate facts and to weigh evidence," and the conclusions he drew stayed tentative. He did not live to see the quarrel joined; he had died in 1935, twenty years before Six St. Thomases, and his labour stood not as a proof but as the patient counter-weight to a thesis he never read.

There is an irony in the timing. By 1955, when Joseph pressed his scepticism to the point of outright denial, the very intellectual world that had licensed it was already giving way. The verificationist confidence that a claim which could not be empirically proved might be counted false—the temper of logical positivism, whose air his method breathed—was in these very years collapsing under its own contradictions, and the philosophers who had built it were beginning to abandon it. Joseph reached his most absolute negation just as the ground beneath his method was giving way. 19

The community's own historians, working after that confidence had passed, drew the line where Hosten's evidence did. Placid Podipara, and after him A. M. Mundadan, honoured the corrections—the privileges misread as families, the song's late redaction—and refused the conclusion: Joseph had earned the right to show the song late, and forfeited it when he pronounced that the apostle had never come to the South. 20

What Oral Tradition Can Bear

Step back, then, from the man to the memory he spent his life auditing, and set the two overreaches aside: the apologist who reads a calendar out of a ballad, and the skeptic who reads a saint out of existence. Ask only what the oral tradition, as a kind of evidence, can bear. Joseph's was the library's discipline, and it had a mirror-image in the very years and the very field he worked in: a way of knowing that trusted not the verifiable document but the felt force of a place. Albert Gille, visiting Palayur at about the time Joseph was framing his first critiques, stood among the old Hindu tanks and the temple-stones set into the church walls and listened to what the place still remembered: how the apostle had won over a Brahmin household by flinging water into the air and holding the drops suspended there, and how the unconverted kin had fled the spot as defiled. He felt the weight of a tradition no document could carry—and took the feeling for a proof of date, as surely as Joseph took silence for disproof. Neither the force of the place nor the lateness of the text can settle the first century. The honest ground lies between them, captive to neither. 21

The word ancient is in fact doing quiet and improper work throughout the literature on Nazrani history, and it repays a moment's attention, because unpicking it is the whole art of reading the sources. It slides between three different things: the antiquity of the community, which is real; the antiquity of the belief in a Thomas mission, which is old and independently attested; and the antiquity of the oral tradition's written texts and numbers, which is not established at all. Apologetic usage lets the first two vouch for the third, and then uses those same late texts and numbers to claim first-century calendar authority for the word "ancient." The circle closes on itself: because the people are ancient, the oral tradition must be old; and because the oral tradition is called ancient, its dates are taken as first-century fact. But a late oral tradition in print is evidence about the rememberers, not about the century it remembers. There is no way out of that circle from the inside. To lean on the tradition to prove its own antiquity, and on that antiquity to certify the tradition, is to argue in a ring; the only exit is a witness from outside the song. The Ramban Pattu's coasts and dates have no such outside witness, and without one they can corroborate nothing but themselves. 22

There is, besides, a deeper reason the oral tradition's coordinates cannot be first-century, and it comes not from theology but from the political history of Kerala itself—the ground traced in Chapter 3. The tradition, and the scholars who defend its continuity, quietly assume that the Kerala of the apostle and the Kerala of the medieval charters are one continuous country, so that a name or a caste or a king may pass unbroken from the first century to the ninth. Modern historiography denies exactly this. Between the Sangam coast of Muziris and the Roman trade—the world of the first three centuries—and the Cēra Perumal state of Mahōdayapuram that emerges only around the ninth, lies not a continuity but a rupture: the "hazy and gap-filled" post-Sangam centuries, the Kalabhra disruption, the slow arrival of the Nambūtiri Brahmins and the temple-and-grant order that were not present in the first century and did not hold power until the eighth. Even the language turns the point: Malayalam separated from Tamil only between the ninth and thirteenth centuries, so that a Malayalam ballad of a first-century apostle is, on its face, a later voice remembering an older event. This is why the furniture of the oral tradition gives it away—its Chōla Perumal, its king of Tiruvanchikkulam, its conversion of Brahmins by the thousand, its ordered castes. That is the ninth-century Perumal world, retrojected onto the first: the tradition's Tiruvanchikkulam a medieval landmark onto which the Sandaruk of the Acts of Thomas was mapped. 23

Set the whole account in order and a single, disciplined conclusion emerges, one that refuses both of the easy roads. The landing will not fix a coast; the date is arithmetic; the seven churches are a later count; the plates are a ninth-century charter, not a first-century one; the caste order in the oral tradition is the Perumal world remembered backward. On none of this can a dated first-century apostolate at Kodungallur be built, and the maximalists err in trying. 24

Yet none of this makes a St. Thomas landing at Maliankara just a fiction, or the Thomas of Cana migration a mere folk-tale. A first-century South Indian apostolic mission, or a fourth-century movement of Mesopotamian Christian merchant-families to the Malabar coast, is entirely credible on the trade-route evidence set out in Part One; Cosmas Indicopleustes attests a Christian community on the west coast by the sixth century, and the people who descended from that world could well have survived the Kalabhra interregnum to be layered over by later migrations. The plates and the song can attest a people and their faith; but the form in which any memory of those ancient times has reached us is a medieval form, and a medieval form cannot deliver a first-century, or even a fourth-century, calendar. What they carry is a charter of belonging, not a birth certificate. 25

What survives, then, is neither the apologist's charter nor the skeptic's dissolution, but the thing itself: a community, anciently and tenaciously, carrying a belief in its own apostolic founding, and localising that belief in the only Kerala it knew. The oral tradition is a witness to that belief. Whether the belief answers to a first-century fact is a question oral tradition was never able to settle—and so the question returns, as it must, to the ground on which Part One left it: the plausibility of the routes, the weight of the earliest witnesses, the long silence and what it does and does not prove. There the matter rests where an honest history must leave it: possible, anciently held, and unproven—which is not the same as disproved. The rhythms of reconstruction tell us, with great precision, how a people remembered. Whether the memory is also a record, they cannot say. A memory that can be neither proved nor disproved—that is a tradition. 26

A brief note on the philosophical air of these decades. The current that Joseph's method rode had its taproot in the logical and analytical turn that Gottlob Frege began with the Begriffsschrift (1879) and that Bertrand Russell and A. N. Whitehead carried forward in Principia Mathematica (1910–13): a drive to make reasoning exact, to ground knowledge in what could be shown, and to distrust whatever could not be. From this soil grew, only in the mid-1920s and 1930s, the logical positivism of the Vienna Circle (Moritz Schlick, Rudolf Carnap, Otto Neurath), carried into English by A. J. Ayer's Language, Truth and Logic (1936), whose "verification principle" held a statement meaningful only if it were true by definition or empirically verifiable, and dismissed the metaphysical and the theological as literally meaningless; a tradition that could not be run through the same lens was held, on this view, not merely unproven but empty. Joseph's own work, across those decades, shared that empirical instinct drawn from the wider analytical turn with its demand that a claim show its credentials or forfeit its standing, and its blind spot, the slide from "unverified" to "false."

Joseph affirmed the North Indian (Gondopharid) apostolate while denying the southern; see K. S. His non-martyr case rested on the two earliest lay witnesses to the Mylapore tomb, and neither bears the weight. Marco Polo (at Mylapore, 1292) reports the death as an accident—a fowler's arrow, aimed at peacocks, striking the praying apostle—but this account is widely judged a late and local legend, "far from original" and "probably of local origin," most likely a folk-etymology of the place-name (Mylapore, from Tamil mayil, "peacock," whence the peacock became the apostle's emblem); it stands at variance with the older and consistent tradition of a martyrdom. Joseph the Indian, the St. Thomas Christian who reached Lisbon, Rome, and Venice in 1501–02, does not report the manner of the death at all: his narrative—which survives not in Vallavanthara but in the sixteenth-century Italian, Latin, and Dutch redactions that Antony Vallavanthara critically edited in India in 1500 AD: The Narratives of Joseph the Indian (Kottayam, 1984)—says only that "the body of St Thomas" lies in the great church at Mylapore, where "he works many miracles" (Vallavanthara, 214). Joseph's inference of a non-martyrdom therefore rests on one garbled witness and one silent one. And Joseph the Indian, writing in 1501, already knew the Mylapore church as St Thomas's own shrine, so the identification of the tomb was no Portuguese confection of the later sixteenth century. On the Mylapore tomb and the Calamina–Mylapore identification, see Chapter 4 above; on Joseph the Indian, Chapter 6 above. The belief in a martyrdom in India is in any case far older than the Portuguese, held by the Church of the East and the Latin martyrologies alike; what remains genuinely undetermined is the exact site of the tomb, not the antiquity of the martyr-belief.

The verification principle proved self-undermining, being itself neither true by definition nor empirically verifiable, and through the 1950s it fell under sustained criticism—above all W. V. O. Quine's "Two Dogmas of Empiricism" (1951), which dismantled the analytic/synthetic distinction on which it rested, and Karl Popper's insistence that a theory is tested by falsification, not verification. The analytical temper outlived the doctrine, and its exponents, scattered from Nazi Europe to Britain and America, seeded the "analytic" philosophy that came to dominate the Anglosphere; but the verificationist criterion of meaning did not survive. Joseph's Six St. Thomases (1955) thus pressed the inference from "unverified" to "false" to its furthest reach at the very moment the philosophy that underwrote it was being abandoned. see footnote 7.

T. K. Joseph, the community's charter, and the traditions under the lamp, c. 1900–1935

The Rhythms of Reconstruction

"The traditions of the Malabar Church are not mere fables; they represent the social contract of a community that has navigated the changing tides of Indian history for two thousand years."

— A. Mathias Mundadan, History of Christianity in India

Sometime in the early nineteen-twenties, a schoolmaster of the St. Thomas Christians sat down with his people's oldest songs and began, line by patient line, to take them apart. T. K. Joseph was a Nazrani himself, and no enemy of the community; but he had the philologist's cold eye, and what he found in the ballads that the community had just begun to print as the charter of its origin was not the first century. He found late language, borrowed words, muddled dates, and a saint's itinerary that would not hold still. He said so, in the learned journals, at the precise moment his community least wished to hear it. For in those same decades the Nazranis' antiquity was bearing real weight—their dignity among their neighbours rested upon it, and so, in the secular courts, did their claim to their own inheritance—and a heritage held up to the philologist's lamp is a hard thing to rest a living claim upon. This chapter follows Joseph's quarrel with the traditions, and the traditions' quarrel with him; and in doing so it returns, by the community's own memory, to the question with which this history began. 1

The Uses of the Past

To see why the quarrel cut so deep, one must recall what the preceding chapters have shown being done to the Nazrani past—not by the community alone, but to it. Long before Joseph, its antiquity had become a thing to be anxiously defended, under two pressures that Chapter 17 and 18 traced. The first was scholarly. The Anglican, Protestant, and secular historians of the Nazranis had turned upon the apostle the corrosive suspicion the age brought to every tradition, and in G. T. Mackenzie's Travancore State Manual that suspicion took on the authority of the government's own record, which filed the community's origins as merely one of four contested opinions. To read in the official gazetteer that one's apostle was unproven was no seminar-room abstraction; it was a public demotion, and the community met it, as Bernard of St. Thomas bitterly recorded, by hunting out old documents to turn remembered antiquity into documented history. The second pressure was confessional, and it bore hardest on the Catholic Syrians. The Latin account inherited from Diamper had cast them as Nestorian heretics rescued from error by the Portuguese, a weak flock that had survived only under Latin tutelage; and although Rome had at last begun to give them back to themselves—native vicars in 1896, a hierarchy of their own rite in 1923—that very dependence was the wound. To answer the charge, to prove an orthodoxy older and less broken than the men who claimed to have saved them, the Pazhayakūr needed Nazrani antiquity as sorely as the Puthankūr did.

To these was added the most concrete pressure of all. The nineteenth century had turned the Nazrani past into evidence, and the early twentieth turned evidence into property. The great church cases—the Seminary suit that split off the Mar Thoma reformers in 1889, the vaṭṭippaṇam litigation that followed the schism of 1912—were fought, in the end, over a single question a secular judge could answer: who was the lawful heir of the ancient church, and therefore who held its funds and its parishes. Under all of this at once, antiquity was no longer only a devotion. It had become a defence, and it had become a deed. 2

The same years hardened identity in other ways. The Catholicate raised at Niranam in 1912 hardened the claim to an indigenous apostolic church—native and self-standing, its charter the Thomas who had come to Kerala itself, the Thomas of oral tradition, asserted before the foreign Thomas of the Acts of Judas Thomas. The personal Vicariate of Kottayam, erected in 1911, hardened purity of descent, writing the Knanaya's claim of pure, endogamous blood into canon law and giving a marriage-boundary the force of a diocese. The tradition itself, meanwhile, passed in these same years from the palm-leaf and the tongue into print: E. M. Philip drew on them for the Malankara cause in 1908, P. U. Lukas gathered the ancient songs in 1910, and Bernard of St. Thomas printed the whole of the Ramban Pattu in his Mar Thoma Kristyanikal in 1916. What had been fluid became fixed; what was sung became a book; and a book could be cited. 3

None of this happened in a quiet room. The landscape of late nineteenth- and early twentieth-century Travancore was defined by an unprecedented wave of communal self-assertion, whose roots Chapter 17 traced back to the breakdown of old martial feudalism and the rise of the modern bureaucratic state. Where standing had once been guaranteed by royal patronage and traditional caste rank, the expansion of English education and government service turned identity into a political arena. In 1891, the Malayali Memorial had brought Syrian Christians, Nairs, and Ezhavas into an unprecedented joint petition against the Tamil Brahmin monopoly in public office. Over the next three decades, that broad front fragmented into distinct, fiercely organized communal bodies. Driven by Dr. Palpu and inspired by Sree Narayana Guru, the Ezhavas mobilized under the SNDP Yogam (1903) to challenge civil exclusion; Mannathu Padmanabhan gathered the Nairs into the Nair Service Society (1914) to consolidate their social and economic standing; and the Thomas Christians, divided in rite but united in ambition, built their own press, schools, and political associations. By the nineteen-twenties and thirties, this communal awakening had reached its climax in the Vaikom temple-entry satyagraha (1924–25) and the Abstention (Nivarttana) agitation (1932–33), in which Christians, Ezhavas, and Muslims united to demand proportional representation in the state legislature against upper-caste dominance. 4

In such a climate, across Travancore and across India, the national movement was teaching every community to state its historical antiquity as a public claim to standing. For a people whose civic dignity rested on being older than the Portuguese, older than caste rivals, as old as an apostle, the songs and the copper plates were not antiquarian curiosities. They were the community's charter of precedence, its title-deeds in a state where numerical representation and social rank were being renegotiated line by line. It was into this boiling cauldron that T. K. Joseph dropped his philology. The offence it gave was not the offence of an unbeliever attacking a faith. It was the offence of a kinsman auditing the family's title-deeds in the very decade they were being held up before the court of public life.

The Man and His Scalpel

Joseph's method was narrow, exact, and—this is the part his opponents least liked to admit—often right. He did not deny that a copper-plate charter had existed; he reinterpreted it through rigorous Malayalam philology, demonstrating that the phrase long misread as "seventy-two families" (confusing vīṭu, house or family, with pēṭu) was in truth eṭupattiraṇṭu pēṭu, "seventy-two privileges," so that the demographic base the Knanaya had built on a mistranslation dissolved into a grant of civic honours. He did not invent forgeries where there were none; he dated the Malabar stone crosses as later imitations of the Mylapore cross, and treated the extant Ramban Pattu as a recent redaction whose language betrayed it. These were not acts of vandalism. They were source-criticism, and much of it has stood. 5

Two things about Joseph are usually forgotten, and both cut against the caricature of him as the man who "denied St. Thomas." First, he did not deny the apostle in India at all: he argued for the northern, Gondopharid mission that the coins and the Acts support, and denied only the southern, Malabar tradition—a targeted skepticism, not a blanket one. Second, he was no lone iconoclast working against the scholars; he worked among them. It was Joseph who translated the Song of Thomas Ramban, and the Jesuit Henri Hosten who printed it. Hosten shared Joseph's critical method and used it to defend the Mylapore tomb; Bernard of St. Thomas, whose printed song Joseph dismantled, defended the tradition from within the community. The debate, in other words, was not faith against reason. It was rigorous men reaching opposite conclusions from the same thin evidence—which is worth remembering before one is tempted to award the argument to either side. 6

The Charter in Copper

Begin where the community's civic memory begins, with the copper plates. In the social order of medieval Malabar a royal charter engraved on copper was the visible proof of standing: the right to the elephant and the palanquin, the ceremonial umbrella, the honours of caste. The Nazranis held such plates, and remembered more than they held; and around the memory of them the whole edifice of antiquity was raised. But the plates say less, and later, than the tradition wants. 7

Consider the date on which so much rests, the AD 345 of the Thomas of Cana migration. It is not written on any plate. It is a calculation—Francis Roz's calculation, made in his Relação sobre a Serra of 1604, where he reckoned some twelve hundred and fifty years back from his own day and arrived at the middle of the fourth century. A seventeenth-century bishop's arithmetic, repeated until it seemed a fact, is the origin of the community's founding date. The earliest Portuguese witnesses had in fact placed the migration far later, in the ninth century, alongside the founding of Quilon; and modern scholarship inclines the same way, reading the lost Cana plates as belonging to the same age and very likely the same issuing world as the Tarisāppaḷḷi plates of the 840s. The plates are genuine enough; it is the fourth-century date that is a construction. 8

The point is not that the migration is a fiction. A movement of Mesopotamian Christian merchant-families to the Malabar coast is entirely credible on the trade-route evidence set out in Part One, and the community that descends from it is real. The point is narrower and more exact: the plates can attest a people and a privilege; they cannot deliver a first-century, or even a fourth-century, calendar. What they carry is a charter of belonging, not a birth certificate. 9

Two Peoples, One Serra

Bound up with the plates is the tradition of the two peoples, the Southists and the Northists. Here precision matters, because the tradition is usually misstated. The Southists—the Knanaya—are a genuinely endogamous body, holding themselves the pure posterity of the colonists who came with Thomas of Cana, and guarding that descent by a strict rule of marriage that has held for centuries. The Northists are not a matching caste of equal definition; they are the general mass of the St. Thomas Christians, the community at large from which the Knanaya keep themselves apart. There is one bounded descent-group and one body it distinguishes itself from, not two symmetrical races. 10

The coherent form in which this is usually told—apostle's converts and noble families on one side, Cana's immigrants on the other—is again Roz's work of 1604, who gathered scattered local memories into a single ordered narrative to defend his see. That the frame is old is certain; Roz had it before any printed song. That it is a frame, shaped for a purpose, is equally certain. The tradition of the two peoples is thus neither ancient bedrock nor modern invention. It is a real and old memory that has been consolidated, at moments of institutional need, into the shape the moment required—by Roz in 1604, and by the apologists and the Vicariate three centuries later.

How the division itself arose is told, tellingly, in ways that will not be reconciled—and the scholarship parts company with the tradition on exactly this point. Roz made it a matter of descent: the apostle's converts and the noble families on one side, the immigrants of Thomas of Cana on the other. Popular tradition made it a matter of two wives, deriving the Southists from Cana's Syrian wife, "pure" and Semitic-blooded, and the Northists from a native wife and the mixing that followed. But the community's own oldest history, the manuscript now at Leiden, knows nothing of two wives and little of purity. It describes what the popular story omits—a settlement at Kodungallur whose shops and houses stood in two facing rows, one ranged to the south and one to the north—and it records, against the later claim of unmixed blood, that the incomers did marry among the natives. From that plain description of the ground Emy Joy reads the very origin of the names, the Southern-side and the Northern-side; while Perczel, working across the other apologetic manuscripts rather than this one, treats the division they mark not as a pedigree but as a caste distinction hardened within the Indian social order. And the asymmetry is the whole of it, for the two sides were never the same kind of thing. The Southists—the Knanaya—did keep a rule of endogamy, marrying within, though never with the flawless purity they later claimed and which the Leiden text quietly denies. The Northists kept no such rule: they are the general body of the community, and they married outward. One bounded, in-marrying line and one open, mingling mass—and it is just this that the crude fable of the two wives half-remembers, the pure Syrian wife standing for the line that kept itself apart and the native wife for the mixing that made the rest. The tradition, mythic as it is, is therefore not quite empty: what it dramatises as blood was in truth a marriage rule, kept on one side and not the other. The antiquity of the feeling is real; the antiquity of the genealogy is a later and defensive elaboration. 11

The Song, and the Word "Ancient"

All roads in this chapter lead back to the song—the Ramban Pattu, the Song of Thomas the Ramban—because it is the song, more than any plate, on which the apologetic case for a dated southern apostolate was built. And the song will not bear the weight. Its own colophon claims a transcription in 1601 by a Thomas Ramban who styles himself the forty-eighth (in some copies the four-hundred-and-eightieth) in descent from a priest the apostle ordained; but the language is modern and Sanskritised, the manuscripts disagree with one another, and the most sober judgement places the surviving redaction late and its date frankly uncertain. Where the tradition says "ancient," the text says "reworked." 12

The word ancient is in fact doing quiet and improper work throughout this literature, and it repays a moment's attention, because unpicking it is the whole art of reading these sources. It slides between three different things: the antiquity of the community, which is real; the antiquity of the belief in a Thomas mission, which is old and independently attested; and the antiquity of the text and its numbers, which is not established at all. Apologetic usage lets the first two vouch for the third—because the people are old and the faith older, the year 52 and the seven churches are received as old too. But a late song is evidence about the singers, not about the century it sings of. 13

Look at what the song actually narrates, and even the first fact—where the apostle landed—dissolves. The Ramban Pattu does not fix a single western landfall. It brings Thomas ashore at Maliankara in the year 50, keeps him there eight days, and sends him "in great hurry" across the peninsula to Mylapore on the eastern coast, where the first sustained mission unfolds; only on a second arrival, a year later, does he found the Kerala churches, and even then a Chōla king's business keeps pulling the story back east. The apostle oscillates between coasts; the very landfall the Portuguese later fixed as Kodungallur the song leaves doubled and loose, and dates now to 50, now to 51, with the familiar "52" supplied by later reckoning. The canonical seven churches are no firmer: the community's own oldest church-history, the manuscript now at Leiden, counts only six, and the notion of a fixed seven cannot be found in any written source before the eighteenth century, the very place-names shifting from telling to telling. The precision the apologists read from the song was put there by later hands. 14

It must be said plainly that the modern defenders of the song, learned men, mistake this fluidity for depth. The most sympathetic of them reads the ballad as preserving memory older than the Acts of Thomas, and rests the claim on a pair of speculative etymologies he calls "enough to prove" a lost second-century document—a great weight on a slender thread. Others present the songs as ancient primary sources while conceding, in the same breath, that their common source is the Acts; another reifies one late and variable list of seven churches into first-century fact; and yet another finds it a "strange coincidence" that the seven churches stand near the old Jewish colonies, and lets the coincidence stand as proof. The modest premise they share—that a late text may still carry old memory—is sound. The immodest conclusion they draw from it—that the memory therefore fixes dates, coasts, and numbers—is the overreach, and it is powered every time by that equivocal word ancient. 15

Joseph's error, it should be said with equal plainness, ran the opposite way. To show that the song is late, derivative, and variable is to establish something real and something narrow: how the memory took its written shape. It is not to establish that no apostle came. When Joseph let his valid demolition of the text harden into a denial of the southern mission, he crossed from source-criticism into the very trap this book named in its first part—the mistaking of absence, or lateness, of evidence for evidence of absence. The song proves neither the apostle nor his absence. It proves a community that held the belief early and deeply enough to sing it. 16

The Kerala That Was Not Yet There

There is a deeper reason the song's coordinates cannot be first-century, and it comes not from theology but from the political history of Kerala itself—the ground traced in Chapter 3. The tradition, and the scholars who defend its continuity, quietly assume that the Kerala of the apostle and the Kerala of the medieval charters are one continuous country, so that a name or a caste or a king may pass unbroken from the first century to the ninth. Modern historiography denies exactly this. 17

Between the Sangam coast of Muziris and the Roman trade—the world of the first three centuries—and the Cēra Perumal state of Mahōdayapuram that emerges only around the ninth, lies not a continuity but a rupture: the "hazy and gap-filled" post-Sangam centuries, the Kalabhra disruption, the slow arrival of the Nambūtiri Brahmins and the temple-and-grant order that were not present in the first century and did not hold power until the eighth. Even the language turns the point: Malayalam separated from Tamil only between the ninth and thirteenth centuries, so that a Malayalam song of a first-century apostle is, on its face, a later voice remembering an older event. This is why the furniture of the Ramban Pattu gives it away—its Chōla Perumal, its king of Tiruvanchikkulam, its conversion of Brahmins by the thousand, its ordered castes. That is the ninth-century Perumal world, retrojected onto the first. The Brahmin conversions and the noble founding families may well remember something true—the community's integration into the high-caste order of early medieval Kerala—but the thing they remember is medieval, and the collapse of it into the apostle's lifetime is the error. 18

Read this way, the song's Tiruvanchikkulam is best understood not as a first-century place but as a medieval one, the coast the singers knew, onto which an inherited story was mapped—the Sandaruk of the Acts of Thomas relocated to a landmark the community could point to. The tradition, carried inland with the Persian migrants who brought the Acts with them, was made Malayali. That is a real and moving thing to have happened. It is simply not a first-century itinerary. 19

And yet the ledger must not be closed too smugly, for there is a kind of evidence the library cannot weigh. Against the philologists stood men who insisted one had to go to the place. Gille's Palayur argument was not numismatic and not Syriac. It was topographical. One had to go there, he said, and feel the force of the place: the church standing among old Hindu tanks, the remembered temple-site, the local reluctance of Brahmins to take food from the village, and the persistent story that Thomas had broken the old ritual order there. Critically, none of this could prove A.D. 52. But it did something the library could not do. It showed why the tradition had survived as more than a paragraph in a Portuguese book. 20

What the Song Can Bear

Set the whole account in order and a single, disciplined conclusion emerges, one that refuses both of the easy roads. The landing will not fix a coast; the date is a bishop's arithmetic; the seven churches are a later count of an older six; the plates are a ninth-century charter, not a first-century one; the caste order in the song is the Perumal world remembered backward. On none of this can a dated first-century apostolate at Kodungallur be built, and Bernard and the maximalists erred in trying. But every one of these findings is a verdict on the sources, not on the event; a reworked memory testifies to the rememberers, and the negative leap from "the song is late" to "the apostle never came" is as unwarranted as the positive one it opposes. Joseph, having earned the first half of that sentence, forfeited it by insisting on the second. 21

What survives, then, is neither the apologist's charter nor the skeptic's dissolution, but the thing itself: a community, anciently and tenaciously, carrying a belief in its own apostolic founding, and localising that belief in the only Kerala it knew. The song is a witness to that belief, and to nothing about the calendar. Whether the belief answers to a first-century fact is a question the song was never able to settle—and so the question returns, as it must, to the ground on which Part One left it: the plausibility of the routes, the weight of the earliest witnesses, the long silence and what it does and does not prove. There the matter rests where an honest history must leave it: possible, anciently held, and unproven—which is not the same as disproved. The rhythms of reconstruction tell us, with great precision, how a people remembered. Whether the memory is also a record, they cannot say. 22

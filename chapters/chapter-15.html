The Carmelite Decades and the Native Revolt, 1724–1787

The Spark Awaited

"Were the St Thomas Christians to be subjects of Carmelites from Europe always?"

— The question abroad in Malabar, on the news of an archbishop's coming, 1786

The story of these decades runs along two channels at once, and the native clergy watched both. On the Catholic side the Carmelite vicars apostolic of Verapoly governed the Pazhayakūr against the standing Padroado claim; on the other the Puthankūr pursued its troubled search for valid orders, a search that would, in these same years, produce the first lasting fracture within the dissident body itself. The Catholic cattanars, watching the Puthankūr line acquire at last what looked like an unimpeachable episcopate from the East, had before them a standing reproach: the separated brethren had found a way to be governed by men of their own, while they themselves remained under Italians.

The Double Jurisdiction

The condition that Chapter 14 left in place did not improve with time; it settled into a chronic affliction. When Innocent XIII ratified the Portuguese Jesuits Pimentel for Kodungallur-Angamaly and Vasconcellos for Cochin in 1721, he confirmed in law the very thing that made the Serra ungovernable, two parallel hierarchies, the one Roman and Carmelite, the other Portuguese and Padroado, each claiming the same flock, each able to frustrate the other and neither able to prevail. The Carmelites held the ground, since the Dutch would tolerate no Portuguese bishop in their territory, and the practical government of the Catholic Thomas Christians lay with the vicars apostolic of Verapoly. But the Padroado claim was never surrendered, and the quarrel between the two ran on, by Tisserant's reckoning, until the Brief Multa praeclara of 1838 all but ended it. 1

For the native clergy the effect of this arrangement was not abstract. A church divided at the top between two foreign masters is a church in which no native voice carries, for each foreign party, competing for the people's loyalty, has reason to keep the native clergy dependent rather than to raise it up. The cattanars of the Serra were numerous, learned in their own rite, rooted in their parishes, and descended, many of them, from the old priestly families of the coast. Yet the path to a mitre was closed to them. The vicars apostolic were Carmelites sent from Europe; the seminary at Verapoly trained native priests but did not produce native bishops; and the long Latinising pressure that had begun at Diamper continued, in gentler forms, to treat the Syrian rite as a thing to be corrected rather than honoured. There was, besides, a particular loss. The Thomas Christians had once had a powerful native office, the archdeacon, the single head of the indigenous clergy who under the old Chaldean order had governed the community in all but sacramental matters and spoken for it before its foreign bishops. The schism of 1653 had split that office in two, the Puthankūr archdeacon becoming the dissident Mar Thoma, the Pazhayakūr keeping a Catholic archdeacon, Mathew Pallil, appointed in 1678. But on the Catholic side the office did not survive the Carmelite century. The Latin prelates, who had distrusted the archdeacon's near-autonomous power and hereditary succession, steadily reduced it, and at length left it without a successor, so that the archdeaconate of the Catholic Thomas Christians quietly became extinct, its native voice extinguished not by a single act but by disuse. The grievance was not that the Carmelites governed badly. It was that they governed as foreigners, that they had let the one native dignity lapse, and that no end to foreign government was in sight. 2

There were, besides, particular wounds. The memory of Mar Simon, the Chaldean bishop who had consecrated the first Carmelite vicar apostolic in 1701 and had then been hurried off under guard to Pondicherry to die, was kept alive in the native tradition as a case of foreign high-handedness, a bishop the Syrians might have made their own, removed lest they should. The intrusion of Bishop Gabriel was a further reminder of how Eastern Bishops from a Church of the East with shifting papal allegiance always made matters worse in serra. Each episode taught the same lesson to those disposed to learn it: that the people longed for a prelate of their own kind, and that the foreign administration stood between them and that desire. 3

The Society Undone

To understand what happened next, the eye must travel up once more from the backwaters of Malabar to the whole frame of Catholic Christendom, for the change that overtook the Serra in these years began far away, in a reversal of the very idea on which the Padroado had been built. That idea, as an earlier chapter set out, was the medieval doctrine of the two swords: the spiritual power wielded by the Church and the temporal power wielded by kings, held to be two distinct powers, the temporal in the older theory ordered to the spiritual and subordinate to it. The Padroado, however, had already strained that doctrine, for under the Habsburgs the distinction itself had begun to collapse: the Catholic crown gathered both swords into one hand, its prelates governing as viceroys and its viceroys as protectors of the faith, until the temporal and the spiritual were fused in a single authority that bore the sword of empire and the sword of the faith together. What came in the eighteenth century was the further and decisive step. The fusion did not merely continue; the order within it was reversed. Where the medieval theory had set the spiritual above the temporal, and the Habsburg system had run them together, the new age would set the temporal above the spiritual outright. 4

The Europe of these decades was completing the passage, begun at the Peace of Westphalia, from the old order of overlapping Christian authorities to one of self-conscious sovereign states, each claiming entire mastery within its own borders. In Spain that passage took a dynastic form: the extinction of the Habsburg line in 1700 and the accession of the French House of Bourbon replaced a loose composite monarchy of kingdoms and inherited privileges with a centralised, absolutist state on the French model. With the new dynasty came the doctrine that gave the reversal its name, regalism: the claim that the sovereign’s authority ran over the Church within his own dominions, so that bishoprics and religious orders were treated as instruments of the crown and not of any power beyond it. The throne no longer served the altar; the altar was to serve the throne. 5

In this reversal the Society of Jesus was the first and greatest casualty, for it was the supreme anomaly that a regalist state could not abide: a wealthy, disciplined, transnational order that answered, over the king’s head, to the pope alone. The order that had pioneered the accommodation of the faith to Indian forms, the method whose long contest with the Mendicants and the Padroado hierarchy was traced in earlier chapters of this history, and had grown rich, learned, and politically formidable in the doing, had by the mid-century gathered enemies in equal measure. One by one the Catholic crowns struck it down, each on the same regalist principle: Portugal expelled the Jesuits in 1759, France in 1764, and Bourbon Spain, under Charles III, from all its dominions in 1767; and the universal suppression of the Society by Clement XIV in 1773 was less the free act of the papacy than its surrender to the combined pressure of the sovereigns. The spiritual sword had been made to cut down its own sharpest blade at the bidding of the temporal. 6

This distant reversal reached the Serra through its Padroado arm, which was a Jesuit arm: it was the Society of Jesus that had held Kodungallur-Angamaly since Ribeiro forced his way in, kept the seminary at Ambazhakkad, and carried the Padroado claim through three generations of archbishops. When the Society fell, that arm fell with it, and the effect was precise. After John Louis Vasconcellos and Salvador dos Reis, who had held the see jointly until 1758, no Jesuit could be sent to fill it, for in the Portuguese dominions there were no longer any Jesuits to send; and when the universal suppression followed in 1773, there was no Society anywhere to revive the claim. The Padroado see that had cost the Carmelites half a century of struggle was emptied not by Rome, nor by the Dutch, nor by any victory of the vicars apostolic, but by the act of the Catholic king who had created it. The order that had stood at the Coonan Cross, that had held Ambazhakkad and Kodungallur against every reverse, simply ceased to exist. 7

The expulsion of the Jesuits was but one stroke of a wider revolution in Portuguese policy, for Pombal was bent on bringing the whole apparatus of the Church under the crown. In the same campaign of regalist reform he turned against the Inquisition itself: by a royal letter of 10 February 1774 the dread tribunal of Goa, which for two centuries had enforced orthodoxy and the statutes of purity of blood across Portuguese India, was abolished and its prisoners set free. It did not stay dead, for after Pombal's fall it was revived under Queen Maria I in 1778, with its powers curtailed, before being finally suppressed in 1812; but the abolition of 1774 marked the moment when the old machinery of exclusion, the same that had barred men of native blood from the orders and the priesthood, was for the first time dismantled by the very power that had built it. The change in the Serra and the change at Goa were of a piece: the Catholic crown of Portugal, in the second half of the eighteenth century, was turning against the instruments of its own missionary past. 8

It is worth pausing on the long arc this completes. The ideology that had barred men of native blood from the priesthood of their own church did not originate in Malabar, nor even in Asia. It was carried east, as an earlier chapter argued, from the Iberian peninsula itself, where the categories of Old Christian and New Christian, and the statutes of purity of blood that codified them, had been forged in the centuries of the Reconquista and turned, after 1492, into an engine of expansion that bore the Inquisition outward to Goa with the Portuguese flag. That this engine should be dismantled at last by the very crown it had served, and dismantled first not in Lisbon but at the colonial edge, in the Goa of 1774, is among the stranger reversals of the whole story: the periphery was purged of the apparatus of exclusion a generation before the centre, for the Portuguese Inquisition in the metropole was not suppressed until 1821. In Spain the same reckoning came later still and more convulsively, the Inquisition abolished and restored through four upheavals of the Napoleonic and liberal decades before its final suppression in 1834. The instrument that the Catholic monarchies of Iberia had built to police the purity of the faith, and had carried to the ends of their empires, was unmade in the end by their own successors, and the church of the Thomas Christians, which had felt the weight of that instrument in the bar against its native clergy, outlived it. 9

What this brought to the Serra was paradoxical. The Padroado claim did not die with its Jesuits, for the title of Kodungallur survived on paper and the Portuguese crown clung to it; but the living Padroado presence on the ground withered, and the practical field was left more than ever to the Carmelites of Verapoly. The vicariate that Rome had planted in 1659 as a temporary expedient under Dutch conditions now found its chief rival gone, and settled into something like a monopoly of the Catholic Serra. That monopoly, however, brought the native grievance to a point rather than relieving it, for the Carmelites were as foreign as the Jesuits had been, and now there was no second Catholic power to play against them. The cattanars who had chafed under two foreign masters found themselves, after 1759, chafing under one that could no longer be balanced by the other. And the suppression laid bare a deeper defect of the whole foreign mission, of which Neill makes much: in two centuries the Jesuits had brought forward scarcely a single native candidate for the priesthood, so that when the order was swept away there was no body of indigenous clergy trained to take its place. The reasons lay deep in the Iberian system traced in an earlier chapter, the statutes of purity of blood that barred men of the soil from the religious orders, a bar the Jesuits of the Padroado were bound to observe as much as any; but whatever its origin, the policy had carried within itself, all along, the seeds of its own undoing. 10 11

The First Fracture in Puthankūr

While the Catholic Serra chafed under its double government, the ordination crisis of Puthankūr came to a head in the middle of the century. Mar Thomas V, confronted by a doubt about the regularity of his own consecration, which had been performed by his predecessor alone without an Antiochian delegate, appealed repeatedly to the East for bishops who could set the matter right. His appeal was at length rewarded, but the manner of the rewarding showed how dearly a foreign episcopate must be bought. To bring the delegates at all, Mar Thomas V had to treat with the Dutch East India Company, undertaking to pay the whole cost of their voyage; and when three West Syrian prelates landed on 23 April 1751, the Company demanded twelve thousand rupees in passage-money and held the bishops in its custody until the sum should be paid. Mar Thomas V could not raise it. The Company then went to law against him, obtained his arrest for the debt, and threatened to deport him; he hid for a time, was taken, and was delivered up. In this extremity, with the very bishops who had come to mend his orders impounded for want of money, he consecrated his nephew as Mar Thomas VI by his own hand alone, the old irregular way once more. Money was at length gathered from the churches and the debt part-paid, and the delegates were released; but they had taken offence that the metropolitan had not come before them in person, and they refused, after all this, to reconsecrate him. 12

They stayed on in Malabar nonetheless, and from their presence the West Syrian reorientation of the Puthankūr Church, in its liturgy, its discipline, and the line of its bishops, became permanent. But the price of a foreign episcopate is foreign quarrels, and the very bishops who had come to secure the succession would in the end break it. 13

It was Mar Thomas VI, the nephew and successor of Mar Thomas V, who at last resolved the validity question, and the manner of its resolution is the most telling event in the whole history of the dissident line. In 1770 he submitted to a fresh consecration at the hands of the resident Antiochian bishops, receiving, as the accounts have it, all the orders from the tonsure upward, and took the name Mar Dionysius, the first head of the line to bear a Greek episcopal name in place of the native Mar Thoma. From the Catholic and academic point of view the act spoke for itself: a man who accepts reconsecration thereby concedes that his earlier orders were wanting, which was precisely the charge the Catholic sources had pressed against the whole line since 1653. The Puthankūr tradition reads it otherwise, as the perfecting of a dignity already real rather than the supplying of one that was void. The divergence is the same that has run through this history from the beginning, and it need not be adjudicated here; what matters for the present chapter is that the resolution and the rupture came together. 14

For in 1772, the very year in which Mar Dionysius secured his unimpeachable orders, the succession that had been bought at such cost began to turn against itself. Of the three delegates who had come in 1751, the senior, Mar Baselios, had died at Kandanad in 1763, leaving two, Mar Gregorios and Mar Ivanios; and it was Gregorios, the elder of the two, who now fell out with Mar Dionysius, by Neill's account over the manner in which the metropolitan provided for him in his old age. Acting alone, without the consent of his fellow delegate and without synod or mandate, Gregorios consecrated in secret a rival, the monk Kattumangatt Kurien of Mulanthuruthy, under the name Mar Koorilose, and designated him his own heir. The act was, as Neill observes, "highly irregular"; yet on a strict reading of canon law it had to be accepted as valid, for a true bishop, whatever the irregularity of the circumstances, had conferred true orders. Here was the deepest irony of the whole long search: the very plenitude of Antiochian orders that the Puthankūr had imported to settle all doubt now furnished, in a single disaffected bishop, the means of a fresh and unsettlable division. Mar Dionysius would not tolerate a rival, and Mar Koorilose was driven out, taking refuge at length at Thozhiyur, or Anjoor, in the territory of the Zamorin beyond the reach of the rajas of Travancore and Cochin. There he and his successors maintained a small independent church, the Malabar Independent Syrian Church, which traced its own orders directly from the Antiochian Gregorios and so could claim, in its own eyes, to be the truest continuation of the very succession the Puthankūr Church had imported at such cost. It was the first lasting division within the dissident body, and it carried a sharp irony forward into the next century: the little church of Thozhiyur, cast out as a rival, would in time become the reservoir of valid orders from which the Puthankūr Church itself, unable to consecrate its own metropolitans, would more than once be obliged to draw. 15

A Native Archbishop, and a Death at Goa

The spark, when it came, came from the Catholic side, and from an unexpected quarter, a genuine attempt to give the Thomas Christians a prelate of their own race. In the 1770s two able Syro-Malabar priests, Joseph Kariattil and Thomas Paremmakkal, were sent to Rome to plead the cause of their community against the Carmelite administration and to ask for native government. They were precisely the kind of men the cattanars had longed to see raised up, learned, Roman in loyalty, and Malayali in blood. Their mission was long, and to those at home it seemed unaccountably delayed; the priest who had gone with them warned the people that the delegation was being detained in Europe, and the suspicion took root that the foreign religious were working to keep native men from the episcopate. 16

In the end the cause succeeded, and Joseph Kariattil was raised to the dignity of Archbishop of Kodungallur-Angamaly, a native metropolitan at last. The news that he had reached India and was on his way home set the Serra alight with hope. Then, at Goa, on his way to take up his see, Kariattil died, in September 1786, of a malignant fever. To the Portuguese authorities it was a natural death. To the Thomas Christians, primed by years of suspicion and by the warning that had come from Europe, it was nothing of the kind: they believed their archbishop had been done away with by the religious from Europe, poisoned to prevent a native from governing the Serra. Whether the belief was just cannot now be determined, and the contemporary evidence does not sustain it; but in the history of a grievance it is not the truth of a charge that moves men, it is their conviction of its truth. The conviction was total, and the agitation it raised was such that it put fear into the heart of the Archbishop of Goa himself. 17

To calm the storm the Archbishop of Goa made an unexpected move. He appointed Kariattil's travelling companion, Thomas Paremmakkal, the other native priest of the Roman mission, as administrator, or governor, of the vacant archdiocese of Kodungallur-Angamaly. The Syrians were elated; one of their most cherished dreams, a metropolitan of their own race, seemed within reach at last. And Paremmakkal, a subtle man placed in an impossible position, played a double game that Tisserant records without flattery: before his own people he supported the belief that Kariattil had been killed, the better to make the appointment of any new Carmelite vicar apostolic impossible, while before the Portuguese authorities he held the contrary view. The Syrian people, for their part, petitioned the Portuguese to lay Paremmakkal's own name before the Holy See as their bishop. 18

The Angamaly Padiyola

It was in this heat, with a native administrator at the head of Kodungallur-Angamaly, a dead archbishop believed murdered, and the long resentment of sixty years brought at last to the boil, that the cattanars of the Serra assembled and drew up the document remembered as the Angamaly Padiyola of 1787. In it the representatives of the Syrian Catholic clergy set down their grievances against the foreign administration and bound themselves to seek the government of native prelates, refusing to submit any longer to a rule that kept men of their own race from the episcopate of their own church. It was not a break with Rome; the cattanars remained Catholic, and meant to. It was a revolt against the foreign manner of Rome's government, an organised insistence, in writing and on oath, that the Thomas Christians be ruled by their own. 19

The immediate crisis was settled, in that same year, by the King of Travancore, in whose dominions most of the parties now lived and to whose arbitration the dispute between the missionaries and the Syrian communities was at length referred. We know of the settlement from the letter that the vicar apostolic Pianazzi and his four remaining missionaries sent to Propaganda on 7 May 1787, a letter that incidentally measures how thin the foreign mission had by then become; for of the once considerable Carmelite establishment only the vicar apostolic and four priests remained, and one of those four was the learned Austrian orientalist Paulinus of St Bartholomew, whose count of the Malabar parishes in this period, eighty-four Catholic and thirty-two dissident, with the Catholics still divided between the jurisdictions of Kodungallur-Angamaly and Verapoly, gives us our clearest census of the divided church. 20

The settlement of 1787 settled nothing fundamental. Paremmakkal governed Kodungallur-Angamaly as administrator until his death, but no native succession was secured; the desire for a metropolitan of their own race was deferred, not satisfied; and the controversies between the Carmelites and the Padroado, which had set the whole condition of grievance in place, would drag on for another half-century until the Brief Multa praeclara of 1838. What the Padiyola did was not to win its object but to declare it, to put on record, in the voice of the native clergy itself, that the Thomas Christians regarded the foreign government of their church as a thing to be ended. That declaration, once made, did not unmake itself. The aspiration to native government, raised to open expression in 1787, would run on through the whole of the nineteenth century, surfacing again and again until, far beyond the bounds of this history, it was at last met. 21

So the spark that Chapter 14 foresaw fell, in the end, not on the dry resentment of the Carmelite decades alone, but on a particular grief, a native archbishop dead at Goa and believed murdered, that gathered all the older resentment into itself. The two channels of this chapter met in that moment. The Puthankūr Church had shown, by 1772, that a people might secure bishops of its own and pay for them in schism; the Catholic cattanars, in 1787, declared that they too would be governed by their own, though they meant to pay no such price and to remain within the Roman communion while they sought it. The one revolt was a long time coming and a long time bearing fruit. But the seed planted at the Coonan Cross more than a century before, that the Thomas Christians were a church and not a mission, and would in the end insist on being governed as one, had now broken surface on both sides of the divide at once. 22

That the delegates remained in Malabar after 1751 and that from their continued presence the West Syrian reorientation of the Puthankūr Church became permanent: Neill II; E. M. Philip, The Indian Church of St. Thomas, chs. xviii–xix. Mar Baselios died at Kandanad in 1763; Mar Gregorios and Mar Ivanios remained, and it was they who would reconsecrate Mar Thomas VI in 1770 (note 14 below) and, in Gregorios’s case, set the Thozhiyur line going in 1772 (note 15 below).

On the reconsecration of Mar Thomas VI by the resident Antiochian bishops in 1770, his receiving thereby all the orders and taking the name Mar Dionysius (Dionysius I): Neill II, who notes that Mar Dionysius thereby acquired "an unexceptionable episcopal consecration." On the two readings of that act, the Catholic and academic view that reconsecration concedes the invalidity of the prior orders, and the Malankara view, given by Philip, that it merely "perfected" a dignity already real, see Chapter 13 above, note 28, and the standing reservation entered there. That Mar Dionysius is the first head of the line to bear a Greek episcopal name rather than the native Mar Thoma is noted by Malankara historiography itself. The date of the reconsecration is given as 1770 by Philip and as 1772 by some authorities, the later date being that of the consecration of Mar Koorilose; see note 15 below.

On the Thozhiyur secession of 1772: in the year Mar Dionysius received his unexceptionable consecration, the Antiochian bishop Mar Gregorios, dissatisfied with Mar Dionysius, consecrated in secret the monk Kattumangatt Kurien of Mulanthuruthy as Mar Koorilose and designated him his heir; the consecration was, in Neill's judgement, highly irregular, performed without synod or mandate, yet on a strict interpretation of canon law to be accepted as valid. Mar Koorilose, driven out by Mar Dionysius, took refuge at Thozhiyur (Anjoor) in the Zamorin's territory, founding the church later known, by the court verdict of 1862, as the Malabar Independent Syrian Church: Neill II; e-GEDSH, "Malabar Independent Syrian Church" (Beth Mardutho); and the official history of the Malabar Independent Syrian Church (misc.co.in), which describes the consecration as "secret but canonically legitimate" and Mar Koorilose as Gregorios's "sole heir." The Malankara mainline tradition, by contrast (E. M. Philip), regards the consecration as performed without the knowledge or approval of Mar Dionysius and the senior delegate, and so as irregular. On the later recourse of the Malankara Church to the Thozhiyur succession, when Mar Philoxenos II of Thozhiyur consecrated three Malankara metropolitans in 1816–1825, a story beyond the bounds of this chapter: e-GEDSH, as above.

On the mission of Joseph Kariattil (Cariati) and Thomas Paremmakkal to Rome, sent to plead the cause of the Syro-Malabar community and to seek native government: Tisserant, 96; and the Varthamanappusthakam of Paremmakkal Thoma Kathanar (1785; English translation by P. J. Podipara, 1971), the foundational native narrative of the mission and of the Pazhayakūr cause, written by one of the two delegates. On the warning carried home that the delegation was being detained in Europe, and the suspicion it bred: Tisserant, 96.

On Joseph Kariattil's elevation to Archbishop of Kodungallur and his death at Goa in 1786 of a malignant fever, and on the popular conviction that he had been poisoned by the European religious to prevent a native from governing the Serra: Tisserant, 96 (chronology at 200: "Joseph Cariati dies at Goa 1786"). Tisserant records the belief as belief and does not endorse it; the contemporary evidence does not sustain the charge of murder. The author's observation that in the history of a grievance the conviction of a wrong moves men more than its truth is offered as comment, not as a finding of the sources.

On the appointment of Thomas Paremmakkal as administrator (governor) of the archdiocese of Kodungallur by the Archbishop of Goa, to calm the agitation: Tisserant, 96. On Paremmakkal's double game, supporting before his own people the belief that Kariattil had been killed, the better to obstruct the appointment of a Carmelite vicar apostolic, while holding the contrary view before the Portuguese authorities, and on the people's petition that his own name be laid before the Holy See: Tisserant, 96–97.

On the Angamaly Padiyola of 1787 as the organised written protest of the Syrian Catholic clergy, framed not as a break with Rome but as a demand for native government: Tisserant, 96–97. The document is named for the assembly at Angamaly; the dispute it expressed was, on Tisserant's account, settled in the same year by the King of Travancore. The fuller native record is the Varthamanappusthakam (see note 16). It should be noted that the Padiyola is here treated as the culmination of the grievances of the Carmelite decades; the framing of those decades as a steady accumulation of resentment is the author's, drawn from the temper of the sources.

On the settlement of 1787 by the King of Travancore, known from the letter of the vicar apostolic Louis Mary of Jesus (Pianazzi) and his four companions to Propaganda, 7 May 1787: Tisserant, 97. On the reduced state of the mission, the vicar apostolic and four missionaries only, one of them Paulinus of St Bartholomew (Philip Wesdin), in Malabar from 1775 to 1789: Tisserant, 97. On Paulinus's census of the parishes, eighty-four Syrian Catholic and thirty-two dissident, the Catholics divided between Kodungallur (20) and Verapoly (64): Tisserant, 97, citing Paulinus a S. Bartholomaeo.

On the controversies between the Carmelites and the Padroado hierarchy of Kodungallur and Cochin continuing until the Brief Multa praeclara of 24 April 1838, after which the apostolic vicariate of Verapoly was officially erected: Tisserant, 97–98. That the aspiration to native government declared in 1787 was deferred rather than satisfied, and ran on through the nineteenth century, is the burden of the chapters that follow in the larger history; its eventual settlement lies beyond the period treated here.

The closing reflection, that the Coonan Cross of 1653 had planted in both the Catholic and the dissident bodies the conviction that the Thomas Christians were a church to be governed as one, and that this conviction broke surface on both sides in the events of 1772 and 1787, is the author's synthesis of the two narratives traced in this chapter, and is offered as interpretation. On the Coonan Cross and its meaning, see Chapter 12 above.

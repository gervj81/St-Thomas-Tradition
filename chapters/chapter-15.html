The Carmelite Decades and the Native Revolt, 1724–1787

The Spark Awaited

"The Malabarians will obey the bishops of their own community."

— Thomas Paremmakkal, Varthamanappusthakam, c. 1785

The story of these decades runs along two channels at once, and the native clergy watched both. On the Catholic side the Carmelite vicars apostolic of Verapoly governed the Pazhayakūr against the standing Padroado claim; on the other the Puthankūr pursued its troubled search for valid orders, a search that would, in these same years, produce the first lasting fracture within itself. The Catholic cattanars, watching the Puthankūr line acquire at last what looked like an unimpeachable episcopate from the East, had before them a standing reproach: the separated brethren had found a way to be governed by men of their own, while they themselves remained under Italians.

The Double Jurisdiction

The condition that Chapter 14 left in place did not improve with time; it settled into a chronic affliction. When Innocent XIII ratified the Portuguese Jesuits Pimentel for Kodungallur-Angamaly and Vasconcellos for Cochin in 1721, he confirmed in law the very thing that made the Serra ungovernable, two parallel hierarchies, the one Roman and Carmelite, the other Portuguese and Jesuit, each claiming the same flock, each able to frustrate the other and neither able to prevail. The Carmelites held the ground, since the Dutch would tolerate no Portuguese bishop in their territory, and the practical government of the Catholic Thomas Christians lay with the vicars apostolic of Verapoly. But the Padroado claim was never surrendered, and the quarrel between the two ran on, by Tisserant's reckoning, until the Brief Multa praeclara of 1838 all but ended it. 1

For the native clergy the effect of this arrangement was not abstract. A church divided at the top between two foreign masters is a church in which no native voice carries, for each foreign party, competing for the people's loyalty, has reason to keep the native clergy dependent rather than to raise it up. The cattanars of the Serra were numerous, learned in their own rite, rooted in their parishes, and descended, many of them, from the old priestly families of the coast. Yet the path to a mitre was closed to them. The vicars apostolic were Carmelites sent from Europe; the seminary at Verapoly trained native priests but did not produce native bishops; and the long Latinising pressure that had begun at Diamper continued, in gentler forms, to treat the East Syrian rite as a thing to be corrected rather than honoured. There was, besides, a particular loss. The Thomas Christians had once had a powerful native office, the archdeacon, the single head of the indigenous clergy who under the old Chaldean order had governed the community in all but sacramental matters and spoken for it before its foreign bishops. The schism of 1653 had split that office in two, the Puthankūr archdeacon becoming the dissident Mar Thoma, the Pazhayakūr keeping a Catholic archdeacon, Parambil Chandy. His nephew Mathew Pallil held the office from 1678. But on the Catholic side the office did not survive the Carmelite century.

The Latin distrust of the archdeaconate ran deep, and it ran early; and it was not wholly without warrant. What troubled the Latin mind was the hereditary succession of the archdeaconate and its near-autonomous power sat ill with the Tridentine order of the Church, in which the bishop was to be supreme in his diocese and no office to descend by right of blood within a single family. Already in 1622 the first Jesuit archbishop of the Serra, Francisco Ros, had reported to his superiors that the archdeaconate was not a hereditary office at all, that the archdeacon held it by no right of inheritance, as some ignorant people, he wrote, wished to claim, and that the man then in the office was no kinsman of his predecessors. The assertion was untrue, as the historians of the Serra agree and as the evidence plainly shows: the dignity passed within a family and often from uncle to nephew, by a hereditary right that was the custom of the native Christians, and Ros himself conceded in the same report that the archdeacon called his predecessors his uncles. But the denial served a purpose, for to call the office elective rather than hereditary was to deny the native family its claim and to open the succession to the prelate's own hand; and from that first argument flowed the long reduction that followed. The Latin prelates pared it steadily away, and at length left the office without a successor, so that the archdeaconate of the Catholic Thomas Christians quietly became extinct, its native office extinguished not by a single act but by disuse. 23

There were, besides, particular wounds. The memory of Mar Simon, the Chaldean bishop who had consecrated the first Carmelite vicar apostolic in 1701 and had then been hurried off under guard to Pondicherry to die, was kept alive in the native tradition as a case of foreign high-handedness, a bishop the Syrians might have made their own, removed lest they should. The intrusion of Bishop Gabriel, a prelate of the Church of the East whose allegiance wavered between Rome and the Traditional patriarchate, was a reminder of another kind, that bishops sent from the Mesopotemian East no less than from the Latin West, could sow confusion as readily as they could remedy it in the Serra. Each episode taught the same lesson to those disposed to learn it: that the people longed for their own tradition and for rule by men of their own kind, and that the foreign administration stood between them and that desire. This longing would, again and again, produce factions within the Pazhayakūr in the years that followed. 4

The Society Undone

While the Jesuits and Carmelites fought their long contest for the Serra, the ecclesiastical and political forces gathering around the Society of Jesus were moving toward an outcome that would have seemed unthinkable half a century before. To understand it, the eye must travel up once more from the backwaters of Malabar to the whole frame of Catholic Christendom, for the change that overtook the Serra in these years began far away, in a reversal of the very idea on which the Padroado had been built.

The principle of the two swords, as an earlier chapter set out, was the medieval doctrine of the order of the two powers: the spiritual, wielded by the Church, and the temporal, wielded by kings, held to be distinct, the temporal ordered to the spiritual and subordinate to it. The Padroado, however, had already strained that doctrine, for under the Habsburgs the distinction itself had begun to collapse: the Catholic crown gathered both swords into one hand, its prelates governing as viceroys and its viceroys as protectors of the faith, until the temporal and the spiritual were fused in a single authority that bore the sword of empire and the sword of the faith together. The iluminismo that came in the eighteenth century carried that movement past fusion to its conclusion, for the order of the swords was now reversed. Where the medieval theory had set the spiritual above the temporal, and the Habsburg system had run the two together, the 'modern' age would set the temporal above the spiritual outright. 5

The Europe of these decades was completing the passage, begun at the Peace of Westphalia, from the old order of an overarching Christendom to one of self-conscious sovereign states, each claiming entire mastery within its own borders. In Spain that passage took a dynastic form: the extinction of the Habsburg line in 1700 and the accession of the French House of Bourbon replaced a loose composite monarchy of kingdoms and inherited privileges with a centralised, absolutist state on the French model. With the new dynasty came the doctrine that gave the reversal its name, regalism: the claim that the sovereign’s authority ran over the Church within his own dominions, so that bishoprics and religious orders were treated as instruments of the crown and not of any power beyond it. The throne no longer served the altar; the altar was to serve the throne. France herself would, within a few years, violently replace the aristocratic sovereign of regalism with the "common man" of nationalism, the new absolute sovereign of the state and the god to be worshipped at its altar, in the culmination of that same movement of illuminismo that the later historians would name the Enlightenment. 6

The Marquis of Pombal

Nowhere was this reversal carried through with more deliberate force than in Portugal, and its instrument was a single extraordinary minister. Sebastião José de Carvalho e Melo, who governed Portugal and its empire from 1750 to 1777 as chief minister to King Joseph I, and whom the king at length raised to be Marquis of Pombal, was the very type of the enlightened absolutist: the reforming statesman who gathered every power of the state into his own hand and bent it, without scruple, to the strengthening of the crown. He had formed his mind as Portuguese envoy in London and Vienna, where he came to see the Church of Rome as an obstacle to reform and to desire a national church dependent upon the state; and once in power he set about making that desire real, subordinating the religious orders, the Inquisition, and the bishops alike to the royal authority. 7

In this reversal the Society of Jesus was the first and greatest casualty, for it was the supreme anomaly that a regalist state could not abide: a wealthy, disciplined, transnational order that answered, over the king’s head, to the pope alone. The order that had pioneered the accommodation of the faith to Indian forms, the method whose long contest with the Mendicants and the Padroado hierarchy was traced in earlier chapters of this history, and had grown rich, learned, and politically formidable in the doing, had by the mid-century gathered enemies in equal measure. For Pombal Society of Jesus was not merely a rival power but a standing obstacle to progress controlling great centres of learning. Portugal expelled the Jesuits in 1759, France in 1764, and Bourbon Spain, under Charles III, from all its dominions in 1767; and the universal suppression of the Society by Clement XIV in 1773 was less the free act of the papacy than its surrender to the combined pressure of the sovereigns. The spiritual sword had been made to cut down its own sharpest blade at the bidding of the temporal. 8

This distant reversal reached the Serra through its Padroado arm, which was a Jesuit arm: it was the Society of Jesus that had held Kodungallur-Angamaly since Ribeiro forced his way in, kept the seminary at Ambazhakkad, and carried the Padroado claim through three generations of archbishops. When the Society fell, that arm fell with it, and the effect was immediate. After John Louis Vasconcellos and Salvador dos Reis, who had held the see jointly until 1758, no Jesuit could be sent to fill it, for in the Portuguese dominions there were no longer any Jesuits to send; and when the universal suppression followed in 1773, there was no Society anywhere to revive the claim. The Padroado see that had cost the Carmelites half a century of struggle was emptied not by Rome, nor by the Dutch, nor by any victory of the vicars apostolic, but by the act of the Portuguese Crown who had created it. 9

The expulsion of the Jesuits was but one stroke of a wider change in Portuguese Asia policy, for Pombal was bent on bringing the whole apparatus of the Church under the State. In the same campaign of regalist reform he abolished the Inquisition of Goa, which for two centuries had enforced orthodoxy and the statutes of purity of blood across Portuguese India; by a royal letter of 10 February 1774 its tribunal was dissolved and its prisoners set free. Revived under Queen Maria I after Pombal's fall, in 1778, it was at last suppressed for good in 1812. The abolition of 1774 marked the moment when the old machinery of exclusion, the same that had barred men of native blood from the orders and the priesthood, was for the first time dismantled by the very power that had built it. 1011

While Pombal freed Portuguese India of the Inquisition and the Amerindians of Brazil from bondage, he turned the Inquisition into a weapon against the Jesuits and chartered great monopoly companies to carry enslaved Africans into the Amazon. What moved him was never the dignity of persons but the power of the state. Where the dismantling of the old Church machinery served him, he dismantled it; where the enslavement of Africans filled the treasury, he enslaved. His was not the abolition of coercion but the redirection of it, the reversal of the two swords made flesh. 12

What Pombal's reforms brought to the Serra was a paradox of their own. The Padroado claim did not die with its Jesuits, for the title of Kodungallur survived on paper and the Portuguese clung to it; but the living Padroado presence on the ground withered, and the practical field was left more than ever to the Carmelites of Verapoly. The vicariate that Rome had planted in 1659 as a temporary expedient under Dutch conditions now found its chief rival gone, and settled into something like a monopoly of the Catholic Serra. That monopoly, however, brought the native grievance to a point rather than relieving it, for the Carmelites were as foreign as the Jesuits had been, and now there was no second Catholic power to play against them. The cattanars who had chafed under two foreign masters found themselves, after 1759, chafing under one that could no longer be balanced by the other. And the suppression laid bare a deeper defect of the whole foreign mission, of which Neill makes much: in two centuries the Jesuits had brought forward scarcely a single native candidate for the priesthood, so that when the order was swept away there was no body of indigenous clergy trained to take its place. The reasons lay deep in the Iberian system traced in an earlier chapter, the statutes of purity of blood that barred men of the soil from the religious orders, a bar the Jesuits of the Padroado were bound to observe as much as any; but whatever its origin, the policy had carried within itself, all along, the seeds of its own undoing. 13 14

The First Fracture in Puthankūr

While the Catholic Serra chafed under its double government, the ordination crisis of Puthankūr came to a head in the middle of the century. Mar Thomas V, confronted by a doubt about the regularity of his own consecration, which had been performed by his predecessor alone without an Antiochian delegate, appealed repeatedly to the East for bishops who could set the matter right. His appeal was at length rewarded, but the manner of the rewarding showed how dearly a foreign episcopate must be bought. To bring the delegates at all, Mar Thomas V had to treat with the Dutch East India Company, undertaking to pay the whole cost of their voyage; and when three West Syrian prelates landed on 23 April 1751, the Company demanded hefty fee in passage-money and held the bishops in its custody until the sum should be paid. Mar Thomas V could not raise it. The metropolitan pressed for so vast a sum fell back on the wealth of the parishes themselves, raising part of it by attaching and selling church property, as at Niranam. Catholic church of Kuravilangad, remembers in its own tradition that it foiled such an attempt upon it. The Company, meanwhile, went to law against him, obtained his arrest for the debt, and threatened to deport him; he hid for a time, was taken, and was delivered up. In this extremity, with the very bishops who had come to mend his orders impounded for want of money, he consecrated his nephew as Mar Thomas VI by his own hand alone, the old irregular way once more. The debt was at length part-paid and the delegates released; but they had taken offence that the metropolitan had not come before them in person, and they refused, after all this, to reconsecrate him. 15

They stayed on in Malabar nonetheless, and from their presence the West Syrian reorientation of the Puthankūr Church, in its liturgy, its discipline, and the line of its bishops, became permanent. But the price of a foreign episcopate is foreign quarrels, and the very bishops who had come to secure the succession would in the end break it. 16

It was Mar Thomas VI, the nephew and successor of Mar Thomas V, who at last resolved the validity question. In 1770 he submitted to a fresh consecration at the hands of the resident Antiochian bishops, receiving, as the accounts have it, all the orders from the tonsure upward, and took the name Mar Dionysius I. However the resolution or orders exacted a larger price- rupture. 17

In 1772, the very year in which Mar Dionysius I secured his unimpeachable orders, the succession that had been bought at such cost began to turn against itself. Of the three delegates who had come in 1751, the senior, Mar Baselios, had died at Kandanad in 1763, leaving two, Mar Gregorios and Mar Ivanios; and it was Gregorios, the elder of the two, who now fell out with Mar Dionysius, by Neill's account over the manner in which the metropolitan provided for him in his old age. Acting alone, without the consent of his fellow delegate and without synod or mandate, Gregorios consecrated in secret a rival, the monk Kattumangatt Kurian of Mulanthuruthy, under the name Mar Koorilose (Cyril), and designated him his own heir. The act was, as Neill observes, "highly irregular"; yet on a strict reading of canon law it had to be accepted as valid, for a true bishop, whatever the irregularity of the circumstances, had conferred true orders. Mar Dionysius would not tolerate a rival, and Mar Koorilose was driven out, taking refuge at length at Thozhiyur, or Anjoor, in the territory of the Zamorin beyond the reach of the rajas of Travancore and Cochin. There he and his successors maintained a small independent church, the Malabar Independent Syrian Church (1862). It was the first lasting division within Puthenkur, and it carried a sharp irony forward into the next century: the little church of Thozhiyur, cast out as a rival, would in time become the reservoir of valid orders from which the Puthankūr Church itself, unable to consecrate its own metropolitans, would more than once be obliged to draw. 18

A Native Archbishop, and a Death at Goa

The Catholic Serra by the 1770s decided to make shrewd and calculated amove for indegenous bishop. Two priests, Joseph Kariattil and Thomas Paremmakkal, were sent to Rome to plead the cause of their community against the Carmelite administration and to ask for native government. They were precisely the kind of men the cattanars had longed to see raised up, learned, Roman in loyalty, and Malayali in blood. Their mission was long, and to those at home it seemed unaccountably delayed; the priest who had gone with them warned the people that the delegation was being detained in Europe, and the suspicion took root that the foreign religious were working to keep native men from the episcopate. 19

Here lay the calculation on which the whole episode turned. A native bishop could not be had by the Roman road passing throgh the Carmelites who held the vicariate of Verapoly under Propaganda would never raise a man of the soil to be vicar apostolic over themselves; the door Kariattil planned to open, andd the road he chsoe to tread ran through the rival authority, the Padroado of the Portuguese crown, with its still-surviving but long-empty archiepiscopal see of Kodungallur-Angamaly. So it was by the Padroado road that Kariattil and Paremmakkal went, pleading their cause not only at Rome but at Lisbon; and it was the crown of Portugal, the old patron whose Jesuits the Thomas Christians had risen against at the Coonan Cross, that now answered the prayer against Propaganda. Passing through Lisbon, Kariattil was appointed by the Queen of Portugal (pombal had died in 1782) to the vacant see of Kodungallur-Angamaly, and Pope Pius VI sanctioned the appointment on 16 December 1782: a native metropolitan at last, by the patronage of the very power the Serra had once defied. The news that he had reached India and was on his way home set the Serra alight with hope. Then, at Goa, on his way to take up his see, Kariattil died, in September 1786, of a malignant fever. To the Portuguese authorities it was a natural death. To the Thomas Christians, primed by years of suspicion and by the warning that had come from Europe, it was nothing of the kind: they believed their archbishop had been done away with by the religious from Europe, poisoned to prevent a native from governing the Serra. Whether the belief was just cannot now be determined, and the contemporary evidence does not sustain it; but in the history of a grievance it is not the truth of a charge that moves men, it is their conviction of its truth. The conviction was total, and the agitation it raised was such that it put fear into the heart of the Archbishop of Goa himself. 20

To calm the storm the Archbishop of Goa made an unexpected move. He appointed Kariattil's travelling companion, Thomas Paremmakkal, the other native priest of the Roman mission, as administrator, or governor, of the vacant archdiocese of Kodungallur-Angamaly. The Syrians were elated; one of their most cherished dreams, a metropolitan of their own seemed within reach at last. And Paremmakkal, a subtle man placed in an impossible position, played a double game that Tisserant records without flattery: before his own people he supported the belief that Kariattil had been killed, the better to make the appointment of any new Carmelite vicar apostolic impossible, while before the Portuguese authorities he held the contrary view. The Pazhayakur, for their part, took up the same road their dead archbishop had taken, petitioning the crown of Portugal, not Propaganda, to lay Paremmakkal's own name before the Holy See as their metropolitan; for Paremmakkal now held, as administrator of Kodungallur-Angamaly, a Padroado office, and it was the Padroado, not the Carmelite vicariate, that might yet be made to yield them a bishop of their own blood. 21

The Angamaly Padiyola

It was in this heat, with a native administrator at the head of Kodungallur-Angamaly, a dead archbishop believed murdered, and the long resentment of sixty years brought at last to the boil, that the cattanars of the Serra assembled and drew up the document remembered as the Angamaly Padiyola of 1787. In it the representatives of the Syrian Catholic clergy set down their grievances against the foreign administration and bound themselves to seek the government of native prelates, refusing to submit any longer to a rule that kept men of their own race from the episcopate of their own church. It was not a break with Rome; the cattanars remained Catholic, and meant to. It was a revolt against the foreign manner of Rome's government, an organised insistence, in writing and on oath, that the Thomas Christians be ruled by their own. 22

The immediate crisis was settled, in that same year, not by Rome but by the King of Travancore, in whose dominions most of the parties now lived and to whose arbitration the dispute between the missionaries and the Syrian communities was at length referred. We know of the settlement from the letter that the vicar apostolic Pianazzi and his four remaining missionaries sent to Propaganda on 7 May 1787, a letter that incidentally measures how thin the foreign mission had by then become; for of the once considerable Carmelite establishment only the vicar apostolic and four priests remained, and one of those four was the learned Austrian orientalist Paulinus of St Bartholomew, we have encounterd before whose count of the Malabar parishes in this period, eighty-four Catholic and thirty-two dissident, with the Catholics still divided between the jurisdictions of Kodungallur-Angamaly and Verapoly, gives us our clearest census of the divided church. 23

The settlement of 1787 settled nothing fundamental. Paremmakkal governed Kodungallur-Angamaly as administrator until his death, but no native succession was secured; the desire for a metropolitan of their own race was deferred, not satisfied; and the controversies between the Carmelites and the Padroado, which had set the whole condition of grievance in place, would drag on for another half-century until the Brief Multa praeclara of 1838. What the Padiyola did was not to win its object but to declare it, to put on record, in the voice of the native clergy itself, that the Thomas Christians regarded the foreign government of their church as a thing to be ended. That declaration, once made, did not unmake itself. The aspiration to native government, raised to open expression in 1787, would run on through the whole of the nineteenth century, surfacing again and again until, far beyond the bounds of this history, it was at last met. 24

There remains a last turn of the screw, and it completes the irony. When the Padroado road too was blocked, when Paremmakkal died without the succession he had been put forward for, and Propaganda turned a deaf ear to the petitions from Lisbon, the Catholic Syrians fell back on the one expedient left to a Malabar church that wanted a bishop of its own and could not get one from the Latins: they looked east. They wrote to the Chaldean Patriarch in Mesopotamia, Joseph IV, exactly as the Puthankūr had written to Antioch; and the letter, received at Mosul by the metropolitan John Hormez while the patriarch was at Rome, brought forth in the end a consecration. After waiting in vain for instructions from Rome, Hormez consecrated an obscure priest of Puthenchira, one Paul Pandari, who came into Malabar under the name of Mar Abraham to be bishop of the Catholic Syrians. The intrusion failed as such intrusions did: he was not permitted to function, the affair displeased Rome, and it was quietly undone. But the meaning of it is hard to miss. The Pazhayakūr, who had kept the Roman obedience through a century and a half precisely to hold fast to an apostolicity that the East could not give them, had come at the last, in their hunger for a prelate of their own, to the very door the Puthankūr had gone to in 1751. Both halves of the divided church, the old allegiance and the new, ended the century reaching toward Mesopotamia for what neither Rome nor Lisbon would grant them, a bishop of their own people. 25

So the spark that Chapter 14 foresaw fell, in the end, not on the dry resentment of the Carmelite decades alone, but on a particular grief, a native archbishop dead at Goa and believed murdered, that gathered all the older resentment into itself. The two channels of this chapter met in that moment. The Puthankūr Church had shown, by 1772, that a people might secure bishops of its own and pay for them in schism; the Catholic cattanars, in 1787, declared that they too would be governed by their own, though they meant to pay no such price and to remain within the Roman communion while they sought it. The one revolt was a long time coming and a long time bearing fruit. But the seed planted at the Coonan Cross more than a century before, that the Thomas Christians were a church and not a mission, and would in the end insist on being governed as one, had now broken surface on both sides of the divide at once. 26

Yet even as the two halves of the divided church bent every thought upon the question of whose hands should ordain their bishops, that question was already being overtaken by a larger one: whose armies would rule the land they lived in. The powers that had framed the whole story of these chapters were passing from the stage. The crown of Portugal that had answered Kariattil’s prayer could no longer guard even its own see; the Dutch Company that had detained the delegates and dictated the passage-money had itself been humbled, decades before, by a native king upon its own coast. And now, while the cattanars swore their padiyola and their metropolitans reached toward Mesopotamia, a conqueror was descending from the hills of Mysore. In 1790 the troops of Tipu Sultan came into the heart of the Serra and burned the Jesuit college at Ambazhakkad, the seminary that had trained the Syrian clergy for more than a century; churches were sacked and whole congregations scattered along the coast. Behind the smoke of his armies a new European power was rising, one that would gather up what the Dutch had let fall. The Serra had contended for generations over who should be its bishop; it was about to be made to answer to a sovereign who cared nothing if it is Pope or Patriarch. 27

To follow the Thomas Christians into the century that came, we must first leave them, and look to the powers that were remaking the ground beneath their feet.

The framing of the suppression as an expression of the new sovereign state, rather than of any quarrel internal to the Church, is the author’s reading.

On Pombal (Sebastião José de Carvalho e Melo, 1699–1782), chief minister to King Joseph I of Portugal from 1750 to 1777, created Count of Oeiras and afterwards Marquis of Pombal: he is the classic figure of enlightened absolutism, the subject of Kenneth Maxwell's study tellingly entitled Pombal: Paradox of the Enlightenment. On his regalist and anti-ultramontane views, formed during his embassies in London and Vienna, his sense of the Roman church as an obstacle to reform, and his aim of a national church dependent upon the state: Jonathan Wright and Jeffrey D. Burson, eds., The Jesuit Suppression in Global Context, the chapter by Emanuele Colombo and Niccolò Guasti. The double image of Pombal as at once a Nero and a Voltaire is a commonplace of the historiography and captures the paradox that Maxwell's title names. On his hostility to Jesuit learning and his reform of education: Pombal condemned the schooling of the Society and, after closing its colleges, sponsored the Compendio historico do estado da universidade de Coimbra, which laid the decay of the university at the Jesuits' door (Wright and Burson, the same chapter); his commission of 1770 reorganized the University of Coimbra, long held to be centuries out of date, and the reform of 1772 added faculties of mathematics and natural philosophy, placing the new science at the centre of the curriculum.

On the suppression of the Society of Jesus: by the decree of the Marquis of Pombal of 3 September 1759 the Jesuits were expelled from Portugal and all its overseas dominions, their property confiscated; the measure extended throughout the Portuguese empire and so to the Padroado missions of India. The universal suppression followed on 16 August 1773 by the Brief Dominus ac Redemptor of Clement XIV. On the Jesuit archbishops of Kodungallur ending with Vasconcellos and dos Reis (1753-58), after whom the see could not be filled, see Tisserant, 188 (episcopal list). The point that the Padroado see was emptied by the act of the Portuguese crown itself, fourteen years before the universal suppression, is the author's.

On the Society of Jesus at the height of its power and the gathering hostility to it in Catholic Europe: the order that had carried the Padroado mission in Malabar (and, at Kodungallur and Ambazhakkad, the Padroado claim against the Carmelites) was by the mid-eighteenth century widely resented by the Bourbon courts as too wealthy, too independent, and too closely bound to Rome. The Jesuit method of accommodation to Indian forms, pioneered in the south by Robert de Nobili, is treated in the earlier chapters of this work. On the Jesuit tenure of Kodungallur-Angamaly through Ribeiro, Pimentel, Vasconcellos, and dos Reis, see Chapter 14 above, and Tisserant, 188.

On Pombal's abolition of the Goa Inquisition as part of the same regalist programme as the expulsion of the Jesuits: the tribunal was abolished by royal letter of 10 February 1774 and its prisoners released; it was revived under Queen Maria I in 1778, with reduced powers, and finally suppressed in 1812. On Pombal's reforms in India generally, including the raising of the Goan native clergy to legal equality with their European counterparts and the abolition of the Inquisition, see I. G. ZŽupanov, Jesuit Missions in Coastal and South India (1543–1773). The connection drawn here between the abolition of the inquisitorial machinery of purity of blood (on which see Chapter 9 above) and the wider turn of the Portuguese crown against the instruments of its own missionary establishment is the author's.

The reading of the abolition of the Iberian inquisitions as the closing of the long arc opened in Chapter 9, the purity-of-blood ideology of the Reconquista carried outward as an engine of expansion, is the author's synthesis. On the dates: the Portuguese Inquisition in the metropole was suppressed in 1821 (the Goa tribunal having been abolished in 1774, revived 1778, and finally suppressed 1812; see note 10 above). The Spanish Inquisition was abolished in four stages, by Joseph Bonaparte through the Decrees of Chamartín in December 1808, by the Cortes of Cádiz on 28 February 1813, again during the Trienio Liberal in 1820, and finally and permanently by a decree of the government of Martínez de la Rosa under the regent María Cristina in July 1834. The point that the apparatus of exclusion was dismantled at the colonial periphery (Goa, 1774) before the metropole is the author's observation.

In 1755 Pombal created the Companhia Geral do Comércio do Grão-Pará e Maranhão, a chartered monopoly over the trade of northern Brazil: Wright and Burson, eds., The Jesuit Suppression in Global Context, the Colombo and Guasti chapter. Pombal abolished the enslavement of the Amerindians of Brazil while the monopoly companies he founded supplied the Amazon with enslaved Africans in their stead, an arrangement that served the revenue of the crown and not any principle of human freedom; on this paradox of reform joined to coercion, Maxwell, Pombal: Paradox of the Enlightenment. The reading of Pombal's whole programme as a redirection rather than a dismantling of coercion, and so as the reversal of the two swords in the service of the sovereign, is the author's, and it governs the interpretation of the dismantling of the Inquisition and the purity-of-blood statutes in the paragraphs that follow.

On the foreign mission’s failure to raise up a native clergy, and the consequence of the suppression: Neill II, in his chapter on the suppression of the Jesuits, observes that in two centuries the Jesuits in India had brought forward hardly a single candidate for the priesthood, and that the suppression of the Order in 1773 “made it plain that the policy contained within itself the seeds of destruction”; he counts the withdrawal of the Jesuits in 1759 and the final suppression of 1773 among the major setbacks of the eighteenth-century missions (Neill I records the same absence of native priests up to 1773). Thekkedath, vol. II, notes the suppression of the Society in 1773 and its revival in 1814. On the deeper structural cause, the Iberian statutes of purity of blood (limpeza de sangue) that excluded men of native or “impure” descent from the religious orders, and which the orders of the Padroado, the Jesuits among them, were constrained to observe, see Chapter 9 above. The application of the point to the Carmelite as well as the Jesuit administration, and to the Syrian Catholics’ want of bishops of their own, is the author’s.

On the effect in Malabar: with the Jesuits gone the Padroado presence on the ground withered, though the title of Kodungallur survived, and the Padroado claim was not extinguished but only checked: the active conflict was all but ended, and the Verapoly vicariate officially erected, by the Brief Multa praeclara of 1838 (note 1 above), while the Cranganore jurisdiction itself, with Padroado appointees as late as the 1840s, was definitively suppressed only by the concordat of 1886. The practical government of the Catholic Thomas Christians, meanwhile, fell more completely than before to the Carmelite vicars apostolic of Verapoly. On the Carmelite method, the training of native priests in the Seminary of Verapoly and the continual visitation of the parishes, and on the chronic shortage of European missionaries (in 1770 only one father was able to visit the Syrian parishes): Tisserant, 93-94. The reading of the post-1759 Carmelite ascendancy as sharpening rather than relieving the native grievance, since the one foreign power could no longer be balanced against the other, is the author's.

On Mar Thomas V's doubt about his own consecration, performed by Mar Thomas IV alone without an Antiochian delegate, and his repeated appeals east: Neill II, on the Mar Thomas succession (Mar Thomas V "plagued by the same uncertainty as to his status," seeking "undoubtedly valid consecration and regularity of succession"); E. M. Philip, The Indian Church of St. Thomas, ch. 17, who states that Mar Thomas V was "oppressed with a doubt about the validity of his dignity." On the arrival of three West Syrian bishops on 23 April 1751, sent in the name of the Patriarch of Antioch, brought through the Dutch at the request and expense of Mar Thomas V: Neill II. The detailed sequence, that Mar Thomas V had agreed to bear the whole cost of the delegates’ voyage; that the Dutch East India Company demanded Rs. 12,000 in passage-money and detained the delegates until payment; that the Company brought a civil suit and procured his arrest for the debt, threatening deportation; that in this extremity he consecrated Mar Thomas VI single-handed; and that the delegates, once released on part-payment, refused to reconsecrate Mar Thomas V because he had not appeared before them in person, is given by E. M. Philip, The Indian Church of St. Thomas, ch. xviii. The three delegates were Mar Baselios (Maphrian, d. Kandanad 1763), Mar Gregorios, and Mar Ivanios (Philip, list of Antiochian prelates). On the raising of the money: Philip, ch. xviii, records that part of the sum was realised “by the attachment and sale of properties belonging to the church at Niranam” (and preserves an autograph diary of Mar Baselios on the passage-money question). The memory that Mar Thomas V also attempted, and failed, to take possession of the rich church at Kuravilangad survives in that parish’s own tradition (G. J. Nidhiry, Kuravilangad: The Sanctified Community); Philip is silent on it, and the Kuravilangad volume, a compiler’s parish history giving no citation for the episode, is followed only for the existence of the tradition. The request and the 1751 arrival belong to Mar Thomas V, not to his successor Mar Thomas VI.

That the delegates remained in Malabar after 1751 and that from their continued presence the West Syrian reorientation of the Puthankūr Church became permanent: Neill II; E. M. Philip, The Indian Church of St. Thomas, chs. xviii–xix. Mar Baselios died at Kandanad in 1763; Mar Gregorios and Mar Ivanios remained, and it was they who would reconsecrate Mar Thomas VI in 1770 (note 17 below) and, in Gregorios’s case, set the Thozhiyur line going in 1772 (note 18 below).

On the reconsecration of Mar Thomas VI by the resident Antiochian bishops in 1770, his receiving thereby all the orders and taking the name Mar Dionysius (Dionysius I): Neill II, who notes that Mar Dionysius thereby acquired "an unexceptionable episcopal consecration." On the two readings of that act, the Catholic and academic view that reconsecration concedes the invalidity of the prior orders, and the Malankara view, given by Philip, that it merely "perfected" a dignity already real, see Chapter 13 above, note 28, and the standing reservation entered there. That Mar Dionysius is the first head of the line to bear a Greek episcopal name rather than the native Mar Thoma is noted by Malankara historiography itself. The date of the reconsecration is given as 1770 by Philip and as 1772 by some authorities, the later date being that of the consecration of Mar Koorilose; see note 18 below.

On the Thozhiyur secession of 1772: in the year Mar Dionysius received his unexceptionable consecration, the Antiochian bishop Mar Gregorios, dissatisfied with Mar Dionysius, consecrated in secret the monk Kattumangatt Kurien of Mulanthuruthy as Mar Koorilose and designated him his heir; the consecration was, in Neill's judgement, highly irregular, performed without synod or mandate, yet on a strict interpretation of canon law to be accepted as valid. Mar Koorilose, driven out by Mar Dionysius, took refuge at Thozhiyur (Anjoor) in the Zamorin's territory, founding the church later known, by the court verdict of 1862, as the Malabar Independent Syrian Church: Neill II; e-GEDSH, "Malabar Independent Syrian Church" (Beth Mardutho); and the official history of the Malabar Independent Syrian Church (misc.co.in), which describes the consecration as "secret but canonically legitimate" and Mar Koorilose as Gregorios's "sole heir." The Malankara mainline tradition, by contrast (E. M. Philip), regards the consecration as performed without the knowledge or approval of Mar Dionysius and the senior delegate, and so as irregular. On the later recourse of the Malankara Church to the Thozhiyur succession, when Mar Philoxenos II of Thozhiyur consecrated three Malankara metropolitans in 1816–1825, a story beyond the bounds of this chapter: e-GEDSH, as above.

On the mission of Joseph Kariattil (Cariati) and Thomas Paremmakkal to Rome, sent to plead the cause of the Syro-Malabar community and to seek native government: Tisserant, 96; and the Varthamanappusthakam of Paremmakkal Thoma Kathanar (1785; English translation by P. J. Podipara, 1971), the foundational native narrative of the mission and of the Pazhayakūr cause, written by one of the two delegates. On the warning carried home that the delegation was being detained in Europe, and the suspicion it bred: Tisserant, 96.

On Joseph Kariattil's elevation and death: he was appointed to the see of Kodungallur-Angamaly by the Queen of Portugal, through the Padroado, and the appointment was sanctioned by Pope Pius VI on 16 December 1782 (Tisserant, 90, 96, and chronology at 200, "Joseph Cariati nominated Archbishop of Cranganore 1782 … dies at Goa 1786"). The appointment is documented independently of Paremmakkal's own narrative, resting on the papal sanction and on the episcopal list of the Cranganore see (Tisserant, 188, after Paulinus a S. Bartholomaeo, 1794, de Nazareth, and Ferroli), which records "Joseph Cariati, a Syro-Malabar, 1782–6, who did not occupy the see." That the Padroado, rather than the Carmelite vicariate under Propaganda, was the only channel by which a native could be raised to the episcopate of the Serra, since the Carmelites would not make a man of the soil vicar apostolic over themselves, is the point pressed here; it is the author's framing, though it follows the logic of the sources. On his death at Goa in 1786 of a malignant fever, and on the popular conviction that he had been poisoned by the European religious to prevent a native from governing the Serra: Tisserant, 96. Tisserant records the belief as belief and does not endorse it; the contemporary evidence does not sustain the charge of murder. The author's observation that in the history of a grievance the conviction of a wrong moves men more than its truth is offered as comment, not as a finding of the sources.

On the appointment of Thomas Paremmakkal as administrator (governor) of the archdiocese of Kodungallur by the Archbishop of Goa, to calm the agitation: Tisserant, 96. On Paremmakkal's double game, supporting before his own people the belief that Kariattil had been killed, the better to obstruct the appointment of a Carmelite vicar apostolic, while holding the contrary view before the Portuguese authorities, and on the people's petition that his own name be laid before the Holy See: Tisserant, 96–97.

On the Angamaly Padiyola of 1787 as the organised written protest of the Syrian Catholic clergy, framed not as a break with Rome but as a demand for native government: Tisserant, 96–97. The document is named for the assembly at Angamaly; the dispute it expressed was, on Tisserant's account, settled in the same year by the King of Travancore. The fuller native record is the Varthamanappusthakam (see note 19). It should be noted that the Padiyola is here treated as the culmination of the grievances of the Carmelite decades; the framing of those decades as a steady accumulation of resentment is the author's, drawn from the temper of the sources.

On the settlement of 1787 by the King of Travancore, known from the letter of the vicar apostolic Louis Mary of Jesus (Pianazzi) and his four companions to Propaganda, 7 May 1787: Tisserant, 97. On the reduced state of the mission, the vicar apostolic and four missionaries only, one of them Paulinus of St Bartholomew (Philip Wesdin), in Malabar from 1775 to 1789: Tisserant, 97. On Paulinus's census of the parishes, eighty-four Syrian Catholic and thirty-two dissident, the Catholics divided between Kodungallur (20) and Verapoly (64): Tisserant, 97, citing Paulinus a S. Bartholomaeo.

On the controversies between the Carmelites and the Padroado hierarchy of Kodungallur and Cochin continuing until the Brief Multa praeclara of 24 April 1838, after which the apostolic vicariate of Verapoly was officially erected: Tisserant, 97–98. That the aspiration to native government declared in 1787 was deferred rather than satisfied, and ran on through the nineteenth century, is the burden of the chapters that follow in the larger history; its eventual settlement lies beyond the period treated here.

On the eastward recourse after the failure of the Padroado route: Tisserant, 103–04. When Propaganda would not act on the petitions from Lisbon, the Syrian Catholics wrote to the Chaldean Patriarch Joseph IV (1757–81, d. 1791); the letter was received at Mosul by the metropolitan John Hormez (himself a convert from the Church of the East, reunited 1778, patriarch only in 1830), acting as administrator of the catholicosate while Joseph IV was at Rome. After sixteen months without instructions from Rome, Hormez consecrated Paul Pandari, a Syro-Malabar priest of Puthenchira, who reached Malabar under the name Mar Abraham; he was not permitted to exercise his office, and the intrusion, which displeased Rome, came to nothing (Tisserant, 104; both Mar Abraham and a patriarchal delegate, Hormisdas, are named in the Act of Union accepted by Mar Dionysius I in 1799). The reading of this as the Pazhayakūr arriving, in their hunger for a native prelate, at the same Mesopotamian door the Puthankūr had gone to in 1751 is the author’s.

The closing reflection, that the Coonan Cross of 1653 had planted in both the Catholic and the dissident bodies the conviction that the Thomas Christians were a church to be governed as one, and that this conviction broke surface on both sides in the events of 1772 and 1787, is the author's synthesis of the two narratives traced in this chapter, and is offered as interpretation. On the Coonan Cross and its meaning, see Chapter 12 above.

On the Mysorean invasion and its effect on the Christians of the Serra: Hyder Ali had overrun the Malabar coast in the 1760s and 1770s, and his son Tipu Sultan carried the war south, breaching the lines of Travancore in 1789–90 (Neill, vol. II, on the extension of British power). The destruction of the Jesuit college at Ambazhakkad by Tipu’s troops in 1790 is recorded by Tisserant, 188; the college, whose scholasticate dated from 1667, had trained an average of some forty Syrian clerics every three or four years. The wider political reckoning, the decline of the Dutch, the rise of Travancore, and the closing of British paramountcy over the coast, is the subject of the chapter that follows; the reading of this as the moment when the question of the church’s bishops began to be overtaken by the question of its sovereigns is the author’s.

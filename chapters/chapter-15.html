The Carmelite Decades and the Native Revolt, 1724–1787

The Spark Awaited

"The Malabarians will obey the bishops of their own community."

— Thomas Paremmakkal, Varthamanappusthakam, c. 1785

The story of these decades runs along two channels at once, and the native clergy watched both. On the Catholic side, the Carmelite vicars apostolic of Verapoly governed the Pazhayakūr against the standing Padroado claim; on the other, the Puthankūr pursued its troubled search for valid orders, a search that would, in these same years, produce the first lasting fracture within itself. The Catholic cattanars, watching the Puthankūr line acquire at last what looked like an unimpeachable episcopate from the East, had before them a standing reproach: the separated brethren appeared to have found a way to be governed by men of their own, while they themselves remained under Italians.

The Double Jurisdiction

The condition that Chapter 14 left in place did not improve with time; it settled into a chronic affliction. When Innocent XIII ratified the Portuguese Jesuits Pimentel for Kodungallur-Angamaly and Vasconcellos for Cochin in 1721, he confirmed in law the very thing that made the Serra ungovernable: two parallel hierarchies, the one Roman and Carmelite, the other Portuguese and Jesuit, each claiming the same flock, each able to frustrate the other and neither able to prevail. The Carmelites held the ground, since the Dutch would tolerate no Portuguese bishop in their territory, and the practical government of the Catholic Thomas Christians lay with the vicars apostolic of Verapoly. But the Padroado claim was never surrendered, and the quarrel between the two ran on, by Tisserant's reckoning, until the Brief Multa praeclara of 1838 all but ended it. 1

For the native clergy, the effect of this arrangement was not abstract. A church divided at the top between two foreign masters is a church in which no native voice carries, for each foreign party, competing for the people's loyalty, has reason to keep the native clergy dependent rather than to raise it up. The seminary at Verapoly trained native priests but did not produce native bishops; and the long Latinising pressure that had begun at Diamper continued, in gentler forms, to treat the East Syrian rite as a thing to be corrected rather than honoured. There was, besides, a particular loss. The Thomas Christians had once had a powerful native office, the archdeacon, the single head of the indigenous clergy who under the old East Syrian order had governed the community in all but sacramental matters and spoken for it before its foreign bishops. The schism of 1653 had split that office in two, the Puthankūr archdeacon becoming the dissident Mar Thoma, the Pazhayakūr keeping a Catholic archdeacon, Parambil Chandy. His nephew Mathew Pallil held the office from 1678. But on the Catholic side the office did not survive the Carmelite century.

The Closing of the Archdeaconate

The Latin distrust of the archdeaconate ran deep, and it ran early; and it was not wholly without warrant. What troubled the Latin mind was that the hereditary succession of the archdeaconate and its near-autonomous power sat ill with the Tridentine order of the Church, in which the bishop was to be supreme in his diocese and no office to descend by right of blood within a single family. Already in 1622 the first Jesuit archbishop of the Serra, Francisco Ros, had reported to his superiors that the archdeaconate was not a hereditary office at all, that the archdeacon held it by no right of inheritance, as some ignorant people, he wrote, wished to claim, and that the man then in the office was no kinsman of his predecessors. The assertion was untrue, as the historians of the Serra agree and as the evidence plainly shows: the dignity passed within a family and often from uncle to nephew, by a hereditary right that was the custom of the native Christians, and Ros himself conceded in the same report that the archdeacon called his predecessors his uncles. But the denial served a purpose, for to call the office elective rather than hereditary was to deny the native family its claim and to open the succession to the prelate's own hand; and from that first argument flowed the long reduction that followed. The Latin prelates pared it steadily away, and at length left the office without a successor, so that the archdeaconate of the Catholic Thomas Christians was quietly extinguished, not by a single act but by disuse. 2

Particular Wounds

There were, besides, particular wounds. The memory of Mar Simon, the Chaldean bishop who had consecrated the first Carmelite vicar apostolic in 1701 and had then been hurried off under guard to Pondicherry to die, was kept alive in the native tradition as a case of foreign high-handedness, a bishop the Syrians might have made their own, removed lest they should. The intrusion of Bishop Gabriel, a prelate of the Church of the East whose allegiance wavered between Rome and the traditional patriarchate, was a reminder of another kind: that bishops sent from the Mesopotamian East no less than from the Latin West could sow confusion as readily as they could remedy it in the Serra. Each episode taught the same lesson to those disposed to learn it: that the people longed for their own tradition and for rule by men of their own kind, and that the foreign administration stood between them and that desire. This longing would, again and again, produce factions in the years that followed. 3

The Society Undone

While the Jesuits and Carmelites fought their long contest for the Serra, the ecclesiastical and political forces gathering around the Society of Jesus were moving toward an outcome that would have seemed unthinkable half a century before. To understand it, the eye must travel up once more from the backwaters of Malabar to the whole frame of Catholic Christendom, for the change that overtook the Serra in these years began far away, in a reversal of the very idea on which the Padroado had been built.

The Reversal of the Two Swords

The principle of the two swords, as Chapter 10 set out, was the medieval doctrine of the order of the two powers: the spiritual, wielded by the Church, and the temporal, wielded by kings, held to be distinct, the temporal ordered to the spiritual and subordinate to it. The Padroado, however, had already strained that doctrine, for under the Habsburgs the distinction itself had begun to collapse: the Catholic crown gathered both swords into one hand, its prelates governing as viceroys and its viceroys as protectors of the faith, until the temporal and the spiritual were fused in a single authority that bore the sword of empire and the sword of the faith together. By the eighteenth century, the shadows of the medieval 'dark ages' had finally retreated before the light of human reason. This illuminismo brought about more than just the collapse of the medieval 'two swords' theory; it reversed the doctrine entirely. If the medieval world had set the spiritual above the temporal, and the Habsburgs had blurred the lines between them, the modern age now performed a clean inversion: it set the temporal outright above the spiritual. 4

The Europe of these decades was completing the passage, begun at Westphalia, from an overarching Christendom to a system of self-conscious sovereign states, each claiming fuller mastery within its own borders. And the reversal was worked, with a fitting irony, by the very house that had once concentrated the two swords as no hand had since Charlemagne. As we saw in Chapter 10, the dominion of Charles V did not pass entire to a single heir: at his abdication in 1556 it was partitioned, the crown of Spain and the Indies going to his son Philip and the Spanish line, the imperial title and the lands of Austria to his brother Ferdinand and the line that ruled at Vienna. The Spanish branch ran its course and died out in 1700, and the French Bourbons took its throne. The first Bourbon king Philip V brought into Spain, after the War of Spanish Succession and the Nueva Planta decrees, a model of centralising monarchy known as Gallicanism (which in Spain became Regalism). But it was the Austrian Habsburg branch, keeping the old imperial crown, that became in these decades a principal seat of the reversal. Under the Empress Maria Theresa, and more sweepingly under her son after her, the court of Vienna ruled the Church within its dominions as an instrument of the crown, governing bishops and religious houses by the sovereign's hand and holding its authority against Rome itself. The house that had gathered both swords into a single Catholic grasp now turned the temporal sword against the spiritual. Later historians would name this whole movement the Enlightenment. France would carry it a step further still: where regalism had set the monarch above the altar, France would cast down the monarch himself and enthrone in his place the common man, the sovereign nation, as the new and absolute power. That violent substitution, worked into blood and law, those same historians would call the Revolution, made for liberty, equality, and fraternity. 5

The Marquis of Pombal

Nowhere was this reversal carried through with more deliberate force than in Portugal, and its instrument was a single extraordinary minister. Sebastião José de Carvalho e Melo, who governed Portugal and its empire from 1750 to 1777 as chief minister to King Joseph I, and whom the king at length raised to be Marquis of Pombal, was the very type of the enlightened absolutist: the reforming statesman who gathered every power of the state into his own hand and bent it, without scruple, to the strengthening of the crown. He had formed his mind abroad, in two embassies that marked him differently. The years in London, where he was sent in 1738, schooled him in trade and revenue, in the power of chartered companies and the wealth a managed commerce might pour into a treasury. The years in Vienna brought him face to face with the reversal itself: sent there in 1745 to mediate a quarrel between the Empress Maria Theresa and the Holy See, he stood in the very court where the Austrian Habsburgs were bending the Church to the crown, and watched a Catholic sovereign hold her authority against Rome. He carried the influence home as the governing conviction of his life, that the Church of Rome was an obstacle to reform and that a national church dependent upon the state must be built in its place; and once in power he set about making it real, subordinating the religious orders, the Inquisition, and the bishops alike to the royal authority. 6

The Society Swallowed

In this reversal, the Society of Jesus was the first and greatest casualty, for it was the supreme anomaly that a regalist state could not abide: a wealthy, disciplined, transnational order that answered, over the king's head, to the pope alone. The order that had pioneered the accommodation of the faith to Indian forms, the method whose long contest with the Mendicants and the Padroado hierarchy earlier chapters have traced, had grown rich, learned, and politically formidable in the doing; and by the mid-century it had gathered enemies in equal measure. For Pombal, the Society of Jesus was not merely a rival power but a standing obstacle to progress, controlling as it did the great centres of learning. Portugal expelled the Jesuits in 1759, France in 1764, and Bourbon Spain, under Charles III, from all its dominions in 1767; and the universal suppression of the Society by Clement XIV in 1773 was less the free act of the papacy than its surrender to the combined pressure of the sovereigns. The illuminismo from Vienna had come full circle: what Pombal had once watched a single empress exact from a pope, the crowns of Catholic Europe now exacted, in concert, from the papacy itself. The spiritual sword had been made to cut down its own sharpest blade at the bidding of the temporal. The Society, once the architect of scientific learning and a catalyst for the Enlightenment, found itself ensnared by its own creation: a rationalist sovereign that no longer recognized the Church's spiritual authority. 7

The Paradox of Reform

This distant reversal reached the Serra through its Padroado arm, which was a Jesuit arm: it was the Society of Jesus that had held Kodungallur-Angamaly since Ribeiro forced his way in, kept the seminary at Ambazhakkad, and carried the Padroado claim through three generations of archbishops. When the Society fell, that arm fell with it, and the effect was immediate. After John Louis Vasconcellos and Salvador dos Reis, who had held the see jointly until 1758, no Jesuit could be sent to fill it, for in the Portuguese dominions there were no longer any Jesuits to send; and when the universal suppression followed in 1773, there was no Society anywhere to revive the claim. The Padroado see that had cost the Carmelites half a century of struggle was emptied not by Rome, nor by the Dutch, nor by any victory of the vicars apostolic, but by the act of the Portuguese Crown that had created it. 8

The Decline of the Inquisition

The expulsion of the Jesuits was but one stroke of a wider change in Portuguese policy in Asia, for Pombal was bent on bringing the whole apparatus of the Church under the State. In the same campaign of regalist reform he abolished the Inquisition of Goa, which for two centuries had enforced orthodoxy and the statutes of purity of blood across Portuguese India. The royal letter of 10 February 1774 dissolved the tribunal and its prisoners were set free. Although revived under Queen Maria I after Pombal's fall, in 1778, it was at last suppressed for good in 1812. 9

While Pombal freed Portuguese India of the Inquisition and the Amerindians of Brazil from the bondage of slavery, he turned the Inquisition at home into a weapon against the Jesuits and chartered great monopoly companies to carry enslaved Africans into the Amazon. What moved him was never the dignity of persons but the power of the state. Where the dismantling of the old Church machinery served him, he dismantled it; where the enslavement of Africans filled the treasury, he enslaved. His was not the abolition of coercion but the redirection of it, the reversal of the two swords made flesh. 10

The Opening Pombal Made

The ripples of Pombal's reforms set off a paradox of their own in the Serra. The Padroado claim did not die with its Jesuits, for the title of Kodungallur survived on paper and the Portuguese clung to it; but the living Padroado presence on the ground withered, and the practical field was left more than ever to the Carmelites of Verapoly. The vicariate that Rome had planted in 1659 as a temporary expedient under Dutch conditions now found its chief rival gone, and settled into something like a monopoly of the Catholic Serra. That monopoly, however, brought the native grievance to a point rather than relieving it, for the Carmelites were as foreign as the Jesuits had been, and now there was no second Catholic power to play against them. The cattanars who had chafed under two foreign masters found themselves, after 1759, chafing under one that could no longer be balanced by the other. At the same time, the Portuguese Padroado was now devoid of the very Jesuits the cattanars had sworn never to obey, even as the regalist Portuguese crown flexed its authority against Rome. Resourceful cattanars would soon exploit this paradox against Rome's Propaganda. But before reaching that climax, we must briefly look towards the Puthankūr. 11

The First Fracture in Puthankūr

The Price of a Foreign Episcopate

While the Catholic Serra chafed under its double government, the ordination crisis of Puthankūr came to a head in the middle of the century. Mar Thomas V, confronted by a doubt about the regularity of his own consecration, which had been performed by his predecessor alone without an Antiochian delegate, appealed repeatedly to the East for bishops who could set the matter right. His appeal was at length answered, but the manner of the answer showed how dearly a foreign episcopate must be bought. To bring the delegates at all, Mar Thomas V had to treat with the Dutch East India Company, undertaking to pay the whole cost of their voyage; and when three West Syrian prelates landed on 23 April 1751, the Company demanded a heavy fee in passage-money and held the bishops in its custody until the sum should be paid. Mar Thomas V could not raise it. The metropolitan, pressed for so vast a sum, fell back on the wealth of the parishes themselves, raising part of it by attaching and selling church property, as at Niranam. The Catholic church of Kuravilangad remembers in its own tradition that it foiled such an attempt upon it. The Company, meanwhile, went to law against him, obtained his arrest for the debt, and threatened to deport him; he hid for a time, was taken, and was delivered up. In this extremity, with the very bishops who had come to mend his orders impounded for want of money, he consecrated his nephew as Mar Thomas VI by his own hand alone, the old irregular way once more. The debt was at length part-paid and the delegates released; but they had taken offence that the metropolitan had not come before them in person, and they refused, after all this, to reconsecrate him. 12

They stayed on in Malabar nonetheless, and from their presence the West Syrian reorientation of the Puthankūr Church, in its liturgy, its discipline, and the line of its metropolitans, became permanent. But the price of a foreign episcopate is foreign quarrels, and the very bishops who had come to secure the succession would in the end break it. 13

The Birth of Thozhiyur

It was Mar Thomas VI, the nephew and successor of Mar Thomas V, who at last resolved the validity question. In 1770 he submitted to a fresh consecration at the hands of the resident Antiochian bishops, receiving, as the accounts have it, all the orders from the tonsure upward, and took the name Mar Dionysius I. But this resolution exacted a larger price: rupture. 14

In 1772, the very year in which Mar Dionysius I secured his unimpeachable orders, the succession that had been bought at such cost began to turn against itself. Of the three delegates who had come in 1751, the senior, Mar Baselios, had died at Kandanad in 1763, leaving two, Mar Gregorios and Mar Ivanios; and it was Gregorios, the elder of the two, who now fell out with Mar Dionysius, by Neill's account over the manner in which the metropolitan provided for him in his old age. Acting alone, without the consent of his fellow delegate and without synod or mandate, Gregorios consecrated in secret a rival, the monk Kattumangatt Kurian of Mulanthuruthy, under the name Mar Koorilose (Cyril), and designated him his own heir. The act was, as Neill observes, "highly irregular"; yet on a strict reading of canon law it had to be accepted as valid, for a true bishop, whatever the irregularity of the circumstances, had conferred true orders. Mar Dionysius would not tolerate a rival, and Mar Koorilose was driven out, taking refuge at length at Thozhiyur, or Anjoor, in the territory of the Zamorin beyond the reach of the rajas of Travancore and Cochin. There he and his successors maintained a small independent church, known after a court verdict of 1862 as the Malabar Independent Syrian Church. It was the first lasting division within Puthankūr, and it carried a sharp irony forward into the next century: the little church of Thozhiyur, cast out as a rival, would in time become the reservoir of valid orders from which the Puthankūr Church itself, unable to consecrate its own metropolitans, would more than once be obliged to draw. 15

A Native Archbishop, and a Death at Goa

By the 1770s the Catholic Serra had resolved upon a way to bid for a bishop of its own blood. Two priests, Joseph Kariattil, once a student at the College of Propaganda in Rome and now a teacher in the seminary at Alangad, and Thomas Paremmakkal, the parish priest of Kadanad, were sent in 1778 to plead the cause of their community against the Carmelite administration and to ask for native government. They were precisely the kind of men the cattanars had longed to see raised up: learned, Roman in loyalty, and Malayali in blood. 16

The success of this new attempt lay in the opening Pombal had made. A native bishop for the Catholic Serra could not be had by the Roman road passing through the Carmelites, who held the vicariate of Verapoly under Propaganda and would never raise a man of the soil to be vicar apostolic over themselves; the door Kariattil planned to open and the road he chose to tread ran through the rival authority, the Padroado of the Portuguese crown, with its still-surviving but long-empty archiepiscopal see of Kodungallur-Angamaly. So it was by the Padroado road that Kariattil and Paremmakkal went, pleading their cause to Lisbon and Rome. Passing through Lisbon, Kariattil was appointed by the Queen of Portugal to the vacant see of Kodungallur-Angamaly (King Joseph I died in 1777, and his daughter, Queen Maria I, ascended the throne. She immediately fired Pombal and he died soon after in 1782). Pope Pius VI sanctioned the appointment on 16 December 1782. The Pazhayakūr had a native metropolitan at last, by the patronage of the very power the Serra had once defied.

The news that the new bishop had reached India and was on his way home set the Serra alight with anticipation. Then, at Goa, on his way to take up his see, Kariattil died, in September 1786, of a malignant fever. To the Portuguese authorities it was a natural death. To the Thomas Christians, primed by years of suspicion and by rumours of a Carmelite plot and European intervention, it was nothing of the kind: they believed their archbishop had been done away with by the religious from Europe, poisoned to prevent a native from governing the Serra. Whether the belief was just cannot now be determined, and the contemporary evidence does not sustain it; but in the history of a grievance it is not the truth of a charge that moves men, it is their conviction of its truth. The conviction was total, and the agitation it raised was such that it put fear into the heart of the Archbishop of Goa himself. 17

The Administrator's Double Game

To calm the storm of the Serra's unrest, and to ensure that the vacant see remained under Padroado jurisdiction rather than falling to the Carmelites of the Propaganda Fide, the Archbishop of Goa made an unexpected move. He appointed Kariattil's travelling companion, Thomas Paremmakkal, the other native priest of the Roman mission, as administrator, or governor, of the vacant archdiocese of Kodungallur-Angamaly. The Syrians were elated; one of their most cherished dreams, a metropolitan of their own, seemed within reach at last. And Paremmakkal, a subtle man placed in an impossible position, played a double game that Tisserant records without flattery: before his own people he supported the belief that Kariattil had been killed, the better to make the appointment of any new Carmelite vicar apostolic impossible, while before the Portuguese authorities he held the contrary view. The Pazhayakūr, for their part, took up the same road their dead archbishop had taken, petitioning the crown of Portugal, not Propaganda, to lay Paremmakkal's own name before the Holy See as their metropolitan; for Paremmakkal now held, as administrator of Kodungallur-Angamaly, a Padroado office, and it was the Padroado, not the Carmelite vicariate, that might yet be made to yield them a bishop of their own blood. 18

The Angamaly Padiyola

It was in this heat, with a native administrator at the head of Kodungallur-Angamaly, a dead archbishop believed murdered, and the long resentment of sixty years brought at last to the boil, that the cattanars of the Serra assembled and drew up the document remembered as the Angamaly Padiyola of 1787. In it the representatives of the Syrian Catholic clergy set down their grievances against the foreign administration and bound themselves to seek the government of native prelates, refusing to submit any longer to a rule that kept men of their own race from the episcopate of their own church. It was not a break with Rome; the cattanars remained Catholic, and meant to. It was a revolt against the foreign manner of Rome's government, an organised insistence, in writing and on oath, that the Thomas Christians be ruled by their own. 19

The immediate crisis was settled, in that same year, not by Rome but by the King of Travancore, in whose dominions most of the parties now lived and to whose arbitration the dispute between the missionaries and the Syrian communities was at length referred. The settlement forced the recognition of Thomas Paremmakkal as administrator of the Serra by the Propaganda, while the churches that had forcefully shut their doors to Carmelite missionaries were to return to order. We know of the settlement from the letter that the vicar apostolic Pianazzi and his four remaining missionaries sent to Propaganda on 7 May 1787, a letter that reveals that this settlement was not achieved through spiritual persuasion or ecclesiastical diplomacy by the Carmelites, but was explicitly enforced by the sovereign Dharma Raja of the Kingdom of Travancore. Here too a temporal sovereign disposed of a church's affairs within his own dominions, the same subordination of the altar to the throne, though arrived at by a wholly different road. One of those four Carmelites was the learned Austrian orientalist Paulinus of St Bartholomew, whom we have encountered before. His count of the Malabar parishes in this period, eighty-four Catholic and thirty-two dissident (the Catholics still divided between the jurisdictions of Kodungallur-Angamaly and Verapoly), gives us our clearest census of the divided church. 20

The settlement of 1787 settled nothing fundamental. Paremmakkal governed Kodungallur-Angamaly as administrator until his death, but no native succession was secured; the desire for a metropolitan of their own race was deferred, not satisfied; and the controversies between the Carmelites and the Padroado, which had set the whole condition of grievance in place, would drag on for another half-century until the Brief Multa praeclara of 1838. What the Padiyola did was not to win its object but to declare it, to put on record, in the voice of the native clergy itself, that the Thomas Christians regarded the foreign government of their church as a thing to be ended. That declaration, once made, did not unmake itself. The aspiration to native government, raised to open expression in 1787, would run on through the whole of the nineteenth century, surfacing again and again until, far beyond the bounds of this history, it was at last met. 21

A Bishop from the East

There remains a last turn of the screw, and it completes the irony. When the Padroado road too was blocked, when Paremmakkal died without the succession he had been put forward for, and Propaganda turned a deaf ear to the petitions from Lisbon, the Catholic Syrians fell back on the one expedient left to a Malabar church that wanted a bishop of its own and could not get one from the Latins: they looked east. They wrote to the Chaldean Patriarch in Mesopotamia, Joseph IV, exactly as the Puthankūr had written to Antioch; and the letter, received at Mosul by the metropolitan John Hormez while the patriarch was at Rome, brought forth in the end a consecration. After waiting in vain for instructions from Rome, Hormez consecrated an obscure priest of Puthenchira, one Paul Pandari, who came into Malabar under the name of Mar Abraham to be bishop of the Catholic Syrians. The intrusion failed as such intrusions did: he was not permitted to function, the affair displeased Rome, and it was quietly undone. But the meaning of it is hard to miss. The Pazhayakūr, who had kept the Roman obedience through a century and a half precisely to hold fast to an apostolicity that the East could not give them, had come at the last, in their hunger for a prelate of their own, to the very door the Puthankūr had gone to in 1751. Both halves of the divided church, the old allegiance and the new, ended the century reaching toward Mesopotamia for what neither Rome nor Lisbon would grant them, a bishop of their own people. 22

The Spark, and the Storm Beyond

So the spark for the revolt fell, in the end, on a particular grief, a native archbishop dead at Goa and believed murdered, that gathered all the older grievances into itself. The two channels of this chapter met in that moment. The Puthankūr Church had shown, by 1772, that a people might secure bishops of its own and pay for them in schism; the Catholic cattanars, in 1787, declared that they too would be governed by their own, though they meant to pay no such price and to remain within the Roman communion while they sought it. That revolt was a long time coming, and a long time bearing fruit. But the seed planted at the Coonan Cross more than a century before, that the Thomas Christians were a church and not a mission, and would in the end insist on being governed as one, had now broken surface on both sides of the divide at once. 23

Yet even as the two halves of the divided church bent every thought upon the question of whose hands should ordain their bishops, that question was already being overtaken by a larger one: whose armies would rule the land they lived in. The powers that had framed the whole story of these chapters were passing from the stage. The crown of Portugal that had answered Kariattil's prayer could no longer guard even its own see; the Dutch Company that had detained the delegates and dictated the passage-money had itself been humbled, decades before, by a native king upon its own coast. And now, while the cattanars swore their padiyola and their metropolitans reached toward Mesopotamia, a conqueror was descending from the hills of Mysore. In 1790 the troops of Tipu Sultan came into the heart of the Serra and burned the Jesuit college at Ambazhakkad, the seminary that had trained the Syrian clergy for more than a century; churches were sacked and whole congregations scattered along the coast. Behind the smoke of his armies a new European power was rising, one that would gather up what the Dutch had let fall. The Serra had contended for generations over who should be its bishop; it was about to be made to answer to a sovereign who cared nothing whether it was pope or patriarch. 24

To follow the Thomas Christians into the century that came, we must first leave them, and look to the powers that were remaking the ground beneath their feet.

On his regalism, anti-ultramontane views, and the project of a state-dependent national church: Emanuele Colombo and Niccolò Guasti, "The Expulsion and Suppression in Portugal and Spain: An Overview," in Jonathan Wright and Jeffrey D. Burson, eds., The Jesuit Suppression in Global Context (Cambridge, 2015), 117–138. He had served as Portuguese envoy at London from 1738 and at Vienna from 1745, the later embassy charged with mediating a quarrel between the Empress Maria Theresa and the Holy See; on the postings and the mediation, and on the distinction drawn in the text between the economic lessons of London and the regalist lesson of Vienna, see Maxwell, Pombal. On his hostility to Jesuit education, the Coimbra University reforms of 1770–1772, and the sponsored Compendio historico do estado da universidade de Coimbra (1771): Maxwell, Pombal, and Colombo & Guasti, as above.

On the suppression of the Society of Jesus: by the decree of the Marquis of Pombal of 3 September 1759 the Jesuits were expelled from Portugal and all its overseas dominions, their property confiscated; the measure extended throughout the Portuguese empire and so to the Padroado missions of India. The universal suppression followed on 16 August 1773 by the Brief Dominus ac Redemptor of Clement XIV. On the Jesuit archbishops of Kodungallur ending with Vasconcellos and dos Reis (1753-58), after whom the see could not be filled, see Tisserant, 188 (episcopal list).

On the Society of Jesus at the height of its power and the gathering hostility to it in Catholic Europe: the order that had carried the Padroado mission in Malabar (and, at Kodungallur and Ambazhakkad, the Padroado claim against the Carmelites) was by the mid-eighteenth century widely resented by the Bourbon courts as too wealthy, too independent, and too closely bound to Rome. The Jesuit method of accommodation to Indian forms, pioneered in the south by Robert de Nobili, is treated in the earlier chapters of this work. On the Jesuit tenure of Kodungallur-Angamaly through Ribeiro, Pimentel, Vasconcellos, and dos Reis, see Chapter 14 above, and Tisserant, 188.

On Pombal's abolition of the Goa Inquisition as part of his regalist programme, and on his reforms raising the Goan native clergy to legal equality vis-à-vis their European counterparts: I. G. Županov, Jesuit Missions in Coastal and South India (1543–1773). The Inquisition of Goa, in Portuguese India, was suppressed by royal letter of 10 February 1774, revived under Queen Maria I in 1778, and finally abolished in 1812: Anant K. Priolkar, The Goa Inquisition (Bombay, 1961). It is to be distinguished from the metropolitan Portuguese Inquisition at Lisbon, which outlasted it and was abolished only with the liberal revolution, effectively in 1820 and formally in 1821; the Spanish Inquisition was suppressed by stages between 1808 and 1834.

In 1755 Pombal created the Companhia Geral do Comércio do Grão-Pará e Maranhão, a chartered monopoly over the trade of northern Brazil: Wright and Burson, eds., The Jesuit Suppression in Global Context, the Colombo and Guasti chapter. Pombal abolished the enslavement of the Amerindians of Brazil while the monopoly companies he founded supplied the Amazon with enslaved Africans in their stead, an arrangement that served the revenue of the crown and not any principle of human freedom; on this paradox of reform joined to coercion, Maxwell, Pombal: Paradox of the Enlightenment.

Neill, Vol. II, 121–131, 152 notes the suppression of the Society in 1773 and its revival in 1814. On the effect in Malabar: with the Jesuits gone the Padroado presence on the ground withered, though the title of Kodungallur survived, and the Padroado claim was not extinguished but only checked: the active conflict was all but ended, and the Verapoly vicariate officially erected, by the Brief Multa praeclara of 1838 (note 1 above), while the Cranganore jurisdiction itself, with Padroado appointees as late as the 1840s, was definitively suppressed only by the concordat of 1886. The practical government of the Catholic Thomas Christians, meanwhile, fell more completely than before to the Carmelite vicars apostolic of Verapoly. On the chronic shortage of European missionaries (in 1770 only one father was able to visit the Syrian parishes): Tisserant, 93-94.

On Mar Thomas V's doubt about his own consecration: Neill II, on the Mar Thomas succession; E. M. Philip, The Indian Church of St. Thomas, ch. 17. On the arrival of the three West Syrian bishops (Mar Baselios, Mar Gregorios, and Mar Ivanios) on 23 April 1751, and the subsequent passage-money dispute with the Dutch, which led to the unilateral consecration of Mar Thomas VI and the delegates' refusal to reconsecrate Mar Thomas V: Neill II; and E. M. Philip, The Indian Church of St. Thomas, ch. xviii. On the raising of the money: Philip, ch. xviii (which preserves an autograph diary of Mar Baselios on the passage-money question). The memory that Mar Thomas V also attempted, and failed, to take possession of the rich church at Kuravilangad survives in that parish's own tradition (G. J. Nidhiry, Kuravilangad: The Sanctified Community); Philip is silent on it. The request and the 1751 arrival belong to Mar Thomas V, not to his successor Mar Thomas VI.

That the delegates remained in Malabar after 1751 and that from their continued presence the West Syrian reorientation of the Puthankūr Church became permanent: Neill II; E. M. Philip, The Indian Church of St. Thomas, chs. xviii–xix. Mar Baselios died at Kandanad in 1763; Mar Gregorios and Mar Ivanios remained, and it was they who would reconsecrate Mar Thomas VI in 1770 (note 14 below) and, in Gregorios's case, set the Thozhiyur line going in 1772 (note 15 below).

On the reconsecration of Mar Thomas VI by the resident Antiochian bishops in 1770, his receiving thereby all the orders and taking the name Mar Dionysius (Dionysius I): Neill II, who notes that Mar Dionysius thereby acquired "an unexceptionable episcopal consecration." On the two readings of that act, the Catholic and academic view that reconsecration concedes the invalidity of the prior orders, and the Malankara view, given by Philip, that it merely "perfected" a dignity already real, see Chapter 13 above, note 28, and the standing reservation entered there. That Mar Dionysius is the first head of the line to bear a Greek episcopal name rather than the native Mar Thoma is noted by Malankara historiography itself. The date of the reconsecration is given as 1770 by Philip and as 1772 by some authorities, the later date being that of the consecration of Mar Koorilose; see note 15 below.

On the Thozhiyur secession of 1772 and the secret consecration of Mar Koorilose: Neill II; e-GEDSH, "Malabar Independent Syrian Church" (Beth Mardutho); and the official history of the Malabar Independent Syrian Church (misc.co.in), which describes the consecration as "secret but canonically legitimate" and Mar Koorilose as Gregorios's "sole heir." The Malankara mainline tradition (E. M. Philip) regards the consecration as irregular because it was performed without the knowledge of Mar Dionysius and the senior delegate. On the later recourse of the Malankara Church to the Thozhiyur succession (1816–1825): e-GEDSH, as above.

On the mission of Joseph Kariattil (Cariati) and Thomas Paremmakkal to Rome, sent to plead the cause of the Syro-Malabar community and to seek native government: Tisserant, 96; and the Varthamanappusthakam of Paremmakkal Thoma Kathanar (1785; English translation by P. J. Podipara, 1971), the foundational native narrative of the mission and of the Pazhayakūr cause, written by one of the two delegates. On the warning carried home that the delegation was being detained in Europe, and the suspicion it bred: Tisserant, 96. The biographical particulars (Kariattil's earlier training at the College of Propaganda and his teaching at Alangad, Paremmakkal's parish of Kadanad, and the dispatch of the embassy in 1778) follow Podipara.

On Joseph Kariattil's elevation and death: he was appointed to the see of Kodungallur-Angamaly by the Queen of Portugal, through the Padroado, and the appointment was sanctioned by Pope Pius VI on 16 December 1782 (Tisserant, 90, 96, and chronology at 200, "Joseph Cariati nominated Archbishop of Cranganore 1782 … dies at Goa 1786"). The appointment is documented independently of Paremmakkal's own narrative, resting on the papal sanction and on the episcopal list of the Cranganore see (Tisserant, 188, after Paulinus a S. Bartholomaeo, 1794, de Nazareth, and Ferroli), which records "Joseph Cariati, a Syro-Malabar, 1782–6, who did not occupy the see." On his death at Goa in 1786 of a malignant fever, and on the popular conviction that he had been poisoned by the European religious to prevent a native from governing the Serra: Tisserant, 96. Tisserant records the belief as belief and does not endorse it; the contemporary evidence does not sustain the charge of murder.

On the appointment of Thomas Paremmakkal as administrator (governor) of the archdiocese of Kodungallur by the Archbishop of Goa: Tisserant, 96, who attributes the move to the need to calm the popular agitation; and Podipara, Hierarchy, 150, who notes that this appointment effectively placed all Syro-Malabarians under the Padroado jurisdiction, leaving none under the Carmelite missionaries of the Propaganda Fide except the Latins. The Archbishop of Goa, Dom Frei Manuel de Santa Catarina, was himself a Discalced Carmelite, making the appointment a strategic move of Padroado jurisdiction rather than personal opposition to his own order. On Paremmakkal's double game, supporting before his own people the belief that Kariattil had been killed, the better to obstruct the appointment of a Carmelite vicar apostolic, while holding the contrary view before the Portuguese authorities, and on the people's petition that his own name be laid before the Holy See: Tisserant, 96–97.

On the Angamaly Padiyola of 1787 as the organised written protest of the Syrian Catholic clergy, framed not as a break with Rome but as a demand for native government: Tisserant, 96–97. The document is named for the assembly at Angamaly; the dispute it expressed was, on Tisserant's account, settled in the same year by the King of Travancore. The fuller native record is the Varthamanappusthakam (see note 16). It should be noted that the Padiyola is here treated as the culmination of the grievances of the Carmelite decades; the framing of those decades as a steady accumulation of resentment is the author's, drawn from the temper of the sources.

On the settlement of 1787 by the King of Travancore, known from the letter of the vicar apostolic Louis Mary of Jesus (Pianazzi) and his four companions to Propaganda, 7 May 1787: Tisserant, 97. On the reduced state of the mission, the vicar apostolic and four missionaries only, one of them Paulinus of St Bartholomaeo, in Malabar from 1775 to 1789: Tisserant, 97. On Paulinus's census of the parishes, eighty-four Syrian Catholic and thirty-two dissident, the Catholics divided between Kodungallur (20) and Verapoly (64): Tisserant, 97, citing Paulinus a S. Bartholomaeo.

On the controversies between the Carmelites and the Padroado hierarchy of Kodungallur and Cochin continuing until the Brief Multa praeclara of 24 April 1838, after which the apostolic vicariate of Verapoly was officially erected: Tisserant, 97–98. That the aspiration to native government declared in 1787 was deferred rather than satisfied, and ran on through the nineteenth century, is the burden of the chapters that follow in the larger history; its eventual settlement lies beyond the period treated here.

On the eastward recourse after the failure of the Padroado route: Tisserant, 103–04. When Propaganda would not act on the petitions from Lisbon, the Syrian Catholics wrote to the Chaldean Patriarch Joseph IV (1757–81, d. 1791); the letter was received at Mosul by the metropolitan John Hormez (himself a convert from the Church of the East, reunited 1778, patriarch only in 1830), acting as administrator of the catholicosate while Joseph IV was at Rome. After sixteen months without instructions from Rome, Hormez consecrated Paul Pandari, a Syro-Malabar priest of Puthenchira, who reached Malabar under the name Mar Abraham; he was not permitted to exercise his office, and the intrusion, which displeased Rome, came to nothing (Tisserant, 104; both Mar Abraham and a patriarchal delegate, Hormisdas, are named in the Act of Union accepted by Mar Dionysius I in 1799).

The closing reflection, that the Coonan Cross of 1653 had planted in both the Catholic and the dissident bodies the conviction that the Thomas Christians were a church to be governed as one, and that this conviction broke surface on both sides in the events of 1772 and 1787, is the author's synthesis of the two narratives traced in this chapter, and is offered as interpretation. On the Coonan Cross and its meaning, see Chapter 12 above.

On the Mysorean invasion and its effect on the Christians of the Serra: Hyder Ali had overrun the Malabar coast in the 1760s and 1770s, and his son Tipu Sultan carried the war south, breaching the lines of Travancore in 1789–90 (Neill, vol. II, on the extension of British power). The destruction of the Jesuit college at Ambazhakkad by Tipu's troops in 1790 is recorded by Tisserant, 188; the college, whose scholasticate dated from 1667, had trained an average of some forty Syrian clerics every three or four years. The wider political reckoning, the decline of the Dutch, the rise of Travancore, and the closing of British paramountcy over the coast, is the subject of the chapter that follows.

The Carmelite Decades and the Native Revolt, 1724–1787

The Spark Awaited

"Were the St Thomas Christians to be subjects of Carmelites from Europe always?"

— The question abroad in Malabar, on the news of an archbishop's coming, 1786

A grievance does not become a revolt in a day. It accumulates, quietly, through a long succession of small humiliations, each forgotten by the party that inflicts it and remembered by the party that suffers it, until some single event, often a trivial one, gathers the whole sediment of resentment into a sudden act. The Angamaly Padiyola of 1787, the assembly at which the cattanars of the Serra at last set their grievances against the Carmelite government down in writing and bound themselves by oath to pursue them, was such an act. To understand why a body of priests who had held to Rome through everything should suddenly organise against Rome's own vicars apostolic, one must follow the slow work of the sixty years that preceded it, the decades in which the Catholic Thomas Christians lived under a foreign episcopate that governed them well enough by its own lights and yet never ceased, in their eyes, to treat them as wards rather than as a church. 1

The story of those decades runs along two channels at once, and the two must be kept in view together, for the native clergy watched both. On the Catholic side, the Carmelite vicars apostolic of Verapoly governed the larger body of the Thomas Christians, the Pazhayakūr, while the Padroado archbishops of Kodungallur pressed their old and never-extinguished claims against them, so that the Catholic Serra was ruled by two foreign authorities at war with each other. On the other side, the Malankara Church, the Puthankūr who had turned to Antioch, pursued its own troubled search for valid orders, a search that brought West Syrian bishops to Malabar and that would, in these same years, produce the first lasting fracture within the dissident body itself. The Catholic cattanars, watching the Malankara line acquire at last what looked like an unimpeachable episcopate from the East, had before them a standing reproach: the separated brethren had found a way to be governed by men of their own, while they themselves remained under Italians.

The Double Jurisdiction

The condition that Chapter 14 left in place did not improve with time; it settled into a chronic affliction. When Innocent XIII ratified the Portuguese Jesuits Pimentel for Kodungallur and Vasconcellos for Cochin in 1721, he confirmed in law the very thing that made the Serra ungovernable, two parallel hierarchies, the one Roman and Carmelite, the other Portuguese and Padroado, each claiming the same flock, each able to frustrate the other and neither able to prevail. The Carmelites held the ground, since the Dutch would tolerate no Portuguese bishop in their territory, and the practical government of the Catholic Thomas Christians lay with the vicars apostolic of Verapoly. But the Padroado claim was never surrendered, and the quarrel between the two ran on, by Tisserant's reckoning, until the Brief Multa praeclara of 1838 all but ended it. 2

For the native clergy the effect of this arrangement was not abstract. A church divided at the top between two foreign masters is a church in which no native voice carries, for each foreign party, competing for the people's loyalty, has reason to keep the native clergy dependent rather than to raise it up. The cattanars of the Serra were numerous, learned in their own rite, rooted in their parishes, and descended, many of them, from the old priestly families of the coast. Yet the path to a mitre was closed to them. The vicars apostolic were Carmelites sent from Europe; the seminary at Verapoly trained native priests but did not produce native bishops; and the long Latinising pressure that had begun at Diamper continued, in gentler forms, to treat the Syrian rite as a thing to be corrected rather than honoured. There was, besides, a particular loss. The Thomas Christians had once had a powerful native office, the archdeacon, the single head of the indigenous clergy who under the old Chaldean order had governed the community in all but sacramental matters and spoken for it before its foreign bishops. The schism of 1653 had split that office in two, the Puthankūr archdeacon becoming the dissident Mar Thoma, the Pazhayakūr keeping a Catholic archdeacon, Mathew Pallil, appointed in 1678. But on the Catholic side the office did not survive the Carmelite century. The Latin prelates, who had distrusted the archdeacon's near-autonomous power since Roz first questioned it, steadily reduced it, and at length left it without a successor, so that the archdeaconate of the Catholic Thomas Christians quietly became extinct, its native voice extinguished not by a single act but by disuse. The grievance was not that the Carmelites governed badly. It was that they governed as foreigners, that they had let the one native dignity lapse, and that no end to foreign government was in sight. 3

There were, besides, particular wounds. The memory of Mar Simon, the Chaldean bishop who had consecrated the first Carmelite vicar apostolic in 1701 and had then been hurried off under guard to Pondicherry to die, was kept alive in the native tradition as a case of foreign high-handedness, a bishop the Syrians might have made their own, removed lest they should. The intrusion of the Nestorian bishop Gabriel, sent from the patriarch of Babylon, who reached Malabar in 1708 and drew off a following among the Thomas Christians before his death at Kottayam in 1730, was a further reminder of how readily the Serra would rally to a bishop of the Eastern rite who came in his own person, and of how little the Carmelite government could do to prevent it except by vigilance. Each episode taught the same lesson to those disposed to learn it: that the people longed for a prelate of their own kind, and that the foreign administration stood between them and that desire. 4

The Other Channel: Antioch, and the First Fracture

While the Catholic Serra chafed under its double government, the Malankara Church was living through the long consequence of its own founding problem, the question of valid orders that had shadowed the line of Mar Thomas from the hand-laying of 1653. The dissident metropolitans had never been able to set that question to rest, because the standard by which they had come to measure validity, that a bishop's orders must descend from the Patriarch of Antioch or his authorised delegate, required the presence in Malabar of a foreign delegate, and the foreign delegates were few and far between. The West Syrian bishop who had come in 1665, Gregorios Abdal Jaleel, had died in 1671; those who followed died in their turn; and each death reopened the gap. 5

The crisis came to a head in the middle of the century. Mar Thomas V, oppressed by a doubt about the validity of his own consecration, which had been performed by his predecessor alone without an Antiochian delegate, appealed repeatedly to the East for bishops who could set the matter right. His appeal was at length rewarded: on 23 April 1751 three West Syrian prelates landed in Malabar, sent in the name of the Patriarch of Antioch. They were the durable Antiochian presence the Malankara Church had long wanted, and from their coming the West Syrian reorientation of that church, in liturgy, in discipline, and in the line of its bishops, became permanent. But the price of a foreign episcopate is foreign quarrels, and the very bishops who came to secure the succession would also break it. 6

It was Mar Thomas VI, the nephew and successor of Mar Thomas V, who at last resolved the validity question, and the manner of its resolution is the most telling event in the whole history of the dissident line. In 1770 he submitted to a fresh consecration at the hands of the resident Antiochian bishops, receiving, as the accounts have it, all the orders from the tonsure upward, and took the name Mar Dionysius, the first head of the line to bear a Greek episcopal name in place of the native Mar Thoma. From the Catholic and academic point of view the act spoke for itself: a man who accepts reconsecration thereby concedes that his earlier orders were wanting, which was precisely the charge the Catholic sources had pressed against the whole line since 1653. The Malankara tradition reads it otherwise, as the perfecting of a dignity already real rather than the supplying of one that was void. The divergence is the same that has run through this history from the beginning, and it need not be adjudicated here; what matters for the present chapter is that the resolution and the rupture came together. 7

For in 1772, the very year in which Mar Dionysius secured his unimpeachable orders, one of the Antiochian bishops who had given them, Mar Gregorios, fell out with him, by Neill's account over the manner in which the metropolitan provided for him in his old age, and consecrated in secret a rival, the monk Kattumangatt Kurien of Mulanthuruthy, under the name Mar Koorilose, designating him his own heir. The act was, as Neill observes, "highly irregular," performed without synod or mandate; yet on a strict reading of canon law it had to be accepted as valid, for a true bishop had conferred true orders. Mar Dionysius would not tolerate a rival, and Mar Koorilose was driven out, taking refuge at length at Thozhiyur, or Anjoor, in the territory of the Zamorin beyond the reach of the rajas of Travancore and Cochin. There he and his successors maintained a small independent church, the Malabar Independent Syrian Church, which traced its own orders directly from the Antiochian Gregorios and so could claim, in its own eyes, to be the truest continuation of the very succession the Malankara Church had imported at such cost. It was the first lasting division within the dissident body, and it carried a sharp irony forward into the next century: the little church of Thozhiyur, cast out as a rival, would in time become the reservoir of valid orders from which the Malankara Church itself, unable to consecrate its own metropolitans, would more than once be obliged to draw. 8

None of this was lost on the Catholic cattanars. The lesson the Malankara Church had learned, painfully and at the cost of schism, was that a people determined to be governed by bishops of its own could in the end secure them. The Pazhayakūr clergy, watching their separated kinsmen acquire an episcopate descended from the East and seated in men who, whatever their origin, ruled a native church, could hardly fail to measure their own condition against it. They had kept faith with Rome, and Rome governed them still by Italians.

A Native Archbishop, and a Death at Goa

The spark, when it came, came from the Catholic side, and from an unexpected quarter, a genuine attempt to give the Thomas Christians a prelate of their own race. In the 1770s two able Syro-Malabar priests, Joseph Kariattil and Thomas Paremmakkal, were sent to Rome to plead the cause of their community against the Carmelite administration and to ask for native government. They were precisely the kind of men the cattanars had longed to see raised up, learned, Roman in loyalty, and Malayali in blood. Their mission was long, and to those at home it seemed unaccountably delayed; the priest who had gone with them warned the people that the delegation was being detained in Europe, and the suspicion took root that the foreign religious were working to keep native men from the episcopate. 9

In the end the cause succeeded, and Joseph Kariattil was raised to the dignity of Archbishop of Kodungallur, a native metropolitan at last. The news that he had reached India and was on his way home set the Serra alight with hope. Then, at Goa, on his way to take up his see, Kariattil died, in September 1786, of a malignant fever. To the Portuguese authorities it was a natural death. To the Thomas Christians, primed by years of suspicion and by the warning that had come from Europe, it was nothing of the kind: they believed their archbishop had been done away with by the religious from Europe, poisoned to prevent a native from governing the Serra. Whether the belief was just cannot now be determined, and the contemporary evidence does not sustain it; but in the history of a grievance it is not the truth of a charge that moves men, it is their conviction of its truth. The conviction was total, and the agitation it raised was such that it put fear into the heart of the Archbishop of Goa himself. 10

To calm the storm the Archbishop of Goa made an unexpected move. He appointed Kariattil's travelling companion, Thomas Paremmakkal, the other native priest of the Roman mission, as administrator, or governor, of the vacant archdiocese of Kodungallur. The Syrians were elated; one of their most cherished dreams, a metropolitan of their own race, seemed within reach at last. And Paremmakkal, a subtle man placed in an impossible position, played a double game that Tisserant records without flattery: before his own people he supported the belief that Kariattil had been killed, the better to make the appointment of any new Carmelite vicar apostolic impossible, while before the Portuguese authorities he held the contrary view. The Syrian people, for their part, petitioned the Portuguese to lay Paremmakkal's own name before the Holy See as their bishop. 11

The Angamaly Padiyola

It was in this heat, with a native administrator at the head of Kodungallur, a dead archbishop believed murdered, and the long resentment of sixty years brought at last to the boil, that the cattanars of the Serra assembled and drew up the document remembered as the Angamaly Padiyola of 1787. In it the representatives of the Syrian Catholic clergy set down their grievances against the foreign administration and bound themselves to seek the government of native prelates, refusing to submit any longer to a rule that kept men of their own race from the episcopate of their own church. It was not a break with Rome; the cattanars remained Catholic, and meant to. It was a revolt against the foreign manner of Rome's government, an organised insistence, in writing and on oath, that the Thomas Christians be ruled by their own. 12

The immediate crisis was settled, in that same year, by the King of Travancore, in whose dominions most of the parties now lived and to whose arbitration the dispute between the missionaries and the Syrian communities was at length referred. We know of the settlement from the letter that the vicar apostolic Pianazzi and his four remaining missionaries sent to Propaganda on 7 May 1787, a letter that incidentally measures how thin the foreign mission had by then become; for of the once considerable Carmelite establishment only the vicar apostolic and four priests remained, and one of those four was the learned Austrian orientalist Paulinus of St Bartholomew, whose count of the Malabar parishes in this period, eighty-four Catholic and thirty-two dissident, with the Catholics still divided between the jurisdictions of Kodungallur and Verapoly, gives us our clearest census of the divided church. 13

The settlement of 1787 settled nothing fundamental. Paremmakkal governed Kodungallur as administrator until his death, but no native succession was secured; the desire for a metropolitan of their own race was deferred, not satisfied; and the controversies between the Carmelites and the Padroado, which had set the whole condition of grievance in place, would drag on for another half-century until the Brief Multa praeclara of 1838. What the Padiyola did was not to win its object but to declare it, to put on record, in the voice of the native clergy itself, that the Thomas Christians regarded the foreign government of their church as a thing to be ended. That declaration, once made, did not unmake itself. The aspiration to native government, raised to open expression in 1787, would run on through the whole of the nineteenth century, surfacing again and again until, far beyond the bounds of this history, it was at last met. 14

So the spark that Chapter 14 foresaw fell, in the end, not on the dry resentment of the Carmelite decades alone, but on a particular grief, a native archbishop dead at Goa and believed murdered, that gathered all the older resentment into itself. The two channels of this chapter met in that moment. The Malankara Church had shown, by 1772, that a people might secure bishops of its own and pay for them in schism; the Catholic cattanars, in 1787, declared that they too would be governed by their own, though they meant to pay no such price and to remain within the Roman communion while they sought it. The one revolt was a long time coming and a long time bearing fruit. But the seed planted at the Coonan Cross more than a century before, that the Thomas Christians were a church and not a mission, and would in the end insist on being governed as one, had now broken surface on both sides of the divide at once. 15

The Synod of Diamper and the Architecture of Subjugation — 1452–1615

The Collapsed Sword

"There are two powers, august Emperor, by which this world is chiefly ruled: the sacred authority of the priests and the royal power."

— Pope Gelasius I to Emperor Anastasius I, Famuli Vestrae Pietatis, 494

Chapter 9 ended in the silence after Mar Abraham's death. No bishop in the Serra. No delegation en route to Mesopotamia. An Inquisition dossier in Roman and Lisbon hands. An Augustinian archbishop in Goa preparing to ride south. To understand what he did when he arrived, and why the Thomas Christians could not stop him, we must step back from the Serra and look at the architecture that produced him. Menezes did not invent the system. He was the system reaching its operational conclusion.

10.1 — THE TWO SWORDS AND THEIR COLLAPSE

The Medieval Architecture

For nearly a thousand years, Western Christendom had organised itself around a single doctrinal proposition. In 494, Pope Gelasius I had written to the Byzantine Emperor Anastasius I that the world was governed by two powers: the auctoritas sacrata of the priests and the regalis potestas of kings. The two were not independent of each other, but distinct in origin and in function. The priest answered to God for the soul; the king answered to God for the body politic. Where they touched, they cooperated; where they conflicted, they contended. Neither could absorb the other without violating the divinely ordained order of things.1

Unam Sanctam, issued by Pope Boniface VIII in 1302, articulated this relationship in the language of two swords: the Spiritual, wielded by the Church, and the Temporal, wielded by rulers but ultimately subject to it. The framework that emerged did not eliminate conflict. Throughout its history, Christendom wrestled continually with the temptation to conflate institutional power with spiritual mandate, and spiritual mandate with institutional power. The long struggles between Empire and Papacy—from the Investiture Controversy to the Avignon Papacy—were fought within this framework, not outside it. What it preserved, despite repeated strain, was a fundamental architectural distinction: the Sword of the Spirit and the Sword of the State remained two instruments, in two hands, ordered toward different domains, even when the sacred claimed primacy over the secular.

The Iberian Transfer and the Dynastic Concentration

In the second half of the fifteenth century, the Two Swords architecture began to dissolve along the Iberian frontier. The dissolution was not a doctrinal rejection of the Gelasian framework, but a gradual transfer under the pressures of expansion and the strategic imperatives of survival imposed by Ottoman advance in the Mediterranean. Through a sequence of papal bulls from the fifteenth century onward, the administration of ecclesiastical structures in the newly encountered oceanic territories was progressively entrusted to the Iberian crowns under the systems of Padroado and Patronato.

The architect of the transfer's deeper ideology was Manuel I of Portugal (r. 1495–1521), who styled himself "Lord of the conquest, navigation and commerce of Ethiopia, Arabia, Persia and India" and who understood his maritime empire not as a commercial venture but as a divinely sanctioned providential project. The Manueline imperial ideology fused crusading, messianic, and apocalyptic strands into a single self-understanding: the king of Portugal was the appointed instrument by which the Catholic faith would, before the end of days, encircle the world. In Castile, Ferdinand and Isabella operated under a closely related providential logic: the Reconquista completed in 1492, the expulsion of the Jews of the same year, and Columbus's voyage four months later were understood as one continuous act of Catholic restoration and expansion. By the early sixteenth century, both Iberian Crowns shared the same self-understanding as the divinely appointed agents of universal Catholic rule. 2

The Crowns increasingly adjudicated matters that properly belonged to Rome and ignored papal directives when they proved inconvenient — a posture captured in the formula obedezco pero no cumplo: I obey, but I do not comply. With papal sanction, the Crowns assumed control over inquisitorial institutions, acquiring the official legal right not only to organise the Church but to police belief within it. By the time Praecelsae Devotionis (1514) confirmed and consolidated the Portuguese privileges, the system was complete: along the entire arc of European maritime expansion, the Spiritual Sword and the Temporal Sword were held in the same hand. The Crown determined where dioceses would be created, who would serve as bishop, what languages the liturgy could employ, and how the Inquisition would operate. Rome retained titular supremacy. Operational sovereignty had passed to Lisbon and Madrid. Within the Iberian self-understanding, this fusion was not a departure from the Two Swords doctrine. It was the operational condition under which the providential project could be executed.3

This Iberian state of mind, which came to be called the Metropole ideology, carried within it a soteriology of a particular type. The Catholic theology of salvation has two dimensions. The interior dimension — justification, grace, faith — is knowable only to God; earthly authorities have no jurisdiction over the soul's actual standing. The exterior dimension — sacrament, observance, public conformity — is the visible sign by which a community of the faithful organises itself in time. The two are related but not coextensive. The Iberian imperial project, in its working operation, began to treat them as if they were. The public act of conversion was reframed as the act by which a non-Christian became a citizen of the Catholic Kingdom, wherever in the world that kingdom reached. Maintaining citizenship required visible Catholic practice; visible practice therefore had to be policed continuously, because behaviour was the only dimension of salvation the imperial apparatus could observe. The interior question of free will and grace was, by the same logic, displaced into the only domain the state could administer. Salvation was being managed and enforced by the King. 4

When Charles of the Habsburg dynasty inherited and consolidated this machinery in 1519, he was simultaneously King of Spain (as Charles I), Lord of the Indies, ruler of the Burgundian Netherlands, King of Naples, Duke of Milan, and Holy Roman Emperor (as Charles V): the largest concentration of personal sovereignty in European history since Charlemagne. He was also, through the Patronato, the legal administrative head of the Catholic Church across the Spanish Indies. The Two Swords were no longer merely held by the same Crown. They were held by the same dynasty, across continents, under a single sovereign who saw himself as the defender of Christendom and the appointed mediator between God and a world empire still in formation. 5

While this worldview envisioned itself as the salvation of the earth, it was deeply fractured by its own internal contradictions. A glaring example of this tension was the pase regio (royal assent), an Iberian legal mechanism dictating that no papal directive could be enforced without the Crown's approval. When Pope Paul III attempted to intervene in 1537 with Sublimis Deus and its companion brief Pastorale Officium—affirming that indigenous peoples were rational beings and threatening excommunication for enslaving them—Charles V, under pressure from colonial interests, effectively neutralized the enforcement of these penalties. The doctrine remained, but its enforcement did not, limiting the operational reach of Rome’s spiritual authority. Stripped of effective papal intervention, these moral and structural tensions reached a critical point in the Valladolid Debate of 1550–1551.

Confronted by mounting reports of colonial abuses and their theological implications, Charles V ordered a temporary suspension of further conquests in 1550 and convened the theologians of Valladolid to determine the moral legality of subjugating the Indigenous peoples. Displaced from Rome to a royal junta in Spain, the matter pitted Bartolomé de las Casas, the Dominican defender of indigenous rights, against the humanist Juan Ginés de Sepúlveda. Drawing on Aquinas, Las Casas contended that the peoples of the New World were rational beings endowed with free will, possessed of legitimate sovereignty over their lands, and incapable of being justly subjugated by force. Sepúlveda argued from Aristotle that the indigenous peoples were natural slaves whom the bearers of higher reason had a duty to govern, that conversion required prior conquest, and that the Spanish Crown was justified in waging war for the salvation of souls. Although Las Casas’s arguments carried significant moral weight, the theologians of the junta failed to issue a definitive verdict. Ruling decisively in his favour would have undermined the economic and legal foundations of the colonial system, driven by the encomienda. The junta lacked the political capacity to produce such a conclusion. 6

The debate succeeded in blocking the publication of Sepúlveda's Democrates Alter, preventing his arguments from being formally endorsed as doctrine. Yet the absence of a definitive ruling allowed colonial practices to continue, shifting forms rather than ceasing. The inability to secure a stable resolution at the level of moral theology made something else necessary: a codified, uniform, and enforceable structure capable of absorbing such contradictions without operational paralysis.

The Codified System

The Metropole imperial system needed an instrument. The Council of Trent (1545–1563) supplied it.

Convened to answer the Reformation, Trent achieved something the Iberian empires required for reasons of their own: a standardised, exportable, enforceable Catholicism. The Tridentine catechisms produced uniform doctrine for missionary use. The Tridentine liturgical reforms produced a uniform calendar and uniform sacramental rites. The Tridentine moral regulations produced behavioural codes — concerning marriage, food, fasting, dress, public devotion — that could be translated directly into the Empire's law.

Inside the soteriological compression already at work in the Iberian empires, Trent was not a corrective. It was an enabling apparatus. The interior dimension of salvation, which had always been understood as God's domain, remained nominally in place; theologians continued to write about it with care. But governance no longer needed it. The Crown's task of extending the Kingdom of Christ across the world could be discharged through the codified, exterior, behavioural framework that Trent had now made available. Within the metropole, Catholicism became less a practice of interior faith and more of a conditioned behavior— observable from outside, measurable, prosecutable and applied by force. The Goa Inquisition's prosecution of local customs including food, dress, language, and song (Chapter 9) is intelligible only inside this compression. The convert who failed to abandon a particular grain, a particular garment, a particular festival was punished not so much for theological error as for civic disruption. Inside the Holy Kingdom, "right" behavior—orthopraxis—constituted the essence of citizenship. It served as the cartaz granting access to both the empire and heaven— because the administrative system was entirely blind to anything but outward compliance. 7

The Final Concentration

Charles V, afflicted by severe gout and the constant strain of navigating the tensions between the theological responsibilities of his Catholic faith and the political necessities of governing a vast and diverse empire, abdicated in 1556. He partitioned his domains: the Imperial title and the Austrian lands to his brother Ferdinand; Spain, the Netherlands, the Italian possessions, and the Indies to his son Philip II. 8

Philip II inherited the Spanish monarchy determined to eliminate what his father had been forced to tolerate: ambiguity. The system reached its peak operational intensity under his reign. The moriscos of Granada were subjected to forced relocation following the Alpujarras Revolt of 1568–71. The Dutch Protestants were subjected to sustained military repression. And in 1571, the Battle of Lepanto delivered a decisive naval victory to the Holy League, marking a turning point in the long contest with the Ottomans. Each, in Philip’s understanding, formed part of a single enterprise governed by a unified mandate. His motto was non sufficit orbis: the world is not enough. It was not a boast. It was the formal expression of a soteriological-imperial logic in which the existing world was an incomplete sketch awaiting its providential filling-in by the dynasty God had appointed to the work. 9

In the Iberian Union of 1580, when Philip II absorbed the Portuguese Crown, the system reached its final concentration. The two empires retained separate laws, separate treasuries, separate colonial bureaucracies. But they were now held by the same king. Both Padroado and Patronato. Both Inquisitions. Both archives. Both maritime systems. 10

Wielded by a single Habsburg hand, the "Two Swords" had become a unified, double-edged instrument. Codified by the Council of Trent and driven by the theology of non sufficit orbis, it was operationalized by administrators who equated theological doctrine with state governance. Under one sovereign will, the Viceroy of the state and the Archbishop of the Padroado advanced the Empire's grand vision. The man who would wield power in Malabar in 1599 was shaped by this system.

The Metropole: A Global Catholic Utopia

The Iberian Metropole was not a place. It was an imperial worldview that imagined the world as a single, ordered Christian commonwealth under Catholic monarchs. However, the ground on which the ideology acted refused, constantly, to match the picture the Metropole held of it.

The Imperial Ideal

  • Universal Monarchy + Universal Church: The king was not merely a ruler of territories but a quasi-universal sovereign. Territorial expansion and spiritual unification were treated as a single undertaking; political authority and religious mission were inseparable; the extension of empire was the extension of Christendom.
  • The "Citizenizing" Hierarchy: The metropole imagined a graded order within its Empire: Catholics — both old Christians and new converts — were citizen vassals of the Kingdom regardless of region; below them stood convertible peoples of other faiths; below those, the infidels and enemies (Muslims, resistant groups). The goal was to transform every subject into a loyal Catholic vassal.
  • A Mirrored Society: The overseas territories were to reproduce the ideal social order the metropole had sought to establish on the Iberian Peninsula itself: one king over many lands, one faith over many peoples, one legal framework applied globally. This demanded the wholesale replication of institutions, from viceroyalties to dioceses, alongside the strict imposition of Iberian legal, religious, social, and urban norms.
  • The Messianic Dimension: The ideology carried a deeply messianic dimension. It cast the expanding Empire as the coming universal Christian order, sometimes framed as a "Fifth Empire". In Portugal, this hope crystallised in Sebastianism: the belief that King Sebastian — who had vanished at the Battle of Alcácer Quibir in 1578, opening the way for Philip II's absorption of the Portuguese Crown and the Iberian Union of 1580 — would one day return as "the Hidden One", the messianic emperor who would rule the Fifth Empire.

The Reality Gap

  • Crown Catholicism vs Roman Catholicism: Rome and the Iberian Crowns regularly diverged on doctrinal questions and ecclesiastical appointments. Rome was preoccupied with the integrity of conversions and the manner in which the faith was applied; the Crowns prioritised order, loyalty, and imperial stability. The result was a recurring tension between tolerance and coercion, with concrete disputes over indigenous rites, conversion methods, and the treatment of local Christians and other peoples. The Iberian empires imagined a Catholic world under the king; the Papacy insisted on a Church above kingdoms. These were two competing universalisms — royal-imperial and papal-spiritual — and they could not coexist without friction.
  • Obedience Without Compliance: Local administrators routinely invoked the formula obedezco pero no cumplo — I obey but I do not comply — to delay, modify, or quietly ignore orders from Lisbon and Madrid they judged impractical or unenforceable.
  • Going Native: The casados of the Estado da Índia, intended as loyal settlers extending the metropole into the Indian Ocean, became known throughout the region as private traders with Asian concubines. The lançados of West Africa embraced local cultures and severed their ties with Portugal. In Asia, renegades formed what local observers called, collectively, "a Portuguese tribe."
  • Appropriation of the Faith: Where the Metropole imagined perfect conversions and strict Latin practice, Goa, Bahia, and Puebla produced Christianities that were indigenously shaped, locally negotiated, and at times openly syncretic.

10.2 — THE PRIMATE OF THE EAST AND THE FLY IN THE TEACUP

The Portuguese Crown's Eastern empire, by the late sixteenth century, ran from the Cape of Good Hope to Japan. Goa was its capital. The Archbishop of Goa held a title that the Padroado had stitched into existence: Primaz do Oriente, Primate of the East. In Portuguese juridical theory, every Christian community within that maritime arc, from Mozambique to Nagasaki, fell under his pastoral jurisdiction.

However, there was an embarrassment.

Down the coast from Goa, in the pepper hinterland of Malabar, there stood an ancient metropolitan see that held a title older and grander than anything Lisbon could grant. The Metropolitan of Angamaly represented the continuation of the older see of Kodungallur and bore, in the canonical formula of the Church of the East, the title Metropolita d'Hindo, Metropolitan and Gate of All India. The title was not merely ceremonial. Within the East Syrian patriarchate, the Metropolitan of India ranked tenth in the hierarchy, with a notional jurisdiction extending across the subcontinent. The see had existed in some form since at least the seventh century. When Pius IV confirmed Mar Abraham as Archbishop of Angamaly in 1565, he was not creating a dignity, but recognising one (Chapter 9).11

Within Habsburg legalism, overlapping jurisdictions were intolerable. To imperial economics, an autonomous Eastern church holding the pepper hinterland was a leak in the system. The Archbishop of Goa could not credibly call himself Primate of the East while a rival Metropolitan with a more ancient title remained in the south, ordaining his own priests in Syriac and praying in his liturgy for the "heretic" Patriarch of Babylon.

The Jesuit treatises of Francis Roz (1586–1588) had provided the theological inventory. The 1594 Enformação do Prelado da Serra had translated that inventory into a charge sheet for the Inquisition. The subsequent papal briefs had authorised the Archbishop of Goa to investigate Mar Abraham's faith, to keep him under surveillance, and upon his death to appoint a Latin Vicar Apostolic and to forbid the entry of any future bishop from Babylon. 12

What the briefs did not provide was the authority to convoke a synod, to legislate liturgical reform, or to alter the canonical status of an independent Metropolitan See. This distinction would matter later. It would matter even more, three centuries later, when canon lawyers came to ask whether the Synod of Diamper had ever, in strict legal terms, taken place at all.

For the moment, the man being prepared to act on the briefs did not concern himself with such distinctions. He carried a different kind of authority. He carried both Swords.

10.3 — THE VACANT SEE

With the See of Angamaly vacant, Menezes swiftly mobilized his administrative machinery.

It is tempting to paint Menezes as an arrogant overlord. He was certainly an administrator, but the arrogance—if that is the right word—belonged less to the man than to the system that appointed him. It was a system that mistook pride for strength of faith and certainty for the right to coerce. What Menezes possessed in unusual concentration was the operational competence to execute that system's logic without theological hesitation or pastoral doubt. He had what neither the Jesuits nor the Franciscans, by the structure of their orders, could fully provide: the willingness to use the Spiritual Sword and the Temporal Sword interchangeably, in the same hand, on the same day, without pausing to separate the divine from the imperial.

The Jesuits had supplied the intelligence reports. They had not supplied the will to act on them. By the late 1590s, the climate inside the Society had shifted decisively against the use of force in Malabar. The accommodation strategy that had produced Vaipicota, Peter Luis, Dionysio's ethnographies, and the improved relations after 1594 was not simply a method; it was the Jesuits' settled reading of what kind of Christianity could take root in the Serra. They had lived among the Thomas Christians for half a century. They knew the chaverpada existed. They knew what Carneiro's expedition had revealed about the community's capacity for armed resistance. They knew that Abraham's testament naming Archdeacon George of the Cross as administrator represented not a bureaucratic preference but the working constitution of an ancient Eastern church.

From December 1597, the Jesuit superiors in Cochin and Malabar tried to restrain Menezes. Roz himself, who had written the treatises that supplied the theological architecture of the coming confrontation, now wrote to Rome urging restraint. In a letter to the Jesuit General of 24 December 1597, he warned that although the Archbishop of Goa was a zealous and saintly prelate acting under papal brief, the community would be lost if force were imposed, and that a gentle approach would achieve far more. 13

Roz was not opposing the reduction of Angamaly to Roman obedience in principle. He was opposing the method. Coercion, in the Jesuit reading, would produce formal compliance without interior conversion, and would leave behind a community that had been defeated rather than persuaded. Roz had already, in 1594, recommended the indigenous Archdeacon George of the Cross as coadjutor bishop of Angamaly. By late 1597 he was alerting his superiors to an imminent danger: the Archbishop of Goa must be restrained before his zeal consumed what five decades of patient work had built. 14

Mar Abraham's testament named Archdeacon George of the Cross as administrator of the diocese, in accordance with Chaldean tradition and the long-established dual-governance practice of the Thomas Christian community: a foreign metropolitan supplying canonical legitimacy, a native archdeacon supplying local authority, the two together forming the working constitution of the Serra. 15

To the Roman canon law, however, Abraham's testament could not bind the future. A Latin diocese in sede vacante required a Vicar Apostolic appointed by competent authority. Within weeks of Mar Abraham's death, Menezes issued a patent letter from Goa appointing Francis Roz as Vicar Apostolic of Angamaly. The Jesuit superiors in Cochin suspended the patent. They knew the ground. They knew the Archdeacon. They knew that the community had sworn a public oath in 1597, in the days immediately following Mar Abraham's death, to petition the Pope directly if a Latin bishop were imposed on Angamaly. 16

The Thomas Christians had already settled the question on their own terms. Archdeacon George, recognised by the community as the legitimate successor to Abraham's administrative authority, took possession of the diocese without waiting for permission from Goa. When Menezes's instruction reached Cochin and was overturned by the Jesuits, the Archbishop of Goa was forced to retreat. He confirmed George as Governor, but only as Governor, and only with Roz and the Rector of Vaipicota imposed as consultants. The Archdeacon, predictably, refused to accept the patent on those terms. He would govern as he had always governed, or not at all. 17

The disagreement was not procedural. It was structural. Two different systems of authority were trying to occupy the same office at the same time.

▣ THE TWO OFFICES OF THE SERRA
  • Bishop of the See (Latin canonical category): the permanent, fully consecrated Ordinary of a diocese. Holds both administrative and sacramental authority. Can ordain priests, consecrate altars, pass excommunication, govern in his own name. Holds office until death, resignation, or papal removal.
  • Governor of the See (Latin canonical category): a temporary administrator appointed during sede vacante, the period when a diocese has no bishop. Holds administrative authority only. Cannot ordain priests or perform episcopal sacraments unless he is already a consecrated bishop. The position expires the moment a new Ordinary is consecrated.
  • Archdeacon of All India (East Syrian / Thomas Christian category): not a temporary placeholder but the permanent indigenous head of the community, hereditary within a dominant family lineage and known in Malayalam as Jathikku Karthavyan, the head of the Jati. The foreign metropolitan, who often spoke no Malayalam, restricted himself to spiritual functions; the Archdeacon wielded administrative, civil, and military authority over the community for life, regardless of episcopal succession.

When Menezes appointed Archdeacon George as Governor of the See, he was using a Latin canonical category to demote a permanent indigenous office into a temporary administrative leash. To the Archdeacon, who had already governed his community by traditional right since 1594, the appointment was an insult dressed as a courtesy.

The arrangement held, uneasily, through 1598. Menezes kept watch from Goa. The Archdeacon kept the Serra. Then reports from Vaipicota that suggested the Archdeacon was consolidating his community's traditional positions reached Goa. For Menezes, this confirmed what the Jesuit accommodation had failed to absorb. The community could not be governed remotely. It would have to be governed in person. Against the counsel of the Society of Jesus, against the warnings of his advisers in Goa, and despite the tears of those who tried to dissuade him from so perilous a venture, the Archbishop of Goa prepared to ride south. The Council of Trent had mandated episcopal residence—that a bishop must reside in his see. Menezes would apply the principle beyond its proper jurisdiction.

On 27 December 1598, he set out.

10.4 — THE JOURNEY

A Landscape of Armed Christianity

The route south passed through a landscape in which religious identity had become entangled with economic and military power for over a century.

When the Portuguese first arrived on the Malabar coast, they encountered Mappila Muslim merchants already entrenched in the spice trade. The Portuguese response, framed in crusading terms and executed by successive captains from Cabral onward, was to treat the Mappilas as enemies of Christ and to break them militarily and commercially. The Mappilas responded in kind. In 1522, local Mappilas attacked Christians at Cochin after a Portuguese factor had beaten a Muslim. Around 1524, after Mar Jacob had persuaded his Thomas Christian flock to sell their pepper directly to the Portuguese, Mappila parties attacked Christian houses and churches in retaliation. In 1545, following Portuguese use of Tiruvangadi as a base against Muslim shipping, Mappila forces again targeted the town, with particular reference to Christian properties. The local rulers, whose economic interests were bound up with established Muslim trade networks, generally favoured those communities over Christians, who were increasingly treated as socially marginal and, in some cases, compelled to move into areas under Portuguese protection. 18

Pressure came not only from Muslim commercial rivals. It came also from the Hindu vassal rajas whose petty kingdoms ringed Cochin. Paravur and Mangate were two such polities, lying immediately north of Cochin and frequently at war with one another. Within these two polities, the Thomas Christians were not a marginal community. They formed a substantial component of the military structure. Portuguese estimates placed their numbers as high as fifty thousand armed men, and the severance of their service would significantly weaken the Raja of Cochin. Trained in arms from an early age, they were widely regarded as among the most capable soldiers on the Malabar battlefield. 19

This military centrality came with a condition. When a Paravur raja redirected his commercial favour from his Christian merchants to Muslim traders while continuing to demand Christian military service against his Mangate rival, effectively expending Christian levies as front-line troops, the arrangement broke. The community's response drew on a principle Mar Jacob had already articulated to the Portuguese Crown: Christians served the rulers who protected them. Where protection was withdrawn, service followed. The chaverpada, drawn from among the kalari-trained Christian fighters, existed precisely to enforce that principle. Carneiro's expedition in the 1560s (Chapter 9) had already encountered two thousand amoces mobilised in defence of Mar Abraham. The Serra was not a disarmed pastoral community, and it had learned, under successive waves of Hindu, Muslim, and European pressure, how to organise its armed capacity as corporate self-defence. 20

Three Techniques

Menezes's journey moved through this landscape carrying both Swords. He spent sixteen days in Kannur and then proceeded to Cochin, where the Portuguese captain and the city authorities received him with full honours. From Cochin, he embarked on a four-month visitation of the Thomas Christian parishes of the Serra. The objective was a synod. The work, between January and June 1599, was to make the synod's outcome inevitable before the synod was convened. 21

Three techniques defined the visitation.

The first was direct subjugation of the Archdeacon. Menezes announced repeatedly his intention to depose George of the Cross and replace him with a relative, the cattanar Thomas Kurian. The threat was credible: the Padroado machinery was visibly in place—the Portuguese captain at Cochin, the viceroy in Goa, the cartaz fleet in the harbour, and the inquisitorial dossier Menezes carried with him. Intermediaries pleaded for the Archdeacon. Menezes drew up a list of conditions and demanded his signature. At Vaipicota, finally, the two men met. The Archdeacon submitted partially, agreeing to terms that included a pledge not to recognise any bishop other than those sent by the Pope and to ensure good attendance at the forthcoming synod. 22

The second was the strategic use of episcopal ordination. Menezes possessed sacramental powers the Archdeacon did not, and he used them deliberately. At Kaduthuruthy, he examined thirty-eight candidates for ordination, required each to renounce the Patriarch of Babylon and submit to the Bishop of Rome, and ordained all thirty-eight in a single ceremony. At Paravur, he repeated the process with fifty candidates. The ordinations served two purposes simultaneously. They added to a clergy aligned with Rome, and they ensured that, when the synod was finally convened, a substantial bloc of newly ordained priests would owe their orders to Menezes personally and would vote accordingly. The pattern was deliberate: a voting majority was being constructed before the meeting that would require it. 23

The third technique was material patronage. Menezes had at his disposal the resources of the Portuguese Crown. He visited the sick, distributed alms, and funded repairs to churches. He also intervened with local rulers in defence of his Christians, reminding the Raja of Cochin that "when our people entered upon friendship with the rajas of Cochin, they were very poor and insignificant princelings of Malabar, whereas today the Portuguese have enriched them and have added to their power." This was a form of authority the Thomas Christians had not experienced from their previous bishops, who had been guests of the community and dependent on its hospitality. The contrast registered. Some of the cattanars concluded that a Portuguese prelate, backed by arms and wealth, might offer the community a more secure place in the local political order than its traditional Eastern hierarchs had ever supplied. 24

By April 1599, the combination of coercion, sacramental engineering, and material patronage had produced a working majority for what Menezes intended to do. The Archbishop withdrew with his Jesuit assistants to the seminary at Vaipicota. For four weeks they drafted, in Portuguese, the decrees that the synod would be required to accept. The decrees were translated into Malayalam in haste. The summons went out, jointly under the names of the Archbishop and the Archdeacon, on 11 May 1599. The synod would convene at Diamper (Udayamperur) on 20 June. The town had been chosen, as the later journal would acknowledge, for its proximity to the Portuguese garrison. 25

10.5 — THE SYNOD

Seven Days at Diamper

The Synod of Diamper convened on 20 June 1599 and continued for seven days.

One hundred and fifty-three cattanars and six hundred and fifty lay representatives assembled. The chapter of the cathedral of Cochin attended in full strength. The Portuguese captain Antony de Noronha was present with his men. Civic officials of Cochin formed the remainder of the Latin contingent. The local Hindu rulers, suspecting that the meeting concerned more than theology, posted observers throughout. 26

Menezes opened the proceedings by celebrating solemn pontifical mass ad tollendum schisma (for the removal of schism). Roz preached. A trilingual interpretation system was established to ensure that the proceedings were understood by all participants: a cattanar named Jacob handled the primary work, with Roz and António Toscano S.J. checking him.

The first day's substantive business was a single decree forbidding any participant, on pain of excommunication, to attend any meeting (juntoes, private assemblies) outside the synod to discuss its proceedings. The second day was given over entirely to the profession of faith. The text had been drafted by Menezes and his Jesuit advisers in the four weeks at Vaipicota. It included the standard Tridentine articles and added an explicit anathema of the Patriarch of Babylon, denounced as "a Nestorian heretic and schismatic, and out of the obedience of the holy Roman church, and for that reason out of a state of salvation." 27

The Thomas Christians present found the procedure unfamiliar and objectionable. They had always understood themselves to be Christians and were unaware of any schism or heresy. Why, then, should they now be required to make a profession of faith as though they had previously lacked it? Menezes, sensing the resistance, removed his mitre, knelt before the altar, and made the entire profession himself in a loud voice. When the Archdeacon followed his example, the murmuring subsided. The proceedings on that day alone took seven hours.

The remaining sessions worked through the catalogue Roz's treatises had compiled. The Eastern liturgical books were declared to contain "Nestorian" errors and were ordered corrected or burned. The married priesthood, ancient in the Serra, was to be brought into Tridentine conformity through enforced celibacy for new ordinations, while episcopal candidates continued to be drawn from the celibate. The Chaldean liturgical year was to be aligned with the Roman calendar. The community's right to receive bishops from Babylon was extinguished. The metropolitan dignity of Angamaly was, within the logic of the decrees themselves, dissolved into the suffragan jurisdiction of Goa. The three hundred pages of decrees, in the English translation later made by Michael Geddes (1694), record the systematic re-engineering of an ancient Eastern church into a province of the Latin Padroado. 28

The Canonical Bluff

The synod's authority to perform any of this was, by the strict canon law that the synod itself professed to enforce, doubtful at best.

The 1595 papal brief had authorised Menezes to investigate the faith of Mar Abraham. The 1597 papal brief had authorised him, upon Abraham's death, to appoint a Latin Vicar Apostolic and to forbid the entry of any future bishop from Babylon. Neither brief contained a single word authorising the convocation of a diocesan synod, the legislation of liturgical reform, or the alteration of the canonical status of the See of Angamaly. Under Tridentine canon law, the convocation of a diocesan synod required either the local Ordinary or a duly commissioned Papal Legate. The Archbishop of Goa was neither. Angamaly, at the date of the synod, was still a fully independent Metropolitan See with its own ancient title; the structural papal mandate that would reduce it to suffragan status would not be issued until 20 December 1599, six months after Diamper. 29

Menezes acted ultra vires. The synod proceeded by canonical bluff, sustained by the visible presence of Portuguese soldiers and by the prior manufacture, through strategic ordinations, of a compliant voting majority. The Thomas Christians, lacking access to the documentary record in Rome that would later reveal the deficit, accepted what appeared to them as overwhelming legitimacy. For his part, Menezes was doing only what seemed inevitable.

Four years later, in 1603, when the dust had begun to settle, the Jesuit Visitor in India received a communication from Francis Roz that would not be aired publicly for centuries. Roz, the man whose treatises had supplied the theological architecture of the synod and who had stood at Menezes's side throughout the proceedings, wrote to his superiors in Rome that the Archbishop had summoned the synod with the help of the Portuguese captain and his soldiers, that the priests had come armed, that the decrees had never received papal approval, and that everything had been done by force. The intelligence officer who had built the weapon had lived to see what an Augustinian administrator could do with it. 30

10.6 — THE PHANTOM VICTORY

Enforcement and Erasure

The decrees of Diamper required execution.

In the weeks following the synod, Menezes toured the parishes of the Serra to enforce its rulings. Liturgical books were collected and burned in public, in acts that mirrored the demonstrative logic of the auto-da-fé in Goa. Cattanars were required to make individual professions of the new faith. The Chaldean offices were silenced. The Patriarch of Babylon was struck from the liturgy. The Archbishop returned to Goa on 16 November 1599, having spent eleven months in Malabar. He had completed, in the language his Jesuit predecessors had not been willing to use, a coercive reduction of an entire community to Latin discipline. 31

On 20 December 1599, Pope Clement VIII issued the bull that retrospectively regularised the new structure. Angamaly was stripped of its Metropolitan status. Its ancient title, Metropolita d'Hindo, was extinguished. The see was reduced to a suffragan of the Latin Archdiocese of Goa. The Archbishop of Goa was now, in fact as well as in claim, the Primaz do Oriente without rival. What had been executed in June was given legal form in December. The transition from execution to archive was remarkably swift. 32

In 1601, Francis Roz was consecrated as the first Latin Bishop of Angamaly. The man who had spent fifteen years inside the Chaldean administration, who had recommended the indigenous Archdeacon for the episcopacy as recently as 1594, who had warned Menezes against excessive zeal in 1597, and who had documented in 1603 the canonical irregularities of the Synod, now wore the mitre that Menezes had created for him. He governed the Thomas Christians until his death in 1624. 33

The triumph was structural and immediate. It was also, beneath the surface, fragile. What had been achieved by force now required to be sustained as legitimacy.

The Long Shadow

The Archdeacon George of the Cross had been forced to swear the new profession of faith in a language he could not understand. He had submitted, but he had not been persuaded. In the years following Diamper, he would slowly reconstruct the parallel system: the palli yogams meeting in the village churches, the chaverpada available if needed, and the older liturgical memory preserved in oral practice. By 1618, when Roz appointed the Jesuit Rector of Vaipicota as his vicar general instead of George of the Cross, the insult would prove unforgiven. Considerable sections of the community withdrew obedience. In a letter of 1622, Roz would write that he had known the Archdeacon for thirty-nine years and that kindness had had no effect on him: the Archdeacon was incorrigible. The Jesuit accommodation strategy, which the Society had warned in 1597 was the only approach that could produce a persuaded Serra, had been buried by Diamper and could not be recovered. 34

The Jornada and Its Rewards

In the years following the Synod, Menezes found himself facing criticism not only from the community he had reduced but also from quarters in India that began, haltingly and often in private correspondence, to question the methods he had used. By 1605, the Archbishop of Goa was defending himself against what he called the accusations of "reckless people" in India. He portrayed himself in a dispatch to the Crown as "a fragile instrument" in God's hand, implying that whatever he had done had been done by divine agency rather than personal ambition. He had already commissioned António de Gouvea to write a narrative in support of his mission. 35

The result was the Jornada do Arcebispo de Goa Dom Frey Aleixo de Menezes, first printed at Coimbra in 1606. Gouvea had not been present at the visitation; he wrote from documents, from Menezes's own dictation, and from the testimony of the Archbishop's confessor. The Jornada did not merely recount the synod. It translated it. A process secured through pressure, managed participation, and constrained consent was rendered as free and joyful submission. At its close, the cattanars appear kneeling, shedding tears of joy. Coercion was recast as conviction. Compliance was presented as assent. What had been a canonical bluff was rewritten as canonical order.

This translation was not incidental. The Habsburg system, for all its concentration of power, remained formally committed to legal procedure and documentary justification. A synod—even a regional one—could be made invalid if coercion were visible. The Jornada supplied the necessary gloss. It converted an act executed under the combined force of the Spiritual and Temporal Swords into a narrative that could be cited, defended, and rewarded. Menezes was cast, ultimately, as the new Cyril: the saint who had cleansed the Primate of the East of Nestorian heresy and handed it, so reduced, to the Habsburg king. 36

It was in this form that the Synod of Diamper would be received by Philip III. In the same year it was printed, Menezes was appointed Acting Viceroy of India. The soteriological-imperial logic of the Metropole had reached its apex in Aleixo de Menezes. The ancient Gelasian doctrine of the Two Swords had always presupposed a distinction between the interceding priest and the ruling prince, but Menezes collapsed this line. From 1606 to 1609 he held both ecclesiastical and civil authority in the Estado da Índia. Then, upon his return to Europe in 1612, he was appointed Archbishop of Braga—a primate seat claiming supremacy over the whole Iberian Peninsula—and Viceroy of Portugal, governing in the Spanish Habsburg king's stead. From 1612 to 1615, he wielded both swords over the entire Iberian homeland. He was the system’s ultimate creation: not a Crown holding the Mitre, but the Mitre wielding the sword. 37

The Archdeacon George of the Cross would later identify Gouvea's Jornada as containing "serious calumnies." It was a fair judgement.38

Half a century later in January 1653, Nazranis would assemble at the granite cross outside the church at Mattancherry, lay their hands on a rope tied to its stone arms, and swear collectively to reject the authority of the Latin Padroado. The cross would bend under their weight. Their oath would become known as the Coonan Cross Oath. It is the subject of the next chapter.

The Two Swords had been collapsed. The community had been forced to formal submission. But the Thomas Christians had not been disarmed, and the moment was waiting.

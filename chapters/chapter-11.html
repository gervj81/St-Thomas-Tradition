The Years Before the Oath — 1601–1624

The Rozian Puzzle

"The exercise of authority makes a man come out of his shell, and it causes the river to overflow its banks."

— Francis Roz to his superiors, 1607

Chapter 10 ended with Menezes departing for Goa in November 1599. A month later, on 20 December 1599, a consistorial act in Rome, taken on the report of Cardinal Gesualdo, preconized the Catalan (from Spanish region of Catalonia) Jesuit Francis Roz as the new bishop of Angamaly, and at the same stroke stripped the see of its metropolitan dignity and reduced it to a suffragan of Goa. Clement VIII's letter ratifying the appointment followed on 15 January 1600. The Synod of Diamper itself was never given formal Roman approval. In January 1601, in the Cathedral of Goa, Francis Roz was consecrated as the first Latin bishop of the see. He was forty-two years old and had lived in India for sixteen years. He had taught Syriac to seminarians at Vaipicota. He understood Malayalam. He had written the treatise that supplied much of the theological architecture of the synod, which he also, in private letters to Rome, documented as a coercive imposition done by force. He governed the Thomas Christians for twenty-three years and died at Kodungallur on 18 February 1624.

This chapter is about the puzzle of those twenty-three years, and about the man at its centre.

The Latin Who Spoke Syriac

The Roz who took possession of the see of Angamaly in 1601 was not the Roz who would die in Kodungallur in 1624. The two men are recognisably the same person, but the operational position the office forced upon him was so different from the position his earlier work had committed him to. The transition can only be told as a gradual, observable transformation of a person who had once been a Jesuit accommodationist, into a Latin bishop who could no longer be either. 1

The earlier Roz is the man already encountered in Chapters 9 and 10. He had arrived in India in 1584. He was sent to the Thomas Christians within months. By 1586 he had begun teaching Syriac at the Jesuit seminary at Vaipicota, the institution Mar Abraham had supported because its accommodationist programme distinguished it from the Latinising agenda of the Goan Padroado. Roz had written his treatise De Erroribus Nestorianorum qui in hac India Orientali versantur in 1586–87. Throughout the same decade he had also recommended to his superiors that any future bishop of Angamaly be drawn from the Chaldean or Maronite tradition, and in letters of 1593 and 1597 had named the indigenous Archdeacon George of the Cross as the most suitable candidate for the coadjutor episcopacy. In December 1597 he had written to the Jesuit General in Rome warning that Menezes's planned visitation would, if conducted by force, lose the community altogether. 2

The Burden of Obedience

The Roz of 1601 carried that record into his consecration. He had not been a candidate for the bishopric. The Jesuit Visitor, Alessandro Valignano had compelled him to accept the office under the vow of obedience. He took it not because he wanted it but because the Society would not allow him to refuse. He was, by his own admission to his superiors, the man who had built the legal weapon that produced the Synod of Diamper, and was now being asked to govern the Serra in the Synod's name. 3

The Thomas Christians had a problem of their own with him. He was a Latin. The community, in its long memory and in the specific oath sworn in 1597 in the days following Mar Abraham's death, had committed itself to refuse a Latin bishop and to petition Rome directly for one of its own rite and nation. By 1601, that petition had not been answered. Rome had declined to intervene. The community had been overtaken by the Padroado machinery. Roz's Latinity was now the constitutive fact of the see.

A Complex Latinity

But his Latinity was not simple. Within a year of his consecration, Roz was writing letters back to Rome and to Madrid arguing that the metropolitan dignity of Angamaly must be restored, that the see must not be a suffragan of Goa, and that the offensive language of the Synod of Diamper, with its repeated denunciation of the Thomas Christians as a "cursed sect of the Nestorians", "disobedient schismatics", "aliens from the holy Catholic faith", "accursed", "excommunicated" and "damnable sect", had overshot the mark and could not be reconciled with what the community actually was. Between 1601 and 1605, he wrote to the Pope, the Iberian Crown, the Cardinal Protector, the Jesuit General, and the Assistant of Portugal repeatedly on these themes. He wanted Diamper softened. He wanted the metropolitan title back. He wanted the see independent of Goa. 4

This was, in form, a Latin bishop petitioning Latin authorities to restore an Eastern church's dignities. In substance, it was the work of a man trying to repair, from within the Latin office that had been imposed on him, the damage that the Latin office had done.

The Repair Work

The repair work had three instruments: a synod, a charter, and a petition. They reached for different audiences and addressed different aspects of the wound, but they were a single sustained project conducted across the first decade of his episcopate.

The Synod of Angamaly

The first was a synod. On 7 December 1603, Bishop Roz convened a new synod at Angamaly. This synod, unlike Diamper, was juridically valid as a diocesan synod under canon law: it was summoned by the local Ordinary in his own see. Its purpose was disclosed by Roz himself in a letter to the Jesuit General Claudio Acquaviva later that month. The Synod of Diamper, Roz wrote, was procedurally invalid. The Indian members had not always been consulted; many had not understood the proceedings; they had signed because Roz himself, then a Jesuit consultor without episcopal authority, had asked them to. Worst of all, Menezes had tampered with the text afterwards: some canons had been added by the Archbishop alone, after the synod had risen, and were never debated by the assembly. 5

In Roz's own words to Acquaviva, written in 1603:

"To tell you the entire truth, some of the canons of the above-said Synod the Archbishop himself added after the Synod was over; not a single canon was discussed or altered, so much so that there was no form of a synod, and it cannot be said to contain anything more than directive principles; they heard them without understanding what was being said, as I can myself bear ample witness as also the other Fathers who understood the language. His Excellency many a time had said that the Synod was intended merely to show them the way of their salvation without obstacles." 6

The Synod of Angamaly of 1603 was Roz's attempt to write a different canon law for the Serra: a body of decrees that retained the Tridentine framework but cancelled Diamper's most disruptive impositions, particularly those that had tampered with local liturgical custom. Roz and his Jesuit collaborators wanted Rome to refuse approval of Diamper and to ratify Angamaly instead. By a combination of careful diplomacy, withheld documents, and the simple fact that the Latin copy of Diamper bound with Clement VIII's coat of arms which had been prepared for Roman approval was never actually delivered to the Curia, Roz succeeded in his first goal. Diamper was never explicitly approved by Rome. Whether his second goal, the substitution of Angamaly's text for Diamper's, was reached is less clear. What is clear is that his refusal to forward Diamper for Roman approval was itself a structural intervention. The synod that had been used to subjugate his community would never carry the universal authority that approval would have given it. 7

The Relação sobre a Serra

The second instrument was a documentary charter. In 1604, Roz wrote the Relação sobre a Serra, a defense of the Malabar Church's antiquity, apostolic origin, and customary independence, drawing on East Syrian books, parish oral traditions, and sixteen years' familiarity with the community. He could not produce the original copper plates: the Thomas of Cana plates had, by Roz's own account, been carried off to Portugal by the Franciscans after the death of Mar Abraham (chapter 9), leaving only a copy in the Cochin factory; and the Quilon (Tarisappalli) plates, which Menezes had seen in 1599 in the keeping of the tarega of Thevalacara, Roz knew only through the translation he commissioned from the cattanar Itimani in October 1601.

Working from these sources, Roz consolidated the fragmented local traditions into a coherent narrative. Roz's Relação championed the theory that the Malabar church was primarily established by Christians who emigrated from Mylapore to escape local persecutions. By identifying the see of Angamaly as the direct continuation of the ancient apostolic see founded by Thomas at Mylapore, Roz paired the western settlements with the eastern memory of the Apostle's tomb, anchoring a single apostolic identity. To this he added the fourth-century arrival of the West Asian merchant Knāi Thomma at Kodungallur, whose privileges were inscribed on the now-lost copper plates. In Roz's reconstruction, the descendants of the Apostle's earlier converts—represented by the four noble families of Cotur, Cataval, Onamthuruth, and Narimattam—and the later immigrant community associated with Knāi Thomma became the later Northist–Southist division.

Roz filled out the treatise with ethnographic observations on the community's high-caste integration. The Thomas Christians, he noted, held a status equivalent to the Nayars and Brahmins, enjoyed royal honors such as elephants, palanquins, and ceremonial umbrellas at public festivities, observed strict ritual purity, and preserved unique domestic rites such as the Thali, the gold medallion cross used in place of a Western ring at marriage. Under the political authority of the native Archdeacon, who marshaled a sword-bearing militia for the local Hindu kings, the Serra operated as an autonomous entity. Roz presented Rome with the portrait of a Church wholly Indian in its social reality yet authentically Eastern in its liturgical life, a defense crafted to safeguard the community from European erasure.

The whole synthesis converged on a single jurisdictional claim. Roz argued that the see of Angamaly was the continuation of the apostolic see Thomas had founded at Mylapore and transferred to Kodungallur, and therefore the oldest see in India, carrying the ancient title the Syriac books gave it: "Metropolitan All Hendo," the metropolitan see of All India. On that footing he mapped its territory on a grand scale, extending down the west coast from Cape Comorin past Quilon, Cochin, Kodungallur, and Kannur, across the Ghats to Mylapore and the whole kingdom of Vijayanagar, or, in an alternative reckoning, all the lands once ruled by the three crowned kings of the south, the Chera, Pandya, and Chola Perumals. The purpose, as Mundadan observes, was to establish Angamaly's apostolic seniority and so resist the absorption of the see into the Padroado as a mere suffragan of Goa. 8

The Petition

The third instrument was a sustained petition. Between 1601 and 1605, Roz had pressed Rome, Madrid, the Jesuit General, and the cardinal protector for the restoration of Angamaly's dignity. The first result was modest: a small concession in the original consistorial bull's framing. The decisive outcomes would follow, but not yet. What followed immediately from the petition was the move. 9

The Move to Kodungallur

In 1605, Roz petitioned Rome to transfer the seat of the see from Angamaly to Kodungallur, the city his own Relação had just identified as the ancient home of the see itself. Beyond the apostolic argument already made, the move carried further advantages: Kodungallur lay under Portuguese garrison and could be defended, while Angamaly stood in the territory of a Hindu raja whose interests did not always align with Portuguese protection; and fixing the metropolitan seat there turned a jurisdictional argument on paper into an occupied fact. The papal brief was issued in 1607. 10

A Move into Custody

To the community, the move read as a further step into Portuguese custody. Kodungallur was a Padroado town. The Latin discipline there was strict: Christians ate fish during Lent rather than the local vegetarian observance, drank wine where their tradition required abstention, and married priesthood was banned from sacerdotal ministry. The pepper-trading Thomas Christian families who lived inland in the Hindu kingdoms had no reason to follow their bishop into a Portuguese fortress, and they did not. The Archdeacon George of the Cross resisted the transfer openly. Most of the Thomas Christians who had previously lived at Kodungallur had already left it for the same reasons. The new metropolitan seat was now in a town nearly emptied of its own metropolitan's people.

This was not the first transfer of the see. Mar Abraham, half a century earlier, had moved his residence between Kodungallur, Vaipicota, and finally Angamaly, settling there precisely because it was outside Portuguese reach. Roz's reverse move undid that settlement. The geography of episcopal residence had become a sign of the community's transition. The bishop who had once written that force would lose the Serra now lived in the Portuguese fortress whose protection his episcopate increasingly required. 11

The Dispute with Cochin

Whatever Roz's intention, the move immediately produced a dispute he had not anticipated. Kodungallur lay within the boundaries of the Latin diocese of Cochin, not of Angamaly, and the bishop of Cochin read the arrival of a metropolitan archbishop inside his diocese as an encroachment on his rights and his territory. What began as a question of overlapping jurisdictions became, over the following years, one of the most sustained and damaging episcopal quarrels of the entire period. 12

The Restoration of Dignity

Rome resolved both problems in sequence. On 22 December 1608, Pope Paul V's constitution Romanus Pontifex, drawing on Roz's accumulated petitions, restored Angamaly's archiepiscopal dignity and expressly affirmed that the see was exempt from any superiority or dominion of the archbishopric of Goa. In January 1609, the Pope issued a separate brief explicitly granting the Pallium (the liturgical woolen band that signifies metropolitan authority) to Archbishop Roz. Later that year, in December 1609, papal briefs settled the jurisdictional boundary with Cochin, formally assigning Kodungallur to the archbishop of Angamaly. The metropolitan title that the December 1599 act had erased was, within a decade, recovered. The Cochin boundary that the move had inflamed was, on paper, resolved. Roz had, working within the system that had imposed the suppression, undone a portion of the suppression. 13

The Predicament of the Thomas Christians

The Thomas Christian community, meanwhile, read the ledger differently. Angamaly's dignity had been restored, but the seat was no longer at Angamaly. The Cochin boundary had been settled, but the settlement confirmed that their metropolitan lived inside a Portuguese garrison town. The apostolic argument that justified the move was Roz's argument, built from his reading of their own traditions, but in practice it had produced an archbishop more deeply embedded in the Padroado apparatus than before. Three instruments of repair had been deployed across a decade. The community was not unaware of what each had achieved. They were also not unaware of what each had cost. 14

Books and the Liturgy

The Translation Of The Books

The repair of the Serra's dignity was conducted in the open, before Rome and the Crown. The repair of its worship was conducted at home, in the books and liturgy, and went almost unseen. Diamper had ordered the Eastern liturgical books corrected or burned. The order had been executed enthusiastically in the post-synodal enforcement tour, but it had also created an immediate practical problem for Roz: an Eastern church needs Eastern books to celebrate its rites, and when the books are destroyed, the rite cannot continue. To purge what the synod called the "Nestorian" content while preserving the rite as a usable liturgy was a translation problem of extraordinary delicacy, and Roz spent much of his episcopate working on it.

He had advantages no other Latin in India possessed. He had been teaching Syriac at Vaipicota since 1586. He could read the East Syrian liturgical books in their original language. He set himself to producing revised Syriac texts that retained the East Syrian rite in form, structure, and language while modifying the specific phrases that the Diamper decrees had identified as objectionable. István Perczel provides essential modern nuance to this archival era, proposing through recent manuscript digitization in Kerala that post Diamper period latinisation did not carry out an absolute, wholesale destruction of the pre-colonial Syriac library. Instead, under Roz's oversight, a highly sophisticated project of copyist transmission and recension took place, which intentionally preserved ancient Eastern text structures while cross-pollinating them to build a massive, complex body of new Catholic Syriac literature.

The Thomas Christians however did not, in their own memory, register this work as service. They registered the destruction of the older books, which had been visible and public, and the imposition of new books, which felt foreign whether they were Latinised or not. The labour that would look, to later scholarship, as 'preservation' was experienced at the time as one more imposition by an archbishop they were learning to distrust.15

The Hybrid Rite

The liturgy, in its own quiet way, was one of the most consequential legacies of Roz's episcopate. Diamper's stated plan was a programme of structural replacement: the existing missals were judged fit to be burned. Menezes was working towards the implementation of the Roman Missal in Syriac: the language would be preserved, but the rite replaced. In the meantime, with no such missals yet to hand, Menezes confined himself to the correction of the existing Syriac text and modified what he considered as errors, without making any substantial structural change to the framework of the Qurbana. The corrected texts he ordered into use were provisional, awaiting replacements from Rome. They never came in his lifetime.

Across his episcopate Roz kept the East Syrian structure standing. He added the sign of the cross (from left to right instead of right to left in Chaldean practice) at the beginning of the Mass in imitation of the Latin rite, and in the Diocesan Statutes of 1606 standardised a Taksa which established a precedent for the incorporation of elements from two different liturgical traditions. This liturgy, inherited by the Catholic portion of the Serra after the divisions of the seventeenth century, survived over the next three centuries, accreting Latin insertions recension after recension until it became a full-blown hybrid. Later, when the Catholic Church set out to reverse the "latinisation", the hybrid Mass was among the first things to be undone. 16

The Special Liturgical Commission for the Syro-Malabar Church, established on 10 March 1954 under the restoration programme of Pope Pius XII, was charged with producing "a reformed edition purged from all those hybridisms which have changed the Syro-Oriental rite of this Church from the time of the Synod of Diamper," while acknowledging in the same breath that the Malabars "have well preserved some ancient ceremonies that the Chaldeans would have lost." Therein lies the ironical twist: because Roz preserved its Eastern bones, the Syro-Malabar Catholics inherited a body they have had to painstakingly disentangle. 17

The bitter liturgical reform controversy in the Syro-Malabar Church that would later cast Roz either as the great archbishop who engineered a beautiful new Syro-Malabar rite from Latin and Chaldean traditions, or as the originator of malum within a pristine Chaldean liturgy, lay several centuries in the future. In his own time, the man is better understood as working to conserve what the system around him was bent on erasing than as a willing agent of erasure, even if the structure he conserved became the seat of the very impurity the Catholic Church would later labour to dissolve. That is the measure of his work as a whole: in synod, charter, petition, and liturgy alike, he laboured to hold the Serra together from within an office whose very logic was pulling it apart. And even as he laboured, the ground beneath the entire Jesuit enterprise in India was beginning to shift. 18

The Ground Shifts

To understand what happened next in the Serra, the chapter must look briefly outside it.

The Society of Jesus was a peculiar order inside the Padroado: its professed members took a fourth vow of obedience to the Pope concerning missions, and its General sat in Rome, so that its centre of gravity was Roman, not Iberian. This had always sat awkwardly with the Padroado, but in these decades the awkwardness sharpened, because the Padroado itself was caught in a larger strain. Since 1580 the crowns of Spain and Portugal had been held by one Habsburg sovereign, and under Philip III that union still held; but it was a union of crowns, not of kingdoms. Portugal kept its own laws, its own administration, and its own overseas apparatus, and the Padroado remained Portuguese machinery, run from Lisbon and guarded as a Portuguese prerogative even though the man who wore the crown was a Spaniard. A fault line was opening between the Habsburg sovereign and the Portuguese state apparatus that served him, a fault line that would, in 1640, rupture outright when Portugal threw off Habsburg rule and proclaimed the Duke of Braganza as King John IV. That rupture lay forty years ahead. But its logic was already at work in the Serra, where the resentments of a Portuguese establishment, against a Roman order, against a Spanish Monarch, against anything that answered past Lisbon, were beginning to bite. 19

The accommodation strategy by which the Society had built its Asian missions, such as those of Ricci in China, Nobili in Madurai, and Roz himself in his earlier Vaipicota years, was producing exactly the doctrinal heterogeneity the Padroado was constituted to eliminate. A Roman religious order working through accommodation, inside an Iberian system working through standardisation, had become a structural anomaly the Portuguese state machinery was increasingly unwilling to absorb. 20

The Shift in Appointments

The Padroado began, quietly and then openly, to favour Portuguese Mendicant and Augustinian-Hieronymite candidates over Jesuits for the senior diocesan bishoprics of the Estado da Índia. The Bishop of Cochin had been a Franciscan, Andrew of St Mary, since 1588. The Augustinian Aleixo de Menezes had held Goa since 1595. The Hieronymite Cristóvão de Sá e Lisboa replaced Menezes at Goa in 1610 and would govern the metropolitan see of the Iberian East until his death in 1622. However, in the mission fields proper, the Fishery Coast, the Madurai mission, the Serra of the Thomas Christians, both Rome and the Crown in Madrid kept the Society in possession against the Portuguese bishops who would have absorbed them. Roz's own appointment ran along the same seam. He had been approved by Rome and assented to by the Crown over his own reluctance, a Catalan rather than a Portuguese, a Jesuit of an order that looked to Rome, set over a see that Rome would soon lift clear of the Portuguese metropolitan at Goa. 21

Operational Pressure on the Fishery Coast

Bishop of Cochin whose encroachment dispute with Roz over Kodungallur had already poisoned relations between the two sees, did not confine his offensive to the archdiocese. In 1608, he succeeded in forcing the Jesuits to surrender their long-established mission stations on the Fishery Coast, the Parava parishes that Francis Xavier had founded sixty years earlier. The Society's Tamil-speaking missionaries were withdrawn; their churches were assigned to clergy under direct episcopal control. The Crown intervened in 1614 to reverse the surrender, but the practical restoration did not occur until 1621. For thirteen years, the most successful missionary field the Society had ever held in Asia had been operationally lost to the Mendicant hierarchy backed by the Padroado. 22

The Doctrinal Assault on Accommodation

The shift showed third, and most strikingly, in doctrine. The Mendicant orders, having recovered institutional advantage, now turned the accommodation method itself into a formal accusation. In February 1619, Cristóvão de Sá convened a theological consultation at Goa to denounce Roberto de Nobili's missionary practice in Madurai, where Nobili had adopted Brahminical dress, language, dietary observance, and social custom in order to make Christian teaching accessible to high-caste Hindus. The consultation marshalled Mendicant theologians, who argued that Nobili's accommodation amounted to crypto-paganism, that the converts produced were Christians in name only, and that the method was a doctrinal compromise the Iberian Crowns could not licitly tolerate. Nobili was put on the defensive. He was required to write his Narratio fundamentorum, a learned treatise defending accommodation against the consultation's charges, and to seek vindication from Rome rather than from the Padroado. Rome, eventually, sided with the Society. But the institutional damage of having had to ask was permanent. The strategy that had built the Society's Asian missions had now been formally arraigned by the Padroado's senior ecclesiastic in Asia as a doctrinal error. 23

The Mendicants pressing for the bishoprics, the Mendicants taking the parishes, the Mendicants charging accommodation as heresy, were not three separate phenomena. They were the working components of a slow, uneven, and contested shift, in which the Padroado was becoming increasingly willing to operate without the Society. The Society had not yet been destroyed, but it could read the trajectory.

The Narrowing Path

For Roz, sitting at Kodungallur at the head of a see that had been built by Jesuit accommodation and would have to be governed against that accommodation if it was to be governed at all, the shift was visible in his daily correspondence. His letters of these years to the Jesuit General read as the letters of a man who knew his Society's position was eroding everywhere around him. The Goa consultation of 1619 on Nobili's accommodation method drew Malabar's leadership into the same Iberian net: the sources diverge on exactly who travelled (Neill has Roz himself going to Goa in company with Nobili to defend the method, while Tisserant has the Archdeacon accompanying Nobili there to answer a charge of heresy before the Inquisition), but on either account the crisis pulled the Serra's governance toward Goa precisely as the Padroado was turning against the Society. 23

Roz's predicament now becomes legible. He failed not because he misunderstood the Serra, but because he understood it too well to govern it under the system imposed on him. The man who had once been the Society's foremost expositor of accommodation in Malabar was now its archbishop, in an office that the system would not allow him to inhabit accommodatively. The next section is what happened to the Archdeacon when the Archbishop ran out of room.

The Break With The Archdeacon

Through the first decade of his episcopate, Roz held the Archdeacon George of the Cross at a careful distance. The two men had known each other since 1584. Roz had recommended George as coadjutor bishop in 1594. The Archdeacon's testament-named administrative authority over the diocese had been confirmed by Menezes in 1597 and again at Diamper. But Roz did not entrust George with the office's actual functions. The Archdeacon was kept formally in place but operationally bypassed. George felt himself, in the formula Tisserant uses, shelved.

The Tridentine Conception

The reason was not personal animus. Roz had brought to his archbishopric the post-Tridentine conception of episcopal office, in which all authority concentrated in the bishop's hands and all subordinates derived their power from his commission. The Archdeacon's traditional authority, exercised by inheritance, recognised by the community, and independent of the archbishop's grant, could not be reconciled with this conception without compromising it. So Roz did not reconcile. He governed alone, or with Jesuit consultors, while George kept the title without the work. 24

The arrangement passed through a first major crisis in 1609–10. In 1609, a Chaldean bishop sent by the patriarch of Babylon (often referred to in contemporary reports as an "Armenian" bishop) arrived in the Serra and was detained by the Portuguese at Alangad. Archdeacon George assisted the bishop in escaping from custody. In response, Roz excommunicated the Archdeacon. Secure in the protection of local rulers and the support of the Bishop of Cochin and several Franciscans who took his side, George ignored the censure. To bypass him, Roz appointed an old cattanar from Kaduthuruthy as a new Archdeacon, in direct violation of the norms of royal patronage. For several years, the Serra was fractured by parallel structures of authority. However, in a sudden volte-face in 1615, the Archdeacon submitted himself to Roz, had his excommunication absolved, and even wrote to the Jesuit General Muzio Vitelleschi a letter of warm commendation of the Society's work. It seemed peace had been secured. The second and decisive break came three years after that. 25

The Rupture of 1618

The decisive break came in 1618–19, around the Goa consultation on Nobili's accommodation method. As discussed above, the sources differ on who travelled to Goa (Roz to champion Nobili, in Neill's account, or the Archdeacon to answer the Inquisition, in Tisserant's), but they agree on what happened next and on its consequence. With the see's governance disrupted by the Goa proceedings, Roz appointed the Superior of the Vaipicota Seminary, a European Jesuit, as Vicar General to govern the diocese. To the Archdeacon, this administrative bypass was the operational completion of seventeen years of being shelved. To replace a Syrian by a European in the office that had defined indigenous authority for centuries was, structurally, the abolition of the Archdeacon's office in everything but name. The appointment had been published without his knowledge, and George refused to recognise it. 26

The dispute escalated quickly. Defiant of the new Vicar General's authority, George withdrew all obedience, initiating a second rupture that split the diocese once again, as the Archdeacon was backed by a substantial portion of the local clergy and laity. The dispute dragged on for nearly six years, during which the administration of the Serra remained deeply divided, and even the coadjutor and successor-designate, Stephen de Brito, along with senior Jesuits, pleaded with the Archbishop to adopt a milder approach toward the native leader. Roz, however, remained unyielding.

In 1622, Roz wrote to his Jesuit superiors in Rome that he had known the Archdeacon for thirty-nine years, since his first arrival in India, and that through all those years George had remained unaltered. Kindness, he wrote, had had no effect on him. The Archdeacon was incorrigible. 27

The letter is the chapter's centrepiece. The man who in 1594 had named George as the most suitable candidate for the Chaldean episcopacy was, twenty-eight years later, telling Rome that George could not be governed. Both judgements were honestly held. The change between them was not the Archdeacon. It was the office that Roz had agreed, under obedience, to take in 1601. 28

The Accommodationist Dissent

Roz was not alone in the Serra during these years. The next generation of Jesuits was already active, and they were already, in private correspondence, dissenting from his approach.

J.M. Campori, the Jesuit who had written to the Jesuit General as early as January 1604 in support of Roz's effort to suppress Diamper, was by the 1620s pleading with Roz to soften his treatment of the Archdeacon. Giacomo Fenicio, the Italian Jesuit whose ethnographic work on Hindu religion would become foundational for European indological studies, took the same position. So did Stephen de Britto, who had been appointed Roz's coadjutor and successor in 1620. All of them argued in essentially the same terms: that the Archdeacon represented the only structure of authority the community recognised as its own, that the archbishop's office was too thinly rooted in the Serra to govern without the Archdeacon's support, and that the policy of confrontation was producing a community that obeyed less every year. 29

The Refusal to Yield

Roz did not relent. His letters of the 1620s repeat the position he had taken in 1622. The Archdeacon was incorrigible. The office of bishop, properly understood, did not negotiate with subordinate dignitaries who refused to accept their subordination. The post-Tridentine theology of episcopal authority, on which Roz had been trained at the Roman College, did not admit the dual-governance structure of the East Syrian jathikku karthavyan tradition as a legitimate arrangement. To accept it would have been, in Roz's own theological framework, to accept a theology of episcopate that did not exist.

There is a particular pathos in the position the second-generation Jesuits found themselves occupying. The accommodation strategy they were now urging was the strategy Roz himself had championed before 1597. The man who had once written to Rome that force would lose the Serra was now, two decades later, the man being urged by his own Society to soften his force. The accommodation strategy had not abandoned Roz. Roz had become, by office and by the obligations of office, unable to inhabit it. 30

The Parallel Church

While Roz governed from Kodungallur and the Archdeacon defied him from Alangad, something quieter was happening in the village churches of the Serra. The community was rebuilding, beneath the level of episcopal disputation, the structures by which it would carry its identity forward when neither Roz nor George was alive to argue.

The Persistence of the Yogams

The palli yogams, the parish assemblies of laymen and married priests who had governed local church affairs in the Serra since long before the Portuguese arrived, continued to meet. They had not been authorised by Diamper, and the post-Diamper reform programme had not specifically dissolved them, but they were not part of the Tridentine parochial structure that Roz's decrees envisaged. They simply persisted, unobtrusively, where the Latin archbishop's enforcement could not reach. They handled disputes, allocated lands, supervised the conduct of the cattanars, and preserved, in the practical knowledge of village life, the working memory of how a Thomas Christian community organised itself.

Beside them, the older liturgical practices that the Diamper decrees had outlawed continued in oral memory. The lenten observances kept by the older generations did not include fish; the married priests continued to be respected; the ancient panchangam of liturgical festivals was maintained alongside the Roman calendar that Diamper had imposed. The oral preservation of these practices through the Roz years and beyond would matter more than any documentary survival. When the Coonan Cross Oath came in 1653, the oath would not need to invent a community to defend. The community had been continuously reproducing itself, in the parish assemblies and in the older priests' memory, throughout the Latin years.

The Ready Sword

The chaverpada was kept available. It was not deployed against Roz. The community's reading of the situation was that armed resistance against an archbishop who was, after all, in formal communion with Rome and recognised by the local rajas as legitimate would be premature and possibly catastrophic. But the corporate capacity to deploy it remained. It would matter when Mar Atallah's arrest in 1652 finally collapsed the community's last reserves of patience. The capacity had not been kept idle. It had been kept ready. 31

The Last Act

By 1622, Roz was sixty-three and ill. In Rome that same year, Pope Gregory XV established the Sacred Congregation for the Propagation of the Faith, an instrument that would in time allow the Society's heirs to bypass the Padroado entirely by appealing directly to a Roman missionary congregation rather than to Lisbon or Madrid. The structural answer to Padroado was being laid down even as Roz lay dying but its operational consequences would not arrive in time for Thomas Christians. 32

The Succession Crisis

The papal bulls authorising Stephen de Britto to succeed him as coadjutor with right of succession had been lost in shipwreck and had not been replaced. The Society of Jesus and the Padroado authorities both feared that if Roz died before the bulls arrived, the see would be vacant in a way that would allow a Chaldean claimant to insert himself into the diocese and recover the community's allegiance to the patriarchate of Babylon. To prevent this, Roz needed to appoint a diocesan administrator whose authority could carry the see through the interregnum until Britto could take possession.

To Roz, the choice of administrator was fraught with decades of history. Although the Archdeacon had submitted in 1615 and had his excommunication (resulting from the 1609 escape incident) absolved, the reconciliation had been short-lived. The appointment of the Vaipicota Rector as vicar general in 1618 had reopened the rupture, and the years 1618 to 1622 had been the most adversarial period of their relationship. 33

But by 1623 Roz had run out of alternatives. The Archdeacon was the only figure in the Serra whose authority the community would recognise during a vacancy, and Britto's arrival could not be guaranteed in time. At the very last moment, Roz relented. He confirmed George of the Cross as administrator of the diocese for the period after his death, with Toscano as co-administrator. The man he had spent twenty-three years calling incorrigible was named, in the end, as the archbishop's interim successor.

The Death of Roz

Roz died at Kodungallur on 18 February 1624. 34

The Archdeacon was, for the first time since 1597, in independent control of the church of the Serra.

The Archdeacon in Control

What he did with that control, in the months between Roz's death and Britto's eventual installation, is the most quietly remarkable episode of the entire post-Diamper period. He set himself, with what Neill calls "commendable zeal", to ending the schism that he himself had created during the years of his quarrel with Roz. He worked to remove the causes of dissension and dispute. When Britto was finally able to take possession of his see, the Archdeacon presented him with a Serra that was free from open schism and offered him an enthusiastic welcome. 35

The Roz years had, on the surface, produced an alienated community, an excommunicated Archdeacon, a metropolitan seat in a Portuguese fortress, and an archbishop dying at odds with the people he had loved before he had been required to govern them. Beneath the surface, in the quieter structures of palli yogams and oral memory and reconciled administration, the community had also been preparing the form of its eventual resistance. The man whom Roz called incorrigible was now governing the Serra by the same accommodationist logic that the second-generation Jesuits had pleaded with Roz to adopt, and was governing it well. The puzzle that Roz had embodied, the man who knew the Serra better than any Latin who came before him and could not save it from what he was administering, closed itself in the same paradox in which it had opened.

The next chapter, on the years between 1624 and 1653, will follow the Serra under Britto, the deepening conflict under Garcia, and the eventual collapse of patience at the Coonan Cross. This chapter ends with Roz in his grave at Kodungallur and the Archdeacon, at last, in possession of his own community.

The community was waiting.

On the structural irony that the second-generation Jesuit accommodation programme was the position Roz himself had championed before 1597, and the limits placed on Roz's capacity to inhabit it by his post-Tridentine episcopal office: Neill I, Ch. 9 n. 82 and 313 (the 1593/1597 letters set beside the 1622 "incorrigible" letter); Tisserant, 71, 74–76. On Alessandro Valignano's foundational role in pioneering the East Asian accommodation strategy, and its subsequent development and testing in Malabar by Roz, see the definitive study by Antony Mecherry, S.J., Testing Ground for Jesuit Accommodation in Early Modern India: Francisco Ros SJ in Malabar (16th–17th Centuries) (Rome: IHSI, 2019).

On the palli yogams as the working parish structure of the Thomas Christians during and after the Roz years, the oral preservation of older liturgical and dietary practices, and the maintained but undeployed chaverpada: Kollaparambil, The Archdeacon of All India, 150–72. On the eventual significance of these preserved structures at the Coonan Cross Oath: Neill I, 319–28.

On the establishment of the Sacred Congregation for the Propagation of the Faith (Sacra Congregatio de Propaganda Fide) by Pope Gregory XV, Inscrutabili Divinae Providentiae, 22 June 1622, and its long-term significance as Rome's structural mechanism for missionary work outside the Iberian Padroado: Neill I, 333; Giovanni Pizzorusso, "Propaganda Fide e le missioni cattoliche tra antico regime e secolo XIX," in Storia del cristianesimo, ed. Emanuela Prinzivalli (Roma: Carocci, 2015); Hsia, The World of Catholic Renewal, 1540–1770, 195–220. The operational consequences for the Malabar church would not arrive until the Carmelite missions of 1657 and after.

On the 1615 reconciliation between Roz and the Archdeacon, and the absolution of the Archdeacon's 1609 excommunication: Kollaparambil, The Archdeacon of All India, 144; Monisha George, "Jathikku Karthavian", IJRCS, 4(1) (Jan 2020), 156. On the deterioration of the relationship between 1618 and 1622 following the appointment of the Vaipicota Rector as vicar general: Neill I, 312–13.

On the lost bulls authorising Stephen de Britto's succession, Roz's last-minute confirmation of the Archdeacon as diocesan administrator, and Roz's death at Kodungallur on 18 February 1624: Neill I, 313; Tisserant, 75–76.

On the Archdeacon's administration of the see between Roz's death (February 1624) and Britto's arrival, his work to end the schism he himself had created during the years of conflict with Roz, and his welcome of Britto: Neill I, 313; Tisserant, 75.

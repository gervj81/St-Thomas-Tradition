PART II: FROM ENCOUNTER TO FRAGMENTATION
Portuguese Arrival to the Modern Denominations, 1490–1930

A Note on Method: Reading Against the Grain
Part I of this study operated in conditions of genuine documentary scarcity. For the first fifteen centuries of the Thomas Christian community, the primary evidence is sparse, scattered, and often temporally remote from the events it purports to describe. The three-tier framework — Legend, Plausible, Historical — was a tool calibrated for that specific problem: how to reason honestly when the evidentiary ground is thin and the gap between tradition and attestation is measured in centuries.
Part II faces an almost opposite problem, and it requires a different method.
From 1498 onward, the documentary record expands dramatically. We have Portuguese royal dispatches and ecclesiastical correspondence. We have the published proceedings of the Synod of Diamper, running to hundreds of canons. We have Jesuit annual letters, Franciscan mission reports, the chronicles of António de Gouvea and Diogo do Couto, the meticulous registers of the Goa Inquisition, and the surveys commissioned by successive Archbishops of Goa. We have, in the later period, British colonial gazetteer entries, census reports, revenue surveys, missionary society archives, and the voluminous judgments of the Travancore and Cochin courts adjudicating property disputes between competing ecclesiastical factions. By the nineteenth century, the Thomas Christians are among the better-documented religious communities in the Indian subcontinent.
And yet — and this is the methodological crux — more documentation does not mean better access to the community itself. It means better access to how others described, classified, managed, and attempted to transform the community. The vast majority of surviving documents from 1498 to approximately 1750 were produced by Portuguese ecclesiastical or colonial administrators. They were written by men whose institutional purpose was either to reform what they found or to justify what they had already done. They are, in the precise sense of the term, interested documents: they were produced to serve specific outcomes, and their silences, distortions, and framings are as historically significant as their explicit contents.
The Thomas Christian community produced its own documentary tradition across this period — liturgical manuscripts, Syriac correspondence, the extraordinary body of Malayalam-Syriac poetry known as the Margam Kali Pattu, the Ramban Pattu, and related compositions, community legal petitions, and the copper plate traditions that continued to be cited long after the original plates were lost. But this counter-archive was systematically targeted. The Synod of Diamper in 1599 ordered the burning of Syriac books on a scale that amounts to a deliberate destruction of the community's written memory. What survived did so accidentally — in Vatican manuscript collections carried to Rome by exiled bishops, in the memories encoded in liturgical practice, in the oral traditions that communities maintain precisely because they cannot trust writing to survive hostile authority.
Part II therefore works with a different analytical framework, organised around four questions that must be asked of every document, Portuguese or indigenous, missionary or community-produced, colonial or traditional:

I. Who Produced This Document, and Why?
The question of authorship is inseparable from the question of purpose. António de Gouvea's Jornada do Arcebispo (1606) is the single most detailed Portuguese account of Archbishop Menezes's campaign to bring the Thomas Christians to Roman obedience, including the Synod of Diamper. It is also a work of deliberate ecclesiastical advocacy, written to justify to Rome and Lisbon what Menezes had done. Gouvea was Menezes's personal chronicler and defender. His account is indispensable — it records events and names and decrees we would not otherwise know — and it is also, at every significant juncture, a document constructed to make Menezes look like a reforming hero and the Thomas Christians look like wayward children gratefully restored to the true church. Reading it requires holding both things simultaneously.
The same scrutiny applies to community-produced tradition. The Ramban Pattu, a Malayalam-Syriac poem celebrating the Kanai Thomman migration, exists in manuscripts no earlier than the 16th or 17th century. It is not a contemporary chronicle of a 4th-century event. It is a community's poetic memory, shaped by the pressures of the moment of its composition — which is to say, composed precisely when the community most needed to assert its antiquity, its independent origins, and its identity against Portuguese attempts at erasure. That does not make it false. It makes it a document that encodes both historical memory and contemporary anxiety simultaneously, and both layers are historically meaningful.

II. What Does This Document Need to Not Say?
Silences are evidence. They require as much attention as explicit statements, often more.
Gouvea's chronicle describes the community's Syriac liturgical books in considerable detail — but only in the act of condemning them. He does not describe what the community believed about those books, what the texts meant to them, how they were used in worship across generations, or what was lost when they were burned. The burning is recorded as an administrative act. The community's experience of it is not recorded at all. That silence is the silence of a document written from inside one institution about another institution it is in the process of destroying.
British colonial census reports from the 19th century are meticulous about the numbers of Thomas Christians, their geographic distribution, their caste classifications, and their economic activities. They are systematically silent about the community's own understanding of its history, the meaning of its liturgical divisions, the significance of its legal disputes over apostolic succession, or the theological content of its internal controversies. The colonial gaze sees populations, not persons; categories, not self-understanding. These documents tell us a great deal about how the community looked to an outsider trained in administrative classification. They tell us almost nothing about how it felt from inside.
Reading against the grain means asking, of every document, what it cannot afford to acknowledge, and why.

III. What Survives by Accident, and What Does That Survival Tell Us?
The distribution of surviving evidence is not random. It reflects the power relations of the periods that produced it. This has a specific and demonstrable consequence for Thomas Christian history: the community's most important medieval documents survive in European archives, not Indian ones.
MS Vatican Syriac 22 — the Pauline Lectionary copied by the fourteen-year-old deacon Zakarya bar Joseph at Kodungallur in 1301, the earliest surviving manuscript produced in the Indian Thomas Christian community — is in Rome. It survived because it was carried there by Mar Joseph Sulaqa when he was deported by the Portuguese Inquisition to defend his orthodoxy, and because the Vatican Library preserved what Portuguese authorities in India, had they found it, would likely have burned. The accident of deportation saved the document. The institutional power that caused the deportation also, inadvertently, preserved the evidence of what it was attempting to suppress.
The Tharisapalli Copper Plates (849 CE), the foundational documentary evidence of the community's legal status and royal recognition, are gone — last reliably recorded in 1611, when Diogo do Couto saw them before they vanished into Portuguese administrative custody. Gouvea himself records their loss, attributing it to "carelessness." What we have instead is a 17th-century Portuguese translation of a palm-leaf copy, archived at the British Library. This is the document that underpins the community's oldest verified legal claims. It survives at two removes from the original, in a language different from both the original and its first copy, preserved by the colonial power that had displaced the community whose rights it records.
The community's own liturgical poetry — the Margam Kali Pattu, the Ramban Pattu, the Veeramanikyam — survived primarily in manuscript copies made after Diamper, often in contexts of deliberate concealment. Their survival is evidence of the community's determination to preserve memory against institutional destruction, which is itself a historical fact of the first importance.

IV. What Do the Secondary Traditions Encode, and at What Layer?
Part I was cautious about the community's founding traditions — the Thomas mission, the Brahmin conversions, the seven churches — because those traditions cannot be independently corroborated and the gap between the claimed events and the earliest written record spans centuries. That caution remains appropriate. But Part II requires a more refined approach to tradition, because the traditions that matter most in this period are not primarily about the apostolic founding. They are about communal identity under pressure: who we are, where we came from, what distinguishes us from those who are trying to change us.
The Brahmin conversion narrative — the claim that Thomas baptized thirty-two Namboodiri Brahmin families, and that the Thomas Christian community descends from these high-caste converts — functions very differently in 1599 than it does in 52 CE. By 1599, it is a live political claim. It is the community's assertion that they predate the Portuguese, that they are not pagans recently evangelized, that they belong to the highest stratum of Kerala's social order, and that no foreign archbishop has the right to tell them how to pray. The tradition encodes an argument. Understanding that argument — its social logic, its historical plausibility, its strategic deployment — is as important as evaluating its literal accuracy.
Similarly, the Knai Thomman tradition — the 4th-century migration of Christian families from Mesopotamia under a merchant named Thomas — is not merely a founding story. It is the Knanaya community's explanation of its own persistent separateness: its strict endogamy, its distinct genetic profile, its refusal to merge with the broader Thomas Christian population across fifteen centuries of shared geography. In the period of Portuguese pressure, Knanaya endogamy becomes both more embattled and more entrenched. The tradition is doing the work that identity traditions always do under siege: it is explaining why the boundaries must hold.
The Pakalomattam family's claim to hereditary authority over the Archdeaconate — the claim to descend from one of the original Brahmin converts, and therefore to hold the community's governance by ancient right — is the most politically consequential of all these secondary traditions in this period. It is the tradition that the Portuguese most urgently needed to displace, and the one the community most urgently needed to defend. Its historical analysis therefore belongs at the centre of Part II, not at its margins.

What This Method Produces
These four questions do not dissolve the evidentiary problem. They cannot recover what was burned at Diamper, or restore the original copper plates, or give us the Thomas Christians' own voice in the proceedings of the Synod. What they can do is allow us to read the surviving record with precision about its limitations — to treat the bias of our sources not as an obstacle to historical understanding but as evidence in its own right.
A Portuguese chronicle that insists the Thomas Christians were ignorant of true Christianity tells us something about Portuguese ecclesiastical politics. It also tells us, by implication, something about what the community's Christianity actually looked like when viewed through a Latin lens — which is not the same thing as what it actually was, but is itself a historically significant fact. A colonial census that classifies the community by caste and occupation without reference to theological self-understanding tells us something about how colonial governance classified religious communities — and, by contrast, illuminates what the community's own self-understanding must have contained that the census could not capture.
Part II will work throughout with this doubled attention: reading documents for what they explicitly record, and simultaneously reading them for what their framings, silences, and distortions reveal about the forces shaping both the community and the record of the community. Where the community's own voice survives — in liturgical tradition, in legal petition, in the Margam Kali and Ramban Pattu poetry, in the act of the Coonan Cross Oath itself — it will be treated as primary evidence, weighted by what we know about the conditions of its production.
The secondary traditions analysed in detail in the chapters that follow — the Brahmin conversions, the Knai Thomman migration, the Pakalomattam Archdeaconate — are not treated as historical claims requiring simple verification or refutation. They are treated as evidence of how a community understands itself across time, evidence that carries its own historical weight independent of whether the events they describe happened exactly as described. Human communities are constituted, in part, by the stories they tell about themselves. Those stories are historical facts, regardless of their literal accuracy. The historian's task is not to replace tradition with fact, but to understand what work tradition is doing — and why, at specific historical moments, certain stories become more urgently necessary than others.
That is the method. Now we turn to the arrival of the Portuguese, and the beginning of the long disruption.

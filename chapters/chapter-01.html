1.1 THE WORLD IN MOTION

The Document That Changes Everything

Sometime between 40 and 70 CE — the same decades in which the apostles were scattering from Jerusalem, in which Paul was writing his letters, in which the events that would eventually become the New Testament were still within living memory — an anonymous Greek-speaking merchant sat down and wrote a handbook.

It was not a work of literature. Its prose is blunt, its vocabulary functional, its author invisible behind the practical accumulation of facts. He was, almost certainly, a trader operating out of Roman Egypt, writing for other traders operating out of Roman Egypt. What he produced was a navigation manual for the Indian Ocean: a working guide to the ports, their rulers, their commercial profiles, their seasonal schedules, and the goods that moved through them. He called it the Periplus of the Erythraean Sea — the Circuit of the Red Sea — though the sea it describes extends from East Africa to the Bay of Bengal and the approaches to China.1

The Periplus is the single most important document for understanding the commercial world into which any apostle would have travelled. Not because it tells us about apostles — it does not mention them — but because of what it assumes. It assumes that its readers already know where India is. It assumes they know the names of the Indian kings, the going prices at the major ports, the risks of the tidal bar at Barygaza, and the timing of the monsoon winds. It was written to update professionals on current conditions, not to announce discoveries. The world it describes was not being explored. It was being managed.

That single fact demolishes the most persistent misconception about early India and the wider world: the idea that India was remote, inaccessible, beyond the horizon of a 1st-century traveller. For the people who lived at the centre of the trade — for the Greek merchants of Alexandria, the Parthian intermediaries of Mesopotamia, the Aramaic-speaking caravan traders of Palmyra, the Arab sailors of the Persian Gulf — India was the destination. And the Periplus was their guidebook to getting there.

We will return to it throughout this section, not as a scholarly citation but as what it actually was: the voice of the commercial world, speaking in real time.

The 1st century CE was not a world of isolated civilisations facing each other across impassable distances. It was a world of overlapping empires, each with its own strategic logic, bound to the others by trade, diplomacy, and the inescapable necessity of managing common borders.

Three powers dominated the western end of the connected world. Understanding their relationship — what they controlled, what they wanted from each other, and crucially, what ran between them — is the foundation of everything that follows.

The Roman Empire at its 1st-century height stretched from Britannia in the northwest to Egypt in the southeast. Its military and administrative psychology was overwhelmingly western and northern: the Rhine-Danube frontier where Germanic tribes pressed, the Parthian border where a civilisational rival pressed from the other side. For the senatorial class in Rome, India was a rhetorical abstraction — a source of luxury goods that arrived, at enormous expense, through a chain of middlemen. Pliny could rage about the gold draining eastward without having met an Indian. Horace could write of merchants sailing to "remote India" without needing to locate it on a map.

But the Roman Empire possessed a split geographic personality. Its western half looked northwest; its eastern half looked southeast. For every senator in Rome who had never seen a Malabar merchant, there was a Greek trader in Alexandria who navigated to Malabar annually. For every Latin poet treating India as a metaphor, there was a merchant in the Egyptian Red Sea port of Berenike who knew the names of the Chera kings, the price of Malabar pepper by weight, and the precise moment in late spring when the southwest monsoon would carry his ship across the Arabian Sea in forty days.2

The Tabula Peutingeriana, Rome's great itinerary map, marks 'Muziris' on the Malabar coast—and marks beside it a Templum Augusti, a Roman trading post. Farther north, flourishing emporia like Barygaza and Barbaricum anchored the sprawling maritime network that funneled the wealth of the East into the Mediterranean. Rome did not mark trading posts on maps for places its merchants had never been.4

The Parthian Empire (247 BCE – 224 CE) stood between Rome and India and controlled everything in between: the overland corridor from the Euphrates through Mesopotamia to the borders of the Indus valley. This is the empire that shaped the world most directly relevant to the Thomas tradition. Its capital was Ctesiphon, near modern Baghdad. Its commercial heartland was the city-complex of Seleucia-Ctesiphon, the greatest emporium of the overland world, where goods from India, Central Asia, and China arrived from the east and were redistributed westward toward Roman Syria.

The Parthians were Iranian dynasts who had absorbed the Hellenistic culture of the Seleucid Empire they replaced. Their court used Greek for diplomacy. Their coins bore Greek legends on one face and Aramaic on the other. They were, culturally, a hinge civilisation — facing west toward the Greco-Roman Mediterranean and east toward India simultaneously. A merchant, missionary, or diplomat moving through the Parthian world found not a hostile barrier but a sophisticated, multilingual commercial civilisation that had been facilitating east-west exchange for centuries. The city of Edessa, capital of the small buffer kingdom of Osroene on the Roman-Parthian border, sat at the precise intersection of these worlds: Aramaic-speaking, deeply connected to both Rome and Parthia, home to an ancient Jewish population, and eventually one of the first cities outside Judaea to embrace Christianity. The people of Edessa had commercial contacts with India. When they said Thomas went there, they were making a claim in a context where the India route was, for their merchant community, routine.5

The Indian Kingdoms at the eastern end of these routes were not a single entity. India in the 1st century was a subcontinent of distinct regional kingdoms with different commercial profiles, different political structures, and different orientations toward the wider world. Understanding this is essential — because the Thomas tradition would eventually associate an apostle with multiple regions of India, and each of those associations rests on a different set of geographic and commercial realities.

The Three Arteries: How Goods — and People — Moved

Before following the routes themselves, one fact must be grasped that the surviving sources, dominated by Roman writers, tend to obscure: the trade running to India in the 1st century ran along not one artery but three, of roughly equal commercial weight. The Romans documented their own system. They could not easily document Parthian or Arab commercial operations, since those ran through rival territory. The result is that later readers have systematically over-imagined the Greco-Roman axis and under-imagined the Persian-Arab axis that carried perhaps more of the actual trade volume. The Periplus is structurally biased toward the Greco-Roman axis, but even it cannot quite conceal the others.

This section traces how the arteries ran. The next describes the Indian worlds they reached.

The Greco-Roman Axis: Egypt to India via the Red Sea

The most extensively documented of the three arteries began in Alexandria. From the Nile port at Koptos, camel caravans crossed the Eastern Desert in approximately twelve days, passing through a series of Roman military stations (praesidia) that provided water, security, and imperial oversight, arriving at the Red Sea ports of Berenike or Myos Hormos. Here, the desert caravan met the ocean.6

From Berenike, vessels made first for the southern Arabian coast — the port of Qana (in modern Yemen), where frankincense was loaded and cargoes were transshipped between Red Sea vessels and those heading for the open Indian Ocean. Many voyages paused at Socotra, the island sitting at the mouth of the Gulf of Aden that served as a staging point for the open-ocean crossing.

Then the monsoon. With the southwest monsoon filling the sails from late May or June, a ship from Socotra or Qana drove eastward across the open Arabian Sea and reached the Malabar coast in approximately forty days. This was the scheduled, institutionalised, annual commercial migration that anchored the Indian Ocean trade system.7

It was a commute. And the physical evidence of that commute survived in the Egyptian desert. The port of Berenike, excavated from its sands in the 1990s, produced in stratified 1st-century layers 7.55 kilograms of black peppercorns stored in a single container. Teak wood from Indian forests. Coconuts. Indian-manufactured beads. And most remarkable of all: a Tamil Brahmi inscription scratched into pottery — a Tamil-language graffito at the Egyptian gateway to the Indian Ocean.8

That graffito alters our understanding of this trade. It is not a Roman account of India; it is an Indian presence in Egypt. A Tamil-speaking individual, almost certainly a merchant or sailor from the Tamil coast, was physically present at Berenike. The map ran in both directions.

A surviving Greek commercial contract — a papyrus now in Vienna, dated to the 2nd century CE — specifies the legal terms for the transport of goods from Muziris on the Malabar coast to Alexandria. This business instrument documents the regular movement of Indian goods westward along a route whose financial and legal framework was already mature enough to require standard contractual language.9

The Persian-Arab Axis: Mesopotamia to India via the Persian Gulf

Less visible in the sources the Western scholarly tradition inherited, but crucial for understanding the world of the Thomas tradition, was the axis running through Parthian Mesopotamia and the Persian Gulf.

This artery began in the great cities of the Parthian heartland: Seleucia-Ctesiphon near modern Baghdad, and the trading cities of the Tigris-Euphrates valley — Edessa, Nisibis, Babylon — where the goods of the overland Silk Road arrived from the east and were redistributed westward. At the southern end of this Mesopotamian corridor stood Charax Spasinu: the great emporium city at the head of the Gulf, the junction where the overland trade met the sea. From Charax, maritime routes ran along the Arabian coast and across to India without crossing any additional political frontier. The 1st-century geographer Isidore of Charax recorded the precise overland stages of the route from Antioch through the Parthian interior to Charax, confirming it as the recognised terminal node where the Silk Road's western arm met the Persian Gulf.10

Inscriptions from the great caravan city of Palmyra in the Syrian desert confirm what the literary sources only hint at. Palmyra stood between Rome and Parthia as a commercial power in its own right. Its inscriptions record voyages to "Scythia" — a term for the northwestern Indian region — and to the Persian Gulf ports. The Palmyrene merchants were not subjects of the Roman commercial system, but rather its rivals and sometimes its partners.11

The Persian-Arab axis carried the frankincense trade, the textile trade, and a substantial share of the spice trade. It also carried the people. The Aramaic-speaking communities — Jewish traders in Babylon and Seleucia-Ctesiphon, caravan merchants from Edessa and Palmyra, Arab sailors along the southern Arabian coast — formed the social infrastructure of this axis. When the later Syriac-speaking community in Edessa said their apostle went to India, the route he would have taken ran along this axis first: eastward through Parthian Mesopotamia to Charax, then by sea to India.

The third artery was the overland Silk Road. However, in this context, it was not primarily a route from China to the Mediterranean; it was a route from the Mediterranean and Parthian worlds to India.

The network of caravan routes running from Roman Syria through Parthian Mesopotamia had a critical branch that turned south. Through Bactria and down the Khyber Pass, the overland network reached Taxila, the great commercial and intellectual capital of northwestern India. From Taxila, the road continued south to the Indus delta and the ports of the northwestern Indian coast.

This branch of the overland network is, for the Thomas tradition, the most directly relevant route. The Acts of Thomas places the apostle in the kingdom of Gondophares — an Indo-Parthian king of northwestern India. Coins bearing Gondophares' name have been excavated across the Gandharan and Indus regions: he was a real historical figure, ruling in the 1st century CE, in precisely the territory that the overland branch of the Silk Road served.12 A traveller moving east through Edessa, through Parthian Mesopotamia, and down the Khyber Pass could reach the territory of Gondophares entirely overland.

Taxila itself was a city of remarkable cosmopolitanism. Its excavated layers yield inscriptions in Greek, Aramaic, and Kharosthi script on the same site; coins with Greek legends on one face; Buddhist monuments; and Parthian artistic motifs. An Aramaic-speaking traveller arriving in Taxila would have found a city whose intellectual and commercial culture had been processing exactly that kind of multilingual, multi-imperial encounter for generations.13

Three Indias: One Subcontinent, Three Commercial Personalities

The three arteries described above did not all run to the same India. India in the 1st century was a subcontinent of distinct regions, each producing different commodities, governed by different political formations, and embedded in different commercial networks. What follows describes the world the traveller found when he stepped off the ship or the caravan.

Northwestern India: The Indus Corridor

The northwest — running from the Khyber Pass through the Punjab to the Indus delta and the port of Barygaza (modern Bharuch, Gujarat) — was the Indian world most directly plugged into both the overland Silk Road and the Persian-Arab maritime axis. Under Indo-Parthian and then Kushana political control in the 1st century CE, this region was already deeply integrated into the commercial civilisation of the Parthian world. Its ruling class used Greek on coinage, patronised Buddhist art in Gandharan style, and maintained diplomatic relations with both Parthia and the nomadic powers of Central Asia.

Barygaza was the region's premier port. The Periplus describes the dangerous tidal bar at the mouth of its gulf, where royal pilots came out in small boats to guide large merchant ships in. It names the commodities flowing outward — cotton textiles, fine muslin, silk, aromatics — and inward — wine, copper, tin, lead. It names the king — Nahapana, a Saka ruler — and notes that he was a great patron of merchant activity.14 This was a port whose commercial profile was managed, not improvised: a regular node in a system that expected regular traffic.

Southwestern India: The Malabar Coast and the Pepper Capital

Round the Saurashtra peninsula and south down the Indian west coast lay the Malabar coast — the territory of the Chera kingdom, and in commercial terms, one of the most strategically important places on earth in the 1st century CE, because it grew pepper.

Piper nigrum, black pepper, grew almost exclusively on the Malabar coast in the ancient world. It was a food preservative, a medicine, and a marker of immense wealth. When the Visigoth king Alaric sacked Rome in 410 CE, his ransom demands included three thousand pounds of it. The people who grew it, transported it from the forested hill country to the coast, and negotiated its sale to foreign merchants were Tamil-speaking Chera subjects. They were the producers and gatekeepers of the commodity that made the entire western Indian Ocean system commercially viable.15

The Periplus calls Muziris the primum emporium Indiae — the foremost emporium of India. The Chera kingdom that controlled it was not a tribal backwater but a political formation sophisticated enough to manage the enormous commercial pressures that the pepper monopoly attracted. The Sangam corpus — the Tamil literary anthologies roughly contemporaneous with the apostolic period — celebrates Chera kings for their role as mediators "between the mountains and the sea," governing the movement of pepper from the Western Ghat hill country to the foreign ships at the coast. Royal protection of merchant activity was a recognised function of kingship.16

Southeastern India: The Coromandel Coast and the Hinge of Two Oceans

Round Cape Komorin, where the Arabian Sea and the Bay of Bengal meet, the Coromandel coast acted as the pivot point of a commercial circuit of a different kind. Where the Malabar coast faced west toward Arabia and the Mediterranean, the Coromandel faced east toward Sri Lanka, the Malay Peninsula, and China. It was the hinge of two oceans.

The Periplus names Kamara, Podukē (Arikamedu), and Sōpatma as recognised commercial ports on this coast, each with its own commodity profile. The largest ships, immense vessels that "cut across" the open Bay of Bengal to reach the Ganges region, operated from here.17 The Chinese Han Shu (Book of Han), compiled contemporaneously with the Periplus, describes the exact same integrated system seen from the opposite, eastern end.18

The Coromandel ports handled pearls from the Pandyan pearl fisheries in the Mannar Gulf, fine muslin textiles from the interior, and semi-precious stone beads and glass beads manufactured at centres like Arikamedu. This last site — identified as the Periplus's Podukē — has been one of the most productive archaeological sites in South India. Among the Arretine ware, Mediterranean glass beads, and Roman lamps recovered from stratified layers, excavators found an unfinished intaglio gemstone: a piece still being carved by a Western craftsman, suggesting not merely the arrival of finished goods but the physical presence of Graeco-Roman artisans working on-site. Arikamedu was not a passive recipient of Mediterranean trade. It was a manufacturing node in two overlapping commercial networks — the Indo-Mediterranean trade from the west and the Bay of Bengal bead-and-textile trade running eastward toward Southeast Asia.19

The Internal Connections: The Roads Within India

These three regions were not sealed off from each other. The Western Ghats were crossed by a series of passes — the Palghat Gap being the most important — that allowed overland movement between the Malabar coast and the Deccan plateau, and thence to the Coromandel coast. A traveller arriving at Barygaza in the northwest could journey overland through the Deccan to reach the Tamil south. This matters because any argument about an apostolic mission that treats "India" as a single destination misreads the geography. The tradition associates Thomas with the northwestern kingdom of Gondophares and the southwestern Malabar coast and the southeastern Coromandel coast. That sequence implies travel across multiple regions along traversable, established routes — not a single miraculous leap to one shore.20

1.4 The Measure of the Trade: Evidence, Counter-Evidence, and Honest Reckoning

The material record of this trade is substantial. But before surveying it, an obligation of honesty requires acknowledging a debate that runs through the modern scholarship: what does the material evidence actually prove?

Roman gold and silver coins bearing the images of Augustus, Tiberius, Claudius, and Nero have been found in substantial quantities on both coasts of South India. On the Malabar coast, hoards near Palayur and Eyyal confirm the western trade route reaching its Indian terminus. On the Coromandel coast and in the Tamil interior, hoards at Karur, Madurai, and across Tamil Nadu confirm penetration beyond the coastal ports into the political heartland of the Tamil kingdoms.21

The coin hoard at Karur deserves a moment. Karur was not a port; it was an inland capital — the seat of the Chera kings. Roman coins did not wash up there by accident. They arrived because the commercial relationship between the Mediterranean world and the Tamil interior had penetrated the political and economic core of the kingdom.22

The excavations at Pattanam, widely identified as ancient Muziris, have produced Roman amphorae sherds, Mediterranean glass beads, torpedo jars used for transporting liquids across the Indian Ocean, and wharf-like structural remains in stratified layers dated to the 1st century BCE through the 3rd century CE. The harbour that the Periplus describes in passing was real, and its physical remains are in the ground.23

Then there is the island of Socotra: a rocky, arid island sitting at the mouth of the Gulf of Aden, at the junction of the Red Sea, the Arabian coast, and the open Indian Ocean. Ships stopped there for water, supplies, and monsoon timing before committing to the open-sea crossing. In the Hoq Cave on its northern coast — apparently a votive site where sailors left offerings and inscriptions — archaeologists have recorded 192 Brahmi inscriptions and one Kharosthi inscription left by Indian merchants and sailors. These are not casual graffiti. They are the accumulated records of generations of an Indian maritime diaspora operating throughout the Arabian Sea, leaving their names at the midpoint of the route just as Tamil-speaking merchants left their names at its Egyptian terminus.24

Together, this evidence — coins in the interior, pottery and infrastructure at the ports, inscriptions at the waypoints — forms a coherent picture of a single integrated system stretching from the Egyptian Red Sea coast to the Tamil interior of India.

A Necessary Caution

But scholarship has rightly questioned what this picture means in human terms. Were the Roman coins circulating as currency, or were they being used as bullion — weighed metal valued for its gold or silver content rather than its imperial stamp? The argument that they functioned as bullion rather than as money matters, because it changes the nature of the contact implied: bullion exchange can be mediated through a chain of intermediaries without any Roman merchant ever setting foot on Indian soil.25

Similarly, questions have been raised about the scale of the trade. Were Strabo's "120 ships" large merchantmen carrying hundreds of tons, or smaller coastal vessels? Was the trade a mass commercial phenomenon, or a thin, high-value exchange involving a small number of professional merchants and luxury goods for elite consumption? Did the archaeological presence of Mediterranean goods at Indian ports necessarily imply broad social contact, or could it reflect a narrow commercial stratum with limited cultural penetration?26

These are serious questions, and they cannot be waved away. The maximalist reading — that the trade was vast, involved direct personal contact between merchants of multiple nationalities, and created a genuinely cosmopolitan social world at the ports — is supported by the weight of evidence, particularly by the Tamil literary sources that describe foreign merchants living in dedicated quarters, burning lamps on the docks at night, and operating under royal protection. But the minimalist reading — that the trade was real but its social footprint was thinner than the material evidence might suggest — remains a legitimate counter-position.

For the specific argument of this chapter, however, even the minimalist reading concedes the essential point: the routes existed, people used them, and goods moved along them in both directions. The question of whether a single individual — an apostle, a merchant, a diplomat — could have physically travelled from the Aramaic-speaking east to the Indian subcontinent is not affected by the debate over trade volume. A missionary does not need a mass market. He needs a route, a ship, and a community at the other end.

The Social Order: Kingdoms, Merchants, and the World That Received the Traveller

Routes carry goods. But a missionary is not a sack of pepper. The question for this section is not whether the Indian ports could receive cargo but whether they could receive people — foreigners who arrived not to trade and leave but to settle, speak, and form relationships within the social fabric of the host community.

The answer, on the evidence of the 1st-century Tamil world, is that the Indian kingdoms were not merely tolerant of foreign merchants but structurally organised to accommodate them.

The institutional precedent was old. The Mauryan Empire (322–185 BCE) had created the first subcontinental administrative framework in Indian history, and its political treatise, the Arthashastra, documents merchant guilds (śreṇi) as legally recognised corporate entities that could make binding contracts, adjudicate commercial disputes, and function as quasi-autonomous economic bodies within the political order.27

By the 1st century CE, the three Tamil kingdoms — the Cheras, the Cholas, and the Pandyas — had developed their own sophisticated forms of this accommodation. The Sangam corpus is the primary witness. These Tamil literary anthologies, composed roughly between the 2nd century BCE and the 3rd century CE, describe a political and commercial world in extraordinary detail. The Pattiṉappālai, a long poem in praise of the Chola capital Puhar (Kaveripattinam), describes the city's commercial quarters, its warehouses stacked with goods from across the seas, its harbour administration, and its distinct residential quarters for different merchant communities. The poem's Puhar is not a fishing village with a jetty. It is a planned commercial city with institutional infrastructure.28

Within this world, foreign merchants — designated Yavana, a term covering Greeks, Romans, and Mediterranean peoples generally — occupied a recognised social position. At Puhar, they had their own quarter in the city, described in the Silappadikāram with the specificity of a permanent feature of the urban landscape. The Sangam puṟam poems celebrate Tamil kings for protecting merchant activity: the king's ability to guarantee safe commerce was a measure of his legitimacy, and his willingness to accommodate foreign traders was a marker of cosmopolitan prestige. These were not communities that grudgingly tolerated outsiders. They were communities that understood foreign commercial presence as part of the order of a well-governed kingdom.29

The fully articulated medieval guild system, documented in later centuries, grew from this institutional soil, but the 1st-century forms cannot be reconstructed in detail. We do not have the institutional documentation for the precise guild forms of the apostolic period. But the Yavana merchant quarters, the śreṇi system, and the Tamil literary evidence for royal patronage of merchant activity confirm that the foundations were already firmly laid. The 1st-century ports were not lawless frontiers. They were managed commercial spaces within kingdoms that had good reason to protect the foreign merchants on whom their wealth depended.30

1.2 THE TONGUE THAT CARRIED THE WORLD

Language, Jewish Presence, and the Communal Networks of the 1st Century

→

We have mapped the world in motion: its routes, its ports, its commodities, its political structures, and the social institutions that received foreign travellers. A reader who has followed this geography knows that India was entirely reachable and that the societies at both ends had the structures to accommodate those who arrived.

But a second, distinct, and perhaps more fundamental question remains: could a traveller from the Aramaic-speaking east function socially and linguistically when he arrived? Could he communicate, find communities of shared heritage, and navigate the social world of the ports well enough to do what a missionary does? The routes answer the question of physical access. Language answers the question of social access.

The answer, in the 1st century CE, is clear. Over centuries, the commercial system had laid a linguistic infrastructure of remarkable reach. Two language families — Greek and Aramaic — between them covered the entire inhabited world from Britain to the South Indian coast. These were the living commercial tongues of the ports, the caravans, and the counting houses of the Indian Ocean world.

Establishing this is not a minor footnote to the plausibility argument. It is its second pillar.

2.1 — Greek: The Language That Followed the Conqueror

Alexander the Great died in 323 BCE in Babylon. His empire fragmented almost immediately, but the language he carried — the vernacular Greek of the Macedonian court (koinē) — survived, spread, and settled. Three centuries after Alexander, Greek was the administrative and commercial language of the entire eastern Roman Empire.

By the 1st century CE, Greek had evolved from a language of conquest into the neutral tongue of commerce. It allowed a merchant from Syria, a sailor from Egypt, an Arab trader from Petra, and an Indian dealer from Barygaza to conduct business across any political frontier. Its reach extended precisely because it had been decoupled from any single power: the Romans administered their eastern empire in Greek; the Parthians used it for diplomacy; the Indo-Parthian kings of northwestern India stamped it on their money as a statement of cosmopolitan legitimacy.

At the Indian ports specifically, Greek was in active commercial use. The Tamil literary sources that describe Mediterranean merchants operating at the ports — arriving, trading, residing seasonally — confirm that a Greek-speaking traveller arriving at Muziris or Arikamedu in the 1st century CE found Greek already spoken as the everyday language of business.31

2.2 — Aramaic: The Older and Deeper Language of the East

If Greek was the language that commerce learned, Aramaic was the language that commerce was born in. Spoken across the Semitic east from the Levant through Mesopotamia to the borders of Persia and India for nearly three thousand years, Aramaic was a family of related dialects united by a common origin and alphabet.32

When the Achaemenid Persian Empire (550–330 BCE) needed an administrative language that could function across a territory stretching from Egypt to the Indus valley, it chose Aramaic because it was already widely spoken as a commercial lingua franca across the Near East. The Persians standardised it into what is known as Imperial Aramaic, deploying it from their Nile satrapies to their Bactrian frontier.33

When Alexander destroyed the Persian Empire, he did not destroy its language. Aramaic persisted as the spoken tongue of the vast majority of former Persian subjects. Underneath the Greek cultural overlay of the Hellenistic ruling class, Aramaic continued as the language of daily life, commercial exchange, and religious practice across the entire former Persian world.

By the 1st century CE, as Seleucid power weakened, local dynasties began to promote their own regional Aramaic dialects to written, official languages. Aramaic in its various regional forms was the spoken language of the Jewish population of Judaea and the Galilee — the primary spoken language of Jesus of Nazareth and his apostles. It was the language of the Nabataean caravan merchants who ran the frankincense routes through northern Arabia, and of the Palmyrene traders whose inscriptions record their journeys to the Persian Gulf and northwestern India.34

An apostle from Galilee, moving eastward through the Aramaic-speaking world, would not have needed to learn a foreign language. He would have been travelling through the extended geography of his own mother tongue.

2.3 — Syriac: How a Local Dialect Became the Language of a Tradition

Among all the regional Aramaic varieties that emerged in the late Hellenistic period, one would prove uniquely consequential for the later preservation of the Thomas tradition. The dialect of Edessa — the commercial frontier city from which the tradition of Thomas's mission to India was eventually transmitted to the wider world — was formalised into what scholars call Syriac.35

Christianity transformed this regional administrative instrument into one of the great literary languages of the ancient world. A Christian community consolidating in Edessa during the 2nd century produced a burst of literary activity that absorbed Hellenistic, Jewish, Gnostic, and Iranian influences. Christianity's second century spread then carried Classical Syriac far beyond Edessa, functioning as the "third lung" of early Christianity alongside Greek and Latin, carrying the faith eastward through the Persian Empire, along the Silk Road, and to the coast of India.3637

✦ THREE SCRIPTS, ONE LANGUAGE

As Syriac spread, its script diversified into three distinct forms — each descended from the same Edessan original.

  • Estrangela: The oldest and most formal Syriac bookhand, descended from the monumental Edessan script.
  • Serto: Associated with the West Syriac tradition, elevated from an already existing Edessan everyday cursive.
  • East Syriac (Madnḥāyā): The script of the Church of the East, evolved in the Persian territories. This is the script in which the texts that reached India arrived. In Kerala, it developed a distinctive local variant.38

Garshuni: When a Community Writes Its Own Language in Someone Else's Script

A phenomenon from a later period deserves brief mention here, because it illuminates the depth of the Syriac connection to Kerala that the subsequent chapters will trace in detail.

As Syriac-speaking communities adopted Arabic as their spoken vernacular after the Islamic conquests — centuries after the period this chapter describes — many retained the Syriac script for writing that Arabic, a practice known as Garshuni. The logic was identity: it was a way of declaring heritage, of maintaining continuity with the Syriac scribal tradition even as the language itself passed out of everyday use.

This practice extended to Malayalam. Garshuni Malayalam — Malayalam written in East Syriac script — is found in manuscripts from the Kerala Christian archives. It is direct evidence that Syriac was embedded in the cultural identity of the Kerala community, functioning as the alphabet in which they expressed themselves even when writing their own vernacular tongue. The phenomenon is not 1st-century evidence; it is a later development whose roots lie in the transmission chain that this chapter reconstructs.39

2.4 — The Aramaic Footprint at the Edge of India

The linguistic reach of Aramaic extended into the writing systems of the territories it had once administered. The Kharosthi script — used across the Indo-Parthian northwest from the 3rd century BCE — is widely held to derive directly from Imperial Aramaic. A scribe working in the Achaemenid administrative tradition adapted the Aramaic alphabet to the sounds of an Indian language.40

Kharosthi was the script of precisely the territory that the Acts of Thomas assigns to the apostle's mission in the Indo-Parthian northwest. By the 1st century CE, it was concentrated in the Gandharan and Taxilan zone — already in decline further afield, being gradually displaced by Brahmi, but still a living script in the very region where the Thomas tradition places the apostle's arrival. An Aramaic-speaking missionary arriving in that territory would have arrived in a world whose script was a daughter of the same Imperial Aramaic alphabet from which his own dialect derived. The Aramaic footprint in the northwest was not a historical memory. It was a structural reality, written on the royal inscriptions and commercial records of the region.

2.5 — The Jewish Presence: The Network That Made a Mission Possible

Every route and language described in this chapter ran through the single most important communal network for any missionary moving east from Judaea: the Jewish diaspora.

By the 1st century CE, Jewish communities were distributed across the entire arc from the Atlantic coast of Spain to the Persian Gulf, and very probably beyond. These communities did not exist as isolated ethnic enclaves. They were embedded in the commercial life of the cities they inhabited, linked to each other by kinship, religious observance, and the institutional structures of the synagogue — which functioned not merely as a house of prayer but as a community centre, a court of law, a hostel for travelling co-religionists, and a node in a network of trust that spanned the commercial world.

A 1st-century Jewish-Christian missionary moving east from Judaea would not have been travelling into foreign territory. He would have been following the living network of his own people, and at each major stop, the synagogue would have provided what every missionary needs: a roof, an audience, and an introduction to the local community.

In Alexandria, the Jewish community was enormous — perhaps a million strong by the most frequently cited contemporary estimate, occupying two of the city's five districts. Alexandrian Jews were not marginal. They were prominent in the grain trade, in banking, and in the commercial networks that organised the Egypt-to-India trade through the Red Sea ports. An apostle leaving Judaea for India via the Egyptian route passed through one of the largest, wealthiest, and most commercially connected Jewish communities in the world — a community whose merchants were among the principal organisers of the very shipping system that carried goods to India.41

In Babylon and Seleucia-Ctesiphon, the Jewish community was older than the Alexandrian one and in some respects more powerful. The descendants of the Babylonian exile, settled in Mesopotamia for over five centuries by the 1st century CE, had their own institutional leadership — the exilarchate, which would later become the premier Jewish political office of the eastern diaspora — their own courts, and their own deeply embedded commercial networks within the Parthian economy. A missionary moving eastward passed through the Jewish heartland of the Parthian world: a community whose Aramaic speech was mutually intelligible with his own, and whose institutional networks offered the hospitality, the local knowledge, and the communal trust that made long-distance travel possible.42

In the Arabian corridor, Jewish merchant communities had been established for centuries in the spice and aromatics trade. The caravan routes that carried frankincense and myrrh from southern Arabia toward the Mediterranean ran through territories where Jewish commercial presence was a long-established fact.43

In India, the ancient Cochin Jewish community traces its origins to the pre-CE period. While the claim cannot be verified with precise 1st-century documentation, it fits perfectly into a Malabar coast thoroughly integrated into the Indian Ocean commercial world, receiving merchants of multiple nations and serving as home to permanent foreign quarters. A parallel maritime narrative exists further north along the Konkan Coast, where the Bene Israel community traditionally believes their ancestors arrived following a 2nd-century BCE shipwreck near Navgaon. Though similarly lacking early epigraphic proof, their deep integration is evident; by the 1st century CE, they were a well-established presence in the region, known locally as "Shanwar Telis" (Saturday oil pressers) due to their strict observance of the Sabbath rest. Ultimately, the famous copper plate grants of later centuries—which officially document royal privileges extended to Jewish communities in Kerala—are the institutional descendants of a hospitable pattern of accommodation that this early commercial world had already firmly established.44

For a Jewish-Christian missionary moving east in the 1st century CE, this chain of communities meant something specific and practical: he arrived at each stage of the journey with the communal fabric of hospitality, local knowledge, and trust already in place. He did not need to establish a network from nothing. The network existed. The diaspora was it.45

2.6 — Two Languages, One Passport, One Honest Reckoning

The combination of Greek and Aramaic was a universal passport across the known world. Greek opened the commercial doors at every major port. Aramaic opened the social doors of the Semitic diaspora communities running through every major city from Antioch to Babylon to the Indian ports.

For an apostle from Galilee, Aramaic was his mother tongue. Greek was the language of the wider Hellenistic world that had surrounded Galilee since before his birth. Moving eastward, he would have found Aramaic-speaking communities at every major stopping point and Greek-speaking merchants at every major port.

But here, honesty requires a pause — the kind of pause that separates rigorous history from advocacy.

The language in which we receive the Thomas tradition is neither Galilean Aramaic nor Koine Greek. It is Syriac — the literary language of Edessa. The Acts of Thomas, the hymns of Ephrem the Syrian, and the apologetic church histories of the Kerala community were all written in Syriac.

If a Galilean apostle spoke to communities on the Indian coast in the 1st century CE, he spoke in Galilean Aramaic. We do not have that layer. Between whatever happened in the 1st century and the sources that reach us today, there is the Syriac layer: the literary and institutional tradition of the Edessan church that claimed Thomas as its own and carried that shaped tradition eastward to India. The pre-Syriac layer, if it existed, is simply not available for comparison.46

This is not a deficiency in our method. It is the condition under which any honest study of the Thomas tradition must operate.

The Kerala Christians call themselves Suṟiyāni Naṣrāṇikal — Syrian Christians. The name is borrowed from the Persian Soryāni, the term for the Christian community of the Sasanian Empire.47 In that single name, the entire genealogy is compressed: a Galilean Jewish movement, carrying into the Aramaic-speaking world, received and transmitted by the Syriac literary tradition of Edessa, reaching the Indian coast through the Persian-Arab commercial corridor, and embedding itself so deeply that the community's self-description still carries the Persian name for the Syriac-speaking church.

Whatever the 1st century produced or did not produce, that name is what the tradition became.

Closing the Chapter: Possibility Established, Proof Deferred

We have assembled the evidence for a single claim: that the world of the 1st century CE was fully capable, in all the key dimensions, of producing an apostolic mission to the Indian subcontinent.

The routes were there — multiple, overlapping, and routinely used. The political and social infrastructure was there — the cosmopolitan Indo-Parthian kingdom receiving overland travellers in the northwest, the Tamil kingdoms accommodating maritime merchants in managed port cities in the south, and the far-reaching Jewish diaspora communities providing a network of trust across every frontier. The languages were there — Greek across major commercial ports, Aramaic across the overland trade corridors, and Syriac as a literary medium through which the tradition would later be preserved.

From the court of Gondophares in the northwest, to the pepper ports of Malabar in the southwest, to the oceanic hinge of the Coromandel coast in the southeast — the doors of the Indian subcontinent were wide open.

So, what physical, 1st-century material evidence exists to prove that an Aramaic-speaking Galilean actually walked through those doors? What inscription, coin, or contemporary document conclusively places Saint Thomas in the territory of Gondophares or on the docks of South India?

The answer must be stated plainly: none that can be directly and securely identified. None.

Is this absence of 1st-century material proof disastrous for the tradition? To a modern mind, accustomed to prioritising direct, datable evidence, it might seem fatal. But to understand the ancient world, we must ask a different question: how does the Thomas tradition stack up against the traditions of the other apostles? If we demand 1st-century archaeological proof for Peter in Rome, James in Spain, John in Ephesus, Matthew in Ethiopia or Bartholomew in Armenia, what would one find?

One would find a similar, echoing silence.

To evaluate early Christian traditions, including the St. Thomas tradition, we cannot demand evidence that the ancient world was not equipped to leave. Instead, we must turn to the evidence that does exist. We must examine the documents through which the tradition is preserved, understand who wrote them, and decipher what they were trying to say.

That is the journey of the next chapter. We leave the physical, cosmopolitan matrix of the 1st century behind and step into the literary and historical world of the 2nd to the 6th centuries. This was the era in which the tradition first entered the cultural corpus. Through a patristic chain of references, and a trail of documents that differ widely in genre, geography, and theological intent, we will trace how the memory of the apostle was written down, debated, and transmitted, before later ecclesiastical traditions consolidated and standardised these memories.

The Carmelite Vicariate and the Padroado, 1701–1724

The Bishop at Alangad

"To rule the Syrians till the Archbishop of Kodungallur could occupy his see."

— The papal brief naming Angelo Francis vicar apostolic, 1700

By the closing years of the seventeenth century the Catholic Thomas Christians had been left, once again, with no bishop standing in any sure succession. The unhappy experiment of the Portuguese coadjutor Raphael de Figueredo had run its course in suspension and disgrace; Figueredo died in 1695. Bishop Parambil Chandy, the indigenous prelate who had held the larger part of the community to Rome, was dead, and so was his faithful Carmelite adviser, Matthew of St Joseph, who died at Cochin in 1691 and was buried at Verapoly. The apostolic visitor Rome had sent to set the house in order, Custodio de Pinho, had died too, probably without ever reaching the Serra, his commission unfulfilled. Every principal actor of the previous generation had left the stage, and the Catholic body was once more headless. 1

Into that vacancy Rome had at last to appoint. There was a rumour that the choice might fall on a Jesuit, and the rumour alone was enough to alarm. The detestation of the Society among the Thomas Christians, the inheritance of Diamper and of Garcia's quarrel with the Archdeacon, ran so hot that a Jesuit bishop would have loosed a storm. Wiser counsel prevailed. By the agreement of 1698, which had opened Malabar to Carmelites who were neither Portuguese nor in the Padroado's service, the way was clear to name one of them.

A Consecrator from Mesopotamia

On 20 February 1700 the pope appointed Fr Angelo Francis of St Teresa, superior of the Verapoly seminary, vicar apostolic of the land of the Malabars. The appointment carried a clause that would shape everything that followed: he was to rule the Syrians only until the Archbishop of Kodungallur could personally occupy his see.

An appointment was not a consecration, and here the new vicar apostolic ran straight into the wall the Dutch conquest had built. To be made a bishop he needed a bishop to make him, and the only bishops within reach of the Serra who stood in undoubted succession were the Padroado prelates, the Bishop of Cochin and the Archbishop of Goa. Neither would serve. When Angelo Francis was given an enthusiastic reception by the communities in union with Rome on 13 February 1701, he found he could obtain consecration neither from the Bishop of Cochin, Peter Pacheco, who in any case did not reside in his own diocese, nor from Goa. The Padroado prelates would not lend their hands to a man whose very appointment they regarded as an affront, the Holy See presuming to set a bishop over Malabar without the approval of Lisbon. 2 3

What rescued the Carmelite was a piece of the same good fortune that had once served Mar Thomas. There happened to be in Malabar a stray Eastern bishop, Mar Simon, and he consented to do what the Padroado would not. Mar Simon is one of the genuinely obscure figures of a period full of them, but the essential facts can be established: he was a Catholic prelate of the Chaldean, that is East Syrian, rite, in communion with Rome; he came from Diarbekir and was metropolitan, titular or actual, of Adana in Asia Minor; his journey to Malabar was bound up with the reunion of the Diarbekir Chaldeans with Rome, and he had passed through Rome and Lisbon on his way, authorised to labour for the return of the Malabar Jacobites. On 22 May 1701, in the church of Alangad, this Mesopotamian bishop consecrated Angelo Francis. 4

The choice of Alangad can hardly have been accidental, and the symmetry was not lost on contemporaries. It was at Alangad, in May 1653, that the twelve cattanars had laid their hands on the Archdeacon and made the consecration that no church would recognise; it was at Alangad, forty-eight years later almost to the month, that a proper consecration was now performed, and again by an Eastern, not a Latin, bishop. The community that had broken itself trying to obtain valid orders from the East had them at last, on the Catholic side, from precisely that quarter. Mar Simon's part in the affair, however, earned him no thanks from the Padroado or the Propaganda. Sources record that once he had served the Carmelites' turn, Simon was found under guard with the French Capuchins at Pondicherry, where he lived out his days, dying there in 1720. What happened in between remains shrouded in mystery, and the Malabar cattanars themselves would later accuse the Carmelites of having forcibly deported the Chaldean prelate to Pondicherry to prevent the Syrian Christians from accepting him as their own bishop. 5

The Padroado Strikes Back

In 1663 the Portuguese power on the coast was being destroyed as Chandy was raised up: Cochin had just fallen, Archbishop Garcia was four years dead, the Padroado prelates were expelled or gone, and there was no Portuguese bishop left on the coast to lodge a complaint, nor a crown in any position to press one. Chandy's vicariate was erected into a vacuum. By 1700 that vacuum had been partly filled. Portugal had recovered its nerve and resumed appointing to the Eastern sees: an Archbishop of Goa since 1691, a Bishop of Cochin resident in the south of his diocese since 1699, and, within months, an Archbishop of Kodungallur. The Padroado had not changed its claims between 1663 and 1701; it had merely regained the men on the ground to assert them. What it could not do in the year of Cochin's fall, it could very much do now. 6

The news that Rome had planted a vicar apostolic on the Malabar coast, by its own authority and without reference to Lisbon, was precisely what the Portuguese crown could not let pass. The Padroado was not a courtesy but a treaty right, the king of Portugal's ancient prerogative of nominating to the sees of the East in return for the burden of maintaining the missions, and a vicar apostolic answering only to Propaganda was a standing denial of it. The crown answered the only way it could, by filling the very see whose vacancy had been the Carmelite's opportunity. On 5 December 1701 the Jesuit John Ribeiro was appointed Archbishop of Kodungallur. This was the old see of the Thomas Christians under another name: the Latin diocese of Angamaly, erected for them after the Synod of Diamper, had been transferred to Kodungallur (Cranganore) and raised to an archbishopric in 1608, so that the Archbishop of Kodungallur was, in Padroado law, the metropolitan of the Serra, the very office the vicar apostolic had been set up to supplant. 7

Ribeiro's appointment was a double provocation, for he was at once a Padroado prelate and a Jesuit, the two things the Thomas Christians had most reason to resist. Yet he was also, by the technical clause of Angelo Francis's own brief, the man whose arrival was supposed to end the Carmelite's mandate. This was the collision the previous chapter foresaw. When Sebastiani raised up a Roman vicar apostolic over a flock that in law still belonged to the Padroado archbishop of Kodungallur, he planted a conflict that lay dormant only so long as no Portuguese prelate could set foot on the coast; the day one reached Kodungallur, the two claims were bound to collide. Ribeiro was that prelate, and his coming sprang the trap. For a year the Dutch and a part of the clergy kept him out, but in 1704, in the face of that opposition, Ribeiro forced his way into the territory of Kodungallur and took up the rule of the archdiocese. He was the first Jesuit prelate to reside in Malabar since the death of Archbishop Garcia in 1659, every Portuguese appointee in the intervening forty-five years having either refused the post or failed to reach it. By the plain words of the brief, his taking possession did not merely rival the vicariate; it extinguished it, for Rome had granted the Carmelite the Syrians only until the Archbishop of Kodungallur should occupy his see. The very instrument by which Rome had governed the Serra carried within it the clause by which the Padroado could undo that government, and Ribeiro had now invoked it. 8

There was a deeper irony in the spectacle of a Jesuit carrying the Padroado's banner against Rome. A century earlier, as Chapter 11 showed, the Society had stood on the other side of this very quarrel. Under the Habsburg union of the crowns, the Jesuits had been the men of Rome and of Madrid in the Malabar mission, kept and defended there by pope and king alike against the Portuguese establishment that distrusted them, the Goa metropolitan and the diocesan bishops of the older orders. The fault line then ran between the international Society and the Portuguese apparatus. The Restoration of 1640 turned it the other way. With the Braganza on the throne, the crown was Portuguese again, and the Padroado it wielded was no longer a machinery that Rome and a Spanish sovereign could override but the prerogative of an independent Portugal jealous of its rights. The Jesuits of the Malabar Padroado, who had once been Rome's instrument against the Portuguese establishment, were now, as the crown's own appointees, the instrument of the Portuguese crown against Rome's Propaganda. The Society had not changed sides; the crown had changed nationality, and carried the Society's allegiance with it. Ribeiro, a Portuguese Jesuit sent by a Portuguese king to hold a Padroado see against a Roman vicar apostolic, was the visible sign of that reversal. 9

His coming reopened every old wound. The Thomas Christians had sworn at the Coonan Cross never to submit to a Jesuit, and they had not changed their minds. On 20 June 1704 a body of cattanars assembled in the church at Kaduthuruthy, the same parish that had figured in so many of the community's councils, and bound themselves by oath, swearing by the Saviour, by the Virgin, and by the twelve apostles, to remain independent of Goa and to obey only the Carmelite vicar apostolic, from whose order, they declared, they had received the truest help in all their necessities, until the pope should hear their appeal. The Padroado archbishop had been in possession of his see a matter of weeks, and already the people he claimed had repudiated him. 10

The Bishop Who Yielded

What is most revealing about Angelo Francis is what he did next, which was to give way. He knew the Portuguese clergy were ranged against him and that he could count on Dutch support; he might have stood on the loyalty of the Syrians and made a fight of it. Instead he chose to honour the limiting clause of his own appointment. Since his brief had named him to rule the Syrians only until the Archbishop of Kodungallur could occupy his see, and since Ribeiro had now done exactly that, Angelo Francis judged that fairness required him to stand down. On 29 June 1704 he issued a circular explaining his decision to the communities that obeyed him, and withdrew to the monastery of Verapoly. 11

It was a scrupulous act, and a disastrous one. To leave the Catholic Syrians divided between a resident Jesuit archbishop they would not obey and a Carmelite vicar apostolic who had voluntarily retired was to hand an advantage to the only party that gained from Catholic disarray, the followers of the Mar Thoma line, whose head was by now firmly in the Jacobite camp. Propaganda saw the danger and moved to undo the damage. On 26 September 1706 the Congregation decided that the jurisdiction of the vicar apostolic must be maintained, that Angelo Francis, in other words, should not have yielded, and that Rome's man, not Lisbon's, was to govern the Serra. 12

An Argument Without End

The decision of 1706 settled the principle and almost nothing else. It had no immediate effect, and had to be confirmed all over again by a plenary vote of the Congregation on 25 June 1708. Even then the matter would not lie still. Apostolic Letters of 13 March and 4 May 1709 turned about and insisted on the rights of the Portuguese prelates, and on the vicar apostolic's duty to bring the dissident Syrians back to obedience, a duty that, given the temper of the Thomas Christians and the hostility of the Dutch Company, was very close to impossible. 13

The sources record this zig-zag, a vicariate affirmed in 1706, reaffirmed in 1708, then undercut in 1709, more fully than they explain it; Tisserant offers only the single observation that Propaganda could not bring itself to abolish the Padroado, and that so compromising an attitude was bound to prolong the competition. That much is certain, but it accounts for only one side of the vacillation. The reversals make better sense, it may be suggested, as the trace of three aims that could not all be served at once. Rome wanted, first, to govern the Serra effectively, which under Dutch rule meant through a vicar apostolic of its own, since no Portuguese bishop could reside there; this was the impulse that had created the vicariate of Malabar in the first place, a Propaganda jurisdiction raised up, in practice, to do the work the Padroado see of Kodungallur could not. Rome wanted, second, to keep faith with the Padroado, the king of Portugal's treaty right of patronage over the Eastern church, which the Holy See had granted in perpetuity and could not in conscience or in law simply abolish; and every time Lisbon protested, this second aim reasserted itself and the limiting clause was tightened. Rome wanted, third, to win back the dissident Puthankūr, which required a bishop the Syrians would actually accept, and they would accept the Carmelite and not the Jesuit. The three aims pulled in different directions. To govern effectively was to favour the vicar apostolic and slight the Padroado; to honour the Padroado was to favour the Jesuit archbishop and cripple the vicar apostolic; and to reconcile the separated was to need the very Carmelite the Padroado was trying to displace. So Rome had quite literally created a vicariate of Malabar to do the work of the Kodungallur see, and then, under Portuguese pressure, kept subordinating that vicariate back to the Kodungallur see it had been created to supplant. The instrument and the institution it was meant to replace were now set to limit each other, and Rome, unwilling to surrender either, oscillated between them. The famous restrictive formula, that the vicar apostolic should govern only the churches and places where the ordinaries could not exercise their own jurisdiction, was less a settlement than a confession that Rome meant to keep both and would decide nothing. What this led to was not only a further hardening of the institutional division between the Puthankūr and the Pazhayakūr, but also a growing distrust and alienation between the Pazhayakūr and the Carmelites. 14

Angelo Francis himself was worn out by the struggle. In January 1712 he set out, an old man, on a visitation of the scattered Christian communities, and kept at it until the September of that year. The effort was more than he could bear, and he died of exhaustion on 17 October 1712, having spent his last strength tramping between the churches of a vicariate whose right to exist Rome was still arguing about. The brief appointing his successor, John Baptist Mary of St Teresa, carried the same crippling reservation as before: he was to have charge of the churches of Kodungallur and Cochin only where the ordinaries could not exercise their own jurisdiction. Propaganda could not bring itself to abolish the Padroado, and the compromise guaranteed that the competition would simply continue. 15

And continue it did. In 1721 Pope Innocent XIII ratified the nomination of two more Portuguese Jesuits, Antonio Pimentel to Kodungallur and Francisco de Vasconcellos to Cochin, so that the Padroado line ran on alongside the Carmelite one. None of these Portuguese prelates could effectively reside among the Thomas Christians; Vasconcellos would hold the see of Cochin for twenty years without ever once being able to live within his own diocese. The pattern set in 1701 had hardened into the permanent condition of the Catholic Thomas Christians: two rival hierarchies, a Roman and a Portuguese, claiming the same flock across a coast where a Calvinist company would let neither freely rule, while a third church, the Puthankūr, went its own separate way entirely. The consecration at Alangad had given the Catholics a bishop; it had not given them peace. Over the next eighty years, the double jurisdiction of the Carmelites and the Padroado would subject the Serra to a wearying, divided rule. The resentment of the indigenous Catholic clergy under foreign Carmelite vicars apostolic would steadily accumulate, waiting for a spark, until the cattanars of the Serra rose at length, in the Angamaly Padiyola of 1787, in open and organised protest against the Carmelite administration, a story for a later chapter. 16

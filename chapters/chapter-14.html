14

The Carmelite Vicariate and the Padroado, 1701–1730

The Bishop at Alangad

"To rule the Syrians till the Archbishop of Kodungallur could occupy his see."

— The papal brief naming Angelo Francis vicar apostolic, 1700

By the closing years of the seventeenth century the Catholic Thomas Christians had been left, once again, with no bishop standing in any sure succession. The unhappy experiment of the Portuguese coadjutor Raphael de Figueredo had run its course in suspension and disgrace; Figueredo died in 1695. Bishop Parambil Chandy, the indigenous prelate who had held the larger part of the community to Rome, was dead, and so was his faithful Carmelite adviser, Matthew of St Joseph, who died at Cochin in 1691 and was buried at Verapoly. The apostolic visitor Rome had sent to set the house in order, Custodio de Pinho, had died too, probably without ever reaching the Serra, his commission unfulfilled. Every principal actor of the previous generation had left the stage but one. Through the long vacancy the Catholic Thomas Christians were not wholly without a head, for their affairs were held together by a native administrator of the old kind, the archdeacon Mathew Pallil, a nephew of the late Bishop Chandy. He governed in the manner of the ancient office, but he was an archdeacon only, never a bishop, for the Carmelites had years before refused Chandy’s wish to raise his nephew to that dignity and set a prelate of their own choosing over the Serra instead. We shall meet his end in its place. 1

Into that vacancy Rome had at last to appoint. There was a rumour that the choice might fall on a Jesuit, and the rumour alone was enough to alarm. The detestation of the Society among the Thomas Christians, the inheritance of Diamper and of Garcia's quarrel with the Archdeacon, ran so hot that a Jesuit bishop would have loosed a storm. Wiser counsel prevailed. By the agreement of 1698, which had opened Malabar to Carmelites who were neither Portuguese nor in the Padroado's service, the way was clear to name one of them.

A Consecrator from Mesopotamia

On 20 February 1700 the pope appointed Fr Angelo Francis of St Teresa, superior of the Verapoly seminary, vicar apostolic of the land of the Malabars. The appointment carried a clause that would shape everything that followed: he was to rule the Syrians only until the Archbishop of Kodungallur could personally occupy his see.

No Hand to Make Him

An appointment was not a consecration, and here the new vicar apostolic ran straight into the wall the Dutch conquest had built. To be made a bishop he needed a bishop to make him, and the only bishops within reach of the Serra who stood in undoubted succession were the Padroado prelates, the Bishop of Cochin and the Archbishop of Goa. Neither would serve. When Angelo Francis was given an enthusiastic reception by the communities in union with Rome on 13 February 1701, he found he could obtain consecration neither from Cochin, whose bishop could not exercise his office in the Dutch-held Serra, nor from Goa. The Padroado prelates would not lend their hands to a man whose very appointment they regarded as an affront, the Holy See presuming to set a bishop over Malabar without the approval of Lisbon. 2

A Stranger from Diarbekir

What rescued the Carmelite was a piece of the same good fortune that had once served Mar Thomas. There happened to be in Malabar a stray Eastern bishop, Mar Simon, and he consented to do what the Padroado would not. Mar Simon is one of the genuinely obscure figures of a period full of them, but the essential facts can be established: he was a Catholic prelate of the Chaldean, that is East Syrian, rite, in communion with Rome; he came from Diarbekir and was metropolitan, titular or actual, of Adana in Asia Minor; his journey to Malabar was bound up with the reunion of the Diarbekir Chaldeans with Rome, and he had passed through Rome and Lisbon on his way, authorised to labour for the return of the Malabar Jacobites. On 22 May 1701, in the church of Alangad, this Mesopotamian bishop consecrated Angelo Francis. That he was a Catholic Chaldean is worth a word, for by this date the old Church of the East, which had split in 1552 into a line that sought union with Rome and a line that did not (the schism traced in Chapter 9), had fractured further into three: the ancient traditionalist patriarchate of Alqosh, near Mosul, which by now stood outside the Roman communion again; the line of Mar Shimun, descended from Sulaqa’s pro-Roman party of 1552, which had in its turn drifted away and formally broken with Rome in 1692; and a newer Catholic patriarchate seated at Diarbekir, the one line then securely in communion with Rome. It was from this last, the Diarbekir Catholics, that Mar Simon came, which is why a bishop of the East could serve the Carmelites’ Roman purpose at all. 3

The choice of Alangad can hardly have been accidental, and the symmetry was not lost on contemporaries. It was at Alangad, in May 1653, that the twelve cattanars had laid their hands on the Archdeacon and made the consecration that no church would recognise; it was at Alangad, forty-eight years later almost to the month, that a proper consecration was now performed, and again by an Eastern, not a Latin, bishop. The community that had broken itself trying to obtain valid orders from the East had them at last, on the Catholic side, from precisely that quarter. Mar Simon's part in the affair, however, earned him no settled place in either the Padroado or the Propaganda order of things. Sources record that once he had served the Carmelites' turn, Simon was found under guard with the French Capuchins at Pondicherry, where he lived out his days, dying there in 1720. What happened in between remains shrouded in mystery, and the Malabar cattanars themselves would later accuse the Carmelites of having forcibly deported the Chaldean prelate to Pondicherry to prevent the Syrian Christians from accepting him as their own bishop. 4

The World the Bishops Lived In

It is worth pausing to see the coast on which all this was happening, for the quarrels of bishops were played out within a political order that none of the churchmen controlled. The Malabar that Angelo Francis was consecrated to govern was a Dutch coast. The Dutch East India Company had taken Cochin from the Portuguese in 1663, and from 1669 it administered its Malabar possessions through a separate commandery seated at Cochin, though still within the wider VOC system centred on Batavia. The Company had reduced the old Portuguese town, made the Raja of Cochin a client, and bound the lesser rajas of the coast by monopoly treaties for pepper, the black gold that was the whole point of being there. 5

Yet the Dutch grip, though real, was narrower than the map suggested. The Company held the forts and the seaward face of the coast; it never achieved the total pepper monopoly it wanted, and the interior, where most of the Thomas Christians actually lived, lay in the hands of Hindu rulers, the Zamorin of Calicut and a patchwork of petty rajas, over whom the Company had influence but not sovereignty. This is why the Jesuits, expelled from Dutch Cochin, had been able simply to withdraw inland to Ambazhakkad in the territory beyond the Company's reach and carry on their seminary and their mission there; and it is why the Padroado archbishops of Kodungallur, when at length they forced their way in, would take up residence not on the Dutch-controlled littoral but inland at Puttenchira. The decisive fact for the Catholic Thomas Christians was that they were the subjects, ecclesiastically, of European powers who could not actually reach them, and the flock, scattered through the lands of half a dozen Hindu princes, was in practice harder to govern than any party to the dispute admitted. 6

Calvinists Keeping Catholics

The religious policy of the Company was, by Iberian standards, an inversion. The Dutch were Calvinists who had no interest whatever in converting Malabar to the Reformed faith; their concern was trade, and their treatment of the Catholic clergy was governed by political calculation, not zeal. A Portuguese priest was a potential agent of Lisbon and therefore an enemy to be excluded; a Carmelite who was neither Portuguese nor a servant of the Padroado was tolerable, within limits. This was the logic of the agreement of 1698, by which the Company had consented to admit Carmelites to Malabar provided they were Belgians, Germans, or Italians, never Portuguese, and the same logic that capped their number at twelve at any time, and usually kept it below that. The Dutch had no wish to see Rome's writ run freely either; what they wanted was a Catholic establishment dependent on Dutch goodwill rather than on a rival European crown. The Carmelite vicariate suited that purpose precisely, and Dutch favour, as much as Roman principle, is why it survived. 7

A Seed in the Sand

Far to the south, a smaller European presence had just taken root that would, in the long run, outlast them all. In 1695 the English East India Company, granted a site by the Rani of Attingal, had begun to fortify a sandy spit at Anjengo, completing the fort by 1699; it was the Company's first permanent settlement on the Malabar coast, a pepper factory deep in Travancore country. In the years of Angelo Francis it was a marginal thing, a single fortified warehouse far from the Serra, of no consequence to the affairs of the Thomas Christians. But it was the seed of the power that would in time displace both the Dutch and the Padroado: when the Dutch surrendered their Malabar settlements in 1795, it would be to the English, and the political frame within which the next century of the Thomas Christians' history unfolded would be a British one. For the moment, that lay far in the future. 8

The Padroado Strikes Back

In 1663, just as Chandy was consecrated, Portuguese power on the Malabar coast was collapsing. Cochin had just fallen to the Dutch; Archbishop Garcia was nearly four years dead; the Padroado prelates were expelled, absent, or powerless; and no Portuguese diocesan bishop remained on the coast either to lodge an effective complaint or to have it enforced by the crown. Chandy’s vicariate was erected into that practical vacuum. By 1701, however, the vacuum had been partly filled. Portugal had recovered both its independent crown and the nerve to press its claims again: Goa had a resident archbishop from 1691, Agostinho da Anunciação of the Order of Christ, and from 1699 Pacheco was established as resident bishop of Cochin in the southern part of his diocese, beyond the reach of the Dutch. The Dutch still barred Portuguese prelates from residing freely in the Serra, but the Padroado had not abated its claims; it was waiting only for an opportunity to reassert them. What it could not do in the year of Cochin’s fall, it could very much attempt now. 9

The news that Rome had planted a vicar apostolic on the Malabar coast, by its own authority and without reference to Lisbon, was precisely what the Portuguese crown could not let pass. The Crown answered by filling the very see whose vacancy had been the Carmelite's opportunity. On 5 December 1701 the Jesuit John Ribeiro was appointed Archbishop of Kodungallur-Angamaly. 10

Ribeiro’s appointment was a double provocation, for he was at once a Padroado prelate and a Jesuit, the two things the Thomas Christians had most reason to resist. Yet by the technical clause of Angelo Francis’s own brief, he was also the man whose arrival was supposed to bring the Carmelite mandate to an end. This was the collision the previous chapter foresaw. When Sebastiani raised up a Roman vicar apostolic over a flock that the older Padroado-Jesuit arrangement still claimed as its exclusive field, he planted a conflict that could remain dormant only so long as no Portuguese Jesuit prelate could set foot on the coast. The day such a prelate reached Kodungallur-Angamaly, the two claims were bound to collide.

Ribeiro was that prelate, and his coming sprang the trap. For a year the Dutch and a section of the clergy kept him out, but in 1704, in the face of that opposition, he forced his way into the territory of Kodungallur-Angamaly and took up the rule of the archdiocese. He was the first Jesuit prelate to reside in Malabar since the death of Archbishop Garcia in 1659, every Portuguese appointee in the intervening decades having either refused the post or failed to reach it. By the plain words of the brief, his taking possession did not merely rival the vicariate; it appeared to extinguish it, for Rome had granted the Carmelite authority over the Serra only until the Archbishop of Kodungallur should occupy his see. The very instrument by which Rome had governed the Serra carried within it the clause by which the Padroado could undo that government, and Ribeiro had now invoked it. Among the Jesuits who came with him was a young German from Westphalia, Ernst Hanxleden, remembered in Malabar as Arnos Pathiri, who would labour there more than thirty years and become a scholar of Sanskrit and Malayalam, composing Malayalam religious verse that the Catholics of Kerala still cherished into modern times; if the Society’s prelates brought the Thomas Christians political grief, its missionaries also gave the language some of its earliest European learning. 11

The Society in Two Minds

There was a deeper irony in the spectacle of a Jesuit carrying the Padroado’s banner against Rome. The Society of Jesus was, by its own deepest instinct, a Roman and international order, bound to the pope with a special obedience and imagined as the most mobile arm of the Catholic Reformation. Yet in Portuguese Asia it lived inside the Padroado world: it travelled on Portuguese ships, worked under Portuguese protection, depended on royal favour, and in Malabar had long enjoyed a crown-protected field against rival Catholic claimants. The Jesuit mission in the Serra was therefore Rome-centred in spirit but Padroado-bound in operation.

The Restoration of the Portuguese crown in 1640 turned that ambiguous arrangement inside out. The crown that had once protected the Jesuit mission as part of a larger Habsburg-Catholic strategy was now again the crown of an independent Portugal, jealous of its Padroado rights and suspicious of Roman intrusion. The Jesuits of Malabar, who had once been shielded against rival Padroado orders, especially the Dominicans, by royal and Roman favour, now appeared as the royal nominees of that same Padroado system against Propaganda’s own vicar apostolic. Ribeiro, a Portuguese Jesuit sent by a Portuguese king to hold Kodungallur-Angamaly against a Carmelite bishop appointed directly by Rome, was therefore not an accidental contradiction. He was the visible sign of the reversal: the old Jesuit claim over the Serra had survived, but the political world that sustained it had changed around it.12

An Oath at Kaduthuruthy

His coming reopened every old wound. The Thomas Christians had sworn at the Coonan Cross never to submit to a Jesuit, and they had not changed their minds. On 20 June 1704 a body of cattanars assembled in the church at Kaduthuruthy, the same parish that had figured in so many of the community's councils, and bound themselves by oath, swearing by the Saviour, by the Virgin, and by the twelve apostles, to remain independent of Goa and to obey only the Carmelite vicar apostolic, from whose order, they declared, they had received the truest help in all their necessities, until the pope should hear their appeal. The Padroado archbishop had been in possession of his see a matter of weeks, and already the people he claimed had repudiated him. 13

The Bishop Who Yielded

What is most revealing about Angelo Francis is what he did next, which was to give way. He knew the Portuguese clergy were ranged against him and that he could count on Dutch support; he might have stood on the loyalty of the Syrians and made a fight of it. Instead he chose to honour the limiting clause of his own appointment. Since his brief had named him to rule the Syrians only until the Archbishop of Kodungallur could occupy his see, and since Ribeiro had now done exactly that, Angelo Francis judged that fairness required him to stand down. On 29 June 1704 he issued a circular explaining his decision to the communities that obeyed him, and withdrew to the monastery of Verapoly. 14

It was a scrupulous act, and a disastrous one. To leave the Catholic Syrians divided between a resident Jesuit archbishop they would not obey and a Carmelite vicar apostolic who had voluntarily retired was to hand an advantage to the only party that gained from Catholic disarray, the followers of the Mar Thoma line, whose head was by now firmly in the Jacobite camp. Propaganda, the Roman Congregation de Propaganda Fide, through which the popes governed the missions and to which the Carmelite vicariate answered, saw the danger and moved to undo the damage. On 26 September 1706 the Congregation decided that the jurisdiction of the vicar apostolic must be maintained, that Angelo Francis, in other words, should not have yielded, and that Rome's man, not Lisbon's, was to govern the Serra. 15

In the same year the Serra lost the last of its native heads. On 20 March 1706 the archdeacon Mathew Pallil died of smallpox, asking the Carmelites with his last breath to mind the Christians of Malabar until Rome should answer. He had governed the vacant see for a decade and more, and the people moved at once to replace him in the old way: twice they assembled at Edapally to elect a successor from among his own kin, and twice the foreign archbishop set their choice aside, declaring the naming of an archdeacon his affair and not theirs. None was ever named. So the indigenous archdeaconate of the Pazhayakūr came quietly to its end, just as the quarrel over who should govern from above was at its height. The Catholic Thomas Christians would not be ruled by a man of their own race again for nearly two hundred years. 16

An Argument Without End

The Congregation’s decision in favour of the vicar apostolic, taken in that same year, settled the principle and almost nothing else. It had no immediate effect, and had to be confirmed all over again by a plenary vote on 25 June 1708. Even then the matter would not lie still. Apostolic Letters of 13 March and 4 May 1709 turned about and insisted on the rights of the Portuguese prelates, and on the vicar apostolic's duty to bring the dissident Syrians back to obedience, a duty that, given the temper of the Thomas Christians and the hostility of the Dutch Company, was very close to impossible. 17

The sources record this zig-zag more fully than they explain it: a vicariate affirmed in 1706, reaffirmed in 1708, then undercut in 1709. Tisserant offers only the single observation that Propaganda could not bring itself to abolish the Padroado, and that so compromising an attitude was bound to prolong the competition. That much is true, but the deeper problem was that Rome had bound two incompatible duties together. To govern the Serra effectively, it had to favour the vicar apostolic and slight the Padroado; only the Carmelites could function under Dutch conditions, and only they had any hope of winning the confidence of the Serra. But the Padroado still possessed an unrepealed juridical claim, and to honour that claim was to favour the Jesuit archbishop of Kodungallur-Angamaly and cripple the very vicariate through which Rome could actually govern. Law and government, right and necessity, had been set against each other.

Law Against Necessity

So Rome was caught between a juridical obligation it had not revoked and a pastoral duty it could not perform without violating that obligation. The instrument and the institution it was meant to replace were now made to limit each other, and Rome, unwilling to surrender either, oscillated between them. The famous restrictive formula, that the vicar apostolic should govern only the churches and places where the ordinaries could not exercise their own jurisdiction, was less a settlement than a confession that Rome meant to keep both and decide nothing. The result was not only a further hardening of the institutional division between the Puthankūr and the Pazhayakūr, but also a growing distrust and alienation between the Pazhayakūr and the Carmelites themselves. 18

Angelo Francis himself was worn out by the struggle. In January 1712 he set out, an old man, on a visitation of the scattered Christian communities, and kept at it until September of that year. The effort was more than he could bear, and he died of exhaustion on 17 October 1712, having spent his last strength tramping between the churches of a vicariate whose right to exist Rome was still arguing about. The brief appointing his successor, John Baptist Mary of St Teresa, carried the same crippling reservation as before: he was to have charge of the churches of Kodungallur and Cochin only where the ordinaries could not exercise their own jurisdiction. Propaganda could not bring itself to abolish the Padroado, and the compromise guaranteed that the competition would simply continue. 19

Two Hierarchies, One Flock

And continue it did. In 1721 Pope Innocent XIII ratified the nomination of two more Portuguese Jesuits, Antonio Pimentel to Kodungallur and Francisco de Vasconcellos to Cochin, so that the Padroado line ran on alongside the Carmelite one. None of these Portuguese prelates could freely and effectively govern the Thomas Christians as a whole; Vasconcellos would hold the see of Cochin for twenty years without ever once being able to live within his own diocese. The pattern set in 1701 had hardened into the permanent condition of the Catholic Thomas Christians: two rival hierarchies, a Roman and a Portuguese, claiming the same flock across a coast where a Calvinist company would let neither freely rule, while a third church, the Puthankūr, went its own separate way entirely. The consecration at Alangad had given the Catholics a bishop; it had not given them peace. Over the next eighty years, the double jurisdiction of the Carmelites and the Padroado would subject the Serra to a wearying, divided rule. The resentment of the indigenous Catholic clergy under foreign Carmelite vicars apostolic would steadily accumulate, waiting for a spark, until the cattanars of the Serra rose at length, in the Angamaly Padiyola of 1787, in open and organised protest against the Carmelite administration, a story for a later chapter. 20

The Other Serra

All this while, the other half of the divided community had been living through a quieter trouble of its own. The Puthankūr were governed by a hereditary succession of Mar Thoma metropolitans, in which the reigning prelate designated his successor from the old archidiaconal house, usually a close kinsman and often a nephew. The regularity of that succession from Mar Thoma I to IV, as we saw in the last chapter, is disputed; but on one episode there is no dispute. As early as 1720 Mar Thomas IV had written east to the Patriarch of Antioch, begging that bishops be sent to him, for the Antiochian delegates who had once served the Serra were now all dead and unreplaced. No bishop came in time. So when he neared his own end, with no delegate at hand to do it, Mar Thomas IV consecrated his chosen nephew, the future Mar Thomas V, with his own hand alone, before dying in 1728. What that act meant is read three ways. To the Pazhayakūr and later academic writers it is an irregular consecration beyond doubt. To the Malankara Jacobites, for whom valid orders flow from Antioch, it was a plain irregularity, forced by necessity and to be repaired as soon as a delegate could be had. To the Malankara Orthodox, who would one day repudiate Antiochene jurisdiction altogether, the same act reads the other way about, as the first truly independent consecration of the Indian Church, performed by its own metropolitan without a foreign hand. 21

Many Yet None

Into this same uncertainty there came, in 1705, another contestant in the Serra, another of those Eastern prelates of an obscure past. Mar Gabriel was a prelate of the Church of the East whose true allegiance is hard to fix, for he wore more than one face: he had made a profession of the Catholic faith at Rome, styled himself Metropolitan of Azerbaijan, and presented himself as a Catholic to the Catholics and as a dissident of the old Eastern stamp to the Puthankūr, so that the sources call him now Nestorian, now Chaldean, and Rome itself ended by disowning him as a pretender. Whatever his warrant, he reached Malabar and worked against both Rome and Antioch. The old pull of the East told at once: he gathered a following reported at around twenty parishes from both sides and held it for more than twenty years, settling at last at Kottayam, where he died about 1730 and was buried. He left a more durable trace than his brief party, for it was on his behalf that there was composed, about 1720, the Malayalam church history, soon turned into Syriac, that survives among the manuscripts of the University of Leiden, a partisan document pleading the East Syrian cause and appealing to the Dutch authorities for protection, which recent scholarship has made a principal source for the self-understanding of the Malabar Christians in this period. It makes a curious pair with Mar Thomas IV’s own letters of these very years, for the two rivals were lobbying the same Dutch power through the same Leiden orientalists at the same moment, the one to keep the Antiochian connection, the other to be rid of it. His career showed once more what the Carmelites and the Mar Thoma metropolitans alike had reason to fear, that the people of the Serra, of either obedience, would still rally for both an independent rule and a legitimate apostolicity. 22

Such was the Serra as the eighteenth century opened: both its halves, the Pazhayakūr who held to Rome and the Puthankūr who looked to Antioch, were ill at ease under the structures that governed them. The pathos of their division lay in its symmetry: the Pazhayakūr had apostolicity in plenty, the unbroken Roman and Padroado orders, but no bishop of their own blood; the Puthankūr had a metropolitan of their own blood, the indigenous Mar Thoma, but wanted an apostolicity others would recognise. Each lacked exactly what the other possessed. Out of this double want, in the two generations that were to follow, would come both a revolt and a split: on the one side the native clergy of the Pazhayakūr would rise for self-government, and on the other the Puthankūr would tear once more along the fault of episcopal orders. The revolt and the split are the matter of the chapter that follows. 23

Podipara, 1971), the foundational native narrative of the Pazhayakūr cause, and it informs the grievances voiced in the Angamaly Padiyola of 1787. The contemporary European sources do not corroborate the charge, and it is reported here as the claim of the indigenous tradition rather than as established fact.

On the Dutch East India Company's conquest of Cochin in 1663 and its administration of the Malabar coast thereafter: from 1669 Dutch Malabar was constituted a separate commandery of the Company seated at Cochin, though still within the wider VOC system centred on Batavia; the Raja of Cochin was reduced to a client and the lesser rulers bound by monopoly treaties for pepper. The standard account is in K. M. Panikkar, Malabar and the Dutch, and in the older translated records (A. Galletti, A. J. van der Burg and P. Groot, The Dutch in Malabar, Madras, 1911); for the present period see also Tisserant, 92–93, on the conditions under which the Carmelite mission worked. The pepper trade, the "black gold" of the European companies, was the whole economic rationale of the Dutch presence.

That the Dutch held the forts and the coast but never the whole pepper trade, and that the interior remained under Hindu rulers (the Zamorin of Calicut and a number of petty rajas), is the consistent picture of the modern Malabar historiography; the Company secured monopoly contracts but not sovereignty over the producing hinterland. On the Jesuits' withdrawal inland to Ambazhakkad after the loss of Cochin, where the Vaipicotta seminary had been moved in 1662 and the scholasticate of the Malabar province established in 1667: Tisserant, 89–90 nn.; Thekkedath, vol. II, 101, 131. On the archbishops of Kodungallur residing at Puttenchira from Ribeiro's time: Tisserant, 91 n. 2. The observation that the Catholic Thomas Christians were the ecclesiastical subjects of European powers physically unable to reach them is the author's.

On the religious policy of the Dutch Company, which excluded Portuguese and Padroado clergy as political agents of a rival crown while tolerating non-Portuguese Carmelites: the agreement of April 1698, negotiated through the Emperor Leopold I, admitted to Malabar only Carmelites who were Belgians, Germans, or Italians (see Chapter 13 above, note 43; Thekkedath, vol. II, 107). Tisserant, 92–93, records that the Dutch authorities allowed no more than twelve Carmelite missionaries in the mission at one time, and that the number was usually lower. That the Dutch, as Calvinists, made no attempt to impose the Reformed faith on Malabar and acted from commercial rather than confessional motives is the general judgement of the historiography; the inference that the vicariate survived partly on Dutch sufferance, as a Catholic establishment dependent on Dutch goodwill rather than on a rival crown, is the author's.

On the English East India Company's settlement at Anjengo (Anchuthengu) in the territory of the Rani of Attingal: a site was granted in 1694, fortification began in 1695, and the fort was completed in 1699; it was the Company's first permanent post on the Malabar coast, a pepper factory in Travancore country. See the standard local histories and the Madras records. The point that this distant southern factory was of no consequence to the Thomas Christians in this period, but was the seed of the British power to which the Dutch surrendered their Malabar settlements in 1795, is the author's framing and looks ahead to a later volume. The decisive blow to Dutch power on the coast fell well after the close of this chapter, at the Battle of Colachel on 10 August 1741, where Marthanda Varma of Travancore routed a Dutch East India Company force and took its commander Eustachius de Lannoy prisoner; the English role there was commercial and material rather than military, an earlier pepper treaty with Travancore having helped break the Dutch monopoly, and Travancore's artillery including English-supplied guns, but the English were not a party to the battle. Colachel and the Dutch decline belong to a later chapter; they are noted here only to mark that the British ascendancy gestured at above was still two generations off.

On the contrast between 1663 and 1701: that the Padroado offered no protest when Sebastiani consecrated Parambil Chandy vicar apostolic in 1663 is explained by the collapse of Portuguese power on the coast in that very year (the fall of Cochin, the death of Archbishop Garcia in 1659, the expulsion of the Padroado clergy), leaving no prelate present to object; whereas by 1700–01 Portugal had resumed appointing to the Eastern sees (Goa, where Agostinho da Anunciação took up residence in September 1691; Cochin, to which Pedro Pacheco OP was appointed on 4 January 1694; and Kodungallur in December 1701), so that a Padroado hierarchy was again in place to assert the king's rights. On the three Portuguese bishops then in India, Augustine of the Annunciation (archbishop of Goa since 1691), Peter Pacheco (resident bishop of Cochin since 1699), and the Jesuit John Ribeiro (archbishop of Cranganore from December 1701), and their joint protest against Angelo Francis's appointment as an infringement of the rights of the king of Portugal under the Padroado: Neill I, 330. The dates of the see of Cochin are easily confused: Pacheco was appointed bishop in 1693–94 (Tisserant, 89 n. 1 and 200, "Peter Pacheco O.P., 1693–1714"; Thekkedath, vol. II, 131, "In 1694 Peter Pacheco"; so too the standard episcopal lists, which give 4 January 1694), but according to Neill, 330, he became resident in the Dutch-free southern part of his diocese only from 1699. Tisserant, 89 n. 1, adds that Cochin had been administered by ecclesiastical governors until 1693. The point for the argument is that, by contrast with 1663, a Padroado episcopate was again in place by 1701. On the partial reconstitution of the Padroado sees, see Chapter 13 above, note 43.

On the nature of the Padroado as the Portuguese crown's right of nomination to the Eastern sees, and on the appointment of the Jesuit John Ribeiro as Archbishop of Kodungallur on 5 December 1701 in response to the direct papal appointment of a vicar apostolic: Tisserant, 90–91; Neill I, 330–31. Ribeiro held the see 1701–16 (Tisserant, 91 n. 2). On the Latin see of Angamaly, erected for the Thomas Christians after the Synod of Diamper and held by Francis Roz (consecrated bishop by Menezes at Goa, 25 January 1601), being transferred to Kodungallur (Cranganore) and raised to metropolitan (archiepiscopal) rank in 1608: Neill I, 311; on Roz as bishop of Angamaly and then archbishop of Kodungallur, Neill I, 310–311. The see is styled Kodungallur-Angamaly hereafter to mark this identity. On the orders of the contemporary Padroado prelates: Pacheco of Cochin was a Dominican (Tisserant, 190–91, "Peter Pacheco o.p."; Thekkedath, vol. II, 131) and Agostinho da Anunciação of Goa a religious of the Order of Christ (catholic-hierarchy.org, Archdiocese of Goa); the see of Kodungallur, by contrast, remained the Jesuit chair, held by Ribeiro and after him by Pimentel S.J. (Tisserant, 188).

On Ribeiro forcing his way into the territory of Kodungallur in 1704 against the opposition of the Dutch and part of the clergy: Tisserant, 91; Neill I, 331. On his being the first Jesuit prelate to reside in Malabar since Archbishop Garcia's death on 3 September 1659, the intervening Portuguese appointees having refused the see or been unable to reach it: Tisserant, 91 n. 2. From Ribeiro's time the archbishops of Kodungallur resided at Puttenchira (Tisserant, 91 n. 2).

On Ernst Hanxleden (1681–1732), the German Jesuit who came as a companion of Archbishop Ribeiro, laboured over thirty years in Malabar, and won repute as a scholar of Sanskrit and Malayalam whose Malayalam religious poems long remained in popular use: Tisserant, 91 n. 2. Hanxleden, known as Arnos Pathiri, is also remembered for pioneering work on Malayalam and Sanskrit grammar and lexicography, though the present sources note only his poetry and learning.

The reversal of the Society's position by the Restoration of 1640 is the author's reading, drawing together the pre-1640 alignment traced in Chapter 11 (where, under the Habsburg union, Rome and the crown in Madrid kept and defended the Jesuit missions against the Portuguese establishment) and the post-1640 situation, in which an independent Portugal under the house of Braganza wielded the Padroado as its own prerogative. On the 1640 Restoration and the end of the sixty-year union with the Spanish crown, see Chapter 13 above, and Boxer, The Church Militant and Iberian Expansion, 1440–1770, 83–85. On Ribeiro as a Portuguese Jesuit appointed by the Portuguese crown to the Padroado see of Kodungallur, the first Jesuit prelate resident in Malabar since Archbishop Garcia: Tisserant, 91 and 91 n. 2; Neill I, 331. The point is not that the Society altered its loyalties but that the crown to which the Malabar Padroado Jesuits answered had, in 1640, passed from Habsburg to Braganza hands.

On the assembly of cattanars at Kaduthuruthy on 20 June 1704 and their oath to remain independent of Goa and to obey only the Carmelite vicar apostolic until the pope should hear their appeal, "since from that order they had received the truest help in all their necessities": Neill I, 331; Tisserant, 91. On the standing refusal to submit to a Jesuit bishop, the legacy of the Coonan Cross oath of 1653, see Chapter 12 above.

On Angelo Francis's decision to submit to the limiting clause of his brief once Ribeiro had taken possession of Kodungallur, though he knew the Dutch and the Syrians would support him, his circular of 29 June 1704, and his withdrawal to the monastery of Verapoly: Tisserant, 91.

On the danger that Catholic division favoured the party of the Mar Thoma line, whose head had by this time become a Jacobite, and on the decision of Propaganda on 26 September 1706 that the jurisdiction of the vicar apostolic must be maintained: Tisserant, 91–92.

On the death of Archdeacon Mathew Pallil and the extinction of the office, the fullest account is J. Kollaparambil, The Archdeacon of All-India (Rome, 1972), 218–20, from the Propaganda archives: Mathew died of smallpox on 20 March 1706, asking the Carmelites to assist the Christians of Malabar until the reply of the Holy See should come; the faithful twice assembled at Edapally to elect a successor from among his nephews, but the archbishop set the election aside as convoked without his authority, and no further archdeacon was ever appointed, so that, in Kollaparambil’s words, "the Malabar Catholics had no more the direction of an Archdeacon after the death of Archdeacon Mathew." The reading of this as the quiet extinction of the indigenous headship, not resumed until the native vicars apostolic of 1896 (note 23 below), is the author’s.

On the 1706 decision having no immediate effect and requiring confirmation by a plenary session of the Congregation on 25 June 1708; and on the Apostolic Letters of 13 March and 4 May 1709 insisting on the rights of the Portuguese prelates and on the vicar apostolic's duty to recover the dissidents, a task rendered impossible by the attitude of the Thomas Christians and of the Dutch Company: Tisserant, 92.

The interpretation here, that the oscillation of 1706–09 reflects two incompatible duties Rome would not choose between, a juridical obligation to the unrevoked Padroado patronage of Portugal and a pastoral duty to govern the Serra effectively (which under Dutch conditions only the Carmelite vicar apostolic could discharge), bound up with the further and equally intractable aim of reconciling the dissident Syrians, is the author's reading of the sequence of decisions recorded in Tisserant, 91–92: the Propaganda decision of 26 September 1706 that the vicar apostolic's jurisdiction must be maintained, its reconfirmation in plenary session on 25 June 1708, and the Apostolic Letters of 13 March and 4 May 1709 insisting on the rights of the Portuguese prelates and on the vicar apostolic's duty to recover the dissidents. On the impossibility of abolishing the Padroado and the resulting prolongation of the conflict, Tisserant, 92. On the original character of the vicariate of Malabar as a Propaganda jurisdiction distinct from, and in practice substituting for, the unsuppressed Padroado see of Kodungallur, see Chapter 13 above, note 22.

On Angelo Francis's visitation from January to September 1712 and his death from exhaustion on 17 October 1712; and on the restrictive clause in the brief appointing his successor John Baptist Mary of St Teresa (Morteo), limiting his charge to the churches of Kodungallur and Cochin "where the ordinaries cannot fully exercise their respective jurisdiction": Tisserant, 92.

On the ratification by Innocent XIII in 1721 of the Portuguese Jesuits Antonio Pimentel (Kodungallur, 1721–52) and Francisco de Vasconcellos (Cochin, 1722–42): Tisserant, 92. On Vasconcellos holding the see of Cochin for twenty years "without ever being able to reside within the limits of his diocese": Neill II, 59. On the resulting permanent condition of two rival Catholic hierarchies over one flock, with the Puthankūr separate, see the chapters following. On the Angamaly Padiyola of 1787, the indigenous Catholic clergy's organised protest against the double jurisdiction and Carmelite rule: Tisserant, 97; the episode is taken up in a later chapter. Native Catholic tradition numbered the deportation and death in Pondicherry of the Chaldean bishop Mar Simon among its long-standing grievances against the Carmelites, a memory of the event as an act of foreign ecclesiastical high-handedness; see note 4 above.

On the Mar Thoma succession and the three ways it is read. The reigning metropolitan designated his successor, usually a nephew; the dispute concerns the consecration. For the consecration of Mar Thomas III and IV the Malankara tradition, Jacobite and Orthodox alike, holds that a legitimate Antiochian delegate officiated: E. M. Philip, The Indian Church of St Thomas, ch. xvii, names the West Syrian bishops Mar Baselios (a Maphrian, who died thirteen days after landing) and Mar Ivanios, deputed by Patriarch Ignatius Abdal Messiah I, arriving in 1685, and says Ivanios consecrated Mar Thomas III in 1685 and Mar Thomas IV in 1688. Neill II, 65, reports the same tradition, naming the resident bishop "John" (1685–97), but judges it "quite uncertain" and, given the anxiety Mar Thomas IV later showed about his own standing, "extremely improbable." On the consecration of Mar Thomas V there is no such dispute of fact: all accounts agree that Mar Thomas IV, having designated his nephew but having no delegate to hand, the Antiochian bishops being dead and unreplaced, consecrated him single-handed before dying on 13 March 1728 (Philip, ch. xvii; Neill II, 65–66, who notes that "by what form of consecration the succession was transmitted the authorities do not reveal"). What is disputed is its meaning: the Catholic and academic writers count it one more uncertain consecration; the Jacobites, holding valid orders to flow from Antioch, treat it as an irregularity later to be cured (whence Mar Thomas IV’s appeal of 1720 and Mar Thomas V’s own doubt, on which see note 23 below); while the Orthodox, who since 1912 repudiate Antiochene jurisdiction, remember it as the first genuinely independent consecration of the Indian Church, performed without a foreign hand. The fuller setting-out of these positions is at Chapter 13 above, note 28, and the reservation entered there holds here too; this chapter adjudicates none of them.

On Mar Gabriel, the East Syrian bishop whose allegiance the sources dispute. By the East Syrian church history written in his interest he was sent by the Catholicos of the East, the traditionalist patriarch of the Alqosh line (Tisserant’s "Elias XI," 1700–22) seated at the monastery of Rabban Hormizd near Mosul, which stood outside the Roman communion; yet Gabriel had submitted a profession of faith to Propaganda at Rome in 1704 and in India presented himself as Chaldean Metropolitan of Azerbaijan and as a Catholic, while functioning among the Syrians as a dissident, so that the literature calls him now Nestorian, now Chaldean. Cardinal Sagribanti, Prefect of Propaganda, disowned him as a pretender (Perczel; Joy). He is in any case to be distinguished from the Catholic Chaldean patriarchate at Diarbekir from which Mar Simon had come (see note 3 above): he reached the coast in 1705 (so Thekkedath, vol. II, 104, citing Malancharuvil, and Perczel’s corrected dating; the date 1708, given by Neill and by Perczel’s own earlier work, is now regarded as erroneous), was rejected by both the Catholics and the Mar Thoma party, gathered a following reported at around twenty parishes, and died at Kottayam about 1730 (the death date is variously given as 1730, 1731, and 1733), being buried at the Cheriapally there. See Tisserant, 91 and 92–96; Neill I, on Gabriel; and the official history of the Malankara Orthodox Syrian Church (mosc.in, "Connection with the Syrian Churches"), which records that with Gabriel's death without a successor the connection of the Indian church with the East Syrian Church came to an end. Both Catholic and Malankara historians treat Gabriel as an intruder; he is significant here as the last East Syrian prelate to contest Malabar in person. On the church history written on Gabriel’s behalf: a narrative composed in Malayalam (Leiden Or. 1214) and translated into Syriac (Or. 1213) by the priest Mattai, now among the Syriac manuscripts of the University of Leiden, of which a Latin translation was published by J. P. N. Land, Anecdota Syriaca, vol. I (Leiden, 1862), 123–27, and the Syriac by S. Giamil (Rome, 1902). The dating has been revised by recent work: Tisserant, 9 and n. 2, who notices the manuscript and Gabriel’s part in securing it, gives its date as about 1770, but the specialist study of both versions by I. Perczel (with G. Kurukkoor, "A Malayalam Church History from the Eighteenth Century") and by Emy Merin Joy, Christian Manuscripts of Kerala (India): Revisiting (MA thesis, Central European University, 2019), places its composition about 1720, in Gabriel’s own lifetime and in his interest, the Syriac version being directed to the Leiden orientalist Johann Heinrich Schaaf (son of the orientalist Karl Schaaf, d. 1729). This church history (Leiden) is to be distinguished from Mar Thomas IV’s contemporary letters to Antioch and to the Dutch: the elder Karl Schaaf corresponded with Mar Thomas IV of the Jacobite party and published his letter of 1709, while the Gabriel history was addressed to the son, Johann Heinrich; the two parties were thus appealing, through the same Leiden channel, to opposite ends, Mar Thomas IV to secure Antiochian bishops and Gabriel to exclude them. The 1720 letter quoted in note 23 below is Mar Thomas IV’s, preserved by Assemani at Rome, not the Leiden manuscript. The text is frankly partisan: it pleads the East Syrian cause, appeals to the Dutch authorities to protect the Syrian Christians against the Portuguese, and seeks to set up a third party between Puthankūr and Pazhayakūr on the ground that both had taken up the Latin discipline of clerical celibacy.

The antithesis drawn here, that the Pazhayakūr had apostolic orders in abundance but no native bishop, while the Puthankūr had a native bishop but lacked assured apostolic orders, is the author’s, as is the forward glance to the two settlements of the next two generations: the Angamaly Padiyola of 1787, the organised demand of the Syrian Catholic clergy for native governance (the “spark”), and the Thozhiyur secession of 1772 (the “split”), both treated in the chapter following. On the temper of the Puthankūr’s want, Mar Thomas IV’s appeal of 1720 (note 21 above) is eloquent: writing to the Patriarch of Antioch that since the deaths of the foreign bishops his church had drifted “like a ship without a rudder,” he begged for “one Patriarch and one Metropolitan and twin priests, who may be philosophers,” and signed himself “the Gate of all India” (the letter, preserved by Assemani, Bibliotheca Orientalis, IV, 466, is translated in Neill II, 65, and in E. M. Philip, The Indian Church of St Thomas, ch. xviii). On Parambil Chandy (Alexander de Campo) as the last bishop of Thomas Christian race on the Catholic side for two centuries: after him the Pazhayakūr were governed by foreign Carmelite vicars apostolic of Verapoly. The Syrians were separated from Latin jurisdiction only in 1887 (Leo XIII, Quod Iam Pridem, 20 May 1887), and at first under European prelates; it was by the brief Quae Rei Sacrae of 28 July 1896 that Leo XIII, responding to the Syrian Catholics’ petitions for bishops of their own race and rite, at last appointed native vicars apostolic, Mar Aloysius (Louis) Pareparambil for Ernakulam, Mar John Menacherry for Trichur, and Mar Mathew Makil for Changanacherry, the three being consecrated at Kandy on 25 October 1896 (Tisserant, on the restoration of the Syro-Malabar hierarchy; and the standard accounts). The observation that this loss was the more lasting of the two wants is the author’s.

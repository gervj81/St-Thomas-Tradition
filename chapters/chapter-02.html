THE APOSTOLIC IMPRINT: TEXT, MEMORY, AND GEOGRAPHY

1st Century CE – 6th Century CE

"Thomas, as tradition relates, obtained Parthia by lot."

— Eusebius of Caesarea, Historia Ecclesiastica, c. 313 CE

The Primary Documentary Tradition: Key Takeaways
The Continuous Chain
A documentary tradition spanning four centuries, from Origen's 'Parthia' (c. 230 CE) to Cosmas's eyewitness reports of a living Indian church in the 6th century.
The Invariant Nucleus
The structural core elements — an apostolic mission, a martyrdom, a physical tomb, and a later relic translation back to Edessa — rest consistently across the earliest witnesses.
Anchored Evidence
The tradition is anchored at one structural end by archaeological confirmation of King Gondophares, and at the other end by independent reports of an established Christian community.
Historical Weight
While not absolute proof of origins, it constitutes a body of evidence too wide, too early, and too consistent to simply be dismissed as a later fabrication.

The previous chapter established that the world of the 1st century CE was organised in a manner that made a journey to India not merely possible but, for a well-connected missionary moving through the Aramaic-speaking Syriac east, operationally straightforward. This chapter asks the next question: what do the earliest documentary traditions say about such a journey, and what is the evidential weight of those traditions?

There is a tendency among scholars to divide the Thomas tradition into two geographic claims: a northwest Indian mission, centred on the historical kingdom of Gondophares in the Taxila/Gandhara region of modern Pakistan; and a south Indian mission, associated with both the Malabar coast and the martyrdom at Mylapore on the Coromandel coast. These two claims have sometimes been treated as contradictory — as competing traditions that cannot both be true. In light of Chapter 1, it is already clear that such a framing is a form of tunnel vision, born of a tendency to see India as two mutually exclusive sealed halves, north and south, when the 1st-century world observed no such boundary. The Indo-Parthian northwest, the pepper coast of Malabar, and the pearl harbours of the Coromandel — though served by different sets of merchants and navigators — were nodes on the same elaborate and interconnected commercial system; the overland Silk Road corridor, the Persian Gulf maritime route, and the monsoon crossing of the Arabian Sea were not hermetically sealed alternatives but complementary arteries of a single world. The question is not which tradition is correct. The question is whether the evidence is better read as layers of a single complex history than as competing claims.

This chapter confines itself to what may be called the primary documentary tradition: written sources from the patristic period, the Acts of Thomas as a literary text, and the travel report of Cosmas Indicopleustes. The community traditions of Kerala — the folk songs, the seven churches, the Brahmin conversions, the Knanaya migration — are a different kind of evidence, later in documentation and shaped by different forces. They are the subject of later chapters. The distinction between these two layers is not a judgement about their relative value. It is a methodological discipline: the two types of evidence must be assessed separately before they can be read together.

2.1 — THE PARTHIAN TRADITION, THE AJT, AND THE EDESSAN RELIC CULT

The earliest stratum of the Thomas tradition — as preserved in Origen (c. 230 CE) and Eusebius (c. 313 CE) — does not place Thomas in India. It places him in Parthia. 'Thomas obtained Parthia by lot' is Eusebius's formulation. Origen, writing a generation earlier, is the first to specify Parthia as Thomas's missionary territory. The northwest claim is therefore the older layer of the tradition, and it has behind it the commercial and geographic infrastructure that Chapter 1 documented.1

There are two second-hand witness reports to a Christian presence in what the ancient world called 'India' that have sometimes been cited as independent witnesses to the tradition.

The first is Pantaenus, the Alexandrian philosopher and teacher, who visited 'India' around 189–190 CE and found Christians already there in possession of the Gospel of Matthew — an account transmitted by Eusebius and Jerome. But 'India' in late-antique usage was an elastic term: it could designate any region from the Arabian Peninsula to the actual subcontinent. A parallel transmission specifies Pantaenus's destination as 'nearer India,' which in the geographic conventions of the period may designate Aksumite Ethiopia or the Red Sea littoral. If so, the earliest supposed witness to Christianity in 'India' may not be about India at all. Moreover, the Christians Pantaenus found attributed their faith to Bartholomew, not to Thomas. The Pantaenus episode's relevance to St Thomas and Christians in India is disputed.2

The second is Theophilus, known as 'the Indian,' an Arian bishop sent by Emperor Constantius II around 354 CE, whose travels are narrated by the church historian Philostorgius. Theophilus built churches for Roman merchants in south Arabia, then visited his native island — variously identified as Socotra, the Maldives, or an island at the mouth of the Indus — before proceeding to 'the other districts of India,' where he found Christians already present and corrected their liturgical practices. These Christians, Philostorgius reports, professed their theological position 'from the earliest antiquity' and required correction only on matters of liturgical custom, not doctrine — a detail implying a community of some age. But the identification of Theophilus's 'India' remains disputed. His itinerary may describe the extended Red Sea and Arabian littoral rather than the subcontinent. Both Pantaenus and Theophilus, whatever the merits of their respective cases, were operating within the Roman Egyptian commercial world — the maritime network running from Alexandria through the Red Sea to the Indian Ocean. Neither belongs to the Syriac-Parthian network from which the Thomas tradition itself originates.3

What matters for the Thomas tradition is Origen's specification of Parthia — not 'India' in the elastic ancient sense, but the Parthian Empire specifically. This does not suffer from the geographic ambiguity that attaches to the Pantaenus and Theophilus references. Eusebius, drawing on Origen and possibly other sources, repeats the claim. Ephrem the Syrian (c. 350–373 CE), writing in Edessa — the very city that claimed possession of Thomas's relics — celebrated Thomas in his hymns: the bones of St Thomas, Ephrem sings, were brought to Edessa from India by a merchant, and the Devil laments that the apostle he murdered in India 'has preceded me in Edessa.' Ephrem's use of 'India' is grounded in Edessa's St Thomas relic cult and its status as the theological home of the Thomas tradition. He is recording the living institutional memory of the city that received the apostle's bones.4

The Acts of Thomas — Text, Genre, and Objections

The Acts of Thomas (Acta Thomae, or Acts of Judas Thomas — AJT) is a lengthy Syriac and Greek text composed in Edessa around 220–240 CE. The narrative is structured as a travel romance: Thomas, assigned India by apostolic lot, is sold by Jesus to an Indian merchant named Abbanes and travels to the court of King Gondophares in the Indo-Parthian northwest, where he is commissioned to build a palace. Instead, Thomas distributes the king's money to the poor, declaring that he has built a palace in heaven. After a series of miracles, conversions, and hymns — including the celebrated Hymn of the Pearl — Thomas moves to the court of a second king, Misdaeus, in a different part of India, where he is eventually martyred by spearing. The text concludes with the translation of his relics.5

The AJT is the primary literary text that places Thomas in India with geographic specificity, naming Gondophares and situating the mission in the northwest. It belongs to the genre of apocryphal Acts — a form of early Christian literature combining theological allegory, miracle narratives, hymns, and travel adventure, in the same genre-family as the Acts of Paul, Acts of Peter, and Acts of John. These texts are not history; they are not intended as history; and their authors did not think of them as history.

The AJT has specific theological characteristics that mark it as a product of a particular strand of early Syriac Christianity. It is strongly encratite — advocating celibacy and the renunciation of marriage as the highest Christian virtue, reflecting a specifically Edessan ascetic tendency. It also contains the Hymn of the Pearl, one of the most striking texts in early Christian literature: a mythological allegory of the soul's descent into matter and return to the divine realm. The objections are familiar: the text is a 3rd-century composition, not a 1st-century record; it reflects the theological preoccupations of Edessa in 220 CE rather than historical reality in Judaea in 52 CE; and its narrative framework is Gnostic rather than apostolic. But this confuses two distinct questions: the theological character of a text's compiler and the historical value of the tradition that compiler inherited.

The Christianity that developed in Edessa and the surrounding region in the 2nd–3rd century had a distinctive regional character: more ascetic than western Christianity, more comfortable with speculative cosmic mythology, more marked by dualist tendencies inherited from the Zoroastrian and Jewish-apocalyptic environment of Mesopotamia. This regional colouring does not mean that the underlying traditions the Edessan community preserved were themselves Gnostic in origin. It means that the community through which those traditions passed left its theological imprint on them — just as the Alexandrian intellectual tradition left its imprint on Origen, and the Latin rhetorical tradition left its imprint on Augustine.6

▣ BARDAISAN AND THE LIMITS OF HERESIOLOGICAL LABELS

Bardaisan of Edessa (154–222 CE) illustrates the point sharply. The Church Fathers classified him as a Valentinian Gnostic: Hippolytus, Epiphanius, and Ephrem all attacked him in these terms. Yet Bardaisan himself wrote actively against Valentinus and Marcion — the very figures his critics used to classify him. The Valentinian label was a polemical verdict imposed by adversaries, not a self-description.

Modern scholarship has largely concluded that the patristic critique is overstated. Bardaisan's speculative tendencies derived as plausibly from his Edessan intellectual environment as from any specific Gnostic school. Ephrem, having spent his life attacking Bardaisan's hymns, nevertheless adopted Bardaisan's own poetic metres and musical settings — composing orthodox hymns to the same tunes to replace Bardaisan's words in the mouths of the Edessan congregation.

The relevance to the AJT is direct. The Hymn of the Pearl, the most distinctively Gnostic text within the AJT, almost certainly originates in the Bardaisanite circle. The same Edessan environment that produced Bardaisan's speculative theology also preserved the institutional memory of Thomas as the apostle of the east. The theological overlay and the historical tradition are separable.7

The Addai Tradition and the Relocation of Thomas

There is, however, a further objection that goes beyond the AJT's theology — one that concerns the institutional context that produced it. Recent scholarship has reconstructed a hypothetical earlier text — a 'Parthian Acts of Thomas,' composed perhaps c. 150–200 CE — that is proposed to have been the literary vehicle of the older Parthian Thomas tradition. The surviving AJT, on this reading, is a deliberate redaction of this earlier text, shifting Thomas's mission from Parthia to India to serve Edessa's own ecclesiastical interests. The hypothesis has been developed by several scholars and is coherent as a model of Edessan literary production. But the text itself is unattested: no manuscript survives, no fragment has been identified, and no ancient author quotes it by name. Its existence is inferred indirectly, and whether the underlying tradition circulated as a formal literary 'Acts' or as a more diffuse body of oral and epistolary tradition cannot be determined on present evidence.8

What motivated the proposed relocation? By the mid-to-late third century CE, Edessene Christians had begun to champion a local apostolic tradition centred on a figure named Addai and his disciples Aggai and Mari. Addai — presented as one of Jesus's seventy disciples, dispatched to Edessa by the Apostle Thomas after the Ascension — was credited with healing King Abgar, converting the city, and founding the church under royal patronage. His disciple Aggai was assigned the evangelisation of the wider Sasanian east; Mari, another disciple, was credited with carrying Christianity through lowland Mesopotamia to the Persian Gulf. Between them, the three figures accounted for the Christianisation of the entire Syriac-speaking world from Edessa to the Gulf. But accounts of Thomas's own Parthian ministry were problematic for Edessenes who valued this tradition and its implications for Edessa's religious primacy — and so, the argument runs, a redactor moved Thomas to India.8a

The chronology adds to the complication. The Thomas tradition is the older of the two. The Parthian Thomas tradition was in circulation by c. 150–200 CE, early enough to reach Origen in Alexandria by c. 225. The Addai tradition, by contrast, enters the documentary record only in the late third century; its earliest attestation is Eusebius (c. 315 CE), who claims to have obtained the Abgar-Jesus correspondence from Edessa's archives. Foundational early Syriac authors — Bardaisan, Aphrahat, Ephrem — make no mention of Addai or of Abgar's conversion. The text Teaching of Addai in its extant form is a 5th-century composition, and the Jesus-Abgar correspondence it contains is an acknowledged literary fabrication. The Addai narrative did not precede the Thomas tradition; it is a roughly contemporary Edessene construction, developed alongside the Indian AJT as part of the same third-century project of ecclesiastical identity-formation.8b

What that project achieved was a territorial partition. The early 4th-century Syriac text, the Teaching of the Apostles (c. 325–350 CE), codifies the result: Thomas preached in India and sent letters back to Edessa; Addai converted Edessa and Mesopotamia; and Aggai evangelized Assyria, Armenia, Media, and regions further east. Within this framework, Thomas is explicitly the senior apostolic authority—it is Thomas who dispatches Addai—but his own mission field is moved beyond Addai's territorial claim. The surviving Acts of Judas Thomas is the literary product of this partition. According to this reading, the AJT did not merely inherit the apostle-king-patronage narrative form that recurs across the early Christian world; rather, it was composed to serve a specific institutional purpose: relocating Thomas from Parthia to India to secure Edessa's exclusive apostolic primacy over Mesopotamia and the Parthian East.8c

▣ ADDAI, AGGAI, AND MARI: THE APOSTOLIC CHAIN OF EDESSA AND MESOPOTAMIA

Three figures dominate the Edessene apostolic tradition, and their names recur throughout the history of the Church of the East. They are easily confused but occupy distinct positions in the tradition's architecture.

Addai (known in Greek as Thaddaeus, sometimes conflated with the apostle of that name in Luke's Gospel) is presented as one of the seventy disciples sent by Jesus. According to the Edessene tradition, it was the Apostle Thomas who dispatched Addai to Edessa after the Ascension to heal King Abgar and establish the church there. Addai is thus the founder-figure of Edessan Christianity — not one of the Twelve, but commissioned by one of them. His name may echo that of a historical Manichaean missionary also called Addai/Adda who was active in Parthian Mesopotamia in the 260s CE, though the relationship between the two figures remains debated.

Aggai is Addai's immediate successor at Edessa — an Edessan native, traditionally described as a silk-worker for the king, whom Addai appointed before his death. According to the Teaching of the Apostles (c. 325–350 CE), Aggai's mission extended well beyond Edessa: he is credited with evangelising Assyria, Armenia, Media, Khuzistan, and territories further east across the Sasanian world. He was eventually martyred by one of Abgar's rebellious pagan sons. Aggai is not Mari; the two are distinct figures with different missions and different hagiographic traditions.

Mari is a separate figure, traditionally a disciple of Addai (not of Aggai). His story is told in the Acts of Mār Mārī (6th–7th century), which traces his mission from Edessa through the lowland Sasanian territories — Seleucia-Ctesiphon, southern Mesopotamia, the Persian Gulf littoral — the same corridor through which, as Chapter 1 established, Christianity may have reached the Indian coast.

The three form a chain: Thomas sends Addai, Addai appoints Aggai as his successor and trains Mari as his disciple, and between them they account for the evangelisation of the entire Syriac-speaking world from Edessa to the Persian Gulf. The chain is a literary construction — the Teaching of Addai is an acknowledged fabrication, and the Acts of Mār Mārī dates to six centuries after the events it describes. Yet the liturgical tradition that bears their names is among the oldest in Christendom. The Anaphora of Addai and Mari — the eucharistic prayer at the heart of the East Syriac Qurbana — has been in continuous use since at least the third century. Its archaic structure, which lacks an explicit institution narrative, led Rome in 2001 to formally recognise its validity — an extraordinary acknowledgment of a liturgical form that predates the standardisation of Western eucharistic prayers. The Anaphora is used to this day by the Assyrian Church of the East, the Chaldean Catholic Church, and — in a form adapted after the Portuguese encounter — by the Syro-Malabar Church of Kerala.8d

The case illustrates a principle that bears directly on the Thomas tradition. The Teaching of Addai is fabricated history. The Anaphora of Addai and Mari is authentic liturgy. The two coexist within the same tradition without contradiction. A tradition can carry fabricated narrative elements and genuine devotional content simultaneously. The historian's task is to distinguish between them — not to assume that the presence of the one invalidates the other.

These objections, taken together, are serious. The AJT alone does not prove that Thomas went to India. But pressed beyond their proper weight, they prove too much — and it is the archaeological evidence that checks them.

The Archaeological Discovery That Changed the Probability

In 1848, coins recovered from across the Punjab, Taxila, and Gandharan regions were identified as bearing a name — in variant spellings across their Greek obverses and Kharosthi reverses — that matched precisely the name of the king in the AJT: Gondophares, Gondaphares, Undophares (Greek); Gudaphara, Gadaphara (Kharosthi). In 1864–1872, a stone inscription discovered at Takht-i-Bahi in the Peshawar district — a Kharosthi text mentioning 'the great king Guduphara' — corroborated the coins. Numismatic analysis established Gondophares's reign as c. 21–51 CE, placing him precisely in the apostolic period. Archaeological excavation of ancient Taxila at Sirkap (beginning 1912, in modern Pakistan) confirmed the city as the headquarters of the Indo-Parthian kings in the 1st century CE.9

The probability that an Edessan author of 220–240 CE, writing centuries after the events described, independently possessed accurate knowledge of a specific Indo-Parthian ruler and placed him correctly within the geography and chronology of the narrative purely by accident is very low. Whatever the AJT is, it knew something real about northwest India. The coins did not validate the AJT's miracles. But they validated its geographic and political memory.

The Gondophares discovery returns us to the Addai objection. The Teaching of Addai — the acknowledged fabrication — has no comparable external confirmation. No archaeological or epigraphic evidence corroborates its historical claims. The AJT, it has been argued along similar lines, merely uses Edessan merchant-community knowledge of the Indo-Parthian world to furnish a narrative fiction — filling a conventional template with geographic detail derived from ongoing commercial contact rather than from historical memory of a 1st-century mission. The argument has merit, and it would be decisive if the AJT's knowledge of Gondophares were the kind of general geographic knowledge any Edessan merchant might possess. But a specific king, correctly named, in the correct territory, in the correct period — this is not general knowledge. The merchant-channel explanation accounts for how such information could have reached Edessa; it does not fully explain why this particular king was remembered — a king who had been forgotten by every other tradition and would remain unknown to modern scholarship for another sixteen centuries. As we shall see in the following section, when the Thomas tradition is compared with every other apostolic foundation tradition in the early Christian world, the pattern is the same. The content is not. It is the content — the independently verifiable detail sitting outside the narrative template — that the historian is obliged to weigh.10

The point sharpens if one takes seriously the hypothetical Parthian tradition discussed above. If a prior tradition existed — one that placed Thomas in the Indo-Parthian world, and that the AJT author inherited — then the most economical explanation for the AJT's preservation of Gondophares is continuity: a name carried forward from an older layer of memory into a newer literary composition. The alternative requires a third-century author to have independently obtained, through commercial channels, accurate knowledge of a specific first-century ruler who had left no trace in any other surviving literary tradition. Both explanations are possible. But a tradition that preserves the name of a verified historical ruler across generations of transmission is a tradition carrying genuine historical content. Moreover, the claim that the AJT deliberately relocated Thomas from Parthia to India is complicated by the fact that for Gondophares's kingdom, the two terms designated the same region. His domain was simultaneously 'Parthian' in its political allegiance and 'Indian' in Greco-Roman geographic vocabulary — the Periplus places it within 'India,' and its coinage bears both Greek royal legends and Kharosthi reverses situating it within the subcontinent's script traditions. If the AJT's author was relocating Thomas from Parthia to India, he kept the Indo-Parthian king — the one figure who bridges both designations. The relocation thesis asks us to believe that the redactor moved the geography while retaining the detail that makes both geographies the same place.10b

The Relic Cult and the Independence of the Tradition

Recent scholarship has proposed that the AJT in its original form was composed as a shrine-narrative — a text designed to sustain the memory of how Thomas's relics arrived in Edessa from India. On this reading, the AJT is not fictional history but a sacred charter: composed around a real set of relics that Edessa genuinely believed came from India, and a real apostolic tradition that Edessa genuinely claimed as its own.11

This reading must be set alongside an argument that bears directly on whether the AJT is the source of the Edessan Thomas tradition or merely its fullest literary expression. Ephrem the Syrian — Edessa's towering giant — sings hymns for the Feast of Thomas in his Carmina Nisibena, composed for liturgical performance in the city that held the relics. Ephrem never quotes the AJT. The most determined opponent of the Bardaisanite tradition in Edessan history, the man who spent decades composing orthodox hymns to the same tunes specifically to drive Bardaisanite words from the congregation's mouths — why would he draw on the AJT's narrative or its geography if the whole work comes from a group he is opposed to? He would not have touched a Bardaisanite text for any purpose. His Thomas must come from elsewhere: from the relics, from the cult, from the institutional memory that predates the AJT and exists independently of it.12

The relocation thesis presented above assumes that the Edessan relic tradition is itself a product of the AJT — that the cult of Thomas's bones at Edessa grew out of the literary narrative rather than preceding it. If so, the entire tradition — relics, cult, patristic references — is ultimately downstream of a single third-century Edessan literary project, and the relic cult carries no independent evidentiary weight. But the argument just presented from Ephrem points in the opposite direction: the relic cult predates the AJT, is independent of it, and may represent an older institutional memory that the AJT merely gave literary form to. The dust is not settled between these two readings, and is unlikely to be resolved on present evidence.13

What can be said is this: the network through which both the relics and the knowledge of Gondophares would have travelled — the Syriac-Aramaic-Parthian corridor running from Edessa through Mesopotamia to the Indo-Parthian northwest, and the parallel Persian Gulf maritime route from Charax Spasinou to Barbaricum and Barygaza — was not the Greco-Roman Egyptian maritime network. It was a different network entirely, where the demographic conditions for early Christian transmission were fundamentally different.14

Before we proceed further, we must ask an obvious question: what happened to the early Christian community in Gondophares's kingdom that the AJT claims was instituted by Thomas? The honest answer is that we do not know. The archaeological record offers almost nothing — but the silence, as we shall see, has its own explanation.

Archaeological Evidence for Early Christianity in Northwest India

The archaeological record for early Christianity in the Indo-Parthian northwest is sparse — but sparseness requires context. The region underwent successive waves of political transformation: Kushan consolidation from the mid-1st century CE, Sasanian expansion in the 3rd century, Hephthalite invasions in the 5th, Islamic conquests in the 7th–8th. Each transition involved the destruction or repurposing of earlier religious sites. Christianity in this region, if it existed in the apostolic period, would have been a minority presence in a landscape dominated by Buddhism, Hinduism, and Zoroastrianism — the kind of community least likely to leave a durable footprint.

Two artefacts sometimes cited in popular discussions must be set aside. The so-called Taxila Cross — a small stone cross reportedly found in the Sirkap excavation area — lacks a secure archaeological context: its stratigraphy is undocumented, its dating uncertain, and it cannot be reliably attributed to the apostolic or even the early Christian period. It may belong to a later Christian presence in the region. The Udaipur temple inscription, which some have claimed contains a reference to early Christian presence, is similarly problematic: the reading is contested, the script identification disputed, and no independent epigraphic authority has confirmed the Christian interpretation. Neither artefact meets the evidentiary standard required to carry historical weight.15

What can be noted, with appropriate caution, are traces in surviving communities. In Tatta (Sindh, modern Pakistan), remnants of communities claiming descent from Thomas Christians have been documented — communities whose members call themselves 'Bar Thoma' (Son of Thomas) and who practise forms of worship difficult to explain except as extremely attenuated survivals of early Christian practice. Near Taxila itself, a village called Gar Thoma has been recorded. These are late and tenuous traces; they prove nothing by themselves. But their presence in the specific geographic territory the tradition assigns to the northwest mission is not nothing.16

2.2 — THE PATRISTIC CHAIN: WHAT THE TRADITION CONTAINS AND WHEN

The previous section argued that the Edessan relic cult — the bones, the feast, the institutional memory carried by Ephrem independently of the AJT — is the ultimate wellspring from which both the literary tradition and the patristic references flow. That relic cult had a liturgical expression which reveals something important about the tradition's structure.

Both the Kerala Thomas Christians and the Syriac churches of Edessa observe July 3rd as the principal feast of the Apostle Thomas — but they commemorate different moments of the same story. For the Kerala community, July 3rd is the Dukhrana: the commemoration of Thomas's martyrdom in India. For Edessa and the west Syriac tradition, the same date marks the arrival of the relics. The tradition preserves July 3rd from at least the 4th century, attested by the Hieronymian Martyrology.

It is worth pausing here over what Ephrem's hymns do and do not claim. Ephrem attests the physical translation of Thomas's bones from India to Edessa — the Carmina Nisibena are explicit on this point. The Devil's lament that the apostle he murdered in India 'has preceded me in Edessa' and was 'here and there also everywhere' is not, however, a forensic statement about the disposition of the remains. Ephrem works through paradox and symbol: as Brock has demonstrated, his theology resists the imposition of prosaic, literalist categories onto poetic expression. The AJT itself goes further, constructing an explicit 'empty tomb' motif: when King Mazdai opens the Indian sepulchre, the bones are gone — taken to the west by one of the brethren. Before 500 CE, the overarching consensus among Mediterranean and Levantine authors was that Thomas's relics resided exclusively in Edessa; no documentary evidence from this period recognises a concurrent relic-holding site in India. Whether the Indian tomb retained a portion of the remains — as later local traditions and the sixteenth-century Portuguese excavations at Mylapore would suggest — is a question that belongs to the secondary traditions examined in later chapters.17

We turn now to the patristic references themselves. The chain spans four centuries and reaches from Alexandria to Spain. Its force is cumulative: it does not rest on any single link, and not every link carries the same weight. What follows traces the chain in chronological order, distinguishing what is securely attested from what is contested.

Origen and Eusebius: The Parthian Stratum

Origen of Alexandria (c. 225 CE) provides the earliest specific placement of Thomas's mission: Parthia. His source was a paradosis — a tradition, whether oral or written — that he likely encountered through the intellectual traffic between Alexandria-Caesarea and Edessa. That such traffic was real is confirmed by Eusebius, who cites Bardaisan and Origen together in the Praeparatio Evangelica, and by the documented visit of Julius Africanus to Edessa, where he encountered Bardaisan among the court nobility. Origen did not invent the Parthian assignment; he received it from a tradition already in circulation.18

Eusebius of Caesarea (c. 313 CE) repeats and formalises the claim: 'Thomas obtained Parthia by lot.' He draws on Origen and possibly other sources. Critically, Eusebius shows no awareness of the Indian Acts of Thomas or any Indian mission — despite having access to the Edessan archives regarding the Addai-Abgar tradition. In the early fourth century, the transition from Thomas as 'Apostle of Parthia' to Thomas as 'Apostle of India' was still incomplete in the broader Mediterranean consciousness.

One observation bears on how these earliest references are read. As noted in the previous section, Gondophares's kingdom was simultaneously 'Parthian' in political allegiance and 'Indian' in Greco-Roman geographic vocabulary. The earliest stratum of the tradition does not necessarily contradict the Indian claim. It may describe the same northwest region under a different name.19

Ephrem: The Secure Fourth-Century Anchor

Ephrem the Syrian (d. 373 CE), writing in Edessa, provides the earliest testimony that is both securely datable and independent of the AJT. His Carmina Nisibena — the critically edited Beck corpus — attest that Thomas was martyred in India and that a merchant transferred his bones to Edessa, causing the Devil to lament that the apostle 'has preceded me in Edessa.' These hymns are tied to the Edessan relic cult and composed for liturgical performance in the city that held the relics. They place the mission in 'India' generically, without naming a specific site or describing the Indian population.20

Further hymns attributed to Ephrem in the older Lamy edition contain strikingly specific ethnographic descriptions: India as a 'land of dark people,' a place 'black from sun burn,' a 'dark bride' awaiting baptism. These details are absent from the AJT. Their attribution to Ephrem is, however, less critically secure than the Beck corpus, and they may belong to a later stratum of Syriac hymnography composed in Ephrem's name. The dating of these specific hymns — whether they are authentic Ephrem (pre-373) or pseudo-Ephrem (possibly fifth or sixth century) — remains an open question with significant consequences for the chronology of what the Edessan tradition knew about India and when it knew it.21

The Late Fourth-Century Witnesses: Wide Reception, Thin Content

Beyond Ephrem, a cluster of writers in the late fourth and early fifth centuries — Gregory of Nazianzus (Constantinople, c. 380), Ambrose of Milan (d. 397), Gaudentius of Brescia (c. 400), Paulinus of Nola (d. 431), John Chrysostom (d. 407) — all treat Thomas's Indian mission as an established fact. None adds new content; each mentions Thomas and India as a passing reference in the course of saying something else. Their value is not as independent witnesses but as evidence of saturation: by the turn of the fifth century, the tradition had permeated Greek, Latin, and Syriac churches from Constantinople to Brescia to Nola. A tradition that needed only to be mentioned, not argued for, had become common knowledge across the late-antique Christian world.22

Jerome and the Calamina Problem

Jerome (d. 420) has been widely cited as the first to name a specific martyrdom site — 'Calamina, a city of India' — in his De Viris Illustribus. But critical textual scholarship has established that this reference is not part of Jerome's authentic text. The 'Calamina' passage appears in a Greek translation of De Viris Illustribus attributed to a contemporary named Sophronius, but the attribution is spurious and the translation cannot date before the seventh century. The insertion was produced to compensate for the absence of any Thomasine itinerary in Jerome's actual work. The Ceresa-Gastaldo critical edition omits the passage. Jerome's authentic text places Thomas among the Parthians, Medes, and Persians, and notes his death in India — but names no city.23

The first named martyrdom site — 'Kalamene' or 'Calamina' — enters the documentary record not with Jerome but with the anonymous Greek and Latin apostolic itineraries that begin circulating around 500 CE: pseudo-Epiphanius, pseudo-Dorotheus, the Breviarium Apostolorum. Modern scholars have proposed that the toponym represents a Greco-Latin rendering of Cholamandalam — the indigenous name for the Coromandel coast where Mylapore is located.23b

Jacob of Serug: The Widening of the Tradition's Horizon

Jacob of Serug (d. 521), a Syriac poet writing in Mesopotamia, composed two memre on Thomas's mission and martyrdom. His narrative combines the AJT's framework — the palace-building motif, the reluctant apostle — with ethnographic descriptions absent from the AJT: India as a land of dark-skinned, fearful people. The detail is not found in the Acts. It is independently echoed in the Penquita liturgical prayers of the West Syriac tradition, confirming that it represents received institutional knowledge rather than one poet's invention.

The demographic profile described — dark-skinned populations, a sunburnt land — is inconsistent with the Aryan and Indo-Scythian populations of the Indo-Parthian northwest where the AJT sets its narrative. It is consistent with Dravidian South India. The Edessan tradition, as Jacob transmits it, describes a regional context within the subcontinent that the AJT does not reach. The tradition's descriptive horizon has widened to encompass the Dravidian south.24

The Convergence at 500–636 CE

In the decades around 500 CE, the documentary tradition acquires what it had not previously possessed: specific South Indian geographic and ethnographic content. The anonymous itineraries name a coastal site. Gregory of Tours (c. 590) describes, on the testimony of a pilgrim named Theodore — otherwise untraceable in any surviving source — 'a monastery and a church of striking dimensions' at the Indian tomb site, with a perpetually burning lamp. Isidore of Seville (c. 636) synthesises the Parthian and Indian itineraries into a sequential narrative, institutionalising 'Calaminia, a city of India' in Latin reference knowledge.

This convergence of specificity is not accidental. It coincides with the documented revival of direct maritime contact between the Mediterranean world and the Indian subcontinent after a period of contraction lasting roughly two centuries — a revival whose circumstances the following sections will trace. The question the evidence poses but does not answer is whether this later specificity represents the tradition itself acquiring South Indian detail through the communities that carried it eastward — or the documentary surfacing of knowledge the tradition had long possessed but had not previously committed to writing. The pattern — a stable core receiving progressive topographic detail as contact intensifies — is consistent with either reading.25

A caveat applies to the entire historiographical chain: some ancient references using the word "India" without further specification may refer to the extended Red Sea world rather than the Indian subcontinent. In classical antiquity, writers frequently operated with a vague geographical understanding, often referring to "Three Indias" — a conceptual geography that could loosely encompass Arabia Felix (Yemen) and Ethiopia alongside India proper.

A further anomaly concerns the manner of the Apostle's death. The Valentinian Gnostic writer Heracleon (c. 170 CE) claimed that Thomas died a natural death — a statement preserved only because Clement of Alexandria quoted it directly in his Stromata. The claim, however, is theological rather than historical. Heracleon was a distinguished leader of the Valentinian school, a Gnostic sect that actively downplayed the supreme glory of martyrdom, dismissing it as mere "confession by the lip." To press this argument, he listed Matthew (Levi), Philip, and Thomas as apostles who avoided martyrdom. Clement vehemently attacked Heracleon's theology and defended the glory of martyrdom, yet did not call out any historical errors in the list — a silence that cannot be methodologically pressed into historical confirmation. Later Church traditions maintain that apostles on that very list, notably Matthew in Ethiopia and Philip in Hierapolis, did indeed achieve martyrdoms — a conviction so entrenched in Western Christian tradition that Caravaggio immortalised it on canvas in The Martyrdom of Saint Matthew. Heracleon's list, in short, serves his polemic, not the Church’s Tradition.25b

Nevertheless, the force of the patristic chain does not rest on each link being a perfectly independent witness, nor on every classical reference unambiguously pointing to the subcontinent. It rests on the tradition's early origins in the Edessan relic cult, its persistence across four centuries without contradiction, and the structural consistency of its core elements — the mission, the martyrdom, the tomb, and the translation of relics — from the very earliest witnesses onward.

2.3 — THE APOSTOLIC FOUNDATION PATTERN: TEMPLATE AND TEST

The patristic chain establishes what is contained in the primary tradition — its antiquity and its core. But it does not, by itself, settle the question of historicity. Two further observations are required, both of which we alluded to in the first chapter and the previous section: one about silence, another about pattern.

No apostolic foundation tradition in the early Christian world possesses 1st-century material evidence. Not Mark's at Alexandria, not Andrew's at Constantinople, not Peter's at Rome in its apostolic-foundation form. The absence of such evidence for the Thomas tradition places it in the company of every other apostolic claim, not outside it.

The second observation concerns a narrative pattern that recurs across the entire Christian world: the emissary-king-patronage form — apostle or emissary arrives, ruler receives, community established.

Ethiopia follows it: Frumentius arrives, King Ezana receives, Christianity established. Ezana's own inscriptions confirm the conversion.26 Armenia follows it: Gregory the Illuminator at the court of Tiridates III. Heavily legendary in narrative form. Accepted as historical.27 Georgia follows it: Saint Nino converts King Mirian III around 327 CE. Accepted.28 The Franks under Clovis, baptised by Remigius around 496–508 CE, follow the identical pattern. Undisputed.29 Constantine himself follows it. The Edict of Milan is independently documented.30

Constantinople, meanwhile, claimed Andrew as the founder of its see — a claim developed transparently to rival Rome's Peter. Acknowledged by all scholars as political fabrication. Yet no one uses Constantinople's fabrication to argue that Peter's connection to Rome must also be fabricated, even though both follow the same template.31

Every comparable apostolic foundation claim is treated as traditional and unverifiable: no one can prove that Mark went to Alexandria, that Thaddaeus went to Armenia, that Andrew went to Constantinople. These claims are settled — settled as unverifiable. They do not generate ongoing debate because there is nothing to argue about.

India is different, and the difference cuts both ways. On the apostolic side, the Thomas tradition is uniquely strong: no other apostolic claim has been confirmed by archaeological evidence naming the specific ruler the tradition places the apostle with. Gondophares is real. His coins are real. His inscription is real. But on the conversion side, India is uniquely weak: every other church in the table possesses a later, independently documented conversion event that anchors the tradition in verifiable history — Ezana's inscriptions in Ethiopia, Gregory the Illuminator in Armenia, Saint Nino in Georgia. India does not. There is no Ezana inscription, no Edict of Milan, no later historical conversion whose acceptance is beyond dispute. As the following sections will show, the community confirmed by Cosmas in the 6th century is real — but how it got there, and when, is precisely what the evidence does not settle.32 Could the Indian tradition be a political fabrication like Constantinople's Andrew? Constantinople's claim was developed and promoted by a single see to serve a specific institutional rivalry with Rome. Unlike the Addai tradition, which remains structurally anchored in Edessa and its institutional claims, the Thomas tradition exhibits diffusion beyond any single ecclesiastical centre: it was received and transmitted across four centuries by writers — Latin, Greek, Syriac — in Alexandria, Caesarea, Milan, Brescia, Gaul, and Spain, none of whom stood to gain from placing an apostle in India. The distinction is not decisive — but it is not nothing.33

The Sasanian Disruption and the Centuries of Silence

Between the apostolic period and the first independently verifiable report of Indian Christians (Cosmas, c. 525–550 CE), there is a gap of nearly five centuries from which no documentary evidence of Christianity in India survives. There is no conversion story from India. The silence has been used to argue that the tradition is a late invention. The silence is real. But it is not inexplicable.

In 224 CE, the Sasanian dynasty overthrew the Parthian Arsacids. The transition was not merely dynastic. The Parthians had been religiously tolerant, administering a loose confederation of vassal kingdoms with diverse faiths. The Sasanians were ideologically different: Zoroastrianism became a state religion with increasing vigour, and the new dynasty defined itself in rivalry with Rome — a rivalry that made Christians in Sasanian territory politically suspect as potential agents of the Roman enemy.

This suspicion reached its most violent expression under Shapur II (309–379 CE), who launched a sustained persecution beginning in 340 CE. The Catholicos of Seleucia-Ctesiphon — Mar Shimun — was martyred on Good Friday 344 CE, along with five bishops and a hundred priests. Two successive catholicoi suffered the same fate. The names of 16,000 martyrs have been preserved; some estimates place the total far higher.34

The Sasanian disruption did not affect the Syriac-Parthian corridor alone. The Roman Egyptian maritime network — the route running from Alexandria through the Red Sea to the Indian Ocean — underwent a parallel contraction. Direct Roman Egyptian contact with the Indian subcontinent, sustained from the first century BCE through much of the third century CE, attenuated during the late third and early fourth centuries as the network's commercial reach contracted and its residential presence at Indian ports thinned. The causes were structural: shifts in trade patterns, increased reliance on Arabian and Aksumite intermediaries, and the broader economic dislocations of the third-century crisis. By the fourth century, the network's direct reach extended no further than Aksumite Ethiopia, south Arabia, and Socotra. Christianity penetrated this network in significant numbers during precisely this contracted phase — with the result that the network became a carrier of Christian culture only after it had lost the capacity to carry that culture to India.35

The Persian Gulf maritime route — the passage from Charax Spasinou to the ports of Barbaricum and Barygaza — was subject to Sasanian control but did not undergo the same structural collapse. It may have remained the thread, however attenuated, through which some contact between the Syriac-speaking Christian world and the Indian subcontinent persisted during the centuries of documentary silence.36

The Sasanian disruption also explains why the Indian Christian community — when it re-emerges in documented history — is under the jurisdiction of the Church of the East rather than any western church. The Church of the East was the institution that survived the Sasanian persecutions, reorganised itself within the Sasanian system, and eventually extended its ecclesiastical network as far east as China.37

Sasanian Interlude: The Interpretive Key

The Sasanian period is the key to unlock the earliest material evidences of South Indian Christianity.

The Geopolitical Interlude

  • The Maritime Competition: To increase their share of lucrative East-West trade, the Sasanian Empire, beginning with Ardashir I (from 224 CE), strengthened and reoriented key Gulf ports—such as Rew-Ardashir in Fars (Persis or Pars) and Astarabadh Ardashir (formerly Charax Spasinou, see Chapter 1)—integrating them into a massive, competitive maritime network across the Indian Ocean. This vibrant trade expansion continued vigorously through the reign of Shahpur II (309–379 CE).
  • The Mercantile Diaspora: Operating within Sasanian maritime expansion, the Persian Christian diaspora established settlements across the Indian Ocean and into Tang China. The Catholicos at Seleucia-Ctesiphon frequently claimed jurisdiction over "the whole East, India, and China".
  • Justinian, Aksum, & Yemen: In the 6th century, Byzantine Emperor Justinian (527–565 CE) attempted to bypass Persian trade dominance by encouraging the Christian Aksumite Empire in Ethiopia (converted under King Ezana in the 4th century, read above) to establish direct Red Sea trade routes to India. This sparked fierce geopolitical competition, culminating in the Sasanian conquest of Yemen (c. 570 CE), which secured strong Persian influence over the Bab al-Mandab strait—the strategic point of the Alexandrian-Red Sea vector.
  • Cosmas's Eyewitness Account: It was during this era of intense mercantile mobility and competing maritime powers that the Alexandrian merchant Cosmas Indicopleustes sailed to India and was able to record his vital eyewitness account of the Indian church and its Persian-appointed bishops as early as 525 CE. Once Sasanian control consolidated, the Alexandrian vector contracted.

The Script Shift

  • Ecclesial Adaptation & Linguistic Hybridity: The wealthy Christian mercantile elite of Fars exhibited significant regional autonomy. To assert their distinct identity within the empire, they utilized the imperial prestige language—Middle Persian (Pahlavi)—in epigraphic and local contexts in place of Syriac. Fascinatingly, Pahlavi itself was a linguistic hybrid: it recorded spoken Persian using an Aramaic-derived alphabet, heavily relying on Aramaic ideograms (heterograms).
  • Timothy I & Administrative Consolidation: From 554 CE to 790 CE, the Metropolitan of Fars had separated his diocese from the Patriarch of Ctesiphon. In the late 8th century, Patriarch Timothy I (c. 780–823 CE) reorganized the Church from Abbasid Baghdad. In 790 CE, he successfully ended this separation, focusing on administrative consolidation, strengthening the hierarchy of the "exterior" provinces, and reinforcing the primacy of Classical Syriac as the universal administrative language.
  • The Church of the East Lifeline: Following the 7th-century Islamic conquests, Pahlavi ceased to be an imperial script, but it survived robustly in the restricted religious usage of both Zoroastrians and the Persian Christians of Fars. It was only with the consolidation of the Seljuk Turks around 1040–1050 CE that Pahlavi completely died out and the old Persian maritime link was definitively severed. Increasingly isolated, the Indian church maintained a sporadic but continuous lifeline to the East Syriac Patriarchate in Baghdad, which supplied bishops and Syriac texts.
  • The Epigraphic Record: The Sasanian Persian commercial sphere and its extended religious lifespan explain why the earliest surviving material artefacts—the Mar Thoma crosses (7th–9th century) and the Tharisappally copper plates (specifically 849 CE)—bear Pahlavi inscriptions.

Cosmas Indicopleustes: The Revival of Contact and the Last Documentary Witness

The revival, when it came, is documented by the testimony of a witness of a different kind. Cosmas Indicopleustes was not a theologian writing about apostolic tradition. He was a merchant — an Alexandrian who had personally sailed the Indian Ocean during the reign of Emperor Justinian — and around 550 CE he composed the Christian Topography, based partly on his own voyages and partly on the reports of merchants at the great entrepôts. His reference to India is a travel report, not a tradition.

Cosmas describes the principal marts of the western Indian coast: Sindu on the Indus frontier, Orrhotha, Calliana (modern Kalyan), Sibor, and the five pepper ports of Male — Parti, Mangarouth, Salopatana, Nalopatana, Poudopatana. 'In Male, where pepper grows,' he writes, 'there is a church of Christians, with clergy and a body of believers.' At Kalliana, there is 'a bishop consecrated in Persia.' On Taprobane (Sri Lanka), another church, with a priest and deacon ordained in Persia.38

By the time of Cosmas's report, the Church of the East has already consolidated its jurisdiction over the Indian Christians — the very process whose Sasanian origins the previous section traced. The community he describes is not a missionary outpost newly planted. It has bishops, clergy, a body of believers, an established connection to the Persian hierarchy. This is institutional depth — the kind that develops over generations.

Cosmas says nothing about Thomas. He says nothing about apostolic origins. He says nothing about a tomb. His geographic range is limited: his Indian itinerary runs exclusively along the western coast. He crosses to Sri Lanka and then lists ports further east — Marallo, Caber, the clove country, Tzinista — from what appears to be compiled merchant intelligence. Not a single Coromandel port appears in his account. Cosmas's silence about the tomb is the silence of a witness who did not visit the relevant territory.39

What Cosmas does establish, independently and beyond dispute, is this: by the mid-6th century, an organised Christian community existed on the western Indian coast, under Persian ecclesiastical jurisdiction — confirmed by a contemporary witness.

The Open Question

At this juncture, the evidence examined across this chapter must be set against a comprehensive challenge. The migration hypothesis proposes that Christianity reached the Indian subcontinent not through an apostolic mission but through the commercial networks of later centuries — specifically through Sasanian merchants after the Church of the East had consolidated its position. The Thomas traditions, on this view, are not records of a first-century apostolic mission but local adaptations of narratives first circulated among Mediterranean and Mesopotamian Christians, carried to the subcontinent centuries after the apostolic period and embedded in Indian topography by the communities that received them.40

The hypothesis must be taken seriously. The AJT — the foundational literary text of the Thomas-India tradition — was composed in Edessa c. 220–240 CE as a vehicle for encratite theology, not as a historical record. It was widely circulated and liturgically embedded; later patristic references to Thomas in India, from the fourth century onward, often drew on its narrative, directly or indirectly. The tradition that the AJT gave literary form to was older — the Edessan relic cult, the July 3rd feast, the liturgical tradition Ephrem attests — but the AJT was the primary channel through which that tradition reached the wider Christian world. A case can therefore be made that what the patristic chain transmits is, in significant part, the literary afterlife of a single Edessan text rather than a convergence of independent witnesses.41

Yet the migration hypothesis must also account for what the evidence contains. The tradition's invariant nucleus — mission, martyrdom, tomb, relic translation — is fixed in the documentary record by the mid-fourth century, attested by Ephrem independently of the AJT, a full century or more before the proposed Sasanian establishment in south India. The AJT preserves independently verified historical content — a detail whose most economical explanation is continuity from an older tradition, not incidental third-century merchant knowledge. And the tradition was accepted without challenge across the late-antique Christian world by writers — Latin, Greek, Syriac — with no institutional stake in placing an apostle in India. A tradition that is structurally complete before the mechanism proposed to explain it has begun, that carries verified historical content, and that permeated the entire Christian world unchallenged, requires an explanation that the migration hypothesis, taken alone, does not fully supply.

What the migration hypothesis does explain is the South Indian specificity that enters the tradition in the decades around 500 CE: the Dravidian demographic descriptions, the Kalamene toponym, the physical tomb site. This specificity coincides with the documented revival of direct contact between the Mediterranean and the Indian subcontinent. It may represent the tradition itself — carried eastward by migrating communities, localised in South Indian topography and demography, and then returning to the Levant through re-established contact, altered by its passage through a world the original narrative had never described. Or it may represent the documentary surfacing of knowledge the tradition had long carried in oral or liturgical form. The evidence does not settle the question.42

What neither reading can sustain is the claim that the South Indian dimension of the Thomas tradition is a post-Portuguese fabrication. It is documented — in Syriac hymns, in Greek and Latin itineraries, in a Gallic bishop's report of a pilgrim's testimony — a full millennium before the Portuguese arrival in 1498. Whatever its origins, it is not a community's self-serving invention. Beyond that, the primary documentary tradition yields no final verdict.

The Nasrani Self-Designation

There is one piece of evidence that connects the Edessan and Indian traditions at the level of communal identity and deserves mention here. The Kerala Thomas Christians have been known from an early period as Nasrani — from the Syriac Naṣrāyā, meaning 'of Nazareth,' the standard Syriac-Aramaic designation for Christians. This is not the Greek, Latin, or Tamil term; it is specifically Syriac. Its presence as the community's self-designation implies that the Christianity which reached the Kerala coast came through a Syriac-Aramaic linguistic channel. The name does not settle the question of when. But it settles the question of through what world — and that world is the one this chapter has traced from Edessa to the Indian coast.43

Let us return briefly to 636 CE Spain. While Isidore of Seville is recording Thomas's martyrdom in India in his Etymologiae, the armies of the first Islamic caliphate are already on the march. In that same year, at the Battle of Yarmouk, the forces of Khalid ibn al-Walid shatter Byzantine power in Syria and open the entire Levant to a new dispensation. Within a generation, the same force will swallow the Sasanian Empire whole — the very empire whose persecutions had severed the Christian networks linking Edessa to India — and within a century it will reach Isidore's Spain. Through these upheavals we will travel in the next chapter, following the tomb tradition as it passes through centuries of Islamic rule, Mongol disruption, and Portuguese maritime expansion. We will learn why, in the first place, Portuguese Christians had to come to Malabar — and what they found when they arrived.

On the asymmetry between the Thomas tradition's uniquely strong apostolic evidence and its uniquely weak conversion evidence: this structural observation has not, to my knowledge, been made explicit in the scholarly literature, though it is implicit in the terms of the debate. The absence of a later documented conversion is also explicable by India's position outside the Roman world: the conversion-event pattern depends on a Christian emissary arriving at a pagan court, which in turn depends on sustained ecclesiastical contact. The Sasanian disruption (discussed below) severed precisely this contact.

Edessa had clear political motives to promote the Thomas tradition: the city's claim to possess the apostle's relics was central to its identity as an apostolic see, and the AJT itself emerged from the Edessan Bardaisanite milieu (see endnotes 8a, 12, and 13 above). The argument from fabrication would be strong if the tradition had remained confined to Edessa and its institutional orbit — as Constantinople's Andrew claim remained essentially a Greek ecclesiastical project (Dvornik, The Idea of Apostolicity, 1958). But the Thomas tradition was received and transmitted by writers with no institutional connection to Edessa and no stake in its apostolic credentials: Origen in Alexandria, Jerome in Palestine, Gregory of Tours in western Gaul, Isidore in Spain. None of these writers needed Thomas in India; none stood to gain from it. The tradition's acceptance beyond Edessa does not prove its historicity, but it distinguishes it structurally from apostolic claims — such as Constantinople's Andrew — that served a single identifiable institutional agenda and were promoted primarily by parties with a direct interest in their acceptance.

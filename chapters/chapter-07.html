THE EMPIRE OF SOULS

The Iberian World-System and the Jesuits — 1492–1560

"There is no longer Jew or Greek, there is no longer slave or free, there is no longer male and female; for all of you are one in Christ Jesus."

— Galatians 3:28 (The theological premise of the Padroado)

The Global Paradigm Shift: Key Takeaways
The Iberian World-System
Written under existential threat, the Papal bulls completely intertwined spiritual salvation with temporal conquest, treating the whole globe as a shared Spanish and Portuguese jurisdiction.
The Pre-Trent Mendicants
Early missionaries like the Dominicans and Franciscans evaluated the Thomas Christians with flexibility, separating acceptable local rites from actual doctrinal heresy.
The Jesuit Machinery
Founded just before the Council of Trent, the Society of Jesus arrived as a highly disciplined intellectual and institutional force. The Jesuits’ Tridentine formation programmed them to view strict Roman uniformity as the sole guarantor of orthodoxy.
The Malabar Palimpsest
The Thomas Christians already possessed an ancient ecclesiastical structure rooted in the Church of the East—a system the post-Trent Padroado-Jesuit Machinery viewed as heretical by default requiring urgent correction and Latin centralization.

7.1 — THE GLOBE DIVIDED

The story of the Thomas Christians cannot be understood in isolation from a global convulsion that was remaking the world in the same decades that Penteado was being locked out of the Kodungallur church. To grasp why the Portuguese shifted from one freelance priest's failed usurpation to the systematic machinery of the Synod of Diamper, the narrative must briefly leave the Malabar coast and trace the forces that would determine the nature of the collision to come.

The Borgia Pope and the Meridian

In January 1492, the armies of Ferdinand and Isabella of Spain entered Granada. The last Muslim emirate in Iberia surrendered. Seven hundred years of Spanish Reconquista were over. In August of the same year, Christopher Columbus sailed from Palos de la Frontera carrying a letter of introduction addressed to the rulers of India and China. The timing was not coincidental. The conquest of Granada had released the military, financial, and ideological energies of the Spanish Crown, and those energies were immediately redirected to finding a westward route to the East. 1

Years earlier, Columbus had stood on the Lisbon docks watching King João II's explorers return from the African coast in triumph. When the Portuguese king dismissed his radical pitch to reach the East by sailing west, Columbus had taken his ambitions to rival Spain. Now, in 1493, the ultimate historical irony occurred. Returning from his first voyage claiming to have reached the Indies, a violent Atlantic storm forced Columbus to drop anchor in Lisbon harbour. He stood before King João II not as a supplicant, but as a conqueror.

João II was furious. He immediately declared that Columbus's discoveries belonged to Portugal under older treaties, and threatened war. Terrified of Portuguese naval supremacy, the Spanish monarchs scrambled for a higher authority. Fortunately for Spain, the sitting pontiff was Alexander VI, a Borgia from Valencia and arguably the most corrupt occupant of the Chair of Peter in the entire history of the Church. In a breathtaking act of geopolitical partisanship, Alexander responded with the bull Inter Caetera, drawing an arbitrary meridian down the middle of the Atlantic. Everything west of the line was gifted to Spain; everything east remained Portugal's.

Refusing to be outmanoeuvred by a Spanish pope, João II bypassed the Vatican entirely. He forced Spain to the negotiating table at the Treaty of Tordesillas (1494), pushing the line further westward. The Portuguese needed more open-ocean sailing room to catch the winds down the African coast and around the Cape. This seemingly technical geographic adjustment would forever alter the destiny of the Americas.

Cabral, swinging too far west on his outward voyage to India in 1500, made landfall on the coast of South America. The territory fell just east of the Tordesillas line, so he claimed it for Portugal and named it the Land of the Holy Cross. It would become Brazil. 2

The symmetry is worth pausing over. The same Cabral who bombarded Calicut, established the first Portuguese factory in Cochin, and carried Joseph the Indian to Lisbon also, on the same voyage, accidentally discovered South America. One fleet, one commander, two continents. The Padroado and the Patronato, which Chapter 5 introduced as papal bulls granting ecclesiastical jurisdiction, were now being realised in flesh and soil across half the planet.

The two Iberian thrusts, east and west, proceeded simultaneously. In 1498, while Vasco da Gama was kneeling before Hindu goddesses in Calicut, Columbus was on his third voyage, exploring the coast of Venezuela. Between 1502 and 1504, while da Gama was burning the Miri and Pacheco was defending Cochin against Zamorin's troops, Columbus was on his fourth and final voyage, shipwrecked in Jamaica. The Portuguese and Spanish empires were expanding in opposite directions, under the same papal mandate, at the same time. 3

The Silver Empires

Then the Spanish conquests accelerated beyond anything the Portuguese pepper trade could match. In 1519, Hernán Cortés landed on the coast of Mexico with a few hundred soldiers. Within two years, employing a combination of military technology, political manipulation of indigenous rivalries, and sheer audacity, he had dismantled the Aztec Empire and seized Tenochtitlan, one of the largest cities in the world. The silver of Mexico began flowing to Seville, the very city where Isidore had written, eight centuries earlier, that the Apostle Thomas rested in a tomb in Calamina, India.

In 1532, Francisco Pizarro repeated the feat in South America, capturing the Inca emperor Atahualpa and conquering Peru. The silver mines of Potosí would eventually produce more wealth than the entire Portuguese spice trade. Spain became the richest kingdom in Europe, funded by American silver and justified by the Patronato. 4

By 1534, when Paul III erected the Diocese of Goa and formally defined the Padroado, the two Iberian empires between them governed or claimed jurisdiction over most of the non-European world. Spain held the Americas, from the silver mines of Mexico to the Inca highlands of Peru. Portugal held Brazil, the oceanic choke points of the East, from Hormuz to Goa to Malacca and as far as the Spice Islands. The same proxy authority that claimed jurisdiction over the Thomas Christians' liturgical books also claimed jurisdiction over the Aztec temples and the Inca sun-worship. The system was singular. Its consequences were not.

7.2 — THE MENDICANT ORDERS AND THE PRE-TRENT FRONTIER

The earliest missionaries on both Iberian frontiers were mendicants: Dominicans and Franciscans. Their operations preceded the Council of Trent (1545–63) and therefore took place within a looser ecclesiastical environment where local variation was still permissible. The mendicant orders possessed their own internal traditions of scholarship and pastoral engagement, and their approach to unfamiliar Christian or non-Christian populations reflected those traditions rather than a centralised institutional programme.

The Mendicant Assessment

On the Malabar coast, this meant that the earliest Portuguese religious were capable of assessing the Thomas Christians on theological rather than purely jurisdictional grounds. The Dominican friar João Caro, stationed in Cochin from 1512 to 1522, examined Mar Jacob's sacramental practice and concluded that it was doctrinally sound. When Mar Jacob cited Caro as his character witness against Penteado's calumnies, he was invoking the authority of an order whose intellectual tradition equipped it to distinguish between heterodox doctrine and unfamiliar rite. 5

The Franciscans operated differently but within the same pre-Trent latitude. Fr. Vicente de Lagos built the College of Santiago at Kodungallur in 1541, which was itself a Latinisation project. But Vicente collaborated with Mar Jacob rather than circumventing him, and the Franciscans of Cochin maintained a personal friendship with the old bishop that lasted decades. When Mar Jacob retired, exhausted and sidelined, it was to the Franciscan monastery of St. Antony that he withdrew. The Franciscans cared for him until his death. Their pastoral framework permitted an East Syrian bishop to function alongside Latin clergy. 6

The American Tragedy and Las Casas

Across the Atlantic, the mendicant orders were confronting a situation of incomparably greater violence. The Spanish encomienda system had granted conquistadors the right to extract labour from indigenous populations in exchange for their 'welfare.' The demographic consequences were catastrophic: within decades of contact, the indigenous populations of the Caribbean, Mexico, and Peru collapsed by as much as ninety per cent, destroyed by a combination of forced labour, displacement, and epidemic disease to which they had no immunity. 7

It was a Dominican friar, Bartolomé de las Casas, who documented these atrocities and waged a decades-long campaign against the Spanish Crown, demanding intervention. Las Casas had himself been an encomendero before his conversion to the indigenous cause. His Brevísima Relación de la Destrucción de las Indias (1552) catalogued the horrors in forensic detail. The Dominican order's training in moral theology and natural law gave Las Casas the intellectual framework to argue that the indigenous peoples possessed rational souls, natural rights, and a claim to liberty that no papal bull could override. 8

The papacy responded. In 1537, Paul III issued Sublimis Deus, explicitly condemning the enslavement of indigenous Americans and declaring them fully human and capable of receiving the faith. A companion document, Pastorale Officium (29 May 1537), attached the penalty of automatic excommunication for anyone who enslaved Native Americans. It was the strongest ecclesiastical sanction the papacy could impose. But it would not hold. 9

7.3 — THE DUM DIVERSAS MUTATION

Sublimis Deus not only failed to protect indigenous Americans but exposed a structural flaw in the Iberian kingdom-church proxy system. The same Dum Diversas (1452) and Romanus Pontifex (1455) that Chapter 5 described as the legal foundations of the Padroado contained language granting Portugal the authority to 'invade, occupy, seize and subdue' the kingdoms of 'Saracens, pagans, and other enemies of Christ,' and to take possession of their belongings 'for the greater glory of the Divine Name.' This language had been rooted in the medieval theology of the 'just war': enemy combatants captured during a legitimate military conflict against Islamic powers could be held in permanent servitude. It was a localised, geopolitical permission, framed within the specific context of the struggle against the Moors of North Africa. 10

Baptism and the Slave Trade

The Portuguese took this narrow medieval doctrine and extended it into something the bulls' authors had never envisioned: a legal framework for commercial human trafficking on an industrial scale. Instead of capturing combatants in actual wars, Portuguese merchants anchored off the West African coast and purchased human beings from local intermediaries, or conducted raids on coastal settlements. To satisfy the 'just war' requirement, officials falsified documentation, classifying the captives as prisoners of legitimate crusading warfare. The sacrament of baptism was repurposed as a laundering mechanism: by baptising captives before embarkation, the traffickers claimed they were rescuing souls from pagan darkness, transforming a commercial crime into an act of Christian charity. 11

When Sublimis Deus prohibited the enslavement of indigenous Americans and Pastorale Officium threatened excommunication for violators, the encomenderos and conquistadors protested furiously. Charles V, Holy Roman Emperor and King of Spain, declared that the documents infringed upon the Crown's right of colonisation. The papacy, weakened and still haunted by the memory of the Sack of Rome barely a decade earlier (1527), when imperial troops had sacked the Vatican itself, could not afford another confrontation with the emperor. Under mounting pressure, Paul III issued Non Indecens Videtur (1538), which annulled Pastorale Officium and effectively suspended the excommunication penalty. The doctrinal principle of Sublimis Deus was not formally revoked, but its enforcement mechanism was gutted. 12

The Asiento System

The consequences were immediate and devastating. The Spanish and Portuguese, who had been fighting bitter wars over the borderlands of South America between Peru and Brazil, suddenly discovered a common interest. They constructed an implicit legal distinction: because Sublimis Deus addressed Native Americans without explicitly mentioning Native Africans, its legal protections did not extend to Native Africans. According to the empires' self-serving legal framework, Africans remained entirely subject to the century-old Dum Diversas dispensation, which had never been formally revoked. The two empires exploited this loophole in a mutual trade agreement. Through the Asiento system, Spain contracted Portuguese merchants to import enslaved Africans to the Americas. This legal gymnastics was used to justify the Atlantic slave trade, the largest forced human trafficking in history.13

Slavery, Law, and Moral Distinction

The question of slavery—often cited as evidence of theological inconsistency—requires a rigorous philosophical distinction between universal moral principles and their contextual juridical articulation.

The Historical Accusation

  • Selective Legal Permissions: Iberian powers drew on papal documents such as Dum Diversas to justify the subjugation of specific non-Christian populations, framing it within recognized medieval juridical categories like the "just war."
  • The Salvific Alibi: Imperial states masked economic exploitation with theological justification, arguing that the physical bondage of the African slave trade was a necessary and justifiable exchange for the spiritual salvation of baptism.
  • The Asymmetry Problem: When Sublimis Deus unequivocally affirmed the liberty of Indigenous Americans, no equivalent protection was immediately extended to Africans—creating the appearance of a severe doctrinal double standard.
  • 'Perpetual Servitude': Terms such as “perpetual servitude” in 15th-century documents originally referred to specific penal or martial contexts. The historical scandal lies in how these narrow categories were expanded into intergenerational chattel slavery.
  • Expansion in Practice: These selective distinctions were operationalized through vast imperial systems like the Asiento, allowing state monopolies to execute large-scale human trafficking under formally defensible legal reasoning.
  • The Modern Reading: From a contemporary perspective—where “slavery” signifies inherently unjust domination—this historical asymmetry is frequently cited as definitive proof that Christian theology itself enabled and engineered colonial oppression.

The Conceptual Clarification

  • Distinction, Not Endorsement: Within the scholastic natural-law framework, the decisive moral distinction is between just (e.g., penal servitude) and unjust forms of domination, not a modern binary between absolute autonomy and all forms of constraint.
  • Continuity of Principle: The identical moral framework that permitted coercion under the strict limitations of medieval doctrine also generated the fierce theological arguments used to challenge its misuse, culminating in documents like Sublimis Deus.
  • From Principle to Legal Form: Universal moral principles are not mechanically applied; they must be translated into concrete legal forms—texts, decrees, and treaties—which define categories and operate within messy historical realities.
  • The Site of Divergence: It is precisely within this juridical translation that ambiguity, selectivity, and exploitation occur, particularly when spiritual decrees are hijacked by the economic and political pressures of empire.
  • Common Misreadings: Historical legal variability is often mistakenly diagnosed as moral relativism, while the demand for absolute, ahistorical moral consistency is mistakenly viewed as authoritarianism.
  • The Ultimate Clarification: The legal application of theology is contextual, but the underlying moral principles remain objective. What appears as a theological contradiction is actually the inevitable tension between an unchanging moral law and its contextual application that opens the door for exploitation.

7.4 — THE FRACTURING OF CHRISTENDOM

The Iberian empires did not operate in a vacuum. The same decades that saw Cortés conquer Mexico and Albuquerque conquer Goa also saw the fracturing of the Western Church itself.

Inaugurated by Martin Luther in 1517, the Protestant Reformation had stripped the papacy of its authority over much of northern Europe. England broke with Rome in 1534. The Scandinavian kingdoms followed. The German principalities fractured along confessional lines. John Calvin established a theocratic republic in Geneva. By the 1540s, the universal Church that the papacy had spent centuries constructing was haemorrhaging entire nations. 14

The Black Legend

Protestant polemicists seized upon Las Casas's writings and weaponised them. The Dutch and English translated the Brevísima Relación, published it widely, and used it to construct what became known as the 'Black Legend': the argument that the genocide in the Americas and the slave ships of the Atlantic were not the crimes of individual adventurers but the inevitable products of Catholic theology and papal authority. The papacy absorbed the moral blame for a system it could no longer control, a system in which the Two Powers, spiritual and secular, had become so intertwined that they could no longer be differentiated. The damage would outlast the Reformation itself: Enlightenment thinkers would later seize upon the same history to argue that institutional religion as such was morally bankrupt. But that would be the Church's next crisis. For the immediate post-Reformation period, the question before the papacy was existential: how do you prevent further fragmentation? 15

The answer would reshape every mission field on the planet, including the Malabar coast.

7.5 — THE COUNCIL OF TRENT AND THE SOCIETY OF JESUS

The answer came in two forms: a council and a society.

The Standardisation of Trent

The Council of Trent (1545–63) undertook the most comprehensive standardisation of Catholic doctrine, liturgy, and institutional practice in the Church's history. Every ambiguity that the Reformers had exploited was addressed. Doctrines on justification, the sacraments, the eucharist, clerical formation, and episcopal authority were defined with a precision designed to eliminate the local variation in which schism could breed. Trent produced a uniform catechism, a uniform breviary, a uniform missal. The message was clear: the Church would survive the Protestant crisis by becoming institutionally uniform. There would be no more room for local accommodation, regional liturgical traditions, or irregular episcopal jurisdictions. 16

The Jesuit Instrument

The instrument designed to implement this programme globally was the Society of Jesus. Founded by Ignatius of Loyola in 1540 and approved by Paul III in the same year, the Jesuits were organised along quasi-military lines: a superior general in Rome, provincial superiors in each mission field, and a chain of obedience that ran from the newest novice to the pope himself. Their formation was the most rigorous in Christendom: years of philosophical and theological study, followed by pastoral training, linguistic education, and practical experience. They were trained to build institutions — seminaries, colleges, printing presses, intelligence networks — that could standardise clerical formation across entire continents. 17

Their mission was not persecution. It was organisation, education, and consolidation. They were trained to identify doctrinal deviation, correct it through education, and prevent its recurrence through institutional control. Where they found Christians with irregular ordinations, they built seminaries to regularise future clergy. Where they found liturgical texts that deviated from Tridentine norms, they established printing presses to produce corrected editions. Where they found episcopal jurisdictions outside Roman authority, they worked to bring them within it. This was the systematic implementation of the Council of Trent's programme across every mission field the Iberians touched.

The consequences of this programme, however, varied enormously depending on what the Jesuits found when they arrived.

7.6 — A TALE OF TWO SALVATIONS

In the borderlands of South America, where the ambitions of Spain and Portugal collided along the river systems of the Paraná, the Jesuits encountered the Guaraní: an indigenous people with no prior Christian formation, no existing ecclesiastical hierarchy, no liturgical texts, no bishops, no archdeacons, no copper-plate constitutions. They were, from the Jesuit perspective, a blank page. The primary threat to their welfare was not spiritual error but physical destruction: Portuguese bandeirantes (slave raiders) from São Paulo were systematically raiding Guaraní settlements, capturing men, women, and children, and marching them to the Brazilian coast for sale. 18

The Guaraní Reductions

The Jesuit response was radical. They retreated into the interior, beyond the reach of the colonial frontier, and built the Reductions: autonomous mission settlements in which the Guaraní were educated, baptised, organised into agricultural communities, and armed with muskets. The Jesuits trained the Guaraní to fight. In 1641, at the Battle of Mbororé, a Guaraní army led by Jesuit priests defeated a Portuguese bandeirante expedition. It was the Counter-Reformation's most extraordinary inversion: the Church militant turned against the very empire that funded it. 19

The Malabar Scenario

On the Malabar coast, the Jesuits encountered the opposite situation. The Thomas Christians were not a blank page. They were a palimpsest — twelve centuries of Syriac liturgy, East Syrian episcopal succession, archdeaconal governance, copper-plate constitutions, kalari training, pepper cultivation, and integration into a Hindu political ecology that no European framework could accommodate. They possessed exactly what Trent had been designed to eliminate: an irregular episcopal jurisdiction, liturgical texts that deviated from the Roman standard, allegiance to a patriarch outside papal authority, and practices that differed from the newly codified norms.

The Jesuits assessed this community using the criteria their formation had given them. They found irregular ordinations and built a seminary (Vaipicota, 1568) to produce correctly formed clergy. They found liturgical texts belonging to the 'Nestorian' Church of the East—a tradition they considered heretical by default—and sought to revise them. They found an archdeacon whose temporal authority exceeded that of any bishop and recognised him as the structural obstacle to Roman jurisdiction. They found a community that resisted Latinisation and concluded that the resistance proved the depth of the infection.20

In the Americas, the Jesuits defied the Portuguese Crown to protect indigenous bodies from enslavement. In India, they deployed the Portuguese Crown to bring indigenous souls under Roman obedience. The contradiction was not hypocrisy. It was the same institutional logic applied to two radically different situations. The Guaraní needed to be saved from the empire. The Thomas Christians, in Jesuit eyes, needed to be saved from themselves.

The Accommodation Paradox

The paradox of the Jesuit enterprise in the East would later emerge as a stark divergence between enforcing strict Roman uniformity and pioneering deep cultural accommodation. Arriving in Goa and Malabar with the standardizing imperatives of Trent, the early Jesuits sought to resolve theological ambiguity, systematically reorganizing ancient, layered Christian societies along rigid European lines. Yet, as the complex realities of Asian civilizations challenged their initial assumptions, the very same Order produced figures who pursued an entirely different paradigm. In China, Matteo Ricci donned the robes of a Confucian scholar; in the South Indian city of Madurai, Roberto de Nobili adopted the dietary laws and thread of a Brahmin ascetic, pushing Catholic accommodation to its controversial limits.

This swing between the extremes—coercive Latinization and radical accommodation—is not unique to the Jesuit enterprise. It reflects a structural tension inherent in any human system that seeks to uphold universal doctrinal standards while operating within changing historical contexts. In practice, equilibrium between uniformity and adaptation is difficult to sustain, and such systems tend to move toward one or the other extreme.

Parallel Jurisdictions

The Vatican's increasing unease with the Padroado-Patronato system would eventually produce two corrective interventions, both consequential and both, in their own way, failures.

In 1622, Pope Gregory XV established the Sacra Congregatio de Propaganda Fide, a centralised Roman agency designed to wrest control of global missionary activity from the Iberian crowns and place it directly under papal authority. The intent was clear: the proxy system had produced slavery in the Americas and ecclesiastical chaos in India, and Rome wanted its missions back. In 1639, Pope Urban VIII issued Commissum Nobis, a broader condemnation of the enslavement of indigenous peoples, specifically targeting Portuguese abuses in Brazil, Paraguay, and Amazonia, and reiterating the penalty of excommunication. 21

Neither intervention achieved its purpose. Commissum Nobis was ignored in the colonies, as its predecessors had been. The Propaganda Fide, far from replacing the Padroado, created a parallel jurisdiction that would produce decades of bitter institutional conflict — Padroado bishops against Propaganda vicars apostolic, Portuguese Jesuits against Italian Carmelites — without limiting the slave trade or preventing the ecclesiastical subjugation of the Thomas Christians. The structural flaw was not in the intention but in the architecture: as long as the Church depended on Iberian ships, Iberian soldiers, and Iberian silver to sustain its global missions, it could not discipline the Iberian crowns.

But we should not jump ahead. These later developments belong to subsequent chapters. Let us return to 1517, the year a chief priest at Kodungallur closed the doors of his church against a Portuguese priest who had usurped it, and an Augustinian friar at Wittenberg nailed ninety-five theses to the door of the Castle Church. One door closed in Kerala; another opened in Saxony. Neither man knew of the other's existence. But the consequences of both acts would converge, decades later, on the same community. The Counter-Reformation machinery that Luther's revolt called into being would be the very machinery deployed to break the resistance that the Kodungallur priest's locked door had inaugurated. How that unfolded is the story of the next chapter.

Portuguese Arrival to the Coonan Cross, 1498–1653

Part II: From Encounter to Fragmentation

A Methodological Shift: Reading Biased Abundance

Part I of this study operated under conditions of genuine documentary scarcity. For the first fifteen centuries of the Thomas Christian community, the primary evidence is sparse, scattered, and often temporally distant from the events it purports to describe. Its analytical framework was therefore calibrated to a specific problem: how to reason with discipline when the evidentiary ground is thin and the gap between tradition and attestation stretches across centuries.

Part II presents an almost inverse challenge, and it demands a different method. From the arrival of Vasco da Gama in 1498 onward, the documentary record expands dramatically. Portuguese royal dispatches, ecclesiastical correspondence, the published proceedings of the Synod of Diamper, Jesuit annual letters, and the detailed registers of the Goa Inquisition render the Thomas Christians one of the most extensively documented religious communities in the early modern Indian subcontinent.

Yet an abundance of documentation does not equate to transparent access to the community itself. The overwhelming majority of surviving records were produced by Padroado officials, missionaries, and colonial administrators—actors whose institutional mandate was to absorb what they perceived as an anomalous church into the normative structures of the Catholic Counter-Reformation. These are not neutral records; they are purposive documents. Their emphases, omissions, and narrative framings are themselves historical data.

To reconstruct this period with methodological rigor, the archive must be read against the grain. Each document must be situated within its context: who produced it, under what constraints, and toward what end? What does it omit—whether by design or by assumption? To read such material without placing it in its precise historical and institutional setting is not merely inadequate; it is methodologically unsound. Even when history must be accessed primarily through the voices that recorded it, those voices must themselves become objects of analysis.

From Ancient Origins to Fast-Paced Political History

The second major shift in Part II is temporal and narrative. While Part I spanned fifteen centuries of gradual, organic community evolution, Part II compresses its focus to a few centuries marked by rapid, intense, and often volatile institutional conflict.

This is, fundamentally, a political history. It traces the collision of two distinct Christian worlds: an indigenous community shaped by immemorial custom, and an Iberian maritime empire structured by Tridentine canon law. Both operated from deeply held convictions regarding ecclesiastical order and spiritual legitimacy. The narrative moves from the initial diplomatic encounters at Cochin to the systematic extension of Padroado authority, the theological confrontation at the Synod of Diamper, the sustained resistance of the indigenous Archdeacons, and finally to the crisis of the Coonan Cross Oath and the ensuing denominational fractures.

The political history is not merely background; it constitutes the analytical framework within which both documentary evidence and inherited tradition must be situated, read, and critically interpreted. The pace of the narrative accelerates because the institutional stakes, on both sides, continually intensify.

The Treatment of Tradition

Because Part II is driven by dense political and institutional developments, it adopts a deliberate structural approach to the community’s deeper traditions.

Extended treatments of foundational narratives and lineage histories, if integrated directly into the main flow, would disrupt the momentum of the unfolding crisis. These elements are therefore presented as call-out boxes and informational panels, positioned at appropriate chronological points. This allows the primary narrative to retain its pace while ensuring that these traditions remain visible and properly contextualized.

The sustained, structural analysis of these traditions resumes in Part III, where the focus shifts to how the community reconstructed its identity in the aftermath of fragmentation.

Portuguese Arrival to the Coonan Cross, 1498–1653

Part II: From Encounter to Fragmentation

A Methodological Shift: Reading Against the Grain

Part I of this study operated in conditions of genuine documentary scarcity. For the first fifteen centuries of the Thomas Christian community, the primary evidence is sparse, scattered, and often temporally remote from the events it purports to describe. The three-tier framework — Legend, Plausible, Historical — was a tool calibrated for that specific problem: how to reason honestly when the evidentiary ground is thin and the gap between tradition and attestation is measured in centuries.

Part II faces an almost opposite problem, and it requires a different method. From the arrival of Vasco da Gama in 1498 onward, the documentary record expands dramatically. We have Portuguese royal dispatches, ecclesiastical correspondence, the published proceedings of the Synod of Diamper, Jesuit annual letters, and the meticulous registers of the Goa Inquisition. The Thomas Christians suddenly become one of the most heavily documented religious communities in the Indian subcontinent.

And yet, more documentation does not mean better access to the community itself. The vast majority of surviving documents from this period were produced by Portuguese ecclesiastical or colonial administrators. They were written by men whose institutional mandate was to bring what they saw as an isolated church into conformity with the universal standards of the Catholic Counter-Reformation. Like all administrative records, they are "interested documents," produced to serve specific institutional outcomes. Their silences and framings are as historically significant as their explicit contents.

To reconstruct the history of this period, we must read the archival record against the grain. We must ask of every document: Who produced it, and why? What does it naturally omit? And what indigenous memory has survived only by accident alongside the official narrative?

From Ancient Origins to Fast-Paced Political History

The second major shift in Part II is temporal and narrative. While Part I spanned fifteen centuries of gradual, organic community evolution, Part II compresses its focus to a few centuries of rapid, intense, and complex institutional conflict.

This is a fast-paced political history. It is the story of two distinct Christian worlds colliding: an indigenous community anchored by immemorial custom, and an Iberian maritime empire anchored by Tridentine canon law. Both sides operated from deeply held convictions about what was ecclesiastically and spiritually right. The narrative moves from the initial, hopeful diplomatic encounters at Cochin to the systematic implementation of the Padroado machinery, the theological confrontation of the Synod of Diamper, the sustained resistance of the indigenous Archdeacons, and finally, the breakdown of the Coonan Cross Oath and the subsequent denominational fractures. The pace of the story accelerates because the institutional stakes for both sides were constantly rising.

The Treatment of Tradition

Because the narrative of Part II is driven by this dense political and institutional history, we have adopted a specific structural approach to the community's deeper traditions.

Deep-dives into enduring traditional elements—such as the community's foundational narratives or the history of specific lineage families—would interrupt the momentum of the unfolding political crisis. Therefore, these traditional elements are handled as call-out boxes and informational panels placed at the appropriate chronological moments in the text. This allows the primary narrative to maintain its pace while making clear that this intense institutional and political history provides the necessary context through which the community's deeper tradition must be understood.

We will pick up the sustained, structural analysis of these traditions once again at the beginning of Part III, when we examine how the community reconstructed its identity in the aftermath of the schism.

For now, we turn to the arrival of the Portuguese, and the beginning of the long disruption.

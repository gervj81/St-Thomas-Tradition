Part II

Portuguese Arrival to the Coonan Cross, 1498–1653

From Encounter to Fragmentation

“Errors, perhaps, there might have been; but heresies — which require pertinacity — no.”

— Archdeacon George of the Cross to the Jesuit General, 1624

Reading Biased Abundance

Part I of this study operated under conditions of genuine documentary scarcity. For the first fifteen centuries of the Thomas Christian community, the primary evidence is sparse, scattered, and temporally remote from the events it purports to describe. Its analytical framework was therefore calibrated to a specific problem: how to reason with historical discipline when the evidentiary ground is thin.

Part II presents an almost inverse challenge, demanding a radically different method. From the arrival of Vasco da Gama in 1498 onward, the documentary record expands dramatically. Portuguese royal dispatches, ecclesiastical correspondence, the published proceedings of the Synod of Diamper, Jesuit annual letters, and the detailed registers of the Goa Inquisition suddenly render the Thomas Christians one of the most extensively documented religious communities in the early modern subcontinent.

Yet an abundance of documentation does not equate to transparent access. The overwhelming majority of these surviving records were produced by Padroado officials, missionaries, and colonial administrators—actors whose explicit institutional mandate was to absorb what they perceived as an anomalous church into the normative structures of the Catholic Counter-Reformation. These are not neutral records; they are purposive instruments of empire.

To reconstruct this period with methodological rigour, the archive must be read against the grain. We must continually ask: who produced this document, under what constraints, and toward what end? Even when the history of the Nazranis must be accessed primarily through the voices of those who sought to subjugate them, those colonial voices must themselves become objects of intense critical analysis.

The Preface identified two polarising questions at the heart of this community’s historiography. Part I engaged the first—the apostolic origin—under conditions of evidentiary scarcity. Part II takes up the second—the question of heresy—under conditions of documentary abundance and ideological bias. Archdeacon George’s distinction between error and heresy, registered in the epigraph above, stands as the implicit lens through which the chapters that follow interrogate the heresy charge.

The Acceleration of History

The second major shift in Part II is temporal and narrative. While the previous section spanned fifteen centuries of gradual, organic community evolution, the following chapters compress their focus into a single century and a half marked by rapid, intense, and volatile institutional conflict.

This is fundamentally a political and theological history tracing the collision of two mutually incomprehensible Christian worlds. The narrative arc follows a tragic but legible trajectory:

  • The Architecture of Misrecognition: We begin with the initial encounters, where an indigenous community shaped by immemorial custom and embedded in a pluralistic Hindu polity was entirely misread by an expanding Iberian maritime empire structured by the crusading binaries of the Reconquista.
  • The Institutional Squeeze: As Portuguese naval dominance enclosed the Indian Ocean, the commonwealth that Part I traced began to unravel: the ancient supply lines of East Syrian bishops were severed. The narrative traces the ensuing jurisdictional wars between the Jesuits, the Mendicant orders, and the Padroado state apparatus, all vying to control a community they did not fully understand.
  • The Collapsed Sword: The climax of this institutional pressure is the Synod of Diamper (1599), where Archbishop Aleixo de Menezes utilized a canonical bluff and the machinery of the state to forcibly dismantle the community’s Eastern ecclesiology and reduce it to Latin suffragan status.
  • The Wreckage of the Settlement: Finally, we examine the breaking point. After decades of patient, parallel resistance by the indigenous Archdeacons, the rigid autocracy of Archbishop Francis Garcia shattered the fragile working settlement, culminating in the Coonan Cross Oath (1653) and the permanent denominational fragmentation of the St. Thomas Christians.

The Architecture of the Page

Because the following chapters are driven by dense, fast-paced political and institutional developments, this section adopts a deliberate structural approach to the community’s deeper, underlying traditions.

Extended treatments of foundational narratives, dynastic legends, and lineage histories—if integrated directly into the main text—would disrupt the momentum of the unfolding crisis. These elements are therefore presented as separate call-out boxes and informational panels, positioned at appropriate chronological points. This allows the primary narrative to retain its political intensity while ensuring that the organic traditions of the Nazranis remain visible and properly contextualised.

The sustained, structural analysis of these traditions will resume in Part III, where the focus shifts to how the community reconstructed its identity in the aftermath of fragmentation. We now turn to the arrival of the Portuguese fleet, and the beginning of a prolonged disruption.

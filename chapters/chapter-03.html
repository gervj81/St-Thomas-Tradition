c. 550 CE – 883 CE

The Tomb, the Trade, and the Long Silence

"In Male, where pepper grows, there is a church of Christians and clergy and faithful, and likewise at Calliana there is a bishop, appointed from Persia."

— Cosmas Indicopleustes, Christian Topography

The documentary tradition examined in Chapter 2 belongs to the late-antique Christian Mediterranean — the world of Origen, Ephrem, the patristic chain, the contested elements that entered the record around 500 CE. What follows belongs to a different era: one shaped by the rise of Islam, the collapse of the Sasanian Empire, the emergence of a new Latin Christendom in western Europe, and, in India, a political transformation that would redraw the map of Indian Christian communities.

The Great Severance

At the Battle of Yarmouk in 636 CE, the Arab armies destroyed Byzantine military power in Syria. Within a generation, the same force had swallowed the Sasanian Empire whole — the last Sasanian shah, Yazdegerd III, was murdered by a local miller at Merv in 651 CE. The two powers that had framed the world described in Chapters 1 and 2, the rivals across whose territories the trade routes ran, were, within fifteen years of each other, either destroyed or permanently diminished.

The consequences for information about Indian Christianity were immediate and devastating — not because the communities ceased to exist, but because the systems that had carried information about them were broken.

Alexandria’s Silted Sails

The Roman Egyptian maritime route through the Red Sea had already contracted during the late third and fourth centuries. It had revived under Aksumite stimulus in the sixth century, producing Cosmas as its final witness. The Islamic conquest of Egypt (639–642 CE) placed this route permanently under Arab control. The Fatimid caliphate that later governed Egypt (969–1171 CE) would prove relatively tolerant toward its Christian subjects; it was the earlier Umayyad period and then the disruption of the Fatimid order by the Seljuks and later the Ayyubids that progressively closed the Alexandrian corridor as a channel for Christian communication. The Indian Ocean trade itself did not stop; Arab and Persian merchants inherited and expanded it. But the route was no longer a channel through which Christian documentary information travelled westward to Mediterranean authors. After Cosmas, no writer working within the Alexandrian tradition would ever again report on Indian Christians. The vector was dead.1

The Baghdad Umbrella

The Syriac corridor from Edessa through Mesopotamia to the Gulf ports underwent a different transformation. The Sasanian political framework was destroyed, but the Church of the East survived. The Arab conquerors, following the Quranic provisions for 'People of the Book,' granted Christians dhimmi status: a subordinate but legally protected position within the Islamic polity. The Patriarch continued in office, and under the Abbasid Caliphate (750–1258 CE), headquartered at Baghdad from 762, operated from the caliphal capital itself, maintaining correspondence with bishoprics across the eastern world. Patriarch Timothy I (780–823), one of the most remarkable administrators in the history of the Church of the East, negotiated directly with the caliphs, translated Aristotle's Topics into Arabic at the caliph al-Mahdi's request, and oversaw a missionary expansion that stretched from Mongolia to Tang Dynasty China. This vector continued to function. But it operated within the caliphate. Its information circulated in Syriac and Arabic, not in Latin or Greek. It did not reach the emerging Latin Christendom of western Europe.2

The Steppe Sieve

The overland routes through Central Asia, already weakened (as Chapter 2 noted), were further disrupted by the succession of Muslim kingdoms and Turkic powers that would eventually produce the Seljuk consolidation in the eleventh century. What had once been a web of interconnected routes became a patchwork of competing jurisdictions through which Christian communication passed with increasing difficulty.

The Silent Diocese

Between Cosmas (c. 550) and the ninth century, the inventory of external evidence for Indian Christianity is brief. Patriarch Ishoʿyahb III (649–659 CE) complains in a letter to Simeon of Rev Ardashir that the Metropolitan of Fars has disrupted ecclesiastical order in India, confirming that an Indian province existed under Persian jurisdiction even as the complaint withholds any geographic specificity (see the Sasanian Interlude in Chapter 2). Patriarch Timothy I lists 'India and China' among his active territories; the Nestorian Stele at Xi'an (781 CE) confirms the extraordinary geographic reach. But neither Ishoʿyahb nor Timothy names any specific Indian location. They confirm that the Church of the East maintained its Indian province; they do not tell us where in India the community was concentrated.3

The western-coast communities attested by Cosmas are not again identified with geographic specificity by any surviving external source for approximately three hundred years. The earliest Arab account of the community dates to c. 851, roughly contemporaneous with the first surviving local Christian documentation rather than preceding it.

The Latin Horizon

Meanwhile, on the other side of the collapsed networks, a new Christian civilisation was taking shape, one that would eventually produce the force that arrived on the Indian coast in 1498.

The western Roman Empire had fallen in 476 CE. What emerged from its ruins was a constellation of Germanic kingdoms gradually Christianised under papal authority and Frankish military power. The coronation of Charlemagne as Holy Roman Emperor by Pope Leo III in 800 CE, within two decades of Timothy I in Baghdad listing India among his active territories, marks the symbolic consolidation of Latin Christendom as a political entity distinct from both Byzantium and the Caliphate. This new civilisation was Latin-speaking, papally centred, and geographically confined to western and central Europe. Its knowledge of the world beyond its frontiers was severely limited.

Charlemagne maintained diplomatic contact with the Abbasid court — the famous exchange of gifts with Caliph Harun al-Rashid included an elephant named Abul-Abbas and a water clock — but these contacts did not carry information about Indian Christians. The Latin church's awareness of eastern Christianity was confined to its encounters with Byzantine Greeks (whom it increasingly regarded as schismatic) and to fragmentary reports from pilgrims to the Holy Land. Of the Church of the East's vast eastern network — stretching from Baghdad through Persia to India, Central Asia, and China — Latin Christendom knew almost nothing. The information systems that might have connected them were either dead (the Alexandrian vector), inaccessible (the Syriac-language Persian Gulf vector, operating within the caliphate), or not yet disrupted enough to produce the refugee intelligence that would later generate legend.

The result was a Latin Christian world simultaneously expanding in political power and contracting in geographic knowledge. It knew that Christians existed somewhere in the East — the apostolic traditions remembered Thomas in India, Bartholomew in Armenia, the Magi in Persia. But it had no current, reliable intelligence about what those communities looked like, how they were governed, or whether they still existed. This ignorance would persist for centuries, deepening with each successive disruption of the remaining channels, until it crystallised into the legend of Prester John — and legend, eventually, into the strategic vision that sent a Portuguese fleet around the Cape of Good Hope.4

The Geography of Silence

The Muvendar Matrix

Among all the documentary evidence examined so far, there is not a single contemporary contribution from the Indian side itself. This silence is striking, especially because the Indian Ocean trade routes converged on the Malabar coast. To understand why the western coast left so little surviving historical documentation, the political history of South India must briefly be traced. Chapter 1 described the commercial infrastructure of the Sangam world — the ports, the pepper trade, the Roman connection. The political framework was governed by three paramount ruling families: the Cēras on the Malabar coast and the adjoining Koṅgu country, the Cōḻas in the Kaveri delta and the Coromandel coast, the Pāṇḍyas in the Vaigai basin and the far south. Beneath these vēntar stood numerous chieftains (vēḷir) controlling smaller territories, including the Āy rulers of the far south, who held the port of Vizhinjam prior to Pāṇḍyan expansion. The economy combined wetland rice agriculture, pastoral activity, and maritime trade. The Periplus and Ptolemy confirm the Cēra ruler at Muziris and the Pāṇḍya king at Madurai as known entities in the Roman commercial world.5

The Kalabhra Churn

From the third century onward, South India entered what historians conventionally term the 'dark age' — an era of profound disruption in which the Sangam political order collapsed and new ruling lineages, religious dispensations, and political technologies reconfigured Tamilakam. The silence surrounding south Indian Christians until the ninth century is not an evidentiary void unique to them; it is a reflection of this broader structural transformation.

The central event was the rise of successor states across the Deccan and the Tamil country. In the Krishna-Godavari delta, the Ikṣvākus replaced the Satavahanas. In the Kannada country, the Kadambas at Banavasi and the Gangas at Talakad established independent kingdoms. In the Tamil deep south, the Kalabhras, a warlike clan that migrated from the northern borders, established dominance over the Kaveri delta by the fourth century. Later Pāṇḍya accounts branded them oppressors of Brahmanism, but recent inscriptions at Pulangurichi show Kalabhra rulers patronising a Jain shrine and two further religious institutions of unspecified affiliation, complicating the received narrative. What is not in dispute is that the old Sangam-era ruling families lost their grip on large parts of their former territories.6

Across this zone, a common process was underway: the increasing penetration of north Indian Brahmanical culture (Vedic sacrifices, Sanskrit literary traditions, the varṇāśrama ideology) into the south Indian political order. Ruling families claimed Kṣatriya status, performed Vedic sacrifices, and made land grants to Brahmins. This was the seedbed of the political system that would, by the ninth century, produce the formalised grant structures within which the Thomas Christians would be documented.

But the transition did not happen at the same pace everywhere.

Sunrise on the Coromandel

In the Coromandel coast the Pallavas emerged by the fourth century and established themselves at Kāñcīpuram. From the fourth century through the ninth, the Pallava territories produced a continuous inscriptional and architectural record: charters in Prakrit, Sanskrit, and Tamil; rock-cut and structural temples; the great port of Mamallapuram. When the Chinese pilgrim Xuanzang visited Kāñcīpuram in the 640s, he described over a hundred Buddhist and eighty Hindu temples. The Pallava state was literate, inscribing, building, and administering continuously throughout the period when the Malabar coast produced nothing.

By the sixth century, the Pāṇḍyas had also revived. Kadungon (590–620) was celebrated in a later inscription as the 'destroyer of the Kalabhra kings.' Within a generation, the Pāṇḍyas were fighting the Pallavas, expanding into the Cēra country, and generating their own inscriptional record. Between the Pallavas in the north and the Pāṇḍyas in the south, the Coromandel coast and inlands had documented political life from the sixth century onward.7

The Silent West

The Malabar coast, by contrast, went entirely silent. The Sangam Cēras — the dynasty that had governed the port economy described in Chapter 1 — disappeared from the record after the third century. No inscriptions, no dateable literary production, no identifiable political authority survives for approximately five hundred years. Historians state the matter plainly: the history of Kerala during this period remains unclear.8

The site where ancient Muziris had stood presumably continued to function as a harbour (its geographic advantages did not change), and external witnesses like Cosmas describe commercial and Christian activity on the western coast. But there is no political authority producing grants, inscriptions, or dated texts that would place any community in a formal institutional context.

The Devotional Storm

From the sixth century onward, a devotional revolution swept across the Tamil country and transformed its political structure. The Pallava king Mahendravarman I (580–630) abandoned Jainism for Śaivism under the influence of the poet-saint Appar. The Pāṇḍya king Nedumaran underwent a similar conversion under the poet-saint Sambandar. The Nāyanārs (Śaiva saints) and Āḻvārs (Vaiṣṇava saints) composed hymns in Tamil that merged northern devotional theology with southern poetic tradition, creating a popular religious force of extraordinary power. The hymns of the first three Nāyanārs (Appar, Sambandar, and Sundarar, collectively called the mūvar) were later compiled into the Tēvāram, which became the liturgical core of Tamil Śaivism. Māṇikka-vāchagar, a minister of the Pāṇḍya king Varaguna II (862–885), composed the Tiruvāsagam, regarded as the peak of bhakti mystical poetry. Temples multiplied across the landscape. The brahmadēya system expanded massively: kings made large-scale land grants to Brahmin settlements, creating a network that managed agrarian production, administered local affairs, and provided the ideological framework of varṇāśramadharma that the new political structure required.9

The Heterodox Eclipse

The primary institutional targets of this movement were the religions that the Brahmanical tradition classified as heterodox (Buddhism and Jainism), whose commercial wealth and institutional infrastructure sustained their ideological power. The hagiographic literature is explicit: saints debate and defeat Buddhist and Jain opponents, kings abandon non-Brahmanical faiths, rival institutions lose patronage. The Periyapurāṇam, a twelfth-century hagiography of the sixty-three Nāyanārs, preserves the narrative of this campaign in devotional form, recording how saints challenged Jain and Buddhist teachers and converted their royal patrons. By the tenth century, Buddhism had effectively disappeared from the Tamil country, and Jainism survived with strength only in Karnataka.

The Silent Observers

No Pallava or Pāṇḍya inscription mentions Christians — neither as a community to be patronised nor as one to be pressured. The silence is total. But total silence from polities that documented Brahmin settlements, Jain temples, Buddhist monasteries, merchant guilds, military campaigns, and irrigation works is itself a datum. What it establishes is that no Pallava or Pāṇḍya authority incorporated a Christian community into its political structure through the grant system — while the Cēra state, when it finally emerged, did exactly that. The implications of this contrast, and the community traditions that may bear on it, will be examined in a later chapter.10

Ritual Kings, Real Lords

The political structure within which the Indian Christian community first appears in dated, physically surviving documentation was itself a new creation of the ninth century.

The Cēra Perumal state, with its capital at Mahōdayapuram (Kodungallur), was built upon and around the Nambudiri Brahmin settlements that had migrated to Kerala from the north in the preceding centuries. Some historians characterise it as a Brahman oligarchy in the guise of a monarchy: the king's power was largely ritual, exercised under the supervision of the Nālu Tali, a council of celibate Brahmins drawn from four major settlements near the capital. Below the Perumal, hereditary chiefs (nāṭuvāḻi) governed territorial divisions (Venad in the south, Kolattunad in the north, Eranadu and other nāṭus between them), exercising political and military power while acknowledging royal suzerainty. The Brahmins controlled the agrarian economy through extensive landholdings (brahmasvam) and temple estates (dēvasvam), and provided the ideological framework that regulated social life. The king was, in effect, the ritual head of a political system whose real power lay with the Brahmin assemblies and the hereditary chiefs.11

The Corporate Polity

The instrument through which this state incorporated communities into the polity was the royally issued charter — a grant conferring specific privileges upon a named community or institution. The political grammar of early medieval South India, as analysed by historians of the period, identifies four types of corporate institution that formed the building blocks of the state: the ūr (peasant locality), the nāṭu (supralocal territorial unit), the brahmadēya (Brahmin settlement), and the nagaram (merchant town). Both brahmadēyas and nagarams could receive taniyūr status: autonomous self-governance within the territorial unit of the nāṭu. This was not a Kerala peculiarity but the common political grammar of Tamilakam, operating across the Cōḻa, Pāṇḍya, and Cēra territories with regional variations.12

Critically, the Cēra polity practised religious pluralism in its grant-giving. Buddhist monasteries, Jain temples, and merchant communities of various backgrounds all received comparable recognition through royally issued charters. This contrasts sharply with the Pāṇḍya territories, where the bhakti movement was restructuring religious life along Brahmanical lines, and with the Pallava state, where no parallel grant to a Christian community is known.

The Rebirth of Quilon

The emergence of this state coincided with two other developments that converged on the same historical moment. The Abbasid shift of the caliphal capital to Baghdad (762 CE) reoriented Persian Gulf trade and pushed Christian merchants from former Sasanian territories toward safer destinations, including the western Indian coast, where they had prior commercial contacts. And the intensifying Cēra-Pāṇḍya military conflict (the Pāṇḍyas had captured the port of Vizhinjam from the Āy kingdom) prompted the Cēra rulers to develop Quilon as a rival port. The founding of Quilon in 825 CE, the beginning of the Malayalam era, was an act of political strategy designed to attract the foreign merchants whose commercial activity the state depended on.13

On the eastern coast, the ninth century also saw the resurgence of a third paramount Tamil power. Vijayalaya Cōḻa captured Thanjavur from the Muttaraiyars c. 848 CE; his son Aditya I defeated the last Pallava king Aparajita c. 897 CE; his grandson Parantaka I took Madurai from the Pāṇḍyas c. 910 CE. The Imperial Cōḻa state intensified the Brahmanical temple-and-grant pattern of its predecessors, projecting Śaiva royal cult and brahmadēya land tenure across a vastly expanded territory.13b

The bhakti pressure on communities classified as heterodox in the Pallava, Pāṇḍya, and resurgent Cōḻa territories of the eastern coast; the migration of Persian Christian merchants westward under Abbasid commercial expansion; the Cēra polity's emergence with its pluralist grant technology and its strategic need for merchant communities — all of these historically align in the ninth century. The documented Christian presence on the western coast begins at exactly this moment.

'Malabar' — The Geography of a Name

The toponym appears in many forms — Mali, Male, Malibar, Malibarat, Malabar — and its geographic reference shifts depending on who is using it and when. The root is the Malayalam malavāram, meaning 'hill-produce' — that is, the pepper and spice country of the Western Ghats. Cosmas uses Male ('in Male, where pepper grows'); the Cairo Geniza documents use Malibarat; Arab geographers use Malibar to denote the entire pepper-producing coast from Kannur in the north to Cape Comorin in the south — over 500 kilometres encompassing multiple kingdoms, port cities, and ecological zones. The word was used by the Jewish, Christian, and Arab trading communities themselves, from whom the Portuguese later adopted it.14

Within this broad region, the specific ports where documentation concentrates changed over time. The ancient toponym 'Muziris' (Muchiri in Sangam Tamil) belongs to the Roman-era commercial world: it appears in the Periplus, in Pliny, in Ptolemy, in the Peutinger Table, and in the Sangam poetry. After the second or third century CE, the name drops out of the documentary record entirely. When documents resume in the ninth century, the documented port is Quilon (Kollam) — the new harbour city founded in 825 CE — not Muziris. The Cēra Perumal capital at the same general location is called Mahōdayapuram (identified with Kodungallur), not Muziris. The identification of Kodungallur with ancient Muziris is a modern scholarly reconstruction, confirmed by the Pattanam excavations, not a continuous naming tradition. No medieval source calls the place Muziris. 'Muziris' and 'Kodungallur' are two different historical identities for the same general area, separated by a five-century gap during which neither name appears in any document.

A Pact in Copper

Community tradition records that in 823 CE, two Persian Christian merchant-leaders, Mar Sapor (Maruvan Sapir Iso) and Mar Prodh (Aphraat), arrived at Quilon from the Persian Gulf, accompanied by a figure known as the 'glorious Sabrisho.' They established a sanctuary and settlement at Quilon, called Tharisapalli, which besides being a place of worship became the centre of economic life of the port town.

The tradition of this migration is preserved in multiple, not entirely concordant, forms — and the discordances themselves are analytically revealing. The earliest surviving account is found in the Syriac apologetic church history preserved in a manuscript at the Thrissur Metropolitan's House. This text presents the arrival as a Catholicos-sent mission: Mar Sapor, having first made a voyage to India and found the Christian community there in spiritual distress, returned to Mesopotamia to report to the Patriarch. The Patriarch then dispatched him again, this time with Mar Prodh and a retinue including bishops, priests, deacons, and settler families from Jerusalem, Baghdad, and Nineveh. According to this account, the initiative came from the Church of the East's central administration — a deliberate mission to rescue and reorganise a distant community.

A second stream of tradition, preserved in Portuguese-era transcriptions of documents now lost — most importantly, a text attributed to Francisco Roz, the first European bishop of the Syrian Christians, now in the British Library — connects the settlement more specifically to the founding of a church and a trading quarter at Quilon, with royal patronage from the Venad ruler. A third stream, found in the community's own oral traditions and folk songs recorded in the early modern period, links Mar Sapor to the broader Thomas of Kana migration tradition, in which a wealthy Persian merchant led a group of settler families to Kerala and was granted land and privileges by the local king. (Both the Kānāyi Thomman tradition and its associated copper plate grants will be taken up in greater detail in subsequent chapters.)

The relationship between these traditions is debated, and the dates they assign range from the fourth century to the ninth. What they share is a common narrative template: a merchant-leader from Persia arrives by sea, establishes a church and a commercial settlement, and receives formal recognition from the local ruler. This is the same apostle-king-patronage pattern identified in Chapter 2 as the standard template for Christian foundation narratives across the Eastern Mediterranean and beyond. The critical question is not whether the template is conventional — it is — but whether the historical kernel within it can be dated. The most secure date is the one inscribed on the plates themselves: 849 CE, when the privileges were formally conferred.15

The date of 823 for the initial arrival has been subject to scholarly revision. The Malayalam era beginning in 825 commemorates the founding of the harbour city of Quilon as an act of Cēra political strategy, not the personal achievement of Mar Sapor. One reading of the seventeenth-century Portuguese accounts suggests that the founding and building of the Tharisapalli is posterior to 825 and should be dated to the 840s. The privileges themselves were conferred in 849 — some twenty-six years after the reported arrival — suggesting that the local ruler granted them not at first sight but having tested the worth and utility of the recipients in the process of wealth mobilisation. The settlers deployed the extensive Persian Gulf mercantile networks they carried with them, activating trade through the Tharisapalli at Kollam.16

More than a Church

The name Tharisapalli itself reveals how the community was classified within the south Indian political system. The word paḷḷi did not originally denote a 'church' in the narrow, later Christian sense, but was associated with a broader category of 'settlement' or 'non-Brahmanical sanctuary' — the institutional category that the Brahmanical order used for all religious establishments outside its own framework. Buddhist sites were paḷḷi. Jain sites were paḷḷi. Christian sites were paḷḷi. The term is distinct from kōvil or ambalam, which denote Hindu temples and shrines. To call a place of worship a paḷḷi was to mark it as institutionally recognised but categorically non-Hindu — belonging to the same broad class as every other non-Brahmanical religious foundation in the Kerala polity.

Tharisapalli is a composite: tarsā, the Early New Persian word meaning 'God-fearer' (the standard Persian designation for a Christian), combined with paḷḷi. It means, literally, 'Christian sanctuary-settlement.' The grant recorded on the plates is not merely for a place of worship — it is for the foundation of a settlement, or emporium (nagaram), situated in the seaport, around the sanctuary called Tharisapalli. The paḷḷi is the institutional anchor; the merchant community organised through the guilds is the commercial body attached to it. The merchant guilds were incorporated through the paḷḷi, not alongside it — just as, in the Brahmanical system, the sabhā (the corporate body of Brahmin landholders) was organised around and through the temple of the brahmadēya. The paḷḷi-nagaram structure was the non-Brahmanical equivalent of the kōvil-brahmadēya structure: the same political technology of incorporation, applied to a different religious category.17

A Four-Script Seal

The grant itself — issued by Ayyan Atikal Tiruvatikal, the sub-ruler of the Venad region under the Cēra king Sthanu Ravi Varma — is the earliest extant, securely dated, physically surviving documentary evidence for the Indian Christian community with royal recognition. It is inscribed mostly in Tamil vattezhuthu script, the standard official script of the Kerala polity, and witnessed in four scripts and four languages: Vattezhuthu, Hebrew, Pahlavi (Middle Persian), and Kufic Arabic. These scripts represent distinct merchant communities present in Quilon in 849 CE. A forged document cannot produce four independently authenticated scripts from different communities with no common interest in fabrication. The cross-cultural witness signatures are the plates' own authentication.18

The Copper Plate Grants

Issued in 849 CE, the Tharisapalli Copper Plates constitute the earliest "evidence" documenting the formal integration of Christians into the socio-economic hierarchy of an Indian polity.

The Physical Document

  • The Vattezhuthu Script: The main body of the royal grant is inscribed in Vattezhuthu, the ancient, rounded syllabic script used for early Malayalam and Tamil. By utilizing the official state script, the Venad ruler formally embedded the Christian community within the native administrative apparatus. Evolving from Tamil-Brahmi, Vattezhuthu eventually gave way to Kolezhuthu. The modern Malayalam Arya Ezhuthu script evolved from a synthesis of these native scripts and the Sanskrit-based Grantha script.
  • The Cosmopolitan Signatures: The ultimate authentication of the plates comes from the guarantor signatures appended at the end. Demonstrating the immense trans-oceanic reality of the port of Quilon, the merchant witnesses signed in three distinct foreign scripts: Kufic Arabic (Muslim), Pahlavi (Christian, Zoroastrian), and Hebrew (Jewish).

The South Indian Plate Tradition

  • The Thomas of Cana Plates: While the physical copper is now lost (last recorded in Portuguese possession), historical memory insists these earlier plates granted the initial aristocratic and mercantile privileges to the first wave of Syrian immigrants to the Malabar coast.
  • The Jewish Copper Plates (c. 1000 CE): Issued by the Cēra ruler Bhaskara Ravi Varman to the Jewish merchant leader Joseph Rabban, granting rights to the Anjuvannam guild. This shows that the paḷḷi-nagaram grant system was the standardized legal mechanism for empowering non-Hindu merchant castes.
  • The Iravikortan Plate (1225 CE): Issued by the King of Venad to the Christian merchant Iravikortan, officially appointing him as the head of the powerful Manigramam guild in Quilon, securing the community's economic dominance into the high medieval period.
  • The Kumari Muttom Inscriptions (c. 1494 CE): Found on the Coromandel coast, these stone inscriptions demonstrate the long-term continuity of this legal instrument. While not a copper plate, they function as a late-medieval continuation of the same tradition, documenting royal patronage and specific socio-economic privileges granted to Christian merchants on the eve of the Portuguese arrival.

The privileges conferred are extensive and specific. The church was given custody of the weights and measures of the city of Kollam — the parakkoṭi, pañcakaṇṭi, and kappan — which the Christians held until 1503, when the Portuguese took them away. This custodianship was not a religious honour; it was an economic function: a corporate body of traders entrusted with ensuring the standardisation of commercial instruments across the port. The community was also given the right to collect tolls on carts carrying merchandise into the market (vayiṉam) and on boats carrying cargo to the port (veṭiyilum), and received the assignment of several service families together with rights to collect specified taxes previously levied by the ruler himself. The community received, in addition, social status markers — the right to use palanquins, drums, parasols — and judicial autonomy to adjudicate internal disputes. These were not acts of exceptional generosity toward a favoured group. They were the standard instrument through which the polity managed its relationship with every commercially significant community.19

Granite Witness

If the copper plates are the documentary anchor of the ninth-century community, the stone crosses are its lithic one. The earliest surviving physical monuments of Indian Christianity are Pahlavi stone crosses, generally dated from the seventh to the ninth century, the same horizon as the plates themselves. Sharing a remarkable resemblance in form, these crosses (some bearing Pahlavi inscriptions, others without) have been recovered on both Indian coasts and on the island that closed the southern sea: at Mylapore on the Coromandel, at five locations across the Malabar interior, at Agassaim/Pilar on the Konkan littoral in Goa, and at Anuradhapura in Sri Lanka.

Collectively, this distribution traces the contours of the Indian Ocean maritime world. Two of the regions, the Konkan coast and Sri Lanka, correspond to the Persian bishoprics explicitly mentioned by Cosmas in the sixth century. The crosses therefore anchor the earliest surviving physical layer of Indian Christianity within the wider Persian-centred Indian Ocean network extending eastward toward China and westward toward the Gulf, while the inscribed forms tie that physical layer directly to the same Pahlavi script that witnesses the Tharisapalli plates we have just examined.a

The Pahlavi Stone Crosses

These stone crosses are the earliest surviving physical artifacts of Indian Christianity.

The Paleographic Evidence

  • The Dead Script: The earliest crosses are carved in Late Pahlavi (Middle Persian). While the spoken Middle Persian language evolved, this script effectively died out for general use after the 7th-century Islamic conquests. However, it continued to be used for a few centuries by Zoroastrian priests (who used cursive Book Pahlavi for their religious texts) and by Persian Christians (who also developed a variant known as Psalter Pahlavi). Eventually, Christian communities of Fars gradually shifted their liturgical language entirely to Syriac. By the 9th and 10th centuries, Pahlavi was completely replaced in wider society by New Persian written in the Perso-Arabic alphabet (read "Sasanian Interlude" infocard in Chapter 2).
  • The Inscription: The inscription has proven difficult to decipher, with multiple interpretations having been given over the decades. A recent and widely supported scholarly reading translates it as: "Our Lord Christ, have pity on Sabrišōʿ, (son) of Čahārbōxt, (son) of Sūray, who bore [brought/made] this (cross)."b
  • The Copper Connection: Because this specific form of Late Pahlavi fossilized early and matches the script used in the 9th-century Tharisappally copper plates, epigraphists generally date the earlier crosses to the 7th or 8th centuries.

The Crosses

  • The Type: All of these are bas-relief crosses carved into stone panels, unlike later free-standing open-air crosses.
  • The Locations: They are found in Mylapore, Goa, and Sri Lanka (Anuradhapura), as well as within the Malabar region at Kottayam, Kadamattom, Muttuchira, Kothanalloor and Alangad. The Mylapore cross and one of the Kottayam crosses are considered the earliest; the other Malabar crosses are considered to be later copies. The Sri Lanka and Kothanalloor crosses does not have a readable inscription like the others.c
  • The Estampage: The remarkable degree of resemblance in inscriptions has led to a general consensus that the crosses were made using a copying method called estampage (lifting a copy of the original by pressing paper onto its inked surface). Furthermore, several of these later crosses (like the smaller Kottayam cross) add a second inscription in Estrangelo Syriac (Galatians 6:14), capturing the shift in community's working liturgical language from Persian (Pahlavi) to Syriac.

The pattern of resemblance has produced an arresting hypothesis. Recent comparative epigraphy of the inscribed crosses points to a single original, almost certainly the Mylapore cross, from which the others were copied by the technique of estampage. Building on this, István Perczel has argued that the proliferation of Pahlavi crosses across Kerala in the ninth century physically attests a migration of Persian Christian merchants from the Coromandel to the Malabar coast in precisely that period. The pressure he identifies as the proximate cause is one this chapter has already named: the rising Brahmanical and bhaktic order whose ascent on the Coromandel had begun to constrain the operating space of non-Hindu commercial communities. The crosses, on this reading, are not merely artifacts of devotion. They are physical receipts of a westward shift driven by exactly the religious and political pressures that the Tharisapalli grant, which arranged formal patronage for a Persian Christian commercial community at Quilon, was simultaneously codifying on the receiving coast.d

The Ninth-Century Nexus

The Masters of the Monsoon

The plates name three merchant guilds as guarantors and protectors of the community's privileges: the Manigramam, the Añcuvaṇṇam, and the Ainurruvar. These were not local curiosities. They were pan-Tamilakam commercial organisations that operated across South India, Sri Lanka, and Southeast Asia.

The Manigramam appears in inscriptions from across the Cōḻa territories, where its members were predominantly Hindu. In Kerala, the guild's ethnic composition was different: by the ninth century, the Kerala Manigramam had evolved into a powerful commercial institution operating among Christian merchants for long-distance Indian Ocean trade, particularly between Kerala and the economic zones of the Persian Gulf, the Red Sea, and the Levant. The same guild name carried different communal content in different regions — a fact that underscores the system's character as a political and commercial framework rather than a confessional one.

The Añcuvaṇṇam was organised by Jewish, Christian, and Islamic merchants from West Asian countries. The Ainurruvar ('the Five Hundred,' also rendered Aiññūṟṟuvar) was a mobile body with an even wider geographic range than either of these, appearing in inscriptions from Karnataka to Sri Lanka to Southeast Asia. From the twelfth century onward, it operated as the umbrella organisation that absorbed both the Manigramam and the Añcuvaṇṇam under a single transregional framework. Statistical analysis of merchant guild inscriptions shows their distribution across the entire south Indian world from the ninth through the sixteenth century, peaking in the twelfth and thirteenth centuries. The Thomas Christian community's commercial life was embedded in this pan-Tamilakam network — not in a local arrangement peculiar to the western coast.20

The Cōḻa state patronised these same merchant guilds, and eleventh- to thirteenth-century Añcuvaṇṇam inscriptions are attested across the Coromandel coast (notably at Vishakhapatnam, 1090 and early thirteenth century). But no surviving Cōḻa charter grants privileges to a Christian community in the paḷḷi-nagaram form distinctive of the Tharisapalli template. The Cōḻas dealt with Christians as members of multi-religious merchant bodies; the Cēras dealt with them as a religiously defined community embedded in the territorial polity. This was the structural difference between the two halves of Tamilakam.20b

The Manigramam and Añcuvaṇṇam jointly held the right to impose customs at Quilon and were entrusted with the judicial authority to adjudicate disputes and protect the community's privileges from encroachment. The merchant guilds functioned as agents of governance, not merely as trade associations. The political logic behind the grants was fiscal: the ruler depended on maritime trade for wealth, the merchants conducted the trade, and the grants were the contractual instrument through which the ruler secured their activity by offering legal security, social status, and institutional autonomy in exchange.

The View from the Dhow

Two years after the plates were issued, an independent external witness confirmed the world they describe. The Akhbār al-Sīn wa-l-Hind (c. 851 CE), an anonymous Arabic compilation drawing on the voyages of the Persian merchant Sulaiman al-Tajir, records that Quilon was the sole Indian port used by large Chinese ships as their transshipment hub for the Persian Gulf trade. The account — the earliest surviving Arab description of the Indian Ocean commercial world — establishes Quilon as a cosmopolitan port at exactly the moment the grants were formalising the Christian community's institutional position within it. The text is not describing Christians specifically; it is describing commercial geography. But the port economy it witnesses is the same one that the Tharisapalli plates are embedding the community within.21

The Akhbār also preserves a notice that has long been read as further confirmation of the shrine's standing within the Arab commercial world. Ten days' sailing from Quilon, the text records, would bring a ship to a place called Betūmā. Reinaud, the text's first European editor in 1845, identified this toponym with the Syriac Beth Thōmā, the 'house of Thomas,' taking it as an Arab attestation of the apostolic shrine at Mylapore. The identification has been widely repeated since. It has also been challenged: the ten-day sailing time, the Akhbār's structure as a route-to-China document, and the toponym's place within a sequence of Southeast Asian landfalls have led specialists in Arabic maritime geography to read Betūmā instead as a port or island along the Malay Peninsula or in the Gulf of Thailand. If the Mylapore identification holds, the notice would be an early Arab witness to the shrine; if it does not, the witness is a homophonic illusion. The point is offered here in that light.22

Around the same time, a Syriac manuscript dated to 874 CE locates the apostle's burial at 'Qalamaya' — the Syriac form of the Kalamene/Calamina toponym already known in Latin and Greek apostolic lists from around 500 CE — and is the earliest surviving text to attribute his martyrdom to Brahmins. The Acts of Judas Thomas had placed the killing under a king called Mazdai; the patristic chain had named the site but not the perpetrators. Here, for the first time in the surviving record, the agency is assigned to a specifically Indian social category.

If this attribution is authentic, it represents a development of considerable interest. The ninth century was precisely the period when varṇāśramadharma was consolidating its hold across the Tamil country and the western coast alike, as we have seen. A martyrdom tradition that names Brahmins as the agents of the apostle's death reflects a landscape in which Brahmanical authority was ascendant. The tradition, it appears, was absorbing additional local Indian elements as it travelled far and wide.22b

Wessex Looks East

In 883 CE, the Anglo-Saxon Chronicles record that King Alfred the Great dispatched envoys with votive offerings to the tomb of St. Thomas in 'India.'23

Alfred's kingdom of Wessex had barely survived the Viking invasions. The Treaty of Wedmore (878) had secured a fragile peace with the Danes only five years earlier. England was partitioned, its monasteries sacked, its libraries burned. The great centres of Anglo-Saxon learning — Lindisfarne, Jarrow, Iona — had been destroyed or abandoned. And in this setting (a kingdom at the edge of the known world, recovering from catastrophe), a ruling king considered it meaningful to send material offerings to an apostolic shrine understood as being somewhere in the distant East.

Where Alfred's information came from, we cannot determine. The Alexandrian corridor had been dead for two and a half centuries. The Latin literary tradition preserved the memory of Thomas in India, but as hagiographic tradition, not current intelligence. Charlemagne's diplomatic contacts with the Abbasid court had not, as far as we know, carried information about Indian Christians to western Europe. Alfred himself was a noted scholar who sought out knowledge from every available source, commissioning translations of Orosius's world history, Boethius's Consolation of Philosophy, and portions of Augustine's Soliloquies. His court attracted scholars from across the surviving Christian world — the Welshman Asser, the Frankish Grimbald, the Old Saxon John. Whether the information about the Indian tomb reached him through a surviving thread of the old pilgrimage networks, through contact with eastern Christians in the Holy Land, or through some channel we cannot trace, the entry testifies to something that the documentary silence might otherwise obscure: the tradition of the apostle Thomas in India had not died. It had reached the farthest surviving outpost of the old Roman Christian world — a kingdom that had itself nearly perished.

Whether Alfred's envoys reached the destination, or some intermediate Christian community, or were turned back by the immense distances and the hostile geography, the Chronicles do not say. What we know is that they certainly could not have made such a journey if they had waited much longer. In 1037, the Seljuk Turks under Tughril Beg consolidated power in Persia and Mesopotamia, severing what remained of the overland and maritime channels that connected western Christendom to the eastern Christian world. The already weakened routes through which Patriarch Timothy I had administered his Indian diocese, through which the Tharisapalli plates' Persian and Arabic witnesses had arrived in Quilon, through which a thread of information about the Indian tomb had somehow reached Anglo-Saxon England — all of these would be cut or attenuated within a century and a half of Alfred's embassy.

The window was closing. Alfred's envoys, if they passed through it, were among the last who could.

The tradition of the apostle Thomas in India had now traversed the full arc of the old Christian world — from Edessa to Alexandria, from Alexandria to Rome, from Rome to the courts of Merovingian Gaul and Visigothic Spain, and finally to a Saxon kingdom recovering from Viking devastation at the western edge of Europe. It had been carried, at each stage, by information systems that were collapsing behind it: the Alexandrian maritime route, the Persian Gulf corridor, the Latin hagiographic tradition, the fragmentary channels through which a king in Wessex could learn that an apostle's tomb existed somewhere in the East. What persisted in India itself — the tradition of the apostle's martyrdom by Brahmins, the eastern shrine at Calamina, and a western coast community now formally embedded in the Cēra polity through the merchant guild system — had entered a new documentary phase: locally attested, commercially significant, institutionally recognised, and bound through that same guild system into a wider commonwealth of Christians spanning the Indian Ocean. But the connection between this living Indian reality and the Latin Christian world that dimly remembered it was about to be severed more completely than ever before.

The centuries that followed brought profound upheavals. The Crusades, the legend of Prester John, the Mongol catastrophe and its paradoxical reopening of trans-Asian routes, the convergence of independent witnesses on the Indian coast, and the final mapping of the subcontinent before the Portuguese set foot—these form the landscape of the next chapter.

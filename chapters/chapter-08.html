SHADOW WORLDS

Two Frontiers the Empire Could Not Control — 1510–1542

"Our country, where our Christians are living, is called Malabar, and it has about 20 towns, of which 3 are famous and strong: Cranganor, Palur and Quilon."

— The bishops' letter of 1504, to Patriarch Elias V

8.1 — THE WORLD OF THE NAZRANIS

The currents of a changing world would soon break upon the Malabar coast, transforming distant pressures into immediate and unavoidable realities. But for Duarte Barbosa writing around 1516, Serra was a land of wondrous discovery and immense opportunity.

Barbosa was a feitor (factor) and linguist who had learned Malayalam and spent over a decade on the Malabar coast. His Livro de Duarte Barbosa contains one of the most detailed ethnographic account of Kerala society any European had yet attempted. He classified the people of Malabar into categories: ruling kings, higher castes (the Nambuthiri Brahmins, the Nairs, the merchant castes, the potters, the washermen, the weavers), lower castes, and 'strangers' without caste. He saw the Nazranis as intimately integrated into this society:

In this city of Coulam there is a great number of Moors and Heathens, and also of Christians of the teaching of St. Thomas, who are closely allied to those of whom I have already spoken in the Kingdom of Cochin... they have here a church of St. Thomas... These Christians are great merchants, and they possess the pepper in this land... 1

The Thomas Christians appeared in his account not as a religious sect but as a social stratum. They were, in Portuguese reckoning, equivalent in rank to the Nairs. They bore arms. They trained in the kalaris, where Christian panikkars (fencing masters) ran schools with as many as eight to nine thousand disciples, both Christians and Nairs, getting trained as a fighting force for the local rulers. They served the Hindu kings as a military and commercial caste whose principal commodity was pepper. Their priests married. Their churches looked like temples. Their festivals were indistinguishable from those of their Hindu neighbours. 2

For Barbosa, they were integrated, commercially vital, and possessed of all the pepper the Portuguese wanted. He recorded this without alarm. He was a commercial agent, not a theologian.

Around the same time, Alvaro Penteado, petulant after getting pushed out of the Kodungallur church, wrote to King Manuel something else entirely. Where Barbosa saw integration, Penteado saw contamination:

As for the Christians of St. Thomas, Your Highness should know that they are completely ruled by the Patriarch of Babylon... who is obeyed with more reverence than the Latins show the Pope. 3

Their customs, sacramental practices, and liturgy did not conform to the Padroado standard. They used leavened bread, not unleavened wafers. Their priests married and served Mass in everyday dress. They did not practise auricular confession. They had no images in their churches:

Their churches are like synagogues... They have crosses in their churches on the altars... but no images or engraved outlines of profiles and faces.

For Penteado, the church and the Christians who had rejected him were teetering on the edge of forgetting they were Christians altogether. He laid before the king a programme of total reform, starting from the replacement of the Nazrani bishops sent from Babylon. 4

The two portraits, composed within months of each other, frame the central paradox of the Portuguese encounter. What is clear from these early records is that the Nazranis possessed a distinctly strong political framework in the Serra and a deep-rooted apostolic self-consciousness within a robust Church of East ecclesiology. This was a community that would oppose any Padroado strong-arming in ways that the populations of Goa or the Coromandel coast simply could not. They would stay beyond Padroado reach. The Portuguese would spend a century trying to change that.

8.2 — MAR JACOB'S LONG GAME

For a quarter of a century after Penteado's humiliation at Kodungallur, the surface arrangement between the Portuguese and the Thomas Christians held. Mar Jacob, the East Syrian bishop who had arrived from Gazarta in 1503 and guided the community through its first turbulent decades of Portuguese contact, proved himself a diplomat of extraordinary patience. He directed pepper to the Portuguese factory at Cochin. He wrote gracious letters to the king of Portugal, praising the monarch's religiosity and offering further commercial and political services. He allowed the Franciscans at Cochin to assist with modest liturgical reforms. He tolerated the Portuguese presence with a courtesy that his detractors mistook for submission and his admirers recognised as strategy. 5

The strategy worked because both sides still needed each other. The Thomas Christians controlled the pepper at its source. The Portuguese controlled the ships that carried it to Lisbon. Mar Jacob understood that cooperation with the Portuguese on commercial matters bought him freedom on ecclesiastical ones. As long as pepper flowed, the Padroado could be kept at arm's length.

In 1524, the old bishop composed a letter to King Manuel I. He thanked the king for a subsidy. He asked, pointedly, for Portuguese help in restoring Christian commercial rights in Kodungallur and in building a fort there to protect the community's pepper trade from diversion to Calicut. He mentioned, almost in passing, that certain Portuguese had treated him badly. And he offered a quiet defence of his own sacramental authority, invoking the Dominican friar João Caro as a character witness against Penteado's calumnies. The letter revealed a man threading a needle: loyal enough to retain Portuguese protection, independent enough to refuse Portuguese jurisdiction. 6

The Burning of the Church

Yet even Mar Jacob could not prevent the erosion of his community's position at Kodungallur. The ancient metropolitan seat was becoming, year by year, less a centre of Thomas Christian life and more a Portuguese military outpost. In the 1520s, the Zamorin's Muslim merchants attacked the Portuguese factory at Kodungallur and burned the Thomas Christians' principal church in the conflagration. The Christians appealed repeatedly to successive Portuguese governors for help in rebuilding. Governor after governor promised assistance. Governor after governor died or was replaced before fulfilling the promise. The stone and mortar, collected for reconstruction, lay crumbling for years. 7

By the time the Portuguese finally erected a massive fortress at Kodungallur between 1523 and 1536, Governor Lopo Soares de Albergaria had already forced the construction of a stone fortress at Quilon (1518). These fortresses, now choking every major pepper-trading port of the Serra, served Portuguese commercial interests rather than Thomas Christian ones. The walls reduced Kodungallur to a garrison town. The Thomas Christians, whose ancestors had been the masters of that city's commerce for centuries, found themselves tenants in their own metropolitan seat.8

8.3 — THE WORLD OF THE CASADOS

If the Nazranis of the Serra was turning out to be one frontier the Portuguese could not control, another was emerging on the opposite coast.

A man named Diogo Fernandes was running the Marakkars (Chapter 4) of the Coromandel coast out of their money. He was a casado.

When Afonso de Albuquerque came to India, he confronted a demographic reality that would haunt the empire for the rest of its existence: there were not enough Portuguese to sustain it. His solution, as Chapter 6 described, was to encourage Portuguese soldiers to marry Indian women, creating the casados (married settlers) who formed the basic demographic unit of the colonial enterprise. These men were allotted petty trades to sustain them, and permitted to set up shops and manufacturing units in the fortified towns. 9

The policy solved the immediate demographic crisis. It also created a monster. Within a generation, the casados had become an autonomous merchant class operating far beyond the government's control. They wore local dress, spoke local languages, intermarried with local families, and traded freely alongside Hindus, Muslims, and Christians. They were Portuguese by baptismal register but Malabar or Coromandel by habit, loyalty, and commercial interest. They answered to no governor unless a governor's warship happened to be anchored in their harbour.

The Wild West of Bay

The Bay of Bengal became the casados' empire. Because the official Estado da Índia concentrated its naval and military resources on the western coast—the pepper route from Malabar to Lisbon—the eastern seaboard remained a fluid frontier. The casados filled this vacuum. They established trading settlements at Nagapattinam, Pulicat, Masulipatnam, and along the coast of Bengal, conducting a private commerce in textiles, rice, and spices that frequently rivalled the Crown's own trade. 10

These were not official Portuguese colonies. They existed by the tolerance of local rulers, particularly the Vijayanagara Empire, whose emperor Krishnadevaraya needed the Portuguese horse trade to sustain his wars against the Deccan sultanates. At the Battle of Raichur (1520), Krishnadevaraya's decisive victory over the Sultanate of Bijapur, Portuguese mercenaries and firearms played a minor but noted role, cementing the alliance of mutual convenience. 11

The casados also engaged in illicit activities, including smuggling, privateering, and piracy. By the late sixteenth century, this shadow network extended to Hooghly, where a casado enclave—initially established under Mughal tolerance during the reign of Akbar, evolved into a semi-autonomous centre of trade, privateering, and slave-raiding till Shah Jahan besieged and dismantled the settlement in 1632, bringing this eastern node of the Portuguese shadow world to a violent end. 11b

8.4 — THE TOMB ON THE OTHER COAST

It was on this wild eastern frontier, in 1517, Diogo Fernandes and his companions, trading at the nearby port of Pulicat, were guided overland by Christian merchants to a ruined brick chapel in Mylapore. They found a stone sepulchre said to be the burial place of the Apostle Thomas. A lamp was burning. The shrine was being maintained by a local Muslim custodian. Nearby was a second tomb, said to belong to an Ethiopian Christian who had accompanied the Apostle. The chapel was in disrepair, the church around it collapsed, but the veneration was alive, sustained by a shared custodianship that predated any European presence by centuries. 12

The Portuguese had known about the tomb since at least 1501, when Joseph the Indian described it to the courts of Lisbon and Rome. King Manuel was fascinated. In 1507, Viceroy Almeida sent four men to the Coromandel coast with specific instructions to gather information 'about the house of the Apostle St. Thomas which, according to what the Christians of Malabar had told the Portuguese, stood on that coast.' Two of the four died en route. The other two returned with a report but, it seems, did not personally visit the site. 13

Penteado himself, from Kodungallur on the opposite coast, had already learned from the Thomas Christians about the tomb's condition. He reported that 'the church had collapsed' and that only 'the tomb of the Apostle and another of an Abyssinian' still stood. 14

The delay in physically reaching the tomb was not indifference but logistics. Between 1500 and 1515, the Estado da Índia was fighting for its survival on the western coast. Ships could not be spared for an archaeological expedition to the opposite side of the peninsula. It was only after Goa (1510), Malacca (1511), and Hormuz (1515) were secured that the Portuguese had the breathing room to turn east.

For the Crown, Mylapore was a strategic turning point. Here was physical, tangible evidence linking the Portuguese enterprise to apostolic antiquity. The tomb placed Portuguese India in the same sacred geography as Santiago de Compostela. King Manuel had already ordered all Portuguese going to India to 'inquire closely into the affairs of these Christians.' The tomb gave that inquiry a focal point.

The Excavation and the Claim

Manuel died in 1521 before he could act on the news. His successor, João III, authorised an official expedition. Around 1523, the Crown’s representative on the Coromandel coast, identified in later accounts as Manuel de Frias, is said to have arrived at Mylapore to assert Portuguese authority over the shrine. The tomb was excavated. The Portuguese reported finding bones, a skull, and the head of a lance stained with blood. Whether these were genuinely apostolic relics or convenient artefacts produced to satisfy an imperial need, we do not know. The Portuguese did not discover the tomb. They annexed it. 15

The Tomb at Mylapore

The Portuguese Claim

  • Royal Initiative (1523): Under King João III, the Crown Factor Manuel de Frias arrives at Mylapore to assert Portuguese control over the apostolic shrine.
  • The Excavation Narrative: Portuguese accounts describe the opening of the tomb and the discovery of bones, a skull identified with the Apostle, and a lance head said to bear traces of blood. These findings are presented as material confirmation of the martyrdom of the Apostle Thomas.
  • Who Records It? The narrative is preserved in later chronicles, especially Gaspar Correia's Lendas da Índia (anecdotal, detail-rich, incorporating local traditions) and João de Barros's Décadas da Ásia (structured imperial history with a legitimizing tone). Together, they frame the event as a rediscovery validating Portuguese custodianship.
  • Relic Resolution: By locating relics at Mylapore, these accounts implicitly affirm that the Apostle’s remains still lay at the site, reinforcing its authority within the Portuguese Padroado.

Historical Reckoning

  • Pre-Portuguese Sacred Geography: The Mylapore shrine was already a long-standing pilgrimage centre. Local Christian memory and wider travel traditions had identified it as the site of martyrdom well before Portuguese intervention.
  • The Edessa Tradition: From the 4th century, Syriac Christianity—associated with the Church of the East—maintained that the relics of the Apostle had been translated out of India, creating a long-standing geographical tension.
  • Mechanism of Circumvention: Portuguese narratives avoid direct contradiction by implicitly adopting a model of partial translation: some relics transferred to Edessa, others remaining at the original tomb, authority anchored in the place of martyrdom. This reconciles competing traditions without explicit dispute.
  • Interpretive Conclusion: The Portuguese did not discover an unknown site. Through excavation narratives and selective historiography, they reinterpreted an existing sacred landscape—effectively appropriating apostolic memory while aligning it with Latin ecclesiastical and imperial claims.

The annexation had immediate consequences. The Portuguese rebuilt the shrine, walled off the surrounding area, and began constructing the European settlement of São Tomé de Meliapor over the ruins. The Christian merchants and the Muslim custodian who had maintained the site were gradually pushed out. Within decades, the tomb that had belonged to everyone belonged to Portugal.

The relic economy that drove this annexation was not cynical. In medieval and early modern Latin practice, physical apostolic relics were the currency of ecclesiastical legitimacy. An altar required a relic to be consecrated. A diocese required an apostolic connection to claim precedence. By physically possessing the bones of the Apostle Thomas, the Padroado acquired a theological trump card against the Thomas Christians' own claim to apostolic authority. The community could point to its traditions, its copper plates, its liturgical memory. The Portuguese could point to the bones.

8.5 — GOA: DAS CAPITAL

By the early 1540s, two “shadow worlds” —the Nazrani Serra and the Coromandel casados— lay beyond Portuguese control while Goa was being forged into the institutional centre that would eventually move to subjugate them.

Albuquerque's conquest of Goa in 1510 had been exceptionally violent. He ordered the systematic slaughter of the Muslim population as a calculated act of terror, while deliberately sparing the Hindus to drive a political wedge between the communities. The conquered island became sovereign Portuguese territory, unlike the coastal forts at Cochin and Kannur, which existed by the grace of local rajas. This sovereignty made Goa unique in the Estado da Índia: it was the one place where Portuguese law ran without qualification, where the Crown could build institutions answerable only to Lisbon. 16

▣ THE THALASSOCRACY: AN EMPIRE OF WATER

The modern imagination often assumes that when Albuquerque captured Goa, he conquered a vast province. He did not. The Estado da Índia was not a territorial empire; it was a thalassocracy, an empire of water built on fortified chokepoints and systematic naval control.

During the mid-sixteenth century, the actual landmass under absolute Portuguese jurisdiction was geographically small. Their sovereign territory was confined to the Velhas Conquistas (the Old Conquests): the central island of Tiswadi (where Goa city stood), and the adjacent districts of Bardez and Salcete, acquired formally by 1543. Everything else in the Estado da Índia was held through a chain of coastal fortresses that existed by treaty, by the tolerance of local rulers, or by the threat of bombardment. The Portuguese expanded inland into the Novas Conquistas (Ponda, Quepem, Sattari) only in the eighteenth century, long after the events of this narrative.

But the Portuguese did not need a massive territorial state to rule the East, because they possessed the cartaz. Any merchant vessel navigating the Indian Ocean was required to purchase one and pay duties at a Portuguese fort. A ship caught without this document was subject to confiscation; its crew risked slaughter or enslavement. The system converted the open ocean into a tollbooth and the Portuguese fleet into its enforcers.

The reach of this system is best illustrated by an extraordinary asymmetry. The Mughal Empire, which commanded armies of hundreds of thousands across the largest state in the subcontinent, possessed no blue-water navy. Consequently, even the Great Mughal was compelled to negotiate with the Portuguese for a cartaz to send the imperial fleet from Surat to the Red Sea for the annual Hajj pilgrimage. The Portuguese granted him one free pass per year for the royal ship. The most powerful ruler in India required a piece of paper from a garrison of a few hundred Europeans to exercise his religious obligations on his own ocean.

This naval reality is the mechanism that closed the world around the Thomas Christians. The Portuguese did not need to send an army into the dense interior of the Serra. By holding the coastal nodes of Quilon, Cochin and Kodungallur, and by patrolling the Arabian Sea with the cartaz system, they closed the door to the outside world. They could choke off the Thomas Christians' pepper exports whenever cooperation faltered. And far more devastatingly, they could blockade the sea lanes through which East Syrian bishops had reached Malabar for over a millennium. The cartaz was, in effect, a theological weapon: it could not change what the Thomas Christians believed, but it could starve them of the bishops who sustained what they practised.

The institutional machinery rose rapidly. Before 1534, a tiny island in the Atlantic had technically held spiritual jurisdiction over the entire Indian Ocean and Brazil: the Diocese of Funchal in Madeira was the sole episcopal seat covering all Portuguese overseas territories. The absurdity of governing churches from Cochin to Canton through a see on an Atlantic sugar island was resolved when the Diocese of Goa was erected in 1534 by Paul III through the bull Aequum Reputamus. Its jurisdiction was staggering: theoretically, it covered all Christians from the Cape of Good Hope to China. The diocese was funded through the Padroado contract: the Pope granted the Crown the right to nominate bishops and control ecclesiastical boundaries, and in return the Crown paid the salaries of the priests and built the churches. In 1557, Goa was elevated to an archdiocese, and the Diocese of Cochin was created as its suffragan. The Padroado now had a formal Latin hierarchy on the Thomas Christians' doorstep. 17

The Rigour of Mercy

Before the Inquisition arrived in 1560, the Goan state had already begun what the Portuguese called the Rigor da Misericórdia: the Rigour of Mercy. From the 1540s onward, Portuguese authorities increasingly targeted Hindu religious institutions in territories under their control: temples were demolished or appropriated, and their lands and revenues redirected to support churches and institutions such as the College of St. Paul Goa. Public Hindu rituals were progressively restricted, and temple personnel faced displacement or expulsion. At the same time, legal mechanisms were introduced to bring vulnerable minors—particularly orphans—under Christian guardianship, facilitating their baptism. 18

8.6 — THE TWO UNGOVERNABLE FRONTIERS

The College of Santiago

In the Serra, close on the heels of the fortress at Kodungallur, Franciscan Vicente de Lagos founded the College of Santiago (St. James) in 1541. Its purpose was simple: to educate Thomas Christian boys in Latin theology, Latin liturgy, and Latin discipline. The college attracted students readily. Parents, impressed by the quality of Western education and the effects of disciplined instruction on their sons' characters, sent their children without apparent hesitation. By 1551, it housed seventy boys and had already produced several ordained priests. 19

The college was a success in every respect except the one that mattered. The Latinised priests it produced were rejected by the Thomas Christian parishes. Not a single graduate of the College of Santiago was permitted to serve in a church of the Serra. The college demonstrated what Penteado's letter had already suggested: the community would accept education but not transformation. 20

Mar Jacob watched all this unfold. He said nothing publicly. But some time between 1542 and 1543, the old bishop quietly withdrew from Kodungallur and retired to the Franciscan monastery of St. Antony in Cochin. He had lived among the Franciscans as a friend for decades. Now he lived among them permanently. Whether this retirement was voluntary or forced remains debated. His permanent residence, Kodungallur, had become a Portuguese centre and less a centre of his Christians. The Portuguese officials had ceased to show him any regard. He was very old, perhaps eighty years of age, and increasingly unable to cope with the heavy tasks of governing a scattered community of Nazranis. 21

The Coromandel Shadow World

On the Coromandel coast and across the Bay of Bengal, the casados had built a private commercial empire that rivalled the Crown's own. They paid no ecclesiastical tithes. They lived outside the parish system. They answered to local rulers, not to the archbishop of Goa. And they were now sitting on the most important relic in Portuguese India: the tomb of the Apostle, which the Crown had physically secured but could not effectively govern because Mylapore was a casado settlement, not a Padroado garrison.

The pepper route from the Serra to the Coromandel coast connected these two shadow worlds. When Thomas Christian pepper growers later resisted Padroado demands, they diverted their crop across the Western Ghats to the Coromandel ports, where the casados carried it to Bengal. The two ungovernable frontiers were economically linked. 22

The Portuguese needed an instrument that could penetrate both worlds simultaneously: one that could enter the Serra's kalaris and convince the cattanars through theological argument, and one that could impose ecclesiastical discipline on the casados through institutional presence. The mendicant orders had tried and failed. The Franciscans had befriended Mar Jacob but could not transform his community. Penteado had been expelled. Vicente de Lagos's college was producing graduates whom no parish would accept. The secular clergy in Goa were too few, too poorly educated, and too compromised by the colonial economy to mount a sustained intellectual campaign.

What was needed was a break. Two came within a few years of each other. Francis Xavier and Jesuits stepped off the ship at Goa on 6 May 1542 with missionary zeal that would reshape the world of casados and conversos. And in the Franciscan monastery at Cochin, around 1550, Mar Jacob died. The old bishop's four decades of courteous immovability had ended. The archdeaconate was now alone.

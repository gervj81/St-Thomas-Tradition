From Reconciliation to the Coonan Cross — 1624–1659

The Wreckage

"I am at peace with my archdeacon, and I wish to keep it so at all costs, for the spiritual good of this church, whatever annoyance he may give me. For I have a long experience of the evils that will befall otherwise."

— Stephen de Britto SJ

Chapter 11 closed with Roz dying at Kodungallur on 18 February 1624 and Archdeacon George of the Cross taking the see in independent administration during the interregnum. George set himself, with what one chronicler called commendable zeal, to ending the schism that he himself had created during his long quarrel with Roz. By the time Stephen de Britto's bulls arrived and Britto could take possession of the see, George had a church free of open dissension to hand him. Britto received an enthusiastic welcome.

The next twenty-nine years are about what happened to that delivered settlement — a settlement that was never stable. It held the rival claims of archbishop and archdeacon in an uneasy suspension that worked only so long as neither side forced the question of whose authority was final. They involve two archbishops, two archdeacons, one viceregal reconciliation that resolved nothing, one Eastern bishop arrested at Mylapore and sent to Goa never to return, and a community that finally, on 3 January 1653, decided that it had been patient long enough.

This chapter is about a settlement built by patience and held together by patience, and about the strain that patience could not, in the end, absorb. It is also about the community whose own patience finally ran out.

The Friendship That Was Not

The Jesuit Annual Letter of 1626 reported that the new Archbishop of Kodungallur was living in his see with the Archdeacon, in apparent peace, giving numerous proofs of humility and of all virtues. Stephen de Britto had been Roz's coadjutor since 1620 and had pleaded with Roz for years to soften his treatment of George of the Cross. Now, with Roz buried and George at last in the office's working partnership rather than its formal subordination, Britto could implement the policy he had advocated. The new policy, in the Jesuit chronicler's framing, was clemency and kindness, by which Britto believed he could gain far more than Roz had gained by severity. Some thought clemency would only encourage the Archdeacon in his perversity. Others thought it might at last produce the persuaded community Roz had failed to produce. 1

The clemency was real. Britto did not appoint a Jesuit Vicar General over the Archdeacon's head. He did not require the cattanars to reaffirm the Diamper professions of faith. He did not press the post-Tridentine episcopal theology that had so corroded Roz's relationship with the same man. He governed, in his first years, alongside the Archdeacon rather than against him. The Serra under early Britto was, on the surface, what Roz had wanted in 1597 and could not produce in 1601: a Latin bishop and an indigenous Archdeacon working together rather than past each other.

But the clemency had a structural limit. Beneath the conciliatory tone, Britto was still maintaining all the claims Roz had made for the episcopate. He believed himself, as bishop, to hold sacramental and jurisdictional authority that no Archdeacon could constitutionally share. The clemency was a method of governance, not a redistribution of authority. The Archdeacon, who had read the Roz years closely, knew the difference. He accepted the warmer tone but did not mistake it for a renunciation of Tridentine episcopal claims. He governed his community by the rights he had always governed it by, and he allowed the Archbishop to govern by the rights the Archbishop claimed, and where the two systems conflicted, he kept his own counsel and waited.

By 1630, Britto had stopped being able to sustain the illusion. He wrote to the Archbishop of Goa in that year:

"Now during the six years that I have governed this church, in spite of the fact that I have always done all in my power to conciliate him in everything that was not against my conscience, I find him as rebellious and as little submissive as he has ever been in the past. Several times already I have been on the point of breaking with him, unable to suffer any longer his innumerable lies, and the wickedness with which he pursues me without rhyme or reason." 2

The friendship, in other words, was not friendship. It was a working coexistence that both men maintained because both men had concluded that the alternatives were worse. The Archdeacon could go on calling himself the Archbishop's friend, and the Archbishop could go on calling himself the Archdeacon's friend, and both could write letters to their respective superiors detailing how the other was impossible. This was not hypocrisy. It was the working settlement.

The Archdeacon Works The System

What George of the Cross did during the Britto years, alongside maintaining the local accommodation, was something he had not been able to do under Roz. He worked the wider Iberian system against itself.

Behind the Archdeacon's options lay a recent change in Rome. In 1622, as Chapter 11 noted, the papacy had established the Sacred Congregation for the Propagation of the Faith (Propaganda Fide), its instrument for reclaiming direct control of the global missions from the Iberian crowns. By commissioning its own missionaries under the new title of missionarius apostolicus, answerable to Rome rather than to the Padroado, the Congregation created a counterweight to the Iberian monopoly over the Asian church. For the Archdeacon it offered what he had lacked since Diamper: a source of ecclesiastical legitimacy outside the Padroado's hierarchy, and one he could turn against it. 3

He had several handles. The first was the shift away from Jesuit dominance that the previous chapter traced, a shift that ran through both Rome and the Crown, though unevenly. At the level of senior appointments and missionary policy, the ground was moving against the Society: Propaganda Fide was building its own channels, the Mendicant orders were pressing their critique of Jesuit accommodation, and even the Padroado had begun to favour Mendicant and other candidates for Indian sees over Jesuit ones. What had not moved was the Crown's grip on the Malabar mission field specifically, where it would continue to shield the Jesuit monopoly. The Archdeacon's opportunity lay in the gap between the two: in setting the broader drift against the Jesuits to work on a mission field the Crown still meant to keep Jesuit. In 1628 he used it to send a confidential dispatch to the Apostolic Collector in Lisbon. The dispatch was a sustained attack on the Jesuits in the Serra. It urged that the next archbishop should not be a Jesuit. It put forward a specific candidate: Francis Donati, a Florentine Dominican who had been appointed missionarius apostolicus by Pope Urban VIII in 1624 and who had, by the time the Archdeacon wrote, learned Malayalam in Cochin and, under Propaganda Fide auspices and with the local raja's protection, built a church and opened a school for Syrian clerics at Kaduthuruthy. 4

The choice of Donati was deliberate. He was not a Padroado missionary but one of Propaganda Fide's own, and a Dominican rather than a Jesuit. He spoke Syriac, of which Garcia would later be unable to learn a single word, and in this he held over Britto an advantage the Archdeacon thought decisive: Donati could participate in the community's liturgy while the Archbishop could not. The archdeacon's dispatch was thus an attempt to set Rome's alternative, and the Padroado's Mendicant orders, against the Jesuit missions, which sat in a no man's land within a three-way jurisdictional fault line between Rome, the Spanish crown, and the Portuguese Padroado. It was a sophisticated piece of political reading.

It very nearly worked. The Apostolic Collector forwarded the letter to Propaganda. Propaganda was sympathetic. There was real consideration in Rome of appointing Donati as coadjutor to Britto, to establish a non-Jesuit succession in the Serra. But the Society, had one final card. 5

The Jesuits appealed to the crown and obtained an ordinance reserving the affairs of the St Thomas Christians of the Serra to the Society and barring every other order from the affairs of the Nazranis. The Dominican Bishop of Cochin would still be protesting the ordinance to Rome more than a decade later. The episode was an early front in a longer institutional conflict. From its founding in 1622 Propaganda Fide had set out to circumvent the Portuguese Padroado through its own missionaries and vicars apostolic, and the Crown and the Portuguese Padroado had answered, here as elsewhere, by closing the field to all but its own. That jurisdictional struggle would outlast everyone in this chapter, hardening over the following two centuries into the rival hierarchies and double jurisdiction whose final reckoning came only with the suppression of the Padroado sees in the nineteenth century. In 1634 Donati was returning toward Rome when he was captured by pirates and put to death. Paulinus a Sancto Bartholomaeo, the Carmelite missionary who served in Malabar in the later eighteenth century and became one of the early historians of its church, expressed the view that if Donati had been appointed coadjutor to Britto, the schism might never have taken place. The Archdeacon's most effective political move had succeeded in identifying the right alternative and had failed to install it. 6

Appealing to the Crown

The Archdeacon did not stop. In 1632 he wrote a long letter to Philip IV of Spain and Portugal, accusing the Jesuits of withholding amounts due to the cattanars from the resources of the Portuguese Crown and arguing more broadly that the Jesuit administration of the Serra was corrupt. The accusations were unfounded. The financial difficulties he named were the consequence of the Dutch ascendancy and the weakening of Portuguese commerce, not of Jesuit malfeasance. But the letter mattered structurally. It located the conflict in the Crown's own procedures and asked the Crown to intervene against the Society. The Archdeacon was building a documentary archive of grievance that could be activated whenever the political environment shifted. 7

The Compromising Document

The most dangerous of George of the Cross's manoeuvres came late in Britto's tenure and remains, to this day, partially obscure in the record. At some point of time and in some manner that is not clear, the Archdeacon persuaded the Archbishop to sign a document. The document was, in effect, a comprehensive constitutional surrender. It returned to the Archdeacon practically all the powers Roz had stripped from him. For the future, the Archbishop was not to confer holy orders, appoint priests to parishes, excommunicate or suspend delinquent priests or laymen, in fact not to do anything of importance, without the consent of the Archdeacon. 8

The document, taken at its word, made the Archdeacon the working ruler of the church of the Serra. The Archbishop became a sacramental head whose every administrative act required the Archdeacon's prior agreement. This was not the dual-governance arrangement of the pre-Diamper period; it was something more comprehensive than that, because it made the indigenous head's consent a condition of the foreign bishop's work. It was the inverse of Diamper.

Britto realised, eventually, what he had signed. In 1636 he made an attempt to recover the document. The Archdeacon, who had read the Britto years closely too, had anticipated this. He surrendered copies. He kept the originals. The originals would surface again at moments when the Archdeacon's leverage required them, and the Archbishop's successors would have to accept that the document existed and could be produced. 9

How Britto came to sign such a thing remains the historiographical puzzle of his tenure. The most plausible reconstruction is that the document emerged from one of the periodic crises in their relationship in which the Archbishop, exhausted, in a difficult moment with his cattanars, accepted a settlement the Archdeacon presented as restoring peace and only later read what the settlement actually said. Whatever the mechanism, the document existed. By the late 1630s, the working constitution of the Serra had been documented in a form that gave the Archdeacon institutional authority Roz would never have conceded.

The Final Years of Peace

Old age took both protagonists. By the late 1630s the Archdeacon was past eighty and Britto was past seventy. Neill records that there seems to have been an unspoken agreement between them that they would not quarrel any more. The last few years of their respective lives were passed in peace. George of the Cross died on 25 July 1640. Britto, before his own death, appointed George's nephew Parambil Tumi, known to the Portuguese as Thomas de Campo, as the new Archdeacon. Britto followed the old Archdeacon to the grave on 3 December 1641. 10

The Serra at Britto's death had a settlement. The settlement was contradictory and unstable. The Archbishop's claims and the Archdeacon's claims could not both be true at the same time. The compromising document existed, was kept hidden, and was known to exist. But it was a settlement that had held for seventeen years, that produced no schism, and that left a community with which a successor could have worked. What the successor did with the settlement is the next story.

The Wrong Man

The authorities had made another of their grievous mistakes. They had chosen as Britto's coadjutor and successor Francis Garcia of the Society of Jesus.

Garcia's portrait, as reproduced in the standard biography, shows the face of an inquisitor. The thin lips are pursed almost in a scowl. The character it suggests is one entirely devoid of flexibility or humour: obstinate rather than resolute, narrow, persistent, and unforgiving. 11

He was fifty-seven years old at his consecration on 1 November 1637 in Goa. He had been in India twenty-five years, but he had spent most of them as a teacher of theology rather than as a working missionary among the people whose church he was now to govern. His experience of southern India was limited to two years on the Fishery Coast and two in Cochin. He did not speak Malayalam. He did not speak Syriac. He had no working acquaintance with the Thomas Christian community, its customs, its parish structures, its families, its memory, or its self-understanding.

Britto, on hearing of the appointment, wrote to the Jesuit General on 1 January 1633 in terms that were studiously charitable but unmistakable in their reservation:

"I am glad, for he has great qualities, and henceforth I can be at peace with regard to the future of this church. But I would have been happier, had he been younger, so as to be able to learn the language, and to acquire some experience of this people, so different from the rest. I am at peace with my archdeacon, and I wish to keep it so at all costs, for the spiritual good of this church, whatever annoyance he may give me. For I have a long experience of the evils that will befall otherwise." 12

The letter is a warning composed in the form of a congratulation. Britto, the Jesuit who had spent two decades in close work with the community, was telling his superiors that the man they had chosen was the wrong age, the wrong temperament, and the wrong formation for the office, and that the working settlement Britto had built with great difficulty would now have to be defended against him.

The letter was not heeded. Garcia took possession of the see on Britto's death in December 1641.

The Power Struggle Begins

Garcia's accession tells the chapter's story not through a single act but through a posture.

He took peaceful possession of the see on 3 December 1641, and he arrived having decided how he meant to govern. He would exercise the whole jurisdiction of the archbishopric himself and exclude the Archdeacon from ecclesiastical governance altogether. He did not regard the powers Britto had conceded as powers he was bound to honour. The new Archdeacon Thomas, who had inherited his uncle's office, his uncle's papers, and his uncle's reading of the situation, was equally clear. He held the original concession document. He knew what it said: that under it the Archbishop could appoint no priest, depose no priest, and conduct no important affair of the Serra without the Archdeacon's prior consent. He was determined to defend the constitutional position that document confirmed. From the first months of the new archiepiscopate, the two men were set against each other over who governed the Serra.

A Standing Deadlock

The Archdeacon's office had been understood for centuries as that of legatus natus, the indigenous head of the community whose authority derived from immemorial custom and whose place in the diocesan administration was not in the gift of any foreign bishop. Britto had governed for seventeen years by leaving that authority substantively undisturbed, whatever the theory. Garcia would not. He meant to be, in fact as well as in title, the sole ruler of the Serra, and that intention, pressed against an Archdeacon equally resolved to yield nothing, produced not a rupture but a grinding stalemate that ran through the whole of the 1640s. 13

Thomas, then less than thirty years old, did what his uncle would have done. He governed his community by the powers the concession document confirmed, produced the document when leverage required, and refused to concede that anything had changed in the constitutional position of his office. He was, institutionally, on firm ground; the document existed and said what it said. But he did not, in these years, break with the Archbishop. He resisted within the relationship, building, as the decade wore on, the external alliances that resistance would need.

Garcia, for his part, refused to see the document as binding upon him. He held that Britto's concessions had been improvident, that no Archbishop could licitly bind his successors to such an arrangement, and that the proper relation between bishop and archdeacon was the post-Tridentine relation in which the archdeacon held his office at the bishop's pleasure and exercised it under the bishop's direction. Garcia was, in his own theological framework, neither novel nor extreme. He held the standard Tridentine position on episcopal authority that Roz had also held and that Britto had quietly suspended for seventeen years. Garcia simply refused to suspend it. 14

No authority could break the deadlock. The Bishop of Cochin, the religious orders Thomas was beginning to cultivate, and eventually the Portuguese Viceroy himself would each be drawn into the quarrel, and none of them could resolve it. The Archdeacon was willing to come to working terms with Garcia provided his constitutional position was respected; Garcia would not part with an inch of his jurisdiction. There was no middle position the two men could both occupy, and the failure of every external intervention only confirmed it. The first of those interventions came from the highest secular authority in Portuguese Asia. 15

The Viceregal Farce Of 1645

In December 1645 the Portuguese Viceroy Philip de Mascarenhas, on his way back to Goa from Ceylon, stopped at Cochin and decided to settle the matter himself. He brought together Garcia, the Archdeacon Thomas, and the Bishop of Cochin (who, as ever in the Padroado's internal divisions, was sympathetic to the Archdeacon against his Jesuit colleague at Kodungallur). The Viceroy required an agreement.

Garcia drafted the agreement on 12 December 1645. The agreement had the form of a settlement that safeguarded the Archdeacon's rights. The Archdeacon was induced to sign it. In substance, the document gave very little away. The crucial third article read:

"Before the archbishop hands over to the vicars the papers of their appointment, these papers will be passed to the archdeacon, who will without demur affix his signature. But even if he refuses to sign these papers, the vicars will all the same take possession of their parishes and govern them." 16

The structure of the article is the entire story. The Archdeacon would be permitted to sign appointment papers; if he refused, the appointments would proceed anyway. His signature was rendered meaningless by the explicit clause that nullified it. Garcia later affirmed in writing that none of the concessions made to the Archdeacon referred to matters of jurisdiction. The Archdeacon's signature on parish appointments was, by Garcia's reading, a courtesy rather than a constitutional act.

Thomas was equal to the situation. The moment the agreement was signed, he had it translated into Malayalam and sent copies to all the churches of the Serra. The Malayalam translation was deliberately doctored. Where the Portuguese text gave him courtesy without authority, the Malayalam text gave him constitutional veto. On the central question, the Malayalam version read:

"When it is a question of conferring orders or appointing vicars or doing any other business, it has been agreed that nothing can be done unless the archbishop and I get together to treat of them." 17

This was not the agreement Garcia had drafted. It was the agreement the Archdeacon had wanted. The Malayalam version circulated through the Thomas Christian parishes as the authoritative version of the viceregal settlement. The community read it as their bishop's submission to their Archdeacon's veto. Garcia, when he discovered the discrepancy, raged. The viceregal reconciliation had collapsed into two competing versions of itself, in two languages, neither of which described an actual working arrangement.

The episode has been read as evidence of the Archdeacon's bad faith. It is also evidence of his desperation. He had been handed a document that was a sham reconciliation, and he had converted it, by translation, into the kind of reconciliation he had needed. That the Malayalam version misrepresented the Portuguese was true. That the Portuguese was already a misrepresentation of what reconciliation required was also true. Both were happening at once.

The Furtado Provocation

In 1647 Garcia did the second thing that should not have been done.

He appointed Father Jerome Furtado as Vicar General. For six years Garcia had governed the Serra over the Archdeacon's objections, but he had not, until now, filled the single most symbolically charged office in the administration over the Archdeacon's head. The Vicar Generalship was precisely the post whose seizure carried the most weight, because it placed a Latin appointee in the Archdeacon's own sphere of governance. It was the gesture Roz had made in 1618 with the Vaipicota Rector, the insult the second-generation Jesuits had spent the rest of Roz's life trying to walk back, and the one Britto had refused to give in seventeen years. Garcia now gave it. The act was deliberate and public, and it communicated, more plainly than any document, that the Archbishop did not consider the Archdeacon's constitutional position binding upon him. 18

Thomas had now reached a conclusion he had been holding off. The Garcia administration would never accommodate his office. No domestic procedure, no viceregal mediation, no inherited document, no alliance with the disaffected orders would produce a working relationship with the Archbishop while Garcia held the see. Thomas had spent six years and the whole political inheritance of his uncle's settlement holding the line against Garcia from within the relationship. The Furtado appointment told him the line could not be held there any longer.

What Thomas did next is what made the Coonan Cross possible. He took the unusual step of writing to the Eastern patriarchs directly: the Coptic Patriarch in Alexandria, the Jacobite Patriarch of Antioch, and the East Syrian Patriarch in Mesopotamia. He asked each of them to send a bishop to the Serra. The petition was, in substance, the same petition the community had sworn in 1597 in the days following Mar Abraham's death, the petition Rome had ignored. By the late 1640s the petition had passed beyond Rome and beyond the Padroado. The Archdeacon was asking the Eastern churches themselves to provide what the Latin hierarchy would not. 19

No reply came from any of the three patriarchs. The communication had probably been intercepted or had been received at courts unable to act. For four years the Serra continued under Garcia's increasingly autocratic administration. The compromising document remained in the Archdeacon's hands but had become institutionally inoperative. Garcia governed by ignoring it. Thomas governed by withholding cooperation from anything he had not approved. The community grew, by all contemporary accounts, increasingly disenchanted with the Latin hierarchy.

Then, in August 1652, an Eastern bishop landed at Mylapore.

The Bishop At Mylapore

The man who arrived at Mylapore was not what the Archdeacon's appeal had asked for. He was something the situation could nonetheless turn into.

His name was Ahatallah. He had been born at Aleppo around 1590. He had been ordained a Monophysite Jacobite priest, had risen to the bishopric of Damascus before 1632, and had then, in that same year, made his submission to Rome. He had spent more than a year in Rome itself, learning Italian fluently. He had asked to be returned to his own country with the mission of bringing the Jacobite Patriarch into Roman obedience. After his return, in circumstances that remain obscure, he claimed to have been elected Patriarch under the title Ignatius, borne by all the Jacobite Patriarchs of Antioch, though he could not occupy the see because of Turkish opposition. He was apparently sent to Persia to look after the churches there. By 1646 he was in Egypt, writing to Propaganda Fide. The reply did not arrive in time. 20

While Ahatallah was waiting in Cairo, Thomas's letter to the Coptic Patriarch arrived. The Coptic Patriarch, with no one else available to send and a letter from a faraway Indian community asking for episcopal help, suggested to Ahatallah that he might undertake the work of the Serra. Ahatallah accepted. He travelled overland and by sea, made his way to Surat, introduced himself to the Capuchin missionaries there, and then, fearing that he would be handed over to the Inquisition, slipped onto a Dutch ship and sailed for Mylapore. He arrived in August 1652.

Mylapore had a Portuguese fortress and a Jesuit college, but the surrounding country was held by a local Hindu raja and was not under Padroado civil jurisdiction. The British had been at Madras since 1640, five or six miles down the coast. The Jesuits suspected Ahatallah from the moment he arrived. He was apprehended and committed to their custody, though, as Neill notes, they treated him kindly and placed few restrictions on his freedom, which is why he remained accessible to visitors. He told them that his full title was Ignatius, Patriarch of the whole of India and of China, and that he had come with full powers from the Pope. 21

The claim was a mixture of substance and exaggeration. Ahatallah was a real bishop. His submission to Rome had been accepted years earlier. But the Coptic Patriarch had no authority to commission him for India, and he carried no papal authorisation for any Indian work. His status in the Serra would be irregular by canon law on any reading. But irregularity is not nullity. Ahatallah was an Eastern bishop in Roman communion who had taken a commission from the wrong Eastern see. He was canonically irregular but not canonically void. 22

The Call of the Serra

Three Malabar clerics, in Mylapore on other business, called on him. One of them, Zachariah Cherian Unni, spent time in conversation with him. They left with a letter from Ahatallah to the heads of the Thomas Christian community. The Archdeacon Thomas received the news with what must have been astonishment. The petition of 1597, repeated in 1647 and unanswered, had at last received a response. The community had its Eastern bishop.

The Decision At Cochin Harbour

The Jesuits at Mylapore, having decided that Ahatallah's claims required formal investigation by the Inquisition at Goa, put him aboard a Portuguese ship for the southwest coast. The ship's route would pass Cochin. Word of Ahatallah's presence in transit reached the Serra. The community concluded what was, on its face, correct: that their bishop was passing within reach.

The Archdeacon assembled a delegation of clergy and laity at Diamper, the same town at which the 1599 synod had taken place. The delegation issued a request to the Archbishop. The request was that Garcia permit Ahatallah to land at Cochin, that the community be allowed to examine his credentials, and that the question of his canonical status be settled in the presence of those whom it most concerned. The ecclesiastical and civil authorities of Cochin, including the Bishop of Cochin and the Captain of the city, supported the request. 23

Garcia refused.

The reasoning Garcia gave was technically correct. He stated that even if Ahatallah had been sent by the Pope, he could not take possession of any see in the Padroado without the prior presentation of the King of Portugal. The pase regio rule that Charles V had used to neutralise Sublimis Deus in 1537 (Chapter 10) applied. By the canon law and by the diplomatic norms governing the Iberian Crown's relationship with Rome, Ahatallah had no admissible standing in any Padroado territory until the King of Portugal had approved his commission, and he had no commission from Rome to begin with.

The reasoning was correct. The decision was a wreckage.

Garcia was aware that the situation was no longer merely about Ahatallah. By 1652, complaints about his administration had reached Rome. He had refused to admit Carmelite missionaries into the Serra against Propaganda Fide's explicit ruling. The Mendicant orders had spent two decades arguing that the Padroado's anti-accommodation hierarchy in Asia was producing exactly the kind of community alienation now visible at Cochin harbour. Rome was capable of meeting high-handedness with high-handedness, as Garcia knew. It was just possible that the unexpected had happened and that Ahatallah did carry genuine Roman papers. If he did, what would become of Garcia's monarchical rule of the Serra? 24

The Archdeacon Thomas, on the other hand, was equally wary. He had read Ahatallah's letter and, being no fool, had spotted the improbabilities in the man's story. The Serra still remembered Mar Joseph Sulaqa and the cycle of Eastern bishops who had arrived with conflicting credentials. Thomas wanted Ahatallah to land. He wanted him to be examined. But he wanted the examination to happen in the Serra, in the presence of the community, where the legitimacy of any verdict would be local rather than imposed from Goa.

Both men feared, for opposite reasons, what a public examination would produce. Thomas feared that Ahatallah might prove a fraud. Garcia feared that he might prove genuine. Their fears collided over the same question: what authority, foreign or local, would pronounce the verdict?

The captain of the Portuguese fleet decided. Without consulting either the Archbishop or the Archdeacon, he ordered the ship not to enter Cochin harbour at all. The fleet sailed on for Goa. Ahatallah was on board. The Thomas Christians, who had assembled at the Cochin shore in their thousands to see their bishop, were left waiting on the beach.

To prevent the riot that the captain feared the disappointment would produce, the Portuguese authorities released a story: Ahatallah, they said, had drowned. He had fallen overboard in Cochin harbour. The story was almost certainly fabricated. Ahatallah was alive on the ship. He would be sent on from Goa to Lisbon and from there toward Rome. He would die in Paris in 1654, en route. The traditions that he was burned at the stake by the Inquisition in Goa are false, propagated by Müllbauer in 1852 and uncritically followed since. The drowning rumour was a Portuguese cover story to avoid violence at Cochin. 25

The Thomas Christians believed the drowning rumour. They believed it for the rest of the seventeenth century and well into the eighteenth. They had reasons to believe it. The Portuguese had spent fifty years preventing communication between the Serra and the Eastern patriarchates. They had searched ships, intercepted letters, and arrested travellers to enforce that prevention. That they would have drowned an Eastern bishop in their own harbour to avoid letting him land was, on the available evidence, exactly the kind of thing the Portuguese would do. The fact that, in this particular case, they had not done it was less convincing than the pattern of their conduct. 26

The community, gathered at the Cochin shore and turning back into the Serra without the bishop they had assembled to greet, had made up its mind. Two weeks later, on 3 January 1653, they assembled again.

The Coonan Cross

The granite cross outside the church of Mattancherry, on the southern edge of Cochin, leaned slightly to one side. The community had always called it the Coonan Cross — coonan, in Malayalam, meaning bent or slanting. It would lean considerably more after what happened on the afternoon of 3 January 1653.

Thousands of Thomas Christians assembled at the church. The number is variously estimated; the surviving sources do not give a precise count, but the gathering was beyond what Mattancherry could hold inside its walls. The clergy and laity gathered in the church and the courtyard. A long rope was tied to the stone arms of the cross and passed back through the assembly so that those who could not physically touch the granite could touch the rope and so participate in what was about to be done. 27

The Archdeacon Thomas led the assembly. He read aloud the substance of the oath that the gathering would swear. The oath was specific. It was not a renunciation of the Catholic faith. It was not a separation from Rome. It was a renunciation of two specific authorities under whom the community had been placed, and by whom the community considered itself to have been wronged.

The first renunciation was of the Society of Jesus. The Jesuits, whom the Thomas Christians had taken to calling the Paulists, were sworn against by name. The community swore that they would no longer obey the Jesuits in any matter touching the church of the Serra.

The second renunciation was of Francis Garcia, Archbishop of Kodungallur. The community swore that they would no longer recognise his authority. He was, by the act of the oath itself, deposed.

The granite cross bent further as thousands of hands pulled on the rope. The Coonan Cross at Mattancherry stands today still bent, the slant deepened by a moment in 1653 in which a community decided that it had been patient long enough. The archive breaks its frame. After decades of petitions, translations, forged settlements, intercepted bishops, and imposed jurisdiction, the community acts in public space rather than in the documentary channels that had failed it.

The Alangad Consecration

The oath was a renunciation. It was not yet a constitution. A community that has rejected its bishop must, if it is to remain a church, find another bishop. The Archdeacon Thomas and his counsellors gathered at Alangad on 22 May 1653 to do this. The choice of Alangad mattered: it was an old Thomas Christian centre with deep memory and strong local autonomy, the same town from which George of the Cross had assisted the Armenian bishop's escape in 1610.

Twelve cattanars were gathered. The Archdeacon Thomas was placed at the centre. The twelve laid their hands on him, the hand-laying being administered, in the later accounts, by one of his closest counsellors, the cattanar Anjilimoottil Ittithommen of Kallisseri. A document was placed on his head: a letter, real or forged, purporting to be from Ahatallah, granting authorisation for the community to choose a bishop for itself. The letter became, in the absence of any other available papal or patriarchal commission, the proxy authorisation. The twelve cattanars proclaimed Thomas the Metropolitan of the Thomas Christians under the name Mar Thomas I. 28

The act was, by the universal law of the Church, both invalid and illicit. Twelve presbyters cannot confer episcopal orders at all: the power to consecrate a bishop belongs to bishops alone, and no bishop took part. Ahatallah's letter, if authentic, was at most an authorisation to choose a bishop, not a power to supply the consecrator the rite required. The community knew this. The archdeacon knew this. The cattanars knew this. They proceeded anyway. The consecration was an act of defiance, a refusal of the Latinisation and the non-Syrian jurisdiction that the community perceived had imprisoned them for fifty-four years. They proceeded knowing the defect, and accepted it as the price of self-government.

Mar Thomas I, the new Metropolitan, immediately began to perform episcopal functions. He ordained priests. He appointed vicars. He gave dispensations in cases of marriage impediment. He governed the Serra as the Archbishop of the Thomas Christians had once governed it, as Mar Abraham had governed it before Diamper, and as the Mar of the East had governed it for centuries before the Portuguese arrived. Most of the community of the Serra recognised him as their legitimate leader, but at the same time sensed the shadow of invalid orders. 29

as far as the community was concerned, the 'Syrians of Malabar' had separated, officially, from those who submitted themselves to the 'foreign' missionaries. The Kodungallur-Angamaly see under Garcia retained, by the most generous later estimates, perhaps a quarter of the community's parishes. The remainder followed Mar Thomas I.

The schism that Roz had spent his life preventing, that Britto had spent his episcopate negotiating around, and that Garcia had spent his episcopate provoking, had now happened. It would never be reversed.

The Wreckage And Its Meaning

Garcia died at Kodungallur on 3 September 1659, having governed for eighteen years a Serra that had largely ceased to obey him. The Carmelite Joseph Sebastiani had arrived in 1657 as an Apostolic Commissary under Propaganda Fide auspices, charged by Rome with reconciling the 'schismatic' community to the Roman Church through a non-Padroado route. He had since returned to Rome, where, in the strictest secrecy, he was consecrated Titular Bishop of Hierapolis on 15 December 1659 and made the first Vicar Apostolic of Malabar under direct Propaganda jurisdiction, the vicariate that would in time evolve into the Archdiocese of Verapoly. The consecration was a calculated Propaganda countermove rather than a response to the vacancy at Kodungallur, for Garcia's death cannot yet have been known in Rome; the design was to set a bishop over the Serra by a route the Padroado did not control, rather than to wait upon a new archbishop of Kodungallur, whose presentation belonged to the Portuguese Crown. Sebastiani was, in form, the rebuke to the Padroado that Garcia had spent his episcopate refusing to acknowledge. He arrived too late.

By 1663 Sebastiani had succeeded in reconciling a substantial portion of the community to Rome through Alexander de Campo, a cousin of Mar Thomas I. The Dutch had taken Cochin in January 1663, and in the brief interval between the conquest and his own expulsion Sebastiani consecrated Mar Alexander as Vicar Apostolic on 1 February 1663. The community split between those who followed Mar Alexander into communion with Rome (the Pazhayakūr, the old party) and those who followed Mar Thomas I (the Puthankūr, the new party). The schism that the Coonan Cross had announced was now structural and would persist into the modern era. 30

The End of the Padroado Settlement

The chapter began with Britto and his settlement. It is worth, at the end, asking what that settlement had been.

It had not been a permanent reconciliation. Britto and George of the Cross had not loved each other, and the compromising document was a shaky achievement built out of mutual exhaustion and old age. But it had held. The settlement was maintained by a willingness, on both sides, to accept that neither could remove the other and that working coexistence was preferable to the alternatives. Both men understood the other's position. Neither tried to abolish it. The compromising document was a written acknowledgment that the two systems of authority, Latin episcopal and indigenous archidiaconal, had to operate in parallel because neither could absorb the other without destroying the church.

Garcia rejected the settlement. He did so not because the settlement was unworkable, but because his theological formation, his temperament, and his lack of local experience all pointed him toward refusing it. He had not been formed inside the Serra. He did not speak its languages. He had not lived through the Roz years and did not know what they had cost. He held the post-Tridentine episcopal theology in its full unmodified form and saw the compromising document as an improvident concession that could be canonically dismissed. He governed by the theology and watched the community walk out from under him.

The contradiction had been there from the beginning, and the accommodation never resolved it. It managed it — year by year, by declining to force the question of whose authority was final. That management was the whole of the achievement, and it was a real one: Britto's seventeen years, and before them Roz's painful late-life reconciliation with George of the Cross, proved that a Latin bishop could govern the Serra for as long as he was willing to leave the unanswerable question unasked. The settlement did not fall under its own weight. It was Garcia's adamancy that forced the question the accommodation had survived by avoiding — and once it was forced, the answer was the Coonan Cross. The patience that had built the settlement and the patience that had borne it ran out together.

The Iberian Metropole had built, across one and a half centuries, an apparatus designed to make this exact ending impossible. The Council of Trent had standardised, the Padroado had codified, the Inquisition had policed, Diamper had imposed, and the Latin hierarchy had been installed. All of it failed in 1653 because its operating logic, by then, was being administered by a man who refused to understand that a community can be governed only with its consent. Roz had understood this and had been broken by knowing it. Britto had understood it and had built his settlement on it. Garcia had not understood it and had not wanted to.

Mar Thomas I would govern the Puthankūr until his death in 1673. The community he had been forced to lead would, in time, receive Mar Gregorios, Metropolitan of Jerusalem under the Jacobite Syriac Orthodox Patriarch of Antioch, in 1665, whose arrival began the enduring West Syrian tradition that would shape the Puthankūr into the modern Malankara Church, while those who remained in, or returned to, communion with Rome would come to be called the Malabar Church. The St Thomas Christian community, having been forced to choose between accepting an authority it could not consent to and becoming a church it had not been before, chose the second.

The settlement had been wrecked. The community had walked out.

It would not return.

Beyond the Padroado Question

Part II opened with a grant of authority. In the crusading bulls of the 1450s, Dum Diversas and Romanus Pontifex, the papacy had handed the Portuguese Crown a divine mandate to sail east, to conquer and convert, and to administer whatever churches it found there, fusing the spice monopoly to the Last Crusade. That grant rested on a misrecognition built into its own language: the bulls imagined "Indians who are said to worship the name of Christ" as allies to be incited against Islam, a phantom Christendom waiting to be corrected and absorbed. The Padroado was the legal form of that grant. Its whole authority over the Thomas Christians flowed from the premise that Rome had given the Crown the right to present the bishops who would govern them.

Part II closes with that same authority being taken back. The creation of the Vicariate of Malabar was the calculated withdrawal of the very authority the bulls had granted two centuries earlier: Rome reclaiming, through Propaganda, the right to govern the Serra without the Crown that the Padroado had been built to serve. The grant of the 1450s and the repossession of 1659 are the two ends of the arch that Part II has traced. Everything between them, from Diamper and the 'Nestorian' label to the Coonan Cross, is the working-out in the Serra of a theology of authority that the Padroado carried in with it and could neither modify nor sustain.

Yet the repossession was itself provisional, and it points past this part. The hand that consecrated Sebastiani could not hold what it had taken: within a few years he was fleeing a Cochin that no longer belonged to Portugal at all. The authority Rome had reclaimed from the Crown was reclaimed over a coast the Crown was about to lose. When the Dutch took Cochin in 1663, the political scaffolding that had carried the Padroado into Malabar for a hundred and sixty years began to come down, and the Thomas Christians, now divided into two churches, would have to make their way in a Malabar where the Portuguese were no longer masters. That collapse, and what the divided community did inside it, belongs to Part III.

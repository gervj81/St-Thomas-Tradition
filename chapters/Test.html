The Marakkars

The history of Cosignees in Copper

Linguistic & Etymological Roots

  • Arabic (Markab): Derived from the word for "boat" or "ship." Local legends suggest native Hindus called early Muslim migrants the "people of Markab," which evolved into Marakkayars.
  • Tamil (Marakkala Rayar): Literally translates to "lords of the ships" (marakkalam: boat, rayar: chieftain). A historically debated copper-plate legend (c. 875 CE) claims a native (Chola or Pandya) ruler Jayaveera Rajaguru Nayani conferred this title on Mohamed Khalji and Arab settlers fleeing Cairo because they arrived via a marakkalam. Furthermore, an 11th-century (1088 CE) inscription in Sumatra records the related early form marakkala-nayan (ship captain).
  • Malayalam (Marakan): Meaning sailor or steersman. In the 16th century, the Portuguese used the honorific plural Maracar in official records to respectfully address prosperous Muslim shipowners.
  • Sinhalese (Marakkala Mininsu): Used in Ceylon to refer to mariners, boatmen, or prosperous Arab and Malabari seafarers.

Settler Chronology

  • 8th–12th C. (Settlement & Expansion): Arab traders intermarried with locals along the Coromandel and Malabar coasts. Following the Chola maritime decline, these communities secured dominant roles in Indian Ocean trade.
  • 13th–15th C. (Maritime Dominance): Ports like Kayal became hubs for Arab horses, gemstones, and pearls. Tamil Muslim merchants gained immense political and economic influence in Southeast Asian royal courts, including Malacca.
  • 16th–17th C. (Colonial Resistance): Portuguese oppression forced figures like Kunjali Marakkar to ally as naval admirals with the Zamorin of Calicut. On the east coast, leaders like Seethakkathi successfully bypassed Dutch monopolies through parallel smuggling networks.
  • 18th–20th C. (Decline & Migration): Marginalized by British monopolies, wealthy Chettiars, and the advent of steam vessels, the community lost its oceanic hold, prompting migrations to Ceylon, Burma, and Malaya as laborers and petty traders.[EN]

The Thomas Christians under Carmelites and British, 1799–1896

The Forging of a People

"Far from having been a low-status, low-caste convert group, the Syrians have a history of power and privilege."

— Susan Bayly, Saints, Goddesses and Kings

Two chapters ago we left the Thomas Christians in the act of reaching eastward, both halves of the divided church looking to Mesopotamia for legitimacy and then we left them altogether, to watch the ground shift beneath their feet. The ground has now shifted. The Dutch are gone from the coast, the old Padroado crown is a spent force, and in their room stand a Hindu sovereign state of Travencore and a Protestant paramount power in the Residency beside it. It is time to return to the Nazranis, and to ask what became of them under these new masters. The answer, traced across the long nineteenth century, is the matter of this chapter, and it has a single shape. Both halves of the Serra, in their different ways, came to feel the same want, the one craving a bishop of its own blood, the other struggling to keep intact the tradition of its fathers. Out of that shared anxiety, sharpened by the slow erosion of the community's old standing in the world, there would grow at last a new thing: a people conscious of itself as a people, and a politics to match. 1

The Honoured Place

To measure what the new century took from the Thomas Christians, one must first see what they had been. It is easy, looking back through the lens of the missionary age, to imagine the Syrians as a small and humble sect, a body of converts dependent upon European patronage; and it would be quite wrong. For many centuries before any European ruled in India the St Thomas Christians had been a community of standing, possessed of what their modern historian calls a history of power and privilege. They were warriors, traders, and church notables, honoured in the Hindu kingdoms among which they lived, holding rights and dignities that their own founder-legends traced to the grants of ancient kings. 2

In the martial society of Kerala they ranked with the Nayars, the sword-bearing aristocracy of the land; in its commerce they held a great share of the inland trade in pepper and the produce of the hills. They were, in short, no marginal people. They were woven into the fabric of the old Hindu order, and their dignity was bound up with it. That order was precisely what the new century would unmake. 3

The Pazhayakūr and the Want of a Bishop

For the Catholic Thomas Christians, the Pazhayakūr, the new century opened in defeat. The native revolt of 1787 had been put down, its leaders reconciled, and its great hope, the dream of a bishop of their own nation that Joseph Kariattil had briefly embodied, buried with him at Goa. Thomas Paremmakkal, who had carried that hope home, governed the community as its administrator until his death in 1799, and after him it passed once more wholly under foreign hands. The vicar apostolic of Verapoly who now ruled Pazhyakur were so reduced in able men that in 1803, for want of any better, the office of bishop was given to a Carmelite of meagre gifts, Raymond of St Joseph. 4

The Chaldean Experiment

The want of a bishop of their own did not die for the Pazhayakūr; it festered. In 1861, a bishop of the Chaldean patriarchate of Mosul, Thomas Rokos, came to Malabar at the invitation of discontented Syrians. His arrival presented an ecclesiastical paradox: the Chaldean patriarchate of Mosul was the faction of the ancient Church of the East that had entered into full communion with Rome, making Rokos a Catholic prelate of the Syrians' own ancestral rite. Yet, because his patriarch had sent him to assert historical Eastern jurisdiction over Malabar without the explicit leave of Rome—which had placed the territory firmly under the Latin Carmelites—Rokos was forbidden all episcopal acts. Though thousands flocked to him, eager to embrace a Catholic bishop of Eastern lineage, his mission eventually collapsed under Roman pressure and he was recalled. Thirteen years later the experiment was repeated with another Chaldean, Elias Mellus, who came in 1874. Sent by the same patriarch to press identical claims of Eastern Catholic jurisdiction, Mellus's mission pushed the crisis to the breaking point. Though Mellus himself would eventually buckle under papal pressure and return to Mesopotamia, he left behind him at Trichur a lasting schism. In a profound historical irony, this orphaned congregation, driven initially by a desire to preserve their Eastern Catholic heritage from Latin control, eventually realized they could no longer rely on a Catholic patriarch. This Trichur faction would finally secure its autonomy by severing ties with Rome altogether, reaching out to the non-Catholic Assyrian Church of the East and welcoming its bishop in 1907. Thus, in their desperate flight from the Carmelites, they walked back into the very theological fold the Portuguese had tried to eradicate three centuries before. 5

Yet alongside the schisms ran a deeper and more fruitful movement. A priest of the Syrians' own soil, Kuriakose Elias Chavara, had in 1831 founded at Mannanam the first indigenous religious congregation of the Malabar church, and from it flowed a renewal of the Pazhayakūr from within: seminaries, schools, a printing press, a disciplined native clergy. It was Chavara, made vicar general of the Syrians, who held the bulk of the community to Rome against Rokos, refusing for himself the mitre the schismatics pressed upon him. The renewal he began would, in time, make a self-governing native church not merely a longing but a possibility. Through it all, as the community's historian observes, the desire for an Indian bishop was always alive. 6

The Puthankūr and the Keeping of the Tradition

The other half of the Serra met a different master and a different trial. The Puthankūr had spent the eighteenth century in search of valid orders from the East, and as the century turned it made one last motion toward Rome: in 1799 its metropolitan, Mar Dionysius I, accepted an act of union and came over for a few months to the Catholic communion, only to draw back again within the year. It was the last such overture. The power that would shape the Puthankūr in the new century was not Rome but the British, and the British came not as conquerors of the church but as its self-appointed protectors. Under the paramountcy described in the last chapter, the Resident at Travancore had become the effective arbiter of the kingdom's affairs, and the affairs of the ancient church fell within his reach: it was the Resident who now held the balance among the Syrian factions, the Resident to whom rival claimants to the office of metropolitan appealed, the Resident whose favour could make or unmake a prelate. 7

The instrument of the British design was the missionary. Claudius Buchanan, visiting in 1806, had carried home to England a vision of the ancient Syrian church reformed and drawn toward communion with the Church of England; and Colonel Munro, the Resident who was for a time also the kingdom's chief minister, gave the vision its chance. Under his patronage the Church Missionary Society (CMS) sent, from 1816, what was called a Mission of Help: not at first an attempt to convert the Syrians or to draw them from their church, but to assist it, to school its clergy, to give it a seminary at Kottayam, and so, in the missionaries' hope, to reform it quietly from within. 8

The help, however, came with a purpose, and the purpose was reformation. The missionaries looked upon the immemorial usages of the Malankara Syrians, the invocations of the saints, the prayers for the dead, the ancient liturgy in its Syriac tongue, the very order of a hierarchy that ordained boys as deacons, with the disapproval of evangelical Protestants, and they pressed, gently at first and then less gently, for change. To the Malankara Syrians this was no help but a threat to the tradition of their fathers, the one possession that the long centuries of foreign rule had not taken from them, and they resisted. The breach came at the Synod of Mavelikara in January 1836, where the metropolitan Mar Dionysius, with Mar Cyril of Thozhiyur, more than fifty cattanars, and a body of laity, refused the missionaries' reforms; the missionaries, as the historian of the mission records, found the opposition much stronger than they had supposed, and the connexion was broken. The Puthankūr had kept its tradition, but at the cost of a fresh division, for some among them had been won by the reformers, and from that seed would grow, in time, a separate reformed church. The keeping of the tradition, like the want of a bishop, had its price. 9

The Falling Status

Behind both anxieties, the Catholic want of a bishop and the Puthankūr struggle for its tradition, lay a deeper movement that touched every Christian of the Serra alike, and touched their Hindu neighbours with them. The new order was dissolving the old foundations of status, and the communities that had stood high in the old world found the ground sinking beneath them. The Nayars, the martial aristocracy of Kerala, had held their dominance by the sword, as the soldiers of the old Hindu kings; but the British peace had no use for a caste of warriors, and the new bureaucratic state that the Residents built rewarded not the sword but the pen, not birth in a martial lineage but education and the holding of office. The dominance of the Nayars, resting upon a military order now made obsolete, began its long decline. 10

The Christians felt a kindred dislocation. Their old standing had rested upon their place in the trade of the country and their honoured rank in the Hindu states; but the colonial economy opened the old trades to all comers and dissolved the protected niches, while the new ladder of advancement, government service, was one the Syrians were ill-placed to climb, for as traders and not servants of the state they had, in the judgement of their historian, no tradition of sirkar service. The result was not simple ruin but anxious transformation. The old marks of standing were passing away, and new ones, education in the European mode, employment in the swelling offices of the state, a voice in its councils, were taking their place; and every community that had once been secure in its rank now found that it must compete for the new prizes or sink. It was, as the deepest student of this society has argued, the colonial order itself that worked the change: by breaking down the old Hindu and Muslim kingdoms with their integrating web of royal honours, it dissolved the settled standing of groups like the Syrians and the Nayars, and pressed them to remake themselves as self-conscious communities, organized to advance their claims. The Thomas Christians, who had been a people of assured dignity in the old order, were being made over, by the pressure of the new, into a community that must assert itself in order to survive. 11

The Common Anxiety and the Politics of Unity

Here the two halves of the divided church, so long set against each other, found themselves facing the same way. The Pazhayakūr under the Carmelites and the Puthankūr under the missionaries had each been brought to feel its subjection to a foreign ecclesiastical power; both had been stripped, by the new economy and the new state, of the old standing that had once made them secure; and both began to sense, beneath the theological quarrels that divided them, a common identity as Nasranis, the ancient Christians of Saint Thomas, a single people with a single inheritance and a single set of interests to defend. 12

The man who seized upon this sense and made a politics of it was a Catholic priest of the Pazhayakūr, Emmanuel Nidhiry of Kuravilangad. Born in 1842 into one of the wealthy land-owning families of his church, educated, as it happened, at the very CMS college at Kottayam that the missionaries had founded, master of Malayalam and Syriac and English alike, Nidhiry rose to be vicar general of his community and the ablest political head it had yet produced. He grasped that the scattered Thomas Christians, Catholic and dissident together, would count for nothing in the new order unless they organized, and he set himself to give them the instruments. In 1887 he founded the newspaper Nasrani Deepika, a voice for the community in print; and in concert with the Jacobite metropolitan Mar Dionysius he helped to bring into being a Syrian association to advance the common interests of the Nasranis in education, in government employment, and in standing before the state. 13

The high mark of this mobilization came in 1891, when the Christians joined with the Nayars and others of Travancore in the great petition called the Malayali Memorial, protesting that the offices of their own state were being filled by outsiders to the exclusion of its native sons, and demanding their rightful share. It was a new kind of act, a community pressing its claims upon the state in the modern political manner, and it announced that the Thomas Christians had become, whatever else they were, a self-conscious people in the public life of their country. 14

Yet it would be too much to call Nidhiry's a disinterested vision of Christian brotherhood. He was a Catholic before he was a Nasrani patriot, and his dealings with the dissident half of the church were not free of design: he had campaigned against the schismatics in his earlier years, and his overtures to the Jacobites in the later 1880s were aimed, in part, at winning them back to Rome. The unity he preached was a unity of the people; the church into which he would have gathered them was his own. The common Nasrani identity was real, and so were the interests it served; but it was never quite the same thing as a reunion of the sundered church, and the distinction would tell. 15

Government by Their Own

For the Pazhayakūr, the long want was at last answered, and answered in the lifetime of the men who had laboured for it. In 1887 Rome lifted the Syrian Catholics out of the Latin vicariate of Verapoly, under which they had chafed for a century, and gave them two vicariates of their own, Trichur and Kottayam, with their own administration and their own clergy. The bishops set over them were still Europeans, Medlycott and Lavigne, and the work was but half done; yet the principle was won, the Syrians were no longer a subject appendage of a Latin see. Then, in 1896, the work was crowned. Rome reorganized the Syro-Malabar church into three vicariates, Trichur, Ernakulam, and Changanacherry, and over them, for the first time since the coming of the Portuguese, it set bishops of the Syrians' own nation: John Menachery, Aloysius Pazheparambil, and Mathew Makil, Thomas Christians governing Thomas Christians at last. 16

The spark that had been struck at the Coonan Cross, that Kariattil had carried to Lisbon and Paremmakkal home again, that had smouldered through a century of foreign rule and flared in the schisms of Rokos and Mellus, had come at last to flame. The Pazhayakūr had a hierarchy of their own people. The Puthankūr, in their own fashion, reached a like settlement, though by a harder road and into a more divided end. Their long struggle to keep the tradition had preserved the ancient church, but it had also broken it: the reformed party that the missionaries had won went its own way, to become in time the Mar Thoma church, while the traditional body held its ground, keeping the old Syriac Orthodox faith and ordering its affairs under its own metropolitans. Here too the end was government by their own; but here too it came at the cost of division. 17

So Part Three closes upon a paradox. The pressure of the foreign powers had driven both halves of the Serra toward the same end, government by men of their own, and had even bred, in Nidhiry and his fellows, a vision of one Nasrani people, undivided, taking its place in the modern world. But the end that came was not one church; it was several: a Syro-Malabar church under its native Catholic bishops, a traditional Malankara church under its own metropolitans, a reformed Mar Thoma church beside it. The vision of unity had yielded a settlement of separate modern churches. And there was a deeper irony still. The colonial order that had threatened the Thomas Christians, that had set foreign bishops and reforming missionaries over them and dissolved the standing they had enjoyed for a thousand years, was the very thing that forged them into what they had now become: self-conscious modern communities, organized, literate, and articulate, able to govern themselves and to press their claims as they had never been able to do in all the centuries of their honoured but unselfconscious antiquity. The powers that came to remake the ground beneath the Thomas Christians had remade the Christians too. They had entered the new century an ancient and a divided people; they left it a modern and a divided people, and the difference was not nothing. The long contest over who should govern the Serra was over. The Thomas Christians governed themselves at last, each church its own, under the new sovereigns of a new world. 18

The chapter returns to the point reached at the close of Chapter 15. The unifying argument, that a shared subjection to foreign ecclesiastical powers, joined to a shared erosion of the community's old social standing, produced among the Thomas Christians a new self-conscious communal identity and a modern politics, is the author's, and it follows the social history of Susan Bayly, Saints, Goddesses and Kings: Muslims and Christians in South Indian Society, 1700–1900, and Robin Jeffrey, The Decline of Nayar Dominance: Society and Politics in Travancore, 1847–1908.

On the antiquity of the Syrians' standing: Bayly, Saints, Goddesses and Kings, insists that the St Thomas Christians were "far from having been a low-status, low-caste convert group," possessing instead "a history of power and privilege" dating from many centuries before European rule, their warriors, traders, and church notables honoured within the Hindu kingdoms in which they had settled, their rights traced in legend and copper-plate deed to the grants of early kings. The quoted phrase is Bayly's; it supplies the epigraph above.

On the parallel between the Syrians and the Nayars as honoured groups of the old order, on the Syrians' share in the inland trade: Bayly, Saints, Goddesses and Kings.

On the aftermath of the Padiyola, the death of Thomas Paremmakkal in 1799 (see Chapter 15 above), and the return of the Pazhayakūr to government by the Italian Carmelite vicars apostolic of Verapoly: Tisserant, Eastern Christianity in India. On the appointment in 1803, for want of qualified men, of the Carmelite Raymond of St Joseph (Roviglia), whom Tisserant calls a man of little accomplishment and meagre ability (he held the office 1803–16): Tisserant.

On the schisms of recourse to the East: the Chaldean bishop Thomas Rokos, an old servant of the patriarchate of Mosul, consecrated in 1860 and forbidden by the Apostolic Delegate any exercise of episcopal acts, who came to Malabar in 1861 and was recalled after his mission collapsed; and the Chaldean Elias Mellus, who came in 1874 (Rome not having been informed) and left a lasting schism centred at Trichur. The reading of both as renewed instances of the impulse seen in the Paul Pandari affair of Chapter 15, the willingness to take a bishop from a foreign East rather than remain under a foreign West, is the author's. The ultimate trajectory of the Mellus schismatics—who formally joined the Assyrian Church of the East under Mar Abimalek Timotheus in 1907, forming the Chaldean Syrian Church of India—is introduced here as a chronological forward completion.

On Kuriakose Elias Chavara, his foundation of the first indigenous religious congregation at Mannanam in 1831 and the renewal of seminaries, schools, and press that followed, and on his role as vicar general in holding the community to Rome against Rokos and declining episcopal office for himself: Tisserant, Eastern Christianity in India. Tisserant's observation that, from the early nineteenth century, "the desire for an Indian bishop was always alive" is quoted here.

On the brief reunion of 1799, when Mar Dionysius I (Mar Thomas VI), called the Great, accepted an act of union on 20 May and came over to the Catholic communion on 21 June 1799, only to revert within some six months: Tisserant, Eastern Christianity in India. On the British Resident's emergence, under the paramountcy described in Chapter 16, as the effective arbiter among the Syrian factions and of the office of metropolitan: Neill, vol. II; Philip, The Indian Church of St Thomas. The vattipanam, the church's deposit lodged with the British, by which the Resident's financial leverage over the church was secured, is treated in the local histories; the present account conveys the dynamic without resting on that term.

On the visit of Claudius Buchanan in 1806 and his vision of the Syrian church reformed toward communion with the Church of England; on Colonel John Munro, Resident and for a time Dewan of Travancore, as patron of the enterprise; and on the Church Missionary Society's "Mission of Help" from 1816, with the seminary at Kottayam: Neill, vol. II (the chapter on the Mission of Help); Philip, The Indian Church of St Thomas.

On the missionaries' pressure for reform of the Syrian usages (the cult of the saints, prayers for the dead, the Syriac liturgy, the ordaining of boys to orders), and on the Synod of Mavelikara of January 1836, attended by Mar Dionysius, Mar Cyril of Thozhiyur, more than fifty cattanars, and laity, at which the reforms were refused and the connexion with the C.M.S. broken: Neill, vol. II, who records that the missionaries found the opposition "much stronger than they had supposed." On the reform party that remained and became in time the Mar Thoma church, and on the Syrians' resistance as a defence of tradition: Philip, The Indian Church of St Thomas; Bayly, Saints, Goddesses and Kings, on the C.M.S. reform policies and the Syrian reaction.

On the decline of the Nayars' dominance as the new bureaucratic colonial state rendered their martial function obsolete and rewarded education and office-holding in its place: Robin Jeffrey, The Decline of Nayar Dominance, whose title states the thesis. The application of the same logic to the social order of Travancore as a whole is the author's gloss.

On the Syrians as traders rather than servants of the state, having, in Jeffrey's words, "no tradition of sirkar service," and so disadvantaged in the competition for the new prizes of education and government employment: Jeffrey, The Decline of Nayar Dominance. On the colonial order's dissolution of the old integrating system of royal honours and its manufacture of self-conscious, mobilized communal identities among groups such as the Syrians and the Nayars: Bayly, Saints, Goddesses and Kings. The synthesis of these into a single account of "anxious transformation" is the author's.

The reading of the two halves of the church as converging, under a shared subjection to foreign powers and a shared loss of standing, upon a common Nasrani identity that overrode their theological division is the author's, drawn from Bayly, Saints, Goddesses and Kings, and Jeffrey, The Decline of Nayar Dominance.

On Father Emmanuel Nidhiry (1842–1904) of Kuravilangad, of a wealthy land-owning family with a tradition of providing priests, educated at the C.M.S. College at Kottayam, master of Malayalam, Tamil, Sanskrit, Syriac, and English, vicar general and the leading political figure of the Romo-Syrians; on his founding of Nasrani Deepika in 1887; and on the Syrian National Union Association, formed in 1888 in co-operation with the Jacobite metropolitan Mar Dionysius, with its early effort for western-style education from 1883: Jeffrey, The Decline of Nayar Dominance (including the biographical appendix), who cites also A. M. Nidhiry, Father Nidhiry (Kottayam, 1971).

On the Malayali Memorial of 1891, the petition of the educated communities of Travancore (Nayars, Syrian Christians, and others) protesting the predominance of non-Malayali outsiders in the state's public service and demanding a fairer share for the country's own people: Jeffrey, The Decline of Nayar Dominance. The reading of it as the announcement of a self-conscious modern communal politics among the Thomas Christians is the author's.

The qualification is important and is supported by Jeffrey, The Decline of Nayar Dominance, whose account of Nidhiry notes that he had campaigned against the schismatics in 1876–82 and that his approaches to the Jacobites in 1886–89 were directed in part at winning them over to Rome. The unity movement is therefore not to be read as a disinterested ecumenism; it carried a Catholic-recruiting purpose, and served at once the communal and the confessional interest. The distinction drawn here between a unity of the people and a reunion of the church is the author's.

On the separation of the Syrian Catholics from the Latin vicariate of Verapoly in 1887 into the two vicariates of Trichur and Kottayam, still under European vicars apostolic (A. E. Medlycott at Trichur, Charles Lavigne at Kottayam); and on the reorganization of 1896 into the three vicariates of Trichur, Ernakulam, and Changanacherry under the first Indian vicars apostolic of the Syro-Malabar church, John Menachery, Aloysius Pazheparambil, and Mathew Makil (who took office on 11 August 1896): Tisserant, Eastern Christianity in India, in the section on the Indian Catholic hierarchy.

The continuity drawn from the Coonan Cross oath of 1653 (Chapter 12) through Kariattil and Paremmakkal (Chapter 15) to the hierarchy of 1896 is the author's. On the division of the Puthankūr into the reformed body that became the Mar Thoma church and the traditional Malankara church that retained the Syriac Orthodox faith under its own metropolitans, the present account follows Philip, The Indian Church of St Thomas, and Neill, vol. II; the internal disputes of the traditional body in the twentieth century lie beyond the scope of this chapter and are touched on only as the period requires.

The closing reading, that the colonial order both threatened the Thomas Christians and forged them into self-conscious modern communities, capable of self-government and of pressing their claims, is the author's, and it draws together the argument of Bayly, Saints, Goddesses and Kings, that communal self-consciousness in south India was in large part a creation of the colonial transformation. The judgement that the long contest over the government of the Serra closed, at the end of the nineteenth century, in self-government purchased at the price of division, is the author's, and brings Part Three to its end.

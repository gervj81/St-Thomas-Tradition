The Thomas Christians under Carmelites and British, 1799–1896

The Forging of a People

"Far from having been a low-status, low-caste convert group, the Syrians have a history of power and privilege."

— Susan Bayly, Saints, Goddesses and Kings

Two chapters ago we left the Thomas Christians in the act of reaching eastward, both halves of the divided church looking to Mesopotamia for legitimacy and then we left them altogether, to watch the ground shift beneath their feet. The ground has now shifted. The Dutch are gone from the coast, the old Padroado crown is a spent force, and in their room stand a Hindu sovereign state of Travancore and a Protestant paramount power in the Residency beside it. It is time to return to the Nazranis, and to ask what became of them under these new masters. The answer, traced across the long nineteenth century, is the matter of this chapter, and it has a single shape. Both halves of the Serra, in their different ways, came to feel the same want, the one craving a bishop of its own blood, the other struggling to keep intact the tradition of its fathers. Out of that shared anxiety, sharpened by the slow erosion of the community's old standing in the world, there would grow at last a new thing: a people conscious of itself as a people, and a politics to match. 1

The Honoured Place

To measure what the new century took from the Thomas Christians, one must first see what they had been. It is easy, looking back through the lens of the missionary age, to imagine the Syrians as a small and humble sect, a body of converts dependent upon European patronage; and it would be quite wrong. For many centuries before any European ruled in India the St Thomas Christians had been a community of standing, possessed of what their modern historian calls a history of power and privilege. They were warriors, traders, and church notables, honoured in the Hindu kingdoms among which they lived, holding rights and dignities that their own founder-legends traced to the grants of ancient kings. 2

In the martial society of Kerala they ranked with the Nayars, the sword-bearing aristocracy of the land; in its commerce they held a great share of the inland trade in pepper and the produce of the hills. They were, in short, no marginal people. They were woven into the fabric of the old Hindu order, and their dignity was bound up with it. That order was precisely what the new century would unmake. 3

The Pazhayakūr and the Want of a Bishop

For the Catholic Thomas Christians, the Pazhayakūr, the new century opened on the ashes of the revolt. The rising of 1787 had been put down and its leaders reconciled; its great hope, the dream of a bishop of their own nation that Joseph Kariattil had briefly embodied, lay buried with him at Goa. What remained of that hope was Thomas Paremmakkal, who as Governador of Kodungallur ruled the Catholic Syrians for thirteen years, a native government in all but the mitre. He held the community with a firm hand; when the appeal to Mesopotamia at last brought forth a bishop, Paul Pandari, it was Paremmakkal himself who shut the door, refusing the intruder any exercise of his orders. But he could not make himself immortal. He died in March 1799, and the want he had contained broke out at once. The parishes elected a governor of their own, Abraham of Kattakkayam; and a body of them, meeting at Changanacherry within weeks of the funeral, went further, accepting Mar Pandari for their bishop and putting forward their deacons for his hands. Rome had the affair undone, and the Padroado set in Paremmakkal's place one more native governador, George Sankoorickal, an alumnus of the Propaganda College. With his death in 1801 the line of native governors ended, and the government of the Catholic Syrians passed once more wholly under foreign hands. 4

Those hands were two, not one. The old Padroado of Portugal still claimed the Catholic Syrians through its archdiocese of Kodungallur, while the Propaganda of Rome ruled them through the Italian Carmelites of the vicariate of Verapoly, and a parish might attach itself to the one or to the other. But on either allegiance the Syrians were now ruled by men who were no bishops among them. The Carmelites of Verapoly, who held the greater number, were by this time so reduced in able men that in 1803, for want of a better, they gave the office of bishop to one of meagre gifts, Raymond of St Joseph; and even his Briefs miscarried on the war-swept sea roads of the age, so that the man chosen in 1803 could not be consecrated until 1807. The Padroado side fared worse, for the crown behind it was going under. 5

The reader will remember from Chapter 15 what Pombal's Portugal had done to the Society of Jesus: expelled it from every Portuguese dominion in 1759, and pressed Rome until the order was suppressed throughout the church in 1773. What followed in the new century was that story played out to its end. In 1807 Napoleon's army marched on Lisbon, and the royal court fled to Brazil, not to return for fourteen years. The liberal revolution of 1820 came next; then the loss of Brazil itself; then a civil war of brother against brother that bled the kingdom until 1834. And when the liberals had won it, they carried Pombal's work to its conclusion: Portugal suppressed not the Jesuits only but every religious order in its dominions and seized their houses, and this in the very generation in which Rome, in 1814, had raised the Society of Jesus back to life. The old regalist quarrel ended with the two swords exchanging places, the pope now protecting what the most faithful of crowns destroyed; and in 1834 Lisbon and Rome broke off relations altogether. A crown in such a state could not man an archdiocese on the Malabar coast. It named archbishops to Kodungallur who never came out to govern; on the spot the archdiocese was kept by an administrator, an episcopal governor, a single Portuguese-speaking officer serving the two sees of Kodungallur and Cochin together. At Verapoly a threadbare mission, at Kodungallur an empty throne: such was the double government of the Catholic Syrians in the decades before the storm broke. 6

Behind both jurisdictions lay the same lack. For more than a century after Kariattil, no bishop of the Pazhayakūr's own nation was appointed. The sees of these Catholic Syrians, the 'Romo-Syrians' as the British records and the Malankara side would later class them, passed through a long procession of foreign administrators and bishops-elect, and the community, as Neill observes, settled into the keeping of the status quo, making no converts and slowly losing ground. The old wish for a prelate of their own blood was not dead; it had only gone underground, waiting for a road. When a road at last opened it forked in two, and the two directions it took would divide the Catholic Syrians for the rest of the century. 7

The Broken Allegiance

The first road began in a wound of jurisdiction. A part of the community had leaned to the Padroado of Kodungallur, which carried for them the memory of the native governors and the near self-rule of Paremmakkal's day, and resented the Carmelites of Verapoly. In 1838 the ground was cut from under them. Hard upon the rupture between Lisbon and Rome, Pope Gregory XVI, by the brief Multa Praeclare, suppressed the Padroado jurisdiction throughout India save at Goa and ordered the Pazhayakūr under the Propaganda vicariate of the Carmelites. To the Padroado-allied Catholic Syrians this was no mere stroke of administration but the loss of the one authority they still owned as their own. A body of them refused to obey. Led by the Padroado administrator of Kodungallur and Cochin, Manuel das Neves, they held to the old allegiance; but an allegiance to a jurisdiction Rome had abolished could give them no bishop. They were now a faction that would not take a Carmelite and could not get a Padroado, and such a faction was bound to go looking for a bishop elsewhere.

They looked where their fathers had once found their bishops, in the East, to the Chaldean patriarch of Mosul, the Rome-aligned head of their own ancestral rite. The appeals began early. As far back as 1849, the priests and people of Kuravilangad, with a hundred and fifty other churches, had written to the patriarch for a bishop of their rite and tongue. Rome's own reforms then sharpened the want. The vicar apostolic, Bernardine of St Teresa (Joseph Baccinelli), was rebuilding the formation of the Syrian clergy, and not without cause: for generations most priests had been trained under a village malpan, a master at whose knee a boy learned to read his Syriac, the practice of the liturgy, and a few elements of theology, and no more. Bernardine replaced this apprenticeship with regular seminaries on the European model, closing some twenty of the little malpanate schools, and about 1855 he decreed that he would ordain no priest who had not passed through his own new houses. The reform raised the clergy, but it also closed a door: the Padroado parishes, having no bishop of their own, were left with no lawful way to ordain their sons at all. By this time a Chaldean priest, Denha Bar-Yona, sent out from Mosul, had settled in the church at Kuravilangad itself, and he fanned the longing. In 1854 the Padroado-aligned leaders met there and resolved to send a deputation to Mosul, and in 1857 it set out, led by Anthony of Kudakkachira and Anthony Thondanat. Kudakkachira died at Baghdad on the road. Thondanat came back; and when in 1858 the vicar apostolic refused to ordain the fifteen candidates he presented, he turned again to Mesopotamia, to press the patriarch harder. 8

The Chaldean Experiment

The patriarch, Joseph VI Audo, did not act rashly. For years he pressed his case at Rome by the proper channels: he reminded Propaganda that Rome's own law, the bull Etsi Pastoralis of Benedict XIV, commanded respect for the Oriental rites; he proposed a commission of visitors, half of Rome's choosing and half of his own, to examine the state of Malabar; and in December 1859 he warned that he would resign his see if the refusal continued, for it trenched upon the rights of his church. Rome answered in the spring of 1860 by side-stepping the question and bidding the Chaldeans not to interfere in Malabar. Then the patriarch acted. Over the express prohibition of Rome he consecrated a bishop for Malabar in September 1860 and sent him out, and in 1861 Thomas Rokos landed in the Serra. His coming was a paradox. The Chaldean patriarchate of Mosul was that part of the ancient Church of the East which had come into communion with Rome, so that Rokos was himself a Catholic bishop of the Pazhayakūr's own rite; yet, because his patriarch had sent him to assert an Eastern jurisdiction over Malabar without the leave of Rome, which had placed the territory under the Latin Carmelites, Rokos was forbidden every episcopal act. Thousands nonetheless flocked to him, glad of a Catholic bishop of the old lineage. But the mission could not stand against the censures of Rome, and within the year he was recalled. When he withdrew, in 1862, he did not go alone. With him went Anthony Thondanat; and in Mesopotamia Thondanat sought a mitre of his own. The Chaldean patriarch, chastened by the censures that had just ended the Rokos affair, refused to lay hands on him; so he went up into the hills of Kurdistan, to the catholicos of that part of the Church of the East which had never entered the Roman communion (catholicos being the ancient title of the church's head beyond the Roman world, as patriarch was of Antioch or Alexandria within it), and was consecrated by the Catholicos Mar Shimun under the name Mar Abdisho. His defiance did not last. He came home, submitted, was absolved in 1865, and settled to live as a simple priest at Mannanam. But his peace with Rome would not prove final.

Thirteen years later the experiment was tried again, and Thondanat had a hand in it once more. Patriarch Audo, having gone to the First Vatican Council resolved to reclaim the ancient privileges of his see, jurisdiction over Malabar among them, and having failed, prepared a fresh campaign; and the motive now was not jurisdiction alone but money, for his own Chaldeans in Mesopotamia were a poor people, barely thirty-five thousand in the hills of Kurdistan, while the Malabar community was near ten times their number and its offerings a prize worth the having. The new campaign was opened by alms-collecting monks, and one of them fixed his headquarters in the old Padroado parish house at Trichur, the town that would for that reason become the centre of all that followed. In 1874 the Chaldean bishop Elias Mellus came in person to press the claim, and the crisis reached its height. Mellus held his ground at Trichur for eight years, and left India only in March 1882; his own final submission to Rome would come only in 1889. But he did not leave his flock headless. For Thondanat the temptation of the old company had proved too strong: he had slipped back among the Chaldean party during the Mellus years, and before embarking Mellus installed him, with Augustine, a monk of Alkosh, as vicar over the faction. The old Mar Abdisho led it until his death at Trichur in 1900. By a last turn of the wheel, the orphaned congregation, which had set out to guard its Eastern Catholic heritage from Latin hands, found that it could no longer look to the Catholic patriarchate at Mosul at all: in 1908 it passed to the non-Catholic Assyrian Church of the East, welcoming that communion's metropolitan, Mar Abimalek Timotheus, and taking the name it still bears, the Chaldean Syrian Church. In their flight from the Carmelites they had come round, after three centuries, to the very allegiance the Portuguese had labored at Diamper to erase. 9

The Renewal from Within

While one part of the Pazhayakūr looked east for a bishop, another was quietly remaking the community from within, and it did so on the side of Rome and the Carmelites. Its author was a priest of the Catholic Syrians' own soil, Kuriakose Elias Chavara. In 1831, with two elder companions, he had founded at Mannanam the first indigenous religious congregation of the Malabar church, known in our day as the Carmelites of Mary Immaculate, the CMI, and from it flowed a renewal that the schisms could not give: seminaries, schools, a printing press, a disciplined native clergy. It was this movement, and not the Chaldean bishops, that the vicar apostolic Bernardine of St Teresa fostered and leaned upon; and when Rokos came, the defence of the Roman allegiance fell to Chavara. He had not sought it. Rokos first tried to win him over, offering him a bishop's own consecration, and the humble religious eluded the temptation; it took a direct order from the vicar apostolic before he would accept even the lesser title of vicar general of the Catholic Syrians, an office the same prelate would gladly have raised to a mitre in earnest. Once installed, Chavara sent the best priests of his young congregation through the parishes to rally clergy and people to Rome, and so held the bulk of the community against the Chaldean party. There is an argument, made by the community's own historians, that had Rome only pressed on and given Chavara or some other Thomas Christian the mitre itself at that hour, the later and worse schism of Mellus need never have grown as it did. As it was, the long want was answered only at the century's end, when in 1896 the Pazhayakūr were given prelates of their own nation to govern them; and through all the years of waiting, as their historian observes, the desire for an Indian bishop was always alive. 10

The Puthankūr and the Keeping of the Tradition

The other half of the Serra met a different master and a different trial. The Puthankūr had spent the eighteenth century in search of valid orders from the East, and as the century turned it made one last motion toward Rome: in 1799 its metropolitan, Mar Dionysius I, accepted an act of union and came over for a few months to the Catholic communion, only to draw back again within the year. It was the last such overture. The power that would shape the Puthankūr in the new century was not Rome but the British, and the British came not as conquerors of the church but as its self-appointed protectors. The first to take the ancient church under his wing was Colonel Macaulay, who made the protection of the Christians a personal cause and corresponded with the aged Dionysius I. The instrument of the British protection was the Anglican missionary. Claudius Buchanan, visiting in 1806, carried home to England an ancient Syriac Bible, the gift of Dionysius and the only entire copy of the Scriptures his church possessed, and with it a vision of the ancient Syrian church reformed and drawn toward communion with the Church of England rather than with Antioch or with Rome. Under that protection the old metropolitan ended his days in 1808, leaving behind him a succession he had laboured to keep within his own house, the house that claimed the ancient archdeacons. 11

The Broken Succession

The house failed within a decade, and the failure nearly cost the Puthankūr their episcopate. Dionysius had consecrated his nephew Thoma VII with his own hands in 1797; Thoma VII outlived him by scarcely fifteen months. As he lay dying at Kandanad, unconscious and past speech, the bystanders dressed his own nephew in the episcopal vestments, set him kneeling by the cot, and named him bishop: so, on the testimony of those present, was made Mar Thoma VIII, and the general body of the faithful never accepted the making. The question of who was truly bishop had, by then, acquired a British edge. In 1808 Colonel Macaulay had accepted from the church a deposit of three thousand star pagodas, lodged with the East India Company as a loan in perpetuity, its interest payable to 'the Bishop or Metropolitan pro tempore, or other regular superior, however denominated, of that ancient Church'; a like sum was lodged for the Catholic Syrians. The vattipanam, as the fund came to be called, was meant as a charity and became, in the words of a historian of the community, a millstone about its neck: competency to draw the interest became the test of who truly headed the church, and the fund would feed a century of litigation that in the end consumed many times its capital. 12

The Exile's Return

A ramban of the north, Joseph of Pulikkottil, contested Mar Thoma VIII's claim to the metropolitan's seat and to the vattipanam that was tied to it, and the new Resident, Colonel Munro, was sympathetic to him; but the ramban was no bishop. The rescue came from the most improbable quarter in the Serra: the little exiled church at Thozhiyur. The reader will recall its origin, the native bishop Koorilose, consecrated in 1772 and driven by Mar Dionysius himself into the Zamorin's country, beyond the reach of Travancore and Cochin. There his successors had kept an undoubted episcopal succession in poverty and obscurity; and when the main body found itself under a metropolitan whose orders no one trusted, it was to Thozhiyur that men turned. The ramban had already, with land from the Rani and Munro's backing, laid the foundation of a college at Kottayam in 1813 for the education of the Syrian clergy. The dispute over the succession went, meanwhile, upon the Company's record: the Resident put questions to the rival parties, and in his written answers of 1813 Mar Thoma VIII rested his whole case upon his blood, asserting that from the year 345 until 1653 the men of his house had been created archdeacons and had held the government of the Syrian churches, and that every metran since had been of the same family. It is in these papers, drawn up for a British inquiry, that the famous claim of the Pakalomattom archdeaconal house is first found set down in a dated document. It did not save him. About March 1815, in St Mary's church at Pazhanji, Philoxenos II of Thozhiyur consecrated the ramban as Pulikkottil Mar Dionysius II; and in January 1816 the government of Travancore issued a proclamation recognizing him as Malankara Metropolitan and requiring all Syrian Christians to obey him, Cochin following with a proclamation of its own. It is here, in a state paper of 1816, that the name by which the Puthankūr church would ever after be known enters the official record for the first time. Whatever the antiquity of Malankara as a name for the country of the Thomas Christians, the title of Malankara Metropolitan was fixed by the Residents' government; and for the next sixty years it would be the government, not the community, that decided who held it. 13

The old archdeaconal line ended in a scene of misery. The aged Thoma IX, consecrated by the dying Thoma VIII, was confronted by the new metropolitan's party at Kadamattom and stripped of robe, staff, mitre, and cross; and the rule of the Pakalomattom house over the Thomas Christians, after nearly two centuries, was done. But the confusion of those years had one more turn in it. Within months of his proclamation Dionysius II was dead, so suddenly that the missionaries who had thought him mending were taken wholly unawares; the deposed Thoma IX sent letters to the parishes bidding them return to him, and some actually set out for the Residency to plead his restoration before their courage failed them. It was an English missionary, Norton, who carried the news of the death to the Resident and recommended for the vacant seat the very man who had consecrated the dead metropolitan; the kattanars assented, saying of him simply that he was a man of prayer; and so in 1817 Philoxenos II of Thozhiyur, bishop of the smallest and poorest church in the Serra, ascended the seat of the Malankara Metropolitan himself, and drew the star-pagoda interest that had become the mark of the lawful head. Broken soon after by smallpox, he consecrated George of Punnathra as his coadjutor, resigned the burden to him as the third Dionysius, and withdrew to his exile church, keeping the title a while longer; and in 1825 he came forth once more to consecrate a fourth, Cheppad Mar Dionysius. The schism of 1772 had returned as the salvation of the church that made it: for a decade and more, every lawful consecration in the Puthankūr passed through Thozhiyur's hands.

The Help That Hurt

Under Colonel Munro's patronage the Church Missionary Society (CMS) proceeded, from 1816, with what was called a Mission of Help: not at first an attempt to convert the Malankara Syrians or to draw them from their church, but to assist it, to school its clergy in the new college at Kottayam, the Pazhaya Seminary of later usage, and so, in the missionaries' hope, to reform it quietly from within. 14

The help, however, turned into reformation. The anglican missionaries looked upon the immemorial usages of the Malankara Syrians, the invocations of the saints, the prayers for the dead, the ancient liturgy in its Syriac tongue, the very order of a hierarchy that ordained boys as deacons, with the disapproval of evangelical Protestants, and they pressed, gently at first and then less gently, for change. To the Malankara Syrians this was no help but a threat to the tradition of their fathers, the one possession that the long centuries of foreign rule had not taken from them, and they resisted. The breach came at the Synod of Mavelikara in January 1836, where the metropolitan Mar Dionysius IV, with Mar Cyril of Thozhiyur, more than fifty cattanars, and a body of laity, refused the missionaries' reforms; the missionaries, as the historian of the mission records, found the opposition much stronger than they had supposed, and the connexion was broken. The gathered church put its refusal in writing, in the manner of its fathers: the Mavelikara Padiyola declared that in matters of faith the Syrians could do nothing without the sanction of the patriarch of Antioch. The Puthankūr had kept its tradition, but at the cost of a fresh division, for some among them had been won by the reformers, and the seed would bear two harvests. Those who went out with the missionaries passed into the Anglican fold, and their descendants, in another century, into the Church of South India (CSI), keeping nothing of the Syrian name. Those who stayed, gathered about Abraham Malpan of Maramon, laboured to reform the church from within, putting the Qurbana into Malayalam and sweeping away the prayers for the dead and the images; a party that would in time become a church of its own, the Mar Thoma Syrian Church, keeping the Syriac inheritance while renouncing what it held to be the accretions of later ages, and claiming to stand upon Scripture and the way of St Thomas in its ancient purity. The keeping of the tradition, like the want of a bishop, had its price. 15

The Falling Status

Behind both anxieties, the Catholic want of a bishop and the Puthankūr struggle for its tradition, lay a deeper movement that touched every Christian of the Serra alike, and touched their Hindu neighbours with them. The new order was dissolving the old foundations of status, and the communities that had stood high in the old world found the ground sinking beneath them. The Nayars, the martial aristocracy of Kerala, had held their dominance by the sword, as the soldiers of the old Hindu kings; but the British peace had no use for a caste of warriors, and the new bureaucratic state that the Residents built rewarded not the sword but the pen, not birth in a martial lineage but education and the holding of office. The dominance of the Nayars, resting upon a military order now made obsolete, began its long decline. 16

The Christians felt a kindred dislocation. Their old standing had rested upon their place in the trade of the country and their honoured rank in the Hindu states; but the colonial economy opened the old trades to all comers and dissolved the protected niches, while the new ladder of advancement, government service, was one the Syrians were ill-placed to climb, for as traders and not servants of the state they had, in the judgement of their historian, no tradition of sirkar service. The result was not simple ruin but anxious transformation. The old marks of standing were passing away, and new ones, education in the European mode, employment in the swelling offices of the state, a voice in its councils, were taking their place; and every community that had once been secure in its rank now found that it must compete for the new prizes or sink. It was, as the deepest student of this society has argued, the colonial order itself that worked the change: by breaking down the old Hindu and Muslim kingdoms with their integrating web of royal honours, it dissolved the settled standing of groups like the Syrians and the Nayars, and pressed them to remake themselves as self-conscious communities, organized to advance their claims. The Thomas Christians, who had been a people of assured dignity in the old order, were being made over, by the pressure of the new, into a community that must assert itself in order to survive. 17

The Common Anxiety and the Politics of Unity

Here the two halves of the divided church, so long set against each other, found themselves facing the same way. The Pazhayakūr under the Carmelites and the Puthankūr under the missionaries had each been brought to feel its subjection to a foreign ecclesiastical power; both had been stripped, by the new economy and the new state, of the old standing that had once made them secure; and both began to sense, beneath the theological quarrels that divided them, a common identity as Nasranis, the ancient Christians of Saint Thomas, a single people with a single inheritance and a single set of interests to defend. 18

The man who seized upon this sense and made a politics of it was a Catholic priest of the Pazhayakūr, Emmanuel Nidhiry of Kuravilangad. Born in 1842 into one of the wealthy land-owning families of his church, educated, as it happened, at the very CMS college at Kottayam that the missionaries had founded, master of Malayalam and Syriac and English alike, Nidhiry rose to be vicar general of his community and the ablest political head it had yet produced. He grasped that the scattered Thomas Christians, Catholic and dissident together, would count for nothing in the new order unless they organized, and he set himself to give them the instruments. In 1887 he founded the newspaper Nasrani Deepika, a voice for the community in print; and in concert with the Jacobite metropolitan Mar Dionysius he helped to bring into being a Syrian association to advance the common interests of the Nasranis in education, in government employment, and in standing before the state. 19

The high mark of this mobilization came in 1891, when the Christians joined with the Nayars and others of Travancore in the great petition called the Malayali Memorial, protesting that the offices of their own state were being filled by outsiders to the exclusion of its native sons, and demanding their rightful share. It was a new kind of act, a community pressing its claims upon the state in the modern political manner, and it announced that the Thomas Christians had become, whatever else they were, a self-conscious people in the public life of their country. 20

Yet it would be too much to call Nidhiry's a disinterested vision of Christian brotherhood. He was a Catholic before he was a Nasrani patriot, and his dealings with the dissident half of the church were not free of design: he had campaigned against the schismatics in his earlier years, and his overtures to the Jacobites in the later 1880s were aimed, in part, at winning them back to Rome. The unity he preached was a unity of the people; the church into which he would have gathered them was his own. The common Nasrani identity was real, and so were the interests it served; but it was never quite the same thing as a reunion of the sundered church, and the distinction would tell. 21

Government by Their Own

For the Pazhayakūr, the long want was at last answered, and answered in the lifetime of the men who had laboured for it. In 1887 Rome lifted the Syrian Catholics out of the Latin vicariate of Verapoly, under which they had chafed for a century, and gave them two vicariates of their own, Trichur and Kottayam, with their own administration and their own clergy. The bishops set over them were still Europeans, Medlycott and Lavigne, and the work was but half done; yet the principle was won, the Catholic Syrians were no longer a subject appendage of a Latin see. Then, in 1896, the work was crowned. Rome reorganized the Syro-Malabar church into three vicariates, Trichur, Ernakulam, and Changanacherry, and over them, for the first time since the coming of the Portuguese, it set bishops of the Catholic Syrians' own nation: John Menachery, Aloysius Pazheparambil, and Mathew Makil, Thomas Christians governing Thomas Christians at last. 22

The spark that had been struck at the Coonan Cross, that Kariattil had carried to Lisbon and Paremmakkal home again, that had smouldered through a century of foreign rule and flared in the schisms of Rokos and Mellus, had come at last to flame. The Pazhayakūr had a hierarchy of their own people. The Puthankūr, in their own fashion, reached a like settlement, though by a harder road and into a more divided end. Their long struggle to keep the tradition had preserved the ancient church, but it had also broken it: the reformed party that the missionaries had won went its own way, to become in time the Mar Thoma church, while the traditional body held its ground, keeping the old Syriac Orthodox faith and ordering its affairs under its own metropolitans. Here too the end was government by their own; but here too it came at the cost of division. 23

So Part Three closes upon a paradox. The pressure of the foreign powers had driven both halves of the Serra toward the same end, government by men of their own, and had even bred, in Nidhiry and his fellows, a vision of one Nasrani people, undivided, taking its place in the modern world. But the end that came was not one church; it was several: a Syro-Malabar church under its native Catholic bishops, a traditional Malankara church under its own metropolitans, a reformed Mar Thoma church beside it. The vision of unity had yielded a settlement of separate modern churches. And there was a deeper irony still. The colonial order that had threatened the Thomas Christians, that had set foreign bishops and reforming missionaries over them and dissolved the standing they had enjoyed for a thousand years, was the very thing that forged them into what they had now become: self-conscious modern communities, organized, literate, and articulate, able to govern themselves and to press their claims as they had never been able to do in all the centuries of their honoured but unselfconscious antiquity. The powers that came to remake the ground beneath the Thomas Christians had remade the Christians too. They had entered the new century an ancient and a divided people; they left it a modern and a divided people, and the difference was not nothing. The long contest over who should govern the Serra was over. The Thomas Christians governed themselves at last, each church its own, under the new sovereigns of a new world. 24

The chapter returns to the point reached at the close of Chapter 15. The unifying argument, that a shared subjection to foreign ecclesiastical powers, joined to a shared erosion of the community's old social standing, produced among the Thomas Christians a new self-conscious communal identity and a modern politics, is the author's, and it follows the social history of Susan Bayly, Saints, Goddesses and Kings: Muslims and Christians in South Indian Society, 1700–1900, and Robin Jeffrey, The Decline of Nayar Dominance: Society and Politics in Travancore, 1847–1908.

On the antiquity of the Syrians' standing: Bayly, Saints, Goddesses and Kings, insists that the St Thomas Christians were "far from having been a low-status, low-caste convert group," possessing instead "a history of power and privilege" dating from many centuries before European rule, their warriors, traders, and church notables honoured within the Hindu kingdoms in which they had settled, their rights traced in legend and copper-plate deed to the grants of early kings. The quoted phrase is Bayly's; it supplies the epigraph above.

On the parallel between the Syrians and the Nayars as honoured groups of the old order, on the Syrians' share in the inland trade: Bayly, Saints, Goddesses and Kings.

For the Padiyola and the Pandari affair, see Chapter 15 above. On Paremmakkal's governorship (1786–99) and his refusal to allow Paul Pandari any exercise of jurisdiction; on the election, after his death on 19/20 March 1799, of Abraham of Kattakkayam by the parish representatives, and the meeting at Changanacherry on 13 April 1799 which accepted Mar Pandari and presented clerics whom he ordained; and on George Sankoorickal, of the Parampil family and an alumnus of the Propaganda College, the last native Governador, dead in 1801: George John Nidhiry, Kuravilangad: The Sanctified Community.

On the appointment in 1803, for want of qualified men, of the Carmelite Raymond of St Joseph (Roviglia), whom Tisserant describes as a man of meagre ability, the Briefs for whose consecration, the first dispatches having been lost, came only in 1807: Tisserant, Eastern Christianity in India. The double subjection of the Catholic Syrians, to the Padroado archdiocese of Cranganore on the one hand and the Propaganda vicariate of the Carmelites at Verapoly on the other, with the freedom of a parish to attach itself to either, is the 'double regime' studied by Thomas Pallipurathukunnel, A Double Regime in the Malabar Church (1663–1716) (Alwaye, 1982); its nineteenth-century phase, from Sankoorickal's death to the suppression of 1838, is the subject of James A. Puliurumpil, A Period of Jurisdictional Conflict in the Suriani Church of India (1800–1838) (1994). See also Neill, vol. II.

For Pombal and the suppressions of 1759 and 1773, see Chapter 15 above. On the ruin of the Catholic missions in this age and its causes, the restoration of the Society of Jesus by Pius VII in 1814, the suppression of the religious orders in Portugal, and the rupture between Lisbon and Rome, from 1834, which left even the bishops Portugal consecrated without papal confirmation: Neill, vol. II, ch. 13. The political chronology, the flight of the court to Brazil in 1807, the revolution of 1820, the independence of Brazil, and the civil war of 1828–34, is common to the standard histories. The archbishops confirmed for Cranganore after 1801 (in 1806, 1819, and 1825) remained absentees, the see being kept by an administrator common to Cranganore and Cochin; Tisserant so describes das Neves in 1838, 'the administrator, or episcopal governor, of the dioceses of Cranganore and Cochin'.

For the Romo-Syrian sees in these decades, a procession of foreign administrators and bishops-elect wedded to the status quo, and for the long absence of a bishop of the people's own nation, see Neill, vol. II. That the old desire for a native prelate now divided into two roads, the eastward appeal to the Chaldeans and the inward renewal under Rome, is the author's framing of a division the sources record but do not so arrange.

On the suppression of the Padroado jurisdiction in India, save at Goa, by the brief Multa Praeclare of Gregory XVI in 1838, and the refusal of a body of Padroado Syrians, led by the administrator of Cranganore and Cochin, Manuel das Neves, to submit: Tisserant, Eastern Christianity in India; George John Nidhiry, Kuravilangad. On the appeal of 1849, when Kuravilangad and a hundred and fifty churches wrote to the Chaldean patriarch; the vicar apostolic's abolition, about 1855, of the village malpan schools, which left the Padroado parishes with no lawful way to ordain their clergy; the residence of the Chaldean priest Denha Bar-Yona at Kuravilangad from 1853; the meeting of 1854; and the deputation of 1857 under Anthony of Kudakkachira and Anthony Thondanat, on which Kudakkachira died at Baghdad: Nidhiry, Kuravilangad, whose source for the petition of 1849 is A. Kanjirathinkal, A Church in Struggle (1984), 54, and for the deputation's funds Bernard Alencherry, Marthoma Christhianikal (1992), 721; Tisserant; and A. M. Koonammakkal, Elements of Syro-Malabar History, which records Kudakkachira's death. Tisserant distinguishes the two priests, whom the Latin-mission documents name Anthony the Founder and Anthony Thondanatta (the Malayalam form Thondanat is used in the text), and gives the ordination refusal of 1858 and Thondanat's second journey. That the whole eastward movement grew from the Padroado grievance is drawn out by the author from these sources.

On Audo's years of canonical pressure before the breach: his reminder to Propaganda of Etsi Pastoralis, the bull of Benedict XIV of 26 May 1742 on the preservation of the Oriental rites, which became the pattern of relations between Latin and Eastern Catholics; his proposal of 7 November 1856 for a four-man commission of visitors, two chosen by Propaganda and two by himself; his letter of 12 December 1859 threatening resignation if he were not allowed to consecrate a bishop for Malabar; and Rome's answer in the spring of 1860, side-stepping the question and requesting the Chaldeans not to interfere: Tisserant, Eastern Christianity in India, 106–08. On the schisms of recourse to the East: the Chaldean bishop Thomas Rokos, an old servant of the patriarchate of Mosul, elected for Malabar on 12 September 1860 and consecrated on the 30th against the prohibition of Rome, forbidden by the Apostolic Delegate any exercise of episcopal acts, who came to Malabar in 1861 and was recalled when his mission collapsed; and the Chaldean Elias Mellus, who came in 1874 and returned to Mesopotamia in 1882, leaving a lasting schism centred at Trichur. The departure of Anthony Thondanat with Rokos; his consecration by the Nestorian Catholicos as Mar Abdisho (Tisserant names the consecrator Simon; Koonammakkal, Elements of Syro-Malabar History, records that Thondanat first approached the Chaldean patriarch, was rejected, and was then consecrated in Kurdistan by the Patriarch Mar Ruwel Shimon); his submission and absolution in 1865, and his later part in the Mellus movement; the substantially financial motive of the second campaign, the Mesopotamian Chaldeans numbering some thirty-five thousand against a far larger Malabar community; and the alms-collecting monks' use of the Padroado parish house at Trichur as their headquarters, which fixed the schism's seat there, all follow Tisserant, Eastern Christianity in India. Patriarch Audo went to the First Vatican Council 'with a firm resolution to reclaim all the ancient privileges of his patriarchate, including jurisdiction over Malabar'; he did not succeed, threw in his lot with the minority on papal infallibility, and signed the definition only on 29 July 1872, protesting that he parted with no patriarchal prerogative: Tisserant, 111–12. Mellus left India on 5 March 1882, having first ordained numbers of unfit candidates for cash, and installed as vicars over the party the monk Augustine of Alkosh and Thondanat, who had relapsed among the schismatics during the Mellus years; in 1887 he was still exhorting his partisans 'to remain faithful to Thondanatta the Bishop, and to Augustine the Chorepiscopus'; his final submission came only in 1889, and he died at Mardin in 1908: Tisserant, 118–19. Mar Abdisho died at Trichur on 16 November 1900: Koonammakkal. The Mellusians, by then reduced to some eight thousand, submitted in 1907–08 to the Nestorian Patriarch Simon XIX Benjamin, who gave them Mar Abimalek Timotheus as bishop (Tisserant, 119); Koonammakkal dates Timotheus's arrival at Trichur to February 1908, and the body has since been called the Chaldean Syrian Church. The reading of both schisms as renewed instances of the impulse seen in the Paul Pandari affair of Chapter 15, the willingness to take a bishop from a foreign East rather than remain under a foreign West, is the author's.

On Kuriakose Elias Chavara, one of the three founders (with Thomas Palackal and Thomas Porukara) of the first indigenous religious congregation at Mannanam in 1831, and on the renewal of seminaries, schools, and press that followed: Tisserant, Eastern Christianity in India. The congregation's names have their own history: its eleven members took their first vows only on 8 December 1855, under Bernardine of St Teresa, who fostered it, as the Servants of Mary Immaculate of Mount Carmel; on 1 October 1860 it became a regular Third Order of the Carmelites, whence the nineteenth-century initials T.O.C.D.; the present name, Carmelites of Mary Immaculate (CMI), dates from 1958 (Tisserant, 98, for the early history). Rokos, says Tisserant, sought to win Chavara with the offer of a bishopric; he eluded it, and only an order of the vicar apostolic moved him to accept even the title of vicar general, the same prelate being willing to make him a bishop in earnest. The judgment that a timely elevation of Chavara, or of any Thomas Christian, to the episcopate might have forestalled the worse schism of Mellus, and that the crisis was resolved only by the grant of native prelates in 1896, is that of A. M. Mundadan, Quest for an Indian Church. Tisserant's remark that from the early nineteenth century "the desire for an Indian bishop was always alive" is quoted in the text.

On the brief reunion of 1799, when Mar Dionysius I (Mar Thomas VI), called the Great, accepted an act of union on 20 May and came over to the Catholic communion on 21 June 1799, only to revert within some six months: Tisserant, Eastern Christianity in India. On the British Resident's emergence, under the paramountcy described in Chapter 16, as the effective arbiter among the Syrian factions and of the office of metropolitan: Neill, vol. II; Philip, The Indian Church of St Thomas. On Colonel Colin Macaulay (Resident 1800–10), his personal commitment to the protection of the Christians, and his correspondence concerning them (in which, tellingly, he still describes the community as 'the Syrian Sect of Nestorians'): John Fenwick, The Forgotten Bishops: The Malabar Independent Syrian Church and its Place in the Story of the St Thomas Christians of South India (2009), 296–98, from the India Office Records. On Buchanan's visits of 1806–07 and the gift by Mar Dionysius I of the ancient Syriac Bible now in the Cambridge University Library, of which Mar Dionysius III later doubted 'the propriety of their giving to Dr Buchanan the only entire copy of their Scriptures': Fenwick, 298–310, who notes that the manuscripts Buchanan carried away are mostly in East Syriac script; Neill, vol. II; E. M. Philip, The Indian Church of St Thomas, who records the death of Mar Dionysius I on 25 Meenam [March] 1808, aged eighty, and his burial at Puthencavu.

Mar Thoma VII was consecrated by Dionysius I alone in 1797 and outlived his uncle by some fifteen months, dying at Kandanad in June 1809; the deathbed making of Mar Thoma VIII, the dying bishop unconscious while the bystanders vested and named his nephew, rests on the near-contemporary account of Ramban Joseph Pulikkottil, with the intention, never honoured, that the consecration be regularized from Antioch: Fenwick, The Forgotten Bishops, 311–14, from MS Mill 192. On the star pagoda fund: Macaulay's letter of 25 October 1808 lodged three thousand star pagodas with the East India Company at eight per cent, the interest 'subject to the Bishop or Metropolitan pro tempore or other regular superior, however denominated, of that ancient Church', with a like deposit for the Pazhayakūr; the judgment that the vattipanam became 'like a millstone hanging round the neck of the community', competency to draw its interest becoming the test of every claim to the headship, is P. Cheriyan's, The Malabar Syrians and the Church Missionary Society (1935): Fenwick, 311–12. Of the fund's origin E. M. Philip, The Indian Church of St Thomas, weighs three versions and favours the tradition that Macaulay, his treasury emptied by the crisis that became the Velu Thampi insurrection, borrowed the sums from 'his Christian friends, viz., the Syrian Metropolitan and the Bishop of Verapoly', the unrepaid loans being converted into perpetual deposits at eight per cent; the bond, he notes, was issued five months after the death of Dionysius I, in the name of Mar Thoma VII.

Ramban Joseph Pulikkottil's representations to Munro, including his statement that 'there is at Chowghaut a Bishop who, in spite of being a poor and humble person, is however complete in the Episcopal Order': Fenwick, The Forgotten Bishops, 322–23, from MS Mill 192. Mar Thoma VIII's answers of 1813 to the Resident's questions assert that St Thomas conferred orders only on the families of Changarapoory and Pakalomattom, and that 'since the year AD 345… till the year 1653… for 1308 years, the persons of this family have been created Arch Deacons and they have held the Ecclesiastic Government of the Syrian Churches in Malialim', every metran since 1653 being of the same family: Fenwick, 318, from the India Office Records. On the standing of this claim, made in a dated document against the silence of the seventeenth-century European records, which name the archdeacons' house Parambil (de Campo) and not Pakalomattom, see Chapters 6 and 13 above. On the college at Kottayam, its foundation stone laid by the ramban in February 1813 on land given by the Rani of Travancore, opened 1815; on Madras's refusal to pay the star-pagoda interest to one who was not a bishop, and the consequent consecration of the ramban by Philoxenos II of Thozhiyur at St Mary's, Pazhanji (E. M. Philip dates it 9 Meenam [March] 1815; Mill thought 'most probably in 1813'; Kaniamparampil gives 1816); and on the Travancore proclamation of 21 Makaram [January] 1816 recognizing him as Malankara Metropolitan and requiring all Syrian Christians to obey him, with a similar proclamation from Cochin: Fenwick, 332–34, citing Philip, The Indian Church of St Thomas, 177, and Cheriyan, 105. Fenwick observes that this was 'the first time under British administration that the State Governments had been active in naming an individual as Malankara Metropolitan', and that 'for the next sixty years it was the Government, not the community, which decided who was Malankara Metropolitan'. Philip, writing from within the community, drew the same moral: from Dionysius II's time the two states began to interfere in the appointment and removal of Syrian bishops, and 'it became a law that no Bishop could freely exercise his episcopal authority unless he was recognised by the States through proclamations enjoining all Syrian Christians to obey him'. The stripping of Mar Thoma IX at Kadamattom is given by Kaniamparampil, The Syrian Orthodox Church in India, quoted in Fenwick, 324; Thoma IX's attempted return after Dionysius II's death, his letters to the parishes, and the party that set out for the Residency but turned back: Fenwick, 324, from MS Mill 192. On the sudden death of Dionysius II late in 1816 ('unexpected by us all, as we supposed him considerably better'), the missionary Thomas Norton's recommendation of Philoxenos II to the Resident, the kattanars' assent ('He is a man of prayer'), Philoxenos II's elevation as Malankara Metropolitan in 1817 and his receipt of the star-pagoda interest, which Fenwick calls 'what was becoming the mark of the legitimate Metropolitan' (Munro remitting him the 240 pagodas of annual interest in February 1817); his smallpox, his consecration of George Kathanar of Punnathra as coadjutor, and his retirement to Thozhiyur with the title: Fenwick, 347–53, quoting Norton's letters of 14 December 1816 and 26 February 1817 and Munro's of 13 February 1817. The date of Dionysius III's consecration is variously given (ca. 1816 in Fenwick's GEDSH article; 1818 in some lists); Dionysius IV followed in 1825.

On the visit of Claudius Buchanan in 1806 and his vision of the Syrian church reformed toward communion with the Church of England; on Colonel John Munro, Resident and for a time Dewan of Travancore, as patron of the enterprise; and on the Church Missionary Society's "Mission of Help" from 1816, with the seminary at Kottayam: Neill, vol. II (the chapter on the Mission of Help); Philip, The Indian Church of St Thomas.

On the missionaries' pressure for reform of the Syrian usages (the cult of the saints, prayers for the dead, the Syriac liturgy, the ordaining of boys to orders), and on the Synod of Mavelikara of January 1836, attended by Mar Dionysius, Mar Cyril of Thozhiyur, more than fifty cattanars, and laity, at which the reforms were refused and the connexion with the C.M.S. broken: Neill, vol. II, who records that the missionaries found the opposition "much stronger than they had supposed." The synod's written declaration, the Mavelikara Padiyola, that in matters of faith the Syrians could do nothing without the sanction of the patriarch of Antioch: Philip, The Indian Church of St Thomas; Neill, vol. II. On the two harvests of the division: the party that followed the missionaries into the Anglican communion, organized from 1879 in the Anglican diocese of Travancore and Cochin at Kottayam and merged in 1947 into the Church of South India (the entry on the Malankara Syriac Orthodox Church in GEDSH dates this seed, like the Mar Thoma church's, from the events of 1836); and the reform party within, gathered about Abraham Malpan of Maramon (1796–1845), malpan of the Kottayam seminary, whose Malayalam Qurbana and abolition of the prayers for the dead date from 1837, and which, after the consecration of his nephew Mathews Mar Athanasios at Mardin in 1843 and the litigation ending in the Royal Court's judgment of 1889, became the Mar Thoma Syrian Church. The characterization of the Mar Thoma church as keeping the Syriac inheritance while renouncing later accretions and standing on Scripture in ancient purity is that church's own self-understanding, and is so presented. On the Syrians' resistance as a defence of tradition: Philip, The Indian Church of St Thomas; Bayly, Saints, Goddesses and Kings, on the C.M.S. reform policies and the Syrian reaction.

On the decline of the Nayars' dominance as the new bureaucratic colonial state rendered their martial function obsolete and rewarded education and office-holding in its place: Robin Jeffrey, The Decline of Nayar Dominance, whose title states the thesis. The application of the same logic to the social order of Travancore as a whole is the author's gloss.

On the Syrians as traders rather than servants of the state, having, in Jeffrey's words, "no tradition of sirkar service," and so disadvantaged in the competition for the new prizes of education and government employment: Jeffrey, The Decline of Nayar Dominance. On the colonial order's dissolution of the old integrating system of royal honours and its manufacture of self-conscious, mobilized communal identities among groups such as the Syrians and the Nayars: Bayly, Saints, Goddesses and Kings. The synthesis of these into a single account of "anxious transformation" is the author's.

The reading of the two halves of the church as converging, under a shared subjection to foreign powers and a shared loss of standing, upon a common Nasrani identity that overrode their theological division is the author's, drawn from Bayly, Saints, Goddesses and Kings, and Jeffrey, The Decline of Nayar Dominance.

On Father Emmanuel Nidhiry (1842–1904) of Kuravilangad, of a wealthy land-owning family with a tradition of providing priests, educated at the C.M.S. College at Kottayam, master of Malayalam, Tamil, Sanskrit, Syriac, and English, vicar general and the leading political figure of the Romo-Syrians; on his founding of Nasrani Deepika in 1887; and on the Syrian National Union Association, formed in 1888 in co-operation with the Jacobite metropolitan Mar Dionysius, with its early effort for western-style education from 1883: Jeffrey, The Decline of Nayar Dominance (including the biographical appendix), who cites also A. M. Nidhiry, Father Nidhiry (Kottayam, 1971).

On the Malayali Memorial of 1891, the petition of the educated communities of Travancore (Nayars, Syrian Christians, and others) protesting the predominance of non-Malayali outsiders in the state's public service and demanding a fairer share for the country's own people: Jeffrey, The Decline of Nayar Dominance. The reading of it as the announcement of a self-conscious modern communal politics among the Thomas Christians is the author's.

The qualification is important and is supported by Jeffrey, The Decline of Nayar Dominance, whose account of Nidhiry notes that he had campaigned against the schismatics in 1876–82 and that his approaches to the Jacobites in 1886–89 were directed in part at winning them over to Rome. The unity movement is therefore not to be read as a disinterested ecumenism; it carried a Catholic-recruiting purpose, and served at once the communal and the confessional interest. The distinction drawn here between a unity of the people and a reunion of the church is the author's.

On the separation of the Syrian Catholics from the Latin vicariate of Verapoly in 1887 into the two vicariates of Trichur and Kottayam, still under European vicars apostolic (A. E. Medlycott at Trichur, Charles Lavigne at Kottayam); and on the reorganization of 1896 into the three vicariates of Trichur, Ernakulam, and Changanacherry under the first Indian vicars apostolic of the Syro-Malabar church, John Menachery, Aloysius Pazheparambil, and Mathew Makil (who took office on 11 August 1896): Tisserant, Eastern Christianity in India, in the section on the Indian Catholic hierarchy.

The continuity drawn from the Coonan Cross oath of 1653 (Chapter 12) through Kariattil and Paremmakkal (Chapter 15) to the hierarchy of 1896 is the author's. On the division of the Puthankūr into the reformed body that became the Mar Thoma church and the traditional Malankara church that retained the Syriac Orthodox faith under its own metropolitans, the present account follows Philip, The Indian Church of St Thomas, and Neill, vol. II; the internal disputes of the traditional body in the twentieth century lie beyond the scope of this chapter and are touched on only as the period requires.

The closing reading, that the colonial order both threatened the Thomas Christians and forged them into self-conscious modern communities, capable of self-government and of pressing their claims, is the author's, and it draws together the argument of Bayly, Saints, Goddesses and Kings, that communal self-consciousness in south India was in large part a creation of the colonial transformation. The judgement that the long contest over the government of the Serra closed, at the end of the nineteenth century, in self-government purchased at the price of division, is the author's, and brings Part Three to its end.

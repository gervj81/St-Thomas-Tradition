The Thomas Christians under Carmelites and British, 1799–1896

The Forging of a People

"Far from having been a low-status, low-caste convert group, the Syrians have a history of power and privilege."

— Susan Bayly, Saints, Goddesses and Kings

Two chapters ago we left the Thomas Christians in the act of reaching eastward, both halves of the divided church looking to Mesopotamia for legitimacy and then we left them altogether, to watch the ground shift beneath their feet. The ground has now shifted. The Dutch are gone from the coast, the old Padroado crown is a spent force, and in their room stand a Hindu sovereign state of Travancore and a Protestant paramount power in the Residency beside it. It is time to return to the Nazranis, and to ask what became of them under these new masters. The answer, traced across the long nineteenth century, is the matter of this chapter, and it has a single shape. Both halves of the Serra, in their different ways, came to feel the same want, the one craving a bishop of its own blood, the other struggling to keep intact the tradition of its fathers. Out of that shared anxiety, sharpened by the slow erosion of the community's old standing in the world, there would grow at last a new thing: a people conscious of itself as a people, and a politics to match. 1

The Honoured Place

To measure what the new century took from the Thomas Christians, one must first see what they had been. It is easy, looking back through the lens of the missionary age, to imagine the Syrians as a small and humble sect, a body of converts dependent upon European patronage; and it would be quite wrong. For many centuries before any European ruled in India the St Thomas Christians had been a community of standing, possessed of what their modern historian calls a history of power and privilege. They were warriors, traders, and church notables, honoured in the Hindu kingdoms among which they lived, holding rights and dignities that their own founder-legends traced to the grants of ancient kings. 2

In the martial society of Kerala they ranked with the Nayars, the sword-bearing aristocracy of the land; in its commerce they held a great share of the inland trade in pepper and the produce of the hills. They were, in short, no marginal people. They were woven into the fabric of the old Hindu order, and their dignity was bound up with it. That order was precisely what the new century would unmake. 3

The Pazhayakūr and the Want of a Bishop

For the Catholic Thomas Christians, the Pazhayakūr, the new century opened on the ashes of the revolt. The rising of 1787 had been put down and its leaders reconciled; its great hope, the dream of a bishop of their own nation that Joseph Kariattil had briefly embodied, lay buried with him at Goa. What remained of that hope was Thomas Paremmakkal, who as Governador of Kodungallur ruled the Catholic Syrians for thirteen years, a native government in all but the mitre. He held the community with a firm hand; when the appeal to Mesopotamia at last brought forth a bishop, Paul Pandari, it was Paremmakkal himself who shut the door, refusing the intruder any exercise of his orders. But he could not make himself immortal. He died in March 1799, and the want he had contained broke out at once. The parishes elected a governor of their own, Abraham of Kattakkayam; and a body of them, meeting at Changanacherry within weeks of the funeral, went further, accepting Mar Pandari for their bishop and putting forward their deacons for his hands. Rome had the affair undone, and the Padroado set in Paremmakkal's place one more native governador, George Sankoorickal, an alumnus of the Propaganda College. With his death in 1801 the line of native governors ended, and the government of the Catholic Syrians passed once more wholly under foreign hands. 4

Those hands were two, not one. The old Padroado of Portugal still claimed the Catholic Syrians through its archdiocese of Kodungallur, while the Propaganda of Rome ruled them through the Italian Carmelites of the vicariate of Verapoly, and a parish might attach itself to the one or to the other. But on either allegiance the Syrians were now ruled by men who were no bishops among them. The Carmelites of Verapoly, who held the greater number, were by this time so reduced in able men that in 1803, for want of a better, they gave the office of bishop to one of meagre gifts, Raymond of St Joseph; and even his Briefs miscarried on the war-swept sea roads of the age, so that the man chosen in 1803 could not be consecrated until 1807. The Padroado side fared worse, for the crown behind it was going under. 5

The reader will remember from Chapter 15 what Pombal's Portugal had done to the Society of Jesus: expelled it from every Portuguese dominion in 1759, and pressed Rome until the order was suppressed throughout the church in 1773. What followed in the new century was that story played out to its end. In 1807 Napoleon's army marched on Lisbon, and the royal court fled to Brazil, not to return for fourteen years. The liberal revolution of 1820 came next; then the loss of Brazil itself; then a civil war of brother against brother that bled the kingdom until 1834. And when the liberals had won it, they carried Pombal's work to its conclusion: Portugal suppressed not the Jesuits only but every religious order in its dominions and seized their houses, and this in the very generation in which Rome, in 1814, had raised the Society of Jesus back to life. The old regalist quarrel ended with the two swords exchanging places, the pope now protecting what the most faithful of crowns destroyed; and in 1834 Lisbon and Rome broke off relations altogether. A crown in such a state could not man an archdiocese on the Malabar coast. It named archbishops to Kodungallur who never came out to govern; on the spot the archdiocese was kept by an administrator, an episcopal governor, a single Portuguese-speaking officer serving the two sees of Kodungallur and Cochin together. At Verapoly a threadbare mission, at Kodungallur an empty throne: such was the double government of the Catholic Syrians in the decades before the storm broke. 6

Behind both jurisdictions lay the same lack. For more than a century after Kariattil, no bishop of the Pazhayakūr's own nation was appointed. The sees of these Catholic Syrians, the 'Romo-Syrians' as the British records and the Malankara side would later class them, passed through a long procession of foreign administrators and bishops-elect, and the community, as Neill observes, settled into the keeping of the status quo, making no converts and slowly losing ground. The old wish for a prelate of their own blood was not dead; it had only gone underground, waiting for a road. When a road at last opened it forked in two, and the two directions it took would divide the Catholic Syrians for the rest of the century. 7

The Broken Allegiance

The first road began in a wound of jurisdiction. A part of the community had leaned to the Padroado of Kodungallur, which carried for them the memory of the native governors and the near self-rule of Paremmakkal's day, and resented the Carmelites of Verapoly. In 1838 the ground was cut from under them. Hard upon the rupture between Lisbon and Rome, Pope Gregory XVI, by the brief Multa Praeclare, suppressed the Padroado jurisdiction throughout India save at Goa and ordered the Pazhayakūr under the Propaganda vicariate of the Carmelites. To the Padroado-allied Catholic Syrians this was no mere stroke of administration but the loss of the one authority they still owned as their own. A body of them refused to obey. Led by the Padroado administrator of Kodungallur and Cochin, Manuel das Neves, they held to the old allegiance; but an allegiance to a jurisdiction Rome had abolished could give them no bishop. They were now a faction that would not take a Carmelite and could not get a Padroado, and such a faction was bound to go looking for a bishop elsewhere.

They looked where their fathers had once found their bishops, in the East, to the Chaldean patriarch of Mosul, the Rome-aligned head of their own ancestral rite. The appeals began early. As far back as 1849, the priests and people of Kuravilangad, with a hundred and fifty other churches, had written to the patriarch for a bishop of their rite and tongue. Rome's own reforms then sharpened the want. The vicar apostolic, Bernardine of St Teresa (Joseph Baccinelli), was rebuilding the formation of the Syrian clergy, and not without cause: for generations most priests had been trained under a village malpan, a master at whose knee a boy learned to read his Syriac, the practice of the liturgy, and a few elements of theology, and no more. Bernardine replaced this apprenticeship with regular seminaries on the European model, closing some twenty of the little malpanate schools, and about 1855 he decreed that he would ordain no priest who had not passed through his own new houses. The reform raised the clergy, but it also closed a door: the Padroado parishes, having no bishop of their own, were left with no lawful way to ordain their sons at all. By this time a Chaldean priest, Denha Bar-Yona, sent out from Mosul, had settled in the church at Kuravilangad itself, and he fanned the longing. In 1854 the Padroado-aligned leaders met there and resolved to send a deputation to Mosul, and in 1857 it set out, led by Anthony of Kudakkachira and Anthony Thondanat. Kudakkachira died at Baghdad on the road. Thondanat came back; and when in 1858 the vicar apostolic refused to ordain the fifteen candidates he presented, he turned again to Mesopotamia, to press the patriarch harder. 8

The Chaldean Experiment

The patriarch, Joseph VI Audo, did not act rashly. For years he pressed his case at Rome by the proper channels: he reminded Propaganda that Rome's own law, the bull Etsi Pastoralis of Benedict XIV, commanded respect for the Oriental rites; he proposed a commission of visitors, half of Rome's choosing and half of his own, to examine the state of Malabar; and in December 1859 he warned that he would resign his see if the refusal continued, for it trenched upon the rights of his church. Rome answered in the spring of 1860 by side-stepping the question and bidding the Chaldeans not to interfere in Malabar. Then the patriarch acted. Over the express prohibition of Rome he consecrated a bishop for Malabar in September 1860 and sent him out, and in 1861 Thomas Rokos landed in the Serra. His coming was a paradox. The Chaldean patriarchate of Mosul was that part of the ancient Church of the East which had come into communion with Rome, so that Rokos was himself a Catholic bishop of the Pazhayakūr's own rite; yet, because his patriarch had sent him to assert an Eastern jurisdiction over Malabar without the leave of Rome, which had placed the territory under the Latin Carmelites, Rokos was forbidden every episcopal act. Thousands nonetheless flocked to him, glad of a Catholic bishop of the old lineage. But the mission could not stand against the censures of Rome, and within the year he was recalled. When he withdrew, in 1862, he did not go alone. With him went Anthony Thondanat; and in Mesopotamia Thondanat sought a mitre of his own. The Chaldean patriarch, chastened by the censures that had just ended the Rokos affair, refused to lay hands on him; so he went up into the hills of Kurdistan, to the catholicos of that part of the Church of the East which had never entered the Roman communion (catholicos being the ancient title of the church's head beyond the Roman world, as patriarch was of Antioch or Alexandria within it), and was consecrated by the Catholicos Mar Shimun under the name Mar Abdisho. His defiance did not last. He came home, submitted, was absolved in 1865, and settled to live as a simple priest at Mannanam. But his peace with Rome would not prove final.

Thirteen years later the experiment was tried again, and Thondanat had a hand in it once more. Patriarch Audo, having gone to the First Vatican Council resolved to reclaim the ancient privileges of his see, jurisdiction over Malabar among them, and having failed, prepared a fresh campaign; and the motive now was not jurisdiction alone but money, for his own Chaldeans in Mesopotamia were a poor people, barely thirty-five thousand in the hills of Kurdistan, while the Malabar community was near ten times their number and its offerings a prize worth the having. The new campaign was opened by alms-collecting monks, and one of them fixed his headquarters in the old Padroado parish house at Trichur, the town that would for that reason become the centre of all that followed. In 1874 the Chaldean bishop Elias Mellus came in person to press the claim, and the crisis reached its height. Mellus held his ground at Trichur for eight years, and left India only in March 1882; his own final submission to Rome would come only in 1889. But he did not leave his flock headless. For Thondanat the temptation of the old company had proved too strong: he had slipped back among the Chaldean party during the Mellus years, and before embarking Mellus installed him, with Augustine, a monk of Alkosh, as vicar over the faction. The old Mar Abdisho led it until his death at Trichur in 1900. By a last turn of the wheel, the orphaned congregation, which had set out to guard its Eastern Catholic heritage from Latin hands, found that it could no longer look to the Catholic patriarchate at Mosul at all: in 1908 it passed to the non-Catholic Assyrian Church of the East, welcoming that communion's metropolitan, Mar Abimalek Timotheus, and taking the name it still bears, the Chaldean Syrian Church. In their flight from the Carmelites they had come round, after three centuries, to the very allegiance the Portuguese had labored at Diamper to erase. 9

The Renewal from Within

While one part of the Pazhayakūr looked east for a bishop, another was quietly remaking the community from within, and it did so on the side of Rome and the Carmelites. Its author was a priest of the Catholic Syrians' own soil, Kuriakose Elias Chavara. In 1831, with two elder companions, he had founded at Mannanam the first indigenous religious congregation of the Malabar church, known in our day as the Carmelites of Mary Immaculate, the CMI, and from it flowed a renewal that the schisms could not give: seminaries, schools, a printing press, a disciplined native clergy. It was this movement, and not the Chaldean bishops, that the vicar apostolic Bernardine of St Teresa fostered and leaned upon; and when Rokos came, the defence of the Roman allegiance fell to Chavara. He had not sought it. Rokos first tried to win him over, offering him a bishop's own consecration, and the humble religious eluded the temptation; it took a direct order from the vicar apostolic before he would accept even the lesser title of vicar general of the Catholic Syrians, an office the same prelate would gladly have raised to a mitre in earnest. Once installed, Chavara sent the best priests of his young congregation through the parishes to rally clergy and people to Rome, and so held the bulk of the community against the Chaldean party. There is an argument, made by the community's own historians, that had Rome only pressed on and given Chavara or some other Thomas Christian the mitre itself at that hour, the later and worse schism of Mellus need never have grown as it did. As it was, the long want was answered only at the century's end, when in 1896 the Pazhayakūr were given prelates of their own nation to govern them; and through all the years of waiting, as their historian observes, the desire for an Indian bishop was always alive. 10

The Puthankūr and the Keeping of the Tradition

The other half of the Serra met a different master and a different trial. The Puthankūr had spent the eighteenth century to secure episcopal succession from the East, and as the century turned it made one last motion toward Rome. In 1799 its metropolitan, Mar Dionysius I, accepted an act of union and came over for a few months to the Catholic communion, only to draw back again within the year. It was the last such overture.

The power that would shape the Puthankūr in the new century was not Rome but the British. They came not as conquerors of the church, but as its self-appointed protectors. The first to take the ancient church under his wing was Colonel Macaulay, who made the protection of the Christians a personal cause and corresponded with the aged Dionysius I. The primary instrument of this British design, however, was the Anglican missionary Claudius Buchanan. After visiting Malabar in 1806, he carried home to England an ancient Syriac Bible, a gift from Dionysius. With it, he carried a vision of the ancient 'Syrian church' reformed and drawn toward communion with the Church of England rather than with Antioch or Rome. The old metropolitan died under this British watchfulness in 1808, leaving behind him a succession he had laboured to keep within his own house, the house that claimed the ancient archdeacons. 11

The Broken Succession

Within a decade of the Metropolitan's death, his archdeaconal house failed, and the failure nearly cost the Puthankūr their episcopate. Dionysius I had consecrated his nephew Thoma VII with his own hands in 1797; yet Thoma VII outlived his uncle by scarcely fifteen months. As he lay dying at Kandanad, unconscious and past speech, a desperate scene unfolded. Bystanders hastily dressed his nephew in episcopal vestments, set him kneeling by the cot, and declared him bishop. Thus, upon the sheer testimony of those present, was Mar Thoma VIII made. Unsurprisingly, the general body of the faithful never accepted the validity of this irregular making.

The question of who was truly bishop had, by then, acquired a sharp British edge. In 1808, Colonel Macaulay had accepted a deposit of three thousand star pagodas from the church. This sum was lodged with the East India Company as a loan in perpetuity, with the interest explicitly payable to "the Bishop or Metropolitan pro tempore, or other regular superior, however denominated, of that ancient Church". This fund, which came to be known as the vattipanam, was intended as a protective endowment. Instead, in the words of a historian of the community, it became a millstone about its neck. The competency to draw the interest on the Company deposit became the practical test of who truly headed the church. Consequently, the fund would feed a century of bitter litigation that, in the end, consumed many times its original capital. 12

The Exile's Return

A northern ramban, Joseph of Pulikkottil, now contested Mar Thoma VIII's claim to the metropolitan's seat and to the vattipanam attached to it. Colonel Munro, the new Resident, was sympathetic to him; but Joseph had one fatal weakness. He was not a bishop. If the Puthankūr were to escape the doubtful succession of Mar Thoma VIII, someone with unquestioned episcopal orders had to consecrate him.

The rescue came from the most unlikely quarter in the Serra: the little exiled church of Thozhiyur. Its origin lay in the schism of 1772, when the native bishop Koorilose, consecrated apart from Mar Dionysius, had been driven by Dionysius himself into the Zamorin's country, beyond the reach of Travancore and Cochin. There, in poverty and obscurity, his successors had preserved an undoubted episcopal line. When the main body found itself under a metropolitan whose orders many would not trust, it was to Thozhiyur that the challengers turned.

By then Joseph Ramban had already begun another work under British patronage. With land from the Rani and Munro's support, he had laid the foundation of a college at Kottayam in 1813 for the education of the Syrian clergy. Meanwhile the succession dispute passed into the Company's record. The Resident put questions to the rival parties, and in his written answers of 1813 Mar Thoma VIII rested his claim upon lineage. From the year 345 until 1653, he said, men of his house had been made archdeacons and had governed the Syrian churches; since 1653, every metran had come from the same family. In these papers, drawn up for a British inquiry, the claim of the Pakalomattom archdeaconal house appears in one of its earliest securely dated forms.

It did not save him. Around 1815, in St Mary's church at Pazhanji, Philoxenos II of Thozhiyur consecrated Joseph Ramban as Pulikkottil Mar Dionysius II. In January 1816 the government of Travancore issued a proclamation recognizing him as Malankara Metropolitan and requiring the Syrians of that obedience to acknowledge him; Cochin followed with a proclamation of its own. From this point the title Malankara Metropolitan entered the official machinery of the state. Whatever the older history of the name Malankara, the office now stood under the eye of the Resident's government. For the next sixty years, it would often be the state, not the community alone, that decided who could effectively hold it. 13

The old archdeaconal claim ended in humiliation. The aged Thoma IX, said to have been consecrated by the dying Thoma VIII, was confronted at Kadamattom by the party of the new metropolitan. They stripped him of robe, staff, mitre, and cross. With that scene the old Mar Thoma line, the house that claimed the ancient archdeacons, lost its rule over the Puthankūr.

But the confusion of those years had one more turn. Within months of his proclamation, Dionysius II was dead. His death came so suddenly that the missionaries, who had thought him recovering, were taken wholly unawares. The deposed Thoma IX tried to seize the moment. He sent letters to the parishes, bidding them return to him; some even set out for the Residency to plead for his restoration, before their courage failed them.

It was an English missionary, Norton, who carried the news of Dionysius II's death to the Resident. He recommended for the vacant seat the very man who had consecrated the dead metropolitan: Philoxenos II of Thozhiyur. The kattanars assented, saying simply that he was a man of prayer. So in 1817 Philoxenos II, bishop of the smallest and poorest church in the Serra, ascended the seat of the Malankara Metropolitan and drew the star-pagoda interest that had become the mark of the lawful head.

Smallpox soon broke him. He consecrated George of Punnathra as his coadjutor, resigned the burden to him as Dionysius III, and withdrew again to his exile church, keeping the title for a while longer. In 1825 he came forth once more to consecrate another successor, Cheppad Mar Dionysius. The schism of 1772 had returned as the salvation of the church that made it. For more than a decade, every secure consecration in the Puthankūr passed through Thozhiyur's hands.

The Help That Hurt

Under Colonel Munro's patronage the Church Missionary Society (CMS) proceeded, from 1816, with what was called a Mission of Help: not at first an attempt to convert the Malankara Syrians or to draw them from their church, but to assist it, to school its clergy in the new college at Kottayam, the Pazhaya Seminary of later usage, and so, in the missionaries' hope, to reform it quietly from within. 14

The help, however, turned into reformation. The Anglican missionaries looked upon the immemorial usages of the Malankara Syrians with the disapproval of evangelical Protestants: the invocation of the saints, the prayers for the dead, the ancient liturgy in its Syriac tongue, and even the old discipline by which boys were ordained as deacons. They pressed, gently at first and then less gently, for change. To the Malankara Syrians this was no help, but a threat to the tradition of their fathers, the one possession that the long centuries of foreign rule had not taken from them.

The breach came at the Synod of Mavelikara in January 1836. There the metropolitan Mar Dionysius IV, with Mar Cyril of Thozhiyur, more than fifty cattanars, and a body of laity, refused the missionaries' reforms. The missionaries, as the historian of the mission records, found the opposition much stronger than they had supposed, and the connection was broken. The gathered church put its refusal in writing, in the manner of its fathers: the Mavelikara Padiyola declared that in matters of faith the Syrians could do nothing without the sanction of the Patriarch of Antioch. Yet the breach did not end reform. Some who remained within the Syrian church gathered about Abraham Malpan of Maramon and laboured to reform it from within, putting the Qurbana into Malayalam and sweeping away the prayers for the dead and the images used in veneration. 15

The Borrowed Shield

There was a quiet irony in the Padiyola's appeal. The authority the synod invoked had had no hand in the church's actual survival. Through all the confusion of the preceding decades no delegate of Antioch had laid hands on a Malankara metropolitan: the succession from Dionysius II to Cheppad Dionysius IV had been kept alive by Thozhiyur alone. Antioch in 1836 was a name, a distant and theoretical majesty, and Dionysius IV reached for it as a shield: an authority high enough to overawe a missionary, perhaps even an empire. But a shield so invoked gave the reformers an opening. Abraham Malpan's nephew Mathew, schooled at the missionaries' own college at Kottayam, made the journey to Mardin, lived seven months in the Antiochian patriarch's house, and came home consecrated as metropolitan: Mathews Mar Athanasios, a reformer armed with the very authority the traditionalists had claimed for their own.

The old Cheppad metropolitan wrote to Mardin that the patriarch had been duped. Mardin sent a bishop, Yuyakim Mar Koorilose, to enquire; and the enquirer, upon arriving, produced papers of his own claiming the metropolitanate for himself. Cheppad, aged and cornered between two Antiochene claims, resigned his charge to Koorilose and retired. For nearly a decade the rivals fought it out before the courts, until in 1852 a royal commission appointed by the Raja of Travancore pronounced Koorilose's document a forgery and proclaimed Mathews Mar Athanasios the true metran. The state that had made a Malankara Metropolitan in 1816 now seated a reformer upon the same throne. When, in 1865, the traditional party answered in kind, sending another Joseph of the Pulikkottil house to Mardin to be consecrated Mar Dionysius V, the Raja's government would not recognize him either. Both parties had carried their legitimacy back to the East; and the see itself remained with the reformer. 16

The Price of Victory

What the traditionalists could not win from the state they sought from the patriarch in person. The Antiochian patriarch Ignatius Peter III took the mission upon himself. First he tried London, where his overtures to the Archbishop of Canterbury and the British government came to nothing. Then he came out to Travancore himself, in early 1875, and stayed nearly two years. He gathered the clergy at Mulanthuruthy in June 1876 and, presiding as 'Patriarch on the Apostolic Throne of Antioch and Holy Father of the Jacobite Syrians of Malankara', declared the reformer deposed, bound every parish to register its title deeds in the patriarch's name, and carved the whole of Malankara into dioceses, in honour of the seven churches of St Thomas the Apostle, setting over them metrans of his own making who were to obey no one but himself. The shield had become a sceptre. Even Dionysius V, who had invited him, could not govern the patriarch's reach; and when at last Peter departed, the relief, a historian records, was general. Mathews Mar Athanasios died half a year later, in 1877, and was buried at Maramon, lying, not seated in the manner of the old metrans, beside the uncle whose reform had made his career. 17

Then came the lawyers. For more than a decade the two parties contested the succession and the trust properties, the vattipanam among them, through the courts of Travancore, until in 1889 the Royal Court of Final Appeal gave judgment. The justices, searching 'use and wont' for a rule, found that the Malankara church had known no bond with the patriarch of Antioch before the seventeenth century, and that for two hundred years its metrans had commonly been made by their own predecessors; and yet the judgment seated Dionysius V, the patriarch's man, on the throne of Malankara and gave his party the churches and the funds. Thomas Mar Athanasios, the reformer's cousin and heir, walked out of the litigation with three parishes, Maramon, Kozhencherry, and Kottarakkara, and from that remnant grew a church of its own, the Mar Thoma Syrian Church, keeping the Syriac inheritance while renouncing what it held to be the accretions of later ages, and claiming to stand upon Scripture and the way of St Thomas in its ancient purity.

But the victors had paid a price of their own, for they had won the churches by pleading the supremacy of Antioch, and the plea was now written into the law of the land. Whether the Malankara Metropolitan was the native head of an Indian church or the servant of a foreign patriarch was henceforth the standing question of the Puthankūr's inner life; and out of that question, in the new century, would grow the great division between the party of the metran and the party of the bawa, the patriarch. Here it is enough to mark where the seed was sown. The keeping of the tradition for the Puthankūr, like the want of a bishop for the Pazhayakūr, had its price. 18

The Falling Status

Behind the ecclesiastical anxieties, the Pazhayakūr's want of a native bishop and the Puthankūr's struggle to keep its tradition, lay a deeper movement that touched all the Nazranis of the Serra, and their Hindu neighbours as well. The new political order was dissolving the old foundations of status, and communities that had stood high in the old world found the ground sinking beneath them. The Nayars, the martial aristocracy of Kerala, had held their dominance by the sword, as the soldiers of the old Hindu kings; but the British peace had no use for a caste of warriors, and the new bureaucratic state that the Residents built rewarded not the sword but the pen, not birth in a martial lineage but English education and the holding of office. The dominance of the Nayars, resting upon a military order now made obsolete, began its long decline. 19

The Nazranis felt a kindred dislocation. Their old standing had rested upon their place in the trade of the country and their honoured rank in the Hindu states; but the colonial economy opened the old trades to all comers and dissolved the protected niches, while the new ladder of advancement, government service, was one the Syrians were ill-placed to climb, for as traders and not servants of the state they had, in the judgement of their historian, no tradition of sirkar service. Those Syrian Christians who moved early through the English schools, especially in the Anglican and Protestant orbit, rose faster into the new world of clerks, teachers, officials, and petitioners. Much of the older Catholic and conservative Syrian world moved more slowly, still bound to the habits of trade, parish, land, and inherited honour.

The result was not simple ruin but anxious transformation. The old marks of standing were passing away, and new ones, education in the European mode, employment in the swelling offices of the state, and a voice in its councils, were taking their place. Every community that had once been secure in its rank now found that it must compete for the new prizes or sink. It was, as one of the leading students of this society has argued, the colonial order itself that worked the change: by breaking down the old Hindu and Muslim kingdoms, with their integrating web of royal honours, it dissolved the settled standing of groups like the Syrians and the Nayars and pressed them to remake themselves as self-conscious communities, organized to advance their claims. Caste hierarchy had not vanished; it had been translated into communal bargaining before the modern state. The Thomas Christians, who had been a people of assured dignity in the old order, were being made over, by the pressure of the new, into a community that had to assert itself in order to survive. 20

The Common Anxiety and the Politics of Unity

Here the two halves of the divided church, so long set against each other, found themselves facing the same way. The Pazhayakūr under the Carmelites and the Puthankūr under missionary pressure had each been brought to feel its subjection to a foreign ecclesiastical power; both had felt the weakening, under the new economy and the new state, of the old standing that had once made them secure; and both began to sense, beneath the theological quarrels that divided them, a common identity as Nazranis, the ancient Christians of Saint Thomas, a single people with a single inheritance and a single set of interests to defend. 21

The man who seized upon this sense and made a politics of it was a Catholic priest of the Pazhayakūr, Emmanuel Nidhiry of Kuravilangad. Born in 1842 into one of the wealthy land-owning families of his church, trained first in the old Syriac world of parish house and malpanate and then at the Old Seminary in Kottayam, where he acquired English as well, Nidhiry rose to be vicar general of his community and the ablest political head it had yet produced. He grasped that the scattered Thomas Christians would count for little in the new order unless they organized, and he set himself to give them the instruments. In 1887 he founded the newspaper Nasrani Deepika, a voice for the community in print; and in concert with the Jacobite metropolitan Mar Dionysius V he helped to bring into being a Syrian association to advance the common interests of the Nazranis in education, government employment, and standing before the state. 22

The high mark of this mobilization came in 1891, when the Nazranis joined with the Nayars and others of Travancore in the great petition called the Malayali Memorial, protesting that the offices of their own state were being filled by outsiders to the exclusion of its native sons, and demanding their rightful share. It was a new kind of act, a community pressing its claims upon the state in the modern political manner, and it announced that the Thomas Christians had become, whatever else they were, a self-conscious people in the public life of their country. 23

Yet it would be too much to call Nidhiry's a disinterested vision of Christian brotherhood. He was a Catholic before he was a Nasrani patriot, and his overtures to the Jacobites in the 1880s were aimed, in part, at winning them to papal union. The common Nasrani identity was real, and so were the interests it served; but it was never quite the same thing as a reunion of the sundered church, and the distinction would tell. 24

Government by Their Own

For the Pazhayakūr, the long want was at last answered, and the first step toward native episcopal government came in the lifetime of the men who had laboured for it. In 1887 Rome lifted the Syrian Catholics out of the Latin vicariate of Verapoly, under which they had chafed for a century, and gave them two vicariates of their own, Trichur and Kottayam, with their own administration and their own clergy. The bishops set over them were still Europeans, Medlycott and Lavigne, and the work was but half done; yet the principle was won: the Catholic Syrians were no longer merely a subject appendage of a Latin see. Then, in 1896, the work was crowned. Rome reorganized the Syrian Catholic vicariates into three, Trichur, Ernakulam, and Changanacherry, and over them, for the first time since the coming of the Portuguese, it set bishops of the Catholic Syrians' own nation: John Menachery, Aloysius Pazheparambil, and Mathew Makil. Thomas Christians were governing Thomas Christians at last. 25

The spark that had been struck at the Coonan Cross, that Kariattil had carried to Lisbon and Paremmakkal home again, that had smouldered through a century of foreign rule and flared in the schisms of Rokos and Mellus, had come at last to flame. The Pazhayakūr had a hierarchy of their own people. The Puthankūr, in their own fashion, reached a like settlement, though by a harder road and into a more divided end. 26

So Part Three closes upon a paradox. The pressure of the foreign powers had driven both halves of the Serra toward the same end, government by men of their own, and had even bred, in Nidhiry and his fellows, a vision of one Nasrani people, undivided, taking its place in the modern world. But the end that came was not one church; it was several: a Syro-Malabar church under its native Catholic bishops, a traditional Malankara church under its own metropolitans, a reformed Mar Thoma church beside it. The vision of unity had yielded a settlement of separate modern churches. And there was a deeper irony still. The colonial order that had threatened the Thomas Christians, that had set foreign bishops and reforming missionaries over them and dissolved the standing they had enjoyed for a thousand years, was the very thing that forged them into what they had now become: self-conscious modern communities, organized, literate, and articulate, able to govern themselves and to press their claims as they had never been able to do in all the centuries of their honoured but unselfconscious antiquity. The powers that came to remake the ground beneath Malabar had remade the Nazranis too. They had entered the new century an ancient and a divided people; they left it modern, self-conscious, governed increasingly by men of their own, and still more divided, and the difference was not nothing. The nineteenth-century contest over who should govern the Serra had reached its settlement. The Thomas Christians governed themselves at last, each church its own, under the new sovereigns of a new world. 27

And if it seemed that the storm first raised at the Coonan Cross had blown itself out at last, that the Serra at the dawn of the twentieth century had come into quiet water, it was not a settlement. It was a lull. The Travancore Darbar had resolved to compile the official history of the state, the work that would become the Travancore State Manual; the Dewan Peishkar Nagam Aiya passed the chapter on the Christians of Malabar to the British Resident, G. T. Mackenzie; and in 1901 Mackenzie published his findings as Christianity in Travancore, observing, with an administrator's weariness, that on their own past the churches held opinions 'wide as the poles asunder', pressed 'not only in academic controversy but also as the basis of litigation for the possession of Church property and of Trust funds'. He meant it as a caution. It proved a signal. Within the decade every church in the Serra was at the printing press, armed with a history of its own; and the storm that had seemed spent broke anew, this time upon the past itself. That storm is the matter of our final part. 28

The departure of Anthony Thondanat with Rokos; his consecration by the Nestorian Catholicos as Mar Abdisho (Tisserant names the consecrator Simon; Koonammakkal, Elements of Syro-Malabar History, records that Thondanat first approached the Chaldean patriarch, was rejected, and was then consecrated in Kurdistan by the Patriarch Mar Ruwel Shimon); his submission and absolution in 1865, and his later part in the Mellus movement; the substantially financial motive of the second campaign, the Mesopotamian Chaldeans numbering some thirty-five thousand against a far larger Malabar community; and the alms-collecting monks' use of the Padroado parish house at Trichur as their headquarters, which fixed the schism's seat there, all follow Tisserant, Eastern Christianity in India. Patriarch Audo went to the First Vatican Council 'with a firm resolution to reclaim all the ancient privileges of his patriarchate, including jurisdiction over Malabar'; he did not succeed, threw in his lot with the minority on papal infallibility, and signed the definition only on 29 July 1872, protesting that he parted with no patriarchal prerogative: Tisserant, 111–12. Mellus left India on 5 March 1882, having first ordained numbers of unfit candidates for cash, and installed as vicars over the party the monk Augustine of Alkosh and Thondanat, who had relapsed among the schismatics during the Mellus years; in 1887 he was still exhorting his partisans 'to remain faithful to Thondanatta the Bishop, and to Augustine the Chorepiscopus'; his final submission came only in 1889, and he died at Mardin in 1908: Tisserant, 118–19. On the division of the Puthankūr into the reformed body that became the Mar Thoma church and the traditional Malankara church that retained the Syriac Orthodox faith under its own metropolitans, the present account follows Philip, The Indian Church of St Thomas, and Neill, vol. II; the internal disputes of the traditional body in the twentieth century lie beyond the scope of this chapter and are touched on only as the period requires.

The closing reading, that the colonial order both threatened the Thomas Christians and forged them into self-conscious modern communities, capable of self-government and of pressing their claims, is the author's, and it draws together the argument of Bayly, Saints, Goddesses and Kings, that communal self-consciousness in south India was in large part a creation of the colonial transformation. The judgement that the long contest over the government of the Serra closed, at the end of the nineteenth century, in self-government purchased at the price of division, is the author's, and brings Part Three to its end.

The Travancore Darbar entrusted the compilation of the Travancore State Manual (3 vols, 1906) to V. Nagam Aiya; 'Mr. Nagam Aiya asked me to assist him with the chapter on Christianity', records G. T. Mackenzie, then British Resident in Travancore and Cochin, whose chapter was published separately, by leave of the Darbar and in advance of the Manual, as Christianity in Travancore (Trivandrum, 1901). The judgments quoted are Mackenzie's own, from his opening pages: the churches' opinions on their early history are 'wide as the poles asunder' (he arranges them in four classes), and are put forward 'not only in academic controversy but also as the basis of litigation for the possession of Church property and of Trust funds', so that 'an official publication must be neutral in such disputes and all that can here be attempted is to set out the facts with little or no comment'. The apologetic church histories that answered in the new century, E. M. Philip's The Indian Church of St Thomas (1908) and Bernard of St Thomas's Marthoma Christianikal among them, together with the assault upon and defence of the apostolic tradition itself, belong to Part Four.

The Metran–Bava schism and the canonical hierarchy, 1909–1923

The Rupture and the Edifice

"The Church in India is not a colony of Antioch, nor is it a mission of Rome; it is the ancient flock of the Apostle Thomas, seeking to govern itself in the modern world."

— Father Placid Podipara, The Syro-Malabar Church

Chapter 18 closed on the edge of a storm, with a Patriarch stepping ashore. The two halves of the Serra came into the twentieth century by one road and left it by two. Each was in search of the same thing, a church governed from within; but where the Puthankūr found their self-government through a rupture with Antioch so deep that it split them yet again, the Pazhayakūr found theirs through Rome, which first partitioned the community to quiet an old quarrel of blood and then, at last, raised over it a native hierarchy of its own rite. This chapter follows both roads, and the institutions they left standing. 1

The Rupture

When Ignatius Abdullah II, Patriarch of Antioch, came ashore in 1909, the body he had crossed the ocean to master had already broken more than once. Thozhiyur had gone in 1772, the Mar Thoma reformers by the Royal Court's verdict of 1889; and, as the earlier chapters showed, each parting had turned in the end on the question no synod could keep out of the civil courts: who was the lawful Malankara Metropolitan, and so who held the vattipanam and the properties bound to that title. The old thread now ran into its gravest test. 2

A Demand and a Refusal

Abdullah had not come merely to bless a distant flock. For two generations the Patriarchs had held over the Puthankūr a supremacy that was real but largely spiritual, exercised at a distance through the bishops they sent and the chrism they blessed. Abdullah meant to turn that honour into government. He required the newly elected Malankara Metropolitan, Vattasseril Geevarghese Mar Dionysius VI, to sign a registered deed, an udampadi, surrendering to the Patriarchal see not obedience in faith alone but the temporal administration of the Indian church, its parishes and, at the centre of it, the vattipanam. Dionysius refused. He did not deny the Patriarch's spiritual primacy; he denied that the primacy carried the title-deeds to Malabar, and he could point to the Royal Court itself, which in 1889 had defined the Patriarch's authority over Malankara as a limited, spiritual one. The spiritual sword was Antioch's; the temporal administration belonged to the Metropolitan and the lay trustees (palliyogam). In 1911 Abdullah answered by excommunicating Dionysius and consecrating a rival Metropolitan, Kochuparambil Paulose Mar Coorilos. The community broke along the line of that quarrel into the Metran Kakshi, the Bishop's Party that held to Dionysius and to native self-rule, and the Bava Kakshi, the Patriarch's Party that held to Antioch. It was the third and gravest fracture of the Puthankūr, and the second to be fought over the vattipanam. 3

Why the Serra Had No Catholicos

The excommunication exposed a weakness at the heart of the autonomy the Metran party wanted. A church that means to govern itself must be able to make its own bishops, and this the Malankara Church could not do. In the West Syrian order the power to consecrate lay with the Patriarch and, beneath him, with one great subordinate: the Catholicos, or Maphrian, of the East, an office seated for centuries not in India but in Mesopotamia, at Tikrit and later Mosul, whose holder ranked second in the whole church and governed the eastern provinces in the Patriarch's name. India had never possessed it. India had only its Malankara Metropolitan, an administrative head who depended for his very orders on prelates sent from the Middle East. So long as that channel ran, the arrangement held. The moment Abdullah closed it, cutting off Dionysius and the supply of bishops with one stroke, the Metran party found itself a church that could not renew its own hierarchy. If it would not bend, it would have to build; and to build, it needed a Catholicos of its own. 4

The Three Ignatiuses

The instrument lay to hand, and it was an odd one: a Patriarch whom Antioch itself had cast out. To see how the champions of Indian self-rule came to be rescued by a deposed man, the quarrel must be followed back to its Ottoman source. Ignatius Abdul Masih II had been the reigning Patriarch of Antioch since 1895. Around 1903, and finally by 1906, the Ottoman state withdrew his firman, the imperial decree that gave a Patriarch legal standing in the empire, and recognised in his place a rival who took the throne as Ignatius Abdullah II, the same Abdullah who would sail for Malabar three years later. Why Abdul Masih fell is remembered in two irreconcilable ways. His partisans held the deposition a purely secular act, a firman revoked by a Muslim sultan at a rival's prompting and therefore canonically worthless: no synod of the church had judged him, and what the state alone had done the state alone could undo. His opponents answered that the Holy Synod had indeed deposed him for misconduct, and that Abdullah's succession was clean. The dispute was never settled, and its very insolubility was what made Abdul Masih useful. 5

For the Metran party the logic was exact. If Abdullah II was no true Patriarch, if the man who had excommunicated Dionysius had himself taken a throne wrongfully emptied, then the excommunication was void and the lawful Patriarch was the deposed Abdul Masih. They invited him; he came. On 15 September 1912, in the ancient church of Niranam, one of the seven the tradition ascribes to the Apostle himself, Abdul Masih did what no prelate on Indian soil had ever done. He revived and relocated the Catholicate of the East to Malabar and consecrated Mar Paulose Ivanios as its first Catholicos, Baselios Paulose I. The office that had sat a thousand years in Mesopotamia now had an Indian seat, and the Malankara Church could at last make its own bishops. Three men named Ignatius had settled the matter between them: Abdullah, who broke with India; Abdul Masih, who armed it; and a third, still to come, who would set the breach in stone. 6

The Metropolitan Who Would Not Be Catholicos

There is an oddity in the settlement of 1912 that repays attention. The natural candidate for the new Catholicate was Dionysius VI himself, the architect of the whole resistance. He did not take it. The Metran party raised an older bishop, Paulose Ivanios, to the supreme spiritual office and left Dionysius where he stood, as Malankara Metropolitan. The reason was not modesty. It was law. From the moment the community split, the deeper question, who governed the Malankara Church, could be fought in Travancore only through the one thing its courts would recognise, which was title, not theology. Dionysius's whole claim rested on his being the lawful Malankara Metropolitan, the administrative head the deeds and precedents named. To exchange that title for the novel and hotly contested dignity of Catholicos might have thrown away the very standing the case depended on. So the offices were kept apart by design: Dionysius held the temporal title that could win in court, while a colleague held the spiritual title that guaranteed the line of bishops. 7

The prize went to law at once, and this was the second vattipanam case. In 1913 the British Secretary of State, unable to say which of two rival Metropolitans might lawfully draw the interest, laid the question before the District Court at Trivandrum in an interpleader suit. What followed vindicated Dionysius's caution, but only after it had first mocked it. The District Court found for the Metran party in 1919; the Patriarchal party appealed, and in 1923 a Full Bench of the Travancore High Court reversed the decree in their favour; the Metran party sought a review, and in 1928 the High Court reversed once more and restored the funds to Dionysius. The money banked a century before to endow a seminary had become, a second time, the token of who led the Malankara Church, and the title to it had swung twice in nine years. Even this settled nothing for long: a fresh suit opened in 1938 and ran until 1958, so that the two vattipanam cases together shadowed the community for the better part of eighty years. 8

Elias III and the Peace That Failed

The rival Patriarchs did not long outlive their quarrel. Abdul Masih II, returned to the Middle East, died in August 1915; Abdullah II followed within three months. With both thrones empty, the Syrian Orthodox synod elected in 1917 a single successor, Ignatius Elias III, and the schism within Antioch itself closed as abruptly as it had opened. India's did not. Elias inherited Abdullah's throne and Abdullah's cause. He held the Catholicate of 1912 to be null, the act of a deposed man, and he favoured the Bava party that acknowledged him. 9

In 1931 Elias came to Malabar in person, invited by the Viceroy, Lord Irwin, to broker a peace between the two factions. He could not. The Metran party would not surrender the autocephaly Niranam had given them, and the Patriarch would not recognise it; the talks broke on the very point that had begun the affair. Elias died in Kerala the next year and was buried at Manjanikkara, the only Patriarch of Antioch to lie in Indian soil. His grave is a shrine now, a sign at once of how far the reach of Antioch had once run and of how completely the two churches had settled into a rest that was not peace. 10

Two Readings, and No Rest

What the settlement finally meant, the Orthodox and the Jacobites have never agreed, and their disagreement turns on the very events just narrated.

To the Malankara Orthodox, the line from Niranam runs straight and true. Dionysius VI was the legitimate Malankara Metropolitan, lawful heir of an unbroken native seat; the Catholicate of 1912 gave that seat, for the first time, a spiritual independence to match its temporal right; and the Constitution of 1934, which merged the two offices so that one man held both the Catholicate and the Metropolitanate, set that independence into modern legal form. On this reading the verdicts of the Indian courts, when they came, are not the accidents of litigation but the secular vindication of a church that had been right all along to keep its own house.

To the Jacobites, the same edifice is void at the foundation. Abdul Masih II was a lawfully deposed Patriarch; a deposed man can consecrate nothing; the Catholicate he raised at Niranam was therefore canonically empty, and the Constitution of 1934 that built upon it was itself void, the consolidation of a rebellion, a device by which one faction dressed a spiritual usurpation in the language of trust law. That a civil court should hand over the properties on the strength of such a document proves only, in this view, that the secular power never grasped what was at stake: the apostolic bond between Antioch and India, which no registrar's deed can sever.

Between the two readings the record itself gives no rest. The temporal fight swung back and forth for decades: the Metran party carried the first vattipanam case on review in 1928, only for a second suit to reopen the whole question from 1938 and run to 1958; and the Constitution of 1934, framed in the year Mar Dionysius VI died, codified an autonomy that the courts of independent India would uphold in 1958, see broken by a failed reconciliation, and uphold again in 1995 and in 2017. A century after Niranam, parishes were still changing hands church by church, under police guard. Whatever the settlement was, it did not end there; it has not ended yet. 11

The Puthankūr had bought their self-government at the price of a permanent division, and that price was still being paid in the courts a century later. The other half of the Serra reached the same end of native self-rule by an entirely different road: not through rupture with a foreign see, but through the patient completion of a hierarchy under one.

The Personal Vicariate

While the Puthankūr fractured over patriarchal authority, the Pazhayakūr faced internal ethnic divisions that threatened their newly won native episcopate. The community had long been divided, along lines of claimed descent, into Southists and Northists. The Southists, the Knanaya, held themselves the pure posterity of the Mesopotamian colonists who had come with Thomas of Cana, and guarded that descent by a strict endogamy; the Northists were not a matching caste of their own but the general body of the St. Thomas Christians, from whom the Knanaya kept apart. In 1896, when Rome established three native vicariates apostolic for the Syro-Malabar Catholics (Trichur, Ernakulam, and Changanacherry), both groups were placed under the same territorial jurisdictions. This territorial arrangement quickly ran into local friction. The Southists, concentrated in the southern parishes, felt their distinct ethnic identity and traditions were being marginalized by the Northist majority. In Changanacherry, where the Southist bishop Mathew Makil governed a mixed diocese, the ethnic tensions reached a head, with Northist cattanars protesting against his administration and Southists petitioning Rome for their own bishop. 12

Rome resolved this impasse not through territorial division, but by establishing a personal jurisdiction. On August 29, 1911, Pope Pius X issued the apostolic letter In universi christiani, creating the Vicariate Apostolic of Kottayam exclusively for the Knanaya Syrian Catholics. Bishop Mathew Makil was transferred to Kottayam as its first vicar apostolic, while the territorial see of Changanacherry was returned to a Northist bishop, Mar Thomas Kurialacherry. This personal diocese, which held jurisdiction over all Knanaya Catholics regardless of their geographic location in Malabar, was a unique canonical creation. It successfully pacified the community, preserving the Knanaya social structure and preventing a schism, while demonstrating Rome's willingness to adapt canonical structures to the ancient social realities of the Serra. 13

Rome Completes the Edifice

The creation of the personal vicariate at Kottayam laid the final foundation stone for the canonical restoration of the Syro-Malabar Church. For over three centuries, since the death of Archbishop Mar Abraham in 1597 and the Synod of Diamper in 1599, the Catholic Syrians had been governed by European prelates—first Portuguese Jesuits under the Padroado, and later Italian Carmelites under the Propaganda Fide. The appointment of native vicars apostolic in 1896 had been a concession, but the vicariates remained provisional missionary structures under the direct supervision of the Propaganda congregation. The final step was to restore a permanent, self-governing hierarchy with a metropolitan archbishop of their own rite. 14

This canonical milestone was achieved on December 21, 1923, when Pope Pius XI issued the constitution Romani Pontifices. The decree established the Syro-Malabar Hierarchy, elevating Ernakulam to a Metropolitan See and naming Mar Augustine Kandathil—who had succeeded Mar Aloysius Pareparambil in 1919—as the first Metropolitan Archbishop of the Syro-Malabar Church. The sees of Changanacherry, Trichur, and Kottayam were designated as suffragan dioceses. With this act, Rome completed the canonical edifice of the Syro-Malabar Church, formally recognizing it as an individual, self-governing eastern rite (sui iuris) in communion with the Holy See. The long struggle for native self-government, which had begun with the protests of the Archdeacon in the seventeenth century and the travel diaries of Paremmakkal in the eighteenth, was at last realized in the canonical law of the modern church. 15

The institutions raised in these years—a Catholicate at Niranam, a personal see at Kottayam, a restored hierarchy at Ernakulam—were also, each of them, acts of memory. To build a community is to fix its past, and the past the Nazranis fixed in these decades was carried less in charters than in song: the Pattu sung at weddings and feasts, and nowhere more tenaciously than in the oral history of the Knanaya, whose very descent the Vicariate of 1911 had just written into canon law. It was in exactly these years that those songs left the palm-leaf and the memory for the printed page—Philip drawing on them in 1908, Lukas gathering the ancient songs in 1910, Bernard of St Thomas printing the whole Ramban Pattu in 1916—so that a fluid, variable tradition became, for the first time, a book; and a book yielded fixed coordinates: a landing in the year 52, a death in 72, seven churches named and counted. The word that licensed the hardening was ancient: because the community was old and its faith older, the numbers too were received as old. 16

And it was upon exactly this hardened precision that the sharpest blade fell, held by a man of the community's own. T. K. Joseph, a St. Thomas Christian himself, turned upon his people's songs the cold philology of dates and loanwords. To follow that quarrel is to step behind the institutions of this chapter to the traditions beneath them—the copper plates, the migration of Knāi Thomma, the song of the Ramban—and to ask what, stripped of the borrowed word ancient, they can still be made to bear. It is to return, by the community's own memory, to the question with which this history began: the Apostle, and the Acts that first carried his name to India.

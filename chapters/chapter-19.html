The Metran–Bava schism and the canonical hierarchy, 1909–1923

The Rupture and the Edifice

"The Church in India is not a colony of Antioch, nor is it a mission of Rome; it is the ancient flock of the Apostle Thomas, seeking to govern itself in the modern world."

— Father Placid Podipara, The Syro-Malabar Church

The twentieth century opened with a search for native self-government across both halves of the divided Serra, but the path to independence led through different fields. For the Puthankūr (the Jacobite Syrians), the struggle for autonomy took the form of a dramatic civil and ecclesiastical rupture between their local metropolitan and the Syrian Orthodox Patriarch of Antioch. For the Pazhayakūr (the Catholic Syrians), the quest for native bishops was answered by Rome, which first partitioned the community to resolve ethnic tensions and then, at last, completed the canonical hierarchy, restoring native self-government under a metropolitan of their own rite. This chapter follows these twin developments, showing how the ecclesiastical battles of the early twentieth century shaped the modern divisions and institutions of the St. Thomas Christians. 1

The Rupture

The power struggle within the Puthankūr community, known as the Metran–Bava (Bishop–Patriarch) dispute, was ignited by the arrival of the Syrian Orthodox Patriarch of Antioch, Ignatius Abdullah II, in Malabar in 1909. For decades, the Patriarchs of Antioch had claimed spiritual supremacy over the Malabar church, but Abdullah II sought to translate this spiritual oversight into total temporal and administrative control. He demanded that the newly elected Malankara Metropolitan, Vattasseril Geevarghese Mar Dionysius VI, sign a registered deed of submission yielding all administrative authority and ownership of church properties—including the trust funds of the Kottayam Seminary—to the Patriarchal see. Mar Dionysius, a man of formidable administrative skill and unbending resolve, refused. He argued that while the Patriarch held supreme spiritual authority, the temporal administration of the Indian church belonged to its own Metropolitan and trustees. 2

This refusal led to an open rupture. In 1911, Patriarch Abdullah II excommunicated Mar Dionysius VI and consecrated a rival metropolitan, Mar Coorilos, splitting the Puthankūr into two hostile factions: the Metran Kakshi (the Bishop's Party) and the Bava Kakshi (the Patriarch's Party). Seeking to secure ecclesiastical legitimacy and independence, the Metran party invited the deposed former Patriarch of Antioch, Ignatius Abdul Masih II, to Malabar. In 1912, Abdul Masih II arrived in India and, on September 15, 1912, at the church in Niranam, established the Catholicate of the East. He consecrated Mar Paulose Ivanios as Catholicos Baselios Paulose I. This act created an autocephalous structure within the Malankara church, giving the Metran party the power to consecrate their own bishops and metropolitan. The schism initiated a century of bitter litigation over parish properties, church funds, and the limits of patriarchal authority, a legal warfare that would harden the sectarian identities of the Orthodox and Jacobite churches. 3

The Personal Vicariate

While the Puthankūr fractured over patriarchal authority, the Pazhayakūr faced internal ethnic divisions that threatened their newly won native episcopate. Since the Knanaya migration of the fourth century (or the ninth century, as critical scholars argued), the St. Thomas Christians had been divided into two endogamous groups: the Southists (Knanaya) and the Northists. In 1896, when Rome established three native vicariates apostolic for the Syro-Malabar Catholics (Trichur, Ernakulam, and Changanacherry), both groups were placed under the same territorial jurisdictions. This territorial arrangement quickly ran into local friction. The Southists, concentrated in the southern parishes, felt their distinct ethnic identity and traditions were being marginalized by the Northist majority. In Changanacherry, where the Southist bishop Mathew Makil governed a mixed diocese, the ethnic tensions reached a head, with Northist cattanars protesting against his administration and Southists petitioning Rome for their own bishop. 4

Rome resolved this impasse not through territorial division, but by establishing a personal jurisdiction. On August 29, 1911, Pope Pius X issued the apostolic letter In universi christiani, creating the Vicariate Apostolic of Kottayam exclusively for the Knanaya Syrian Catholics. Bishop Mathew Makil was transferred to Kottayam as its first vicar apostolic, while the territorial see of Changanacherry was returned to a Northist bishop, Mar Thomas Kurialacherry. This personal diocese, which held jurisdiction over all Knanaya Catholics regardless of their geographic location in Malabar, was a unique canonical creation. It successfully pacified the community, preserving the Knanaya social structure and preventing a schism, while demonstrating Rome's willingness to adapt canonical structures to the ancient social realities of the Serra. 5

Rome Completes the Edifice

The creation of the personal vicariate at Kottayam laid the final foundation stone for the canonical restoration of the Syro-Malabar Church. For over three centuries, since the death of Archbishop Mar Abraham in 1597 and the Synod of Diamper in 1599, the Catholic Syrians had been governed by European prelates—first Portuguese Jesuits under the Padroado, and later Italian Carmelites under the Propaganda Fide. The appointment of native vicars apostolic in 1896 had been a concession, but the vicariates remained provisional missionary structures under the direct supervision of the Propaganda congregation. The final step was to restore a permanent, self-governing hierarchy with a metropolitan archbishop of their own rite. 6

This canonical milestone was achieved on December 21, 1923, when Pope Pius XI issued the constitution Romani Pontifices. The decree established the Syro-Malabar Hierarchy, elevating Ernakulam to a Metropolitan See and naming Mar Augustine Kandathil—who had succeeded Mar Aloysius Pareparambil in 1919—as the first Metropolitan Archbishop of the Syro-Malabar Church. The sees of Changanacherry, Trichur, and Kottayam were designated as suffragan dioceses. With this act, Rome completed the canonical edifice of the Syro-Malabar Church, formally recognizing it as an individual, self-governing eastern rite (sui iuris) in communion with the Holy See. The long struggle for native self-government, which had begun with the protests of the Archdeacon in the seventeenth century and the travel diaries of Paremmakkal in the eighteenth, was at last realized in the canonical law of the modern church. 7

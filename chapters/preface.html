Setting the Stage

Preface

History is often written backward, tracing the monumental institutions of the present to their supposed inevitability in the past. But history was not lived backward. It was lived forward, by individuals navigating immediate realities, physical constraints, and the immense complexities of the worlds they inhabited.

The tradition that St. Thomas the Apostle travelled beyond the boundaries of the Roman Empire to establish the Christian faith in India is one of the oldest, most resilient, and most debated narratives in historical theology. For centuries, it has been dismissed by skeptics as pious legend and defended by believers as unquestionable truth. This book attempts something different.

We begin not with proof texts or theology, but with geography and trade. Before asking if Thomas went to India, we must first establish how anyone could have travelled there. By mapping the commercial and linguistic realities of the 1st century CE, we reconstruct the cosmopolitan matrix that connected the Mediterranean basin to the Malabar Coast.

From there, we follow the threads of evidence—archaeological, textual, and ecclesiastical—across the centuries, tracing how a deeply rooted tradition was transmitted through the Syriac-speaking world and eventually crystallised into the institutional identity of the St. Thomas Christians of Kerala.

But uncovering origins is only the first half of the story. The narrative then turns to the sixteenth century, where this ancient, organic identity encounters the expanding Iberian maritime empire. From the initial diplomatic engagements at Cochin to the theological confrontation at the Synod of Diamper and the rupture marked by the Coonan Cross Oath, this book traces how two distinct Christian worlds—one anchored in immemorial custom, the other structured by Tridentine canon law—interacted, conflicted, and ultimately fragmented the community.

The final part of this study follows the long and complex aftermath of that fragmentation. It traces the struggles of the Pazhayakoor community as it navigated Latin ecclesiastical structures, internal reform, and shifting political realities, eventually emerging in its modern form as the Syro-Malabar Church.

This is not a work of apologetics, nor an attempt to construct a narrative that legitimises one group over another. It is a work of historical synthesis: a critical reading of the available sources undertaken, as far as possible, with methodological rigour, written from within the Nazrani historical experience. It invites the reader—especially fellow Nazranis—to retrace the ancient routes, examine the surviving evidence, inhabit the intensity of political upheavals, and weigh the history for themselves. Ultimately, it is neither useful nor prudent to assign blame to the past, for what we are is, inescapably, the product of that history.

— Gerald Johnson

Setting the Stage

Preface

History is often written backward, tracing the monumental institutions of the present to their supposed inevitability in the past. But history was not lived backward. It was lived forward, by individuals navigating immediate realities, physical constraints, and the immense complexities of the worlds they inhabited.

The tradition that St. Thomas the Apostle travelled beyond the boundaries of the Roman Empire to establish the Christian faith in India is one of the oldest, most resilient, and most debated narratives in historical theology. Much of the modern discourse surrounding this community has remained trapped within two narrow, polarizing questions: Did St. Thomas actually go to India? and Were the Nazranis of Malabar heretics? Their origins have been either dismissed by skeptics as pious legend or defended by believers as unquestionable truth. Their later history has been either prosecuted by Latin reformers as a heritage of error requiring correction, or mourned by Nazranis as an authentic tradition systematically dismantled, its books consigned to the flames at Diamper. This book engages both, but resists being bogged down by the mere "historicity" of an origin or the polemics of heresy. It attempts something different.

Rather than stalling at the apostolic arrival, the narrative first explores the organic crystallisation of an ancient identity, born from cosmopolitan maritime networks and sustained through deep ecclesiastical ties to the Syriac-speaking world. It then turns from theological judgment to lived experience, tracing how this indigenous Christian world collided with an expanding Iberian empire during the profound rupture of the sixteenth century. From there it takes up the long aftermath: the community's examination and defence of its own traditions, undertaken against the backdrop of the intense political and ecclesiastical rivalries that fragmentation produced.

This is not a work of apologetics, nor an attempt to construct a narrative that legitimises one group over another. It is a work of historical synthesis: a critical reading of the available sources undertaken, as far as possible, with methodological rigour, written from within the Nazrani historical experience. It invites the reader—especially fellow Nazranis—to retrace the ancient routes, examine the surviving evidence, inhabit the intensity of political upheavals, and weigh the history for themselves. Ultimately, it is neither useful to assign blame to the past, nor prudent to romanticise it, for what we are is, inescapably, the product of that history.

— Gerald Johnson

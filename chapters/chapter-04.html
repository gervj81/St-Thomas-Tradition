1037 CE – 1498 CE

The Legend, the Witnesses, and the Horizon

The previous chapter closed with Alfred the Great's envoys setting out for the tomb of St. Thomas in 883 CE, the tradition's farthest reach across a darkening world. Within a century and a half, the window through which those emissaries might have passed would be closed altogether. What followed was not a single story but two: a legend developing in the West, and a documentary record accumulating in the East.

The Turkic Portcullis

The consolidation of Seljuk Turkish power in Persia and Mesopotamia from 1037 CE onward accomplished what the Arab conquests four centuries earlier had not: the effective severance of the remaining channels connecting the Church of the East's Indian province to its institutional centre at Baghdad.

The Arab caliphates had disrupted the Alexandrian vector and transformed the Persian Gulf corridor, but the Church of the East had survived, indeed flourished, within the caliphal system. Patriarch Timothy I had operated from Baghdad itself, negotiating with caliphs and administering bishoprics from the Euphrates to India and to China. The Seljuks shattered this arrangement. Tughril Beg's capture of Baghdad in 1055, though it left the Abbasid caliphate nominally intact, placed real military power in the hands of Turkic warlords whose interest in maintaining the Persian Gulf's ecclesiastical networks was negligible. The overland routes along which episcopal appointments, liturgical texts, and theological correspondence had flowed were attenuated or cut.

In 1071, the Seljuk sultan Alp Arslan defeated the Byzantine emperor Romanos IV Diogenes at Manzikert, opening Anatolia to Turkic settlement. In the same year, Seljuk forces captured Jerusalem from the relatively tolerant Fatimid caliphate. For the Christians of India, the Seljuk ascendancy meant increasing isolation from the patriarchal authority that had governed them for centuries. For Latin Christendom, it meant something else: the political conditions for the Crusades, and with them a new and intensely distorted awareness of the eastern Christian world.1

The Sovereign Shadow

It was precisely into this atmosphere of disrupted communication and heightened western curiosity that the legend of Prester John emerged, growing in three distinct stages over half a century.

The Seed (1122): In 1122 CE, a man identifying himself as a patriarch from India arrived in Rome. Two independent texts record that this "John" gained an audience with Pope Calixtus II, describing a magnificent homeland where one of the rivers of paradise deposited gold and gemstones. Critically, he reported that a church outside his city housed the body of the apostle Thomas, which bestowed annual miracles. The visitor was likely an impostor from Edessa, but the effect was profound: it deposited into the Latin European consciousness the image of a distant eastern Christian kingdom, explicitly associated with St. Thomas.2

The Name (1145): Two decades later, Bishop Hugh of Jabala reported to the Pope that a powerful Christian ruler named "John," a descendant of the Magi governing beyond Persia, had recently destroyed Muslim forces. The Bishop had been frantically dispatched to Europe by the Crusader princes of Antioch and Jerusalem to beg Pope Eugene III for military relief because the Turkic warlord Imad ad-Din Zengi had just annihilated Edessa. The fall of Edessa sent shockwaves of sheer terror through Latin Christendom (and would shortly trigger the Second Crusade). The German chronicler Otto of Freising recorded the Bishops report to Pope as the first written mention of Presbiter Johannes, or Prester John. The actual historical event was the 1141 Battle of Qatwan, where a Buddhist East Asian leader crushed the Seljuk Sultan. By the time the news reached the Crusader States, the steppe warrior had been mistakenly transformed into an Eastern Christian king.34

The Letter (c. 1165–1170): The legend was immortalized by the fabricated Letter of Prester John, which survives in over 469 Latin manuscripts. This text was likely Hohenstaufen propaganda born out of the Investiture Controversy (the bitter, centuries-long struggle between the Holy Roman Emperors and the Papacy over supreme authority in Christendom). By describing a utopian, impossibly wealthy eastern empire ruled by a monarch who was both a king and a "Prester" (priest), the Hohenstaufen faction was presenting a model of combined secular and religious power that completely bypassed the Pope. This highly embellished letter describes the 'Three Indias' as a land of marvels, monsters, and moral perfection, possessing a fountain of youth and the body of St. Thomas. Embedded within these fantastical claims was a garbled form of the genuine Syriac and patristic tradition: the body of St. Thomas lay in a kingdom in India. This association would fundamentally shape European exploration.5

The Steppe and the Altar

The Mongol conquests of the thirteenth century constituted the greatest single catastrophe in the history of the Church of the East. The devastation of Central Asia under Genghis Khan (1219 CE onward) destroyed the network of metropolitan sees, monasteries, and trade routes that had sustained the church's eastern expansion. In 1258, Hulagu Khan sacked Baghdad itself: the caliphal capital, the city from which the Patriarch of the Church of the East had administered his vast communion for five centuries. The last Abbasid Caliph, al-Musta'sim, was executed.

The Mongol Paradox

In that same year, three Ortonese galleys under Leone Acciaiuoli collected relics associated with the apostle Thomas from Chios (where they had been taken after Edessa Outremer fell to Zengi in 1144) and transported them to Ortona on the Adriatic coast. The bones of the apostle reached their final European resting place at the exact moment the institutional centre of the Church of the East was destroyed. But the shrine at Mylapore continued to be mentioned independently, as it had since at least the sixth century. The tomb tradition and the relic tradition had always been two separate things.6

Yet the Mongol period contained a paradox. Several Mongol khans had Christian wives or mothers, a product of the Church of the East's earlier missionary success on the Mongolian steppe. Hulagu's own wife, Dokuz Khatun, was an Eastern Christian; she protected the Patriarch even as Hulagu's armies destroyed the caliphate around him.

A Pilgrim on the Patriarch’s Throne

This contradiction found its dramatic embodiment in Mar Yahballaha III. Around 1275, two monks from the Mongol world (Rabban Bar Sauma, an older and highly educated monk of Uyghur or Öngüd background, and his younger disciple Markos) set out from Khanbaliq (Beijing) on a pilgrimage to Jerusalem. Their journey took them westward across Central Asia and Persia, but the ongoing conflict between the Mongols and the Mamluks made travel to Jerusalem impossible. They halted at Maragha in the Ilkhanate. In 1281, the death of the reigning Patriarch created a vacancy, and the assembled bishops, recognising the political advantage of a patriarch with Mongol connections, chose the young Markos, who took the name Mar Yahballaha III. A Mongol-born monk who had never intended to become anything more than a pilgrim found himself administering the most geographically extended church in Christendom from a seat in Azerbaijan.

Steppe Diplomacy, Latin Silence

Rabban Bar Sauma, meanwhile, was dispatched by the Ilkhan Arghun on an extraordinary diplomatic mission to Europe in 1287–1288, carrying proposals for a Mongol-Frankish alliance against the Mamluks. He visited Constantinople, Rome (where he met the newly elected Pope Nicholas IV), Paris (where he met Philip IV), and Bordeaux (where he met Edward I of England). He celebrated the Eucharist in the East Syriac liturgy of Addai and Mari before the Pope. It was the closest encounter between the Church of the East and the papacy in the entire medieval period — and it produced no lasting result.7

It was this same Mongol-facilitated reopening of routes (the Pax Mongolica, enforced by military terror across the largest contiguous empire in history) that allowed missionaries, merchants, and diplomats to travel from the Mediterranean to China and India for the first time in centuries.8

Shoring the Shrine

While the Prester John legend was crystallising in the West, the Syriac documentary tradition continued to record the same tomb, without any awareness that Latin Europe was fantasising about it.

By the twelfth and thirteenth centuries, the identification becomes more specific. Ishoyahb of Nisibis (bishop, 1187–1222 CE) attests that the body of Thomas remains in India. Solomon of Basra, writing in the same period, specifies the site as 'Mayluph,' unmistakably Mylapore on the Coromandel coast. And Bar Hebraeus (d. 1286), the great Syriac polymath, equates Mylapore with Calamina, completing the identification chain that runs across six centuries: Calamina = Mylapore. The tomb site that was generating legend in Europe was being documented with increasing precision in the Syriac world.9

These shifting toponyms track the macroeconomic geography of Eurasian trade. We have seen how the emergence of 'Calamina' in the fifth and sixth centuries broadly corresponds to the growing importance of Persian Gulf maritime networks under Sasanian hegemony and to a brief late-antique revival of the Roman Red Sea vector. As commercial gravity shifted away from the Roman Red Sea system, diffuse references to 'India' gave way to a more specific node embedded within Syriac–Persian trading circuits. In a similar way, the appearance of the local name 'Mylapore' (Mahluph) in thirteenth-century sources aligns with the renewed connectivity of the Pax Mongolica.

The precise location denoted by the toponym has been debated. Most scholarly proposals have therefore had to work backwards: fitting Mylapore to Calamina on the strength of the thirteenth-century Syriac attestation and the later eyewitness accounts that definitively place the tomb at Mylapore. Whether the early uses of 'Calamina' actually referred to Mylapore, or whether Mylapore was read back into an older toponym whose original referent is now lost, cannot be determined from the textual evidence.

The Persisting 'Calamina' Problem
  • Paulinus a S. Bartholomaeo (1794): Cemented the Calamina–Mylapore identification by arguing that the continuous pilgrimages to the Coromandel coast by diverse Eastern Christian denominations empirically validated the ancient textual martyrologies.a
  • A.E. Medlycott (1905): Asserted the historical synonymy of Calamina with Mylapore, arguing that the global absence of any rival tomb claim, coupled with continuous local veneration, fixed the ancient toponym to this specific site.b
  • Fr. Bernard of St. Thomas (early 20th c.): Proposed that 'Calamina' was a scribal corruption of 'Caliana' (Kalliana) in Cosmas, noting that ancient Mylapore's association with the goddess Kali yielded forms like 'Kali' or 'Caliani' that unfamiliar Mediterranean copyists mutated into the attested toponym (see Chapter 3, endnote 2 for further discussion on Calliana).c
  • Henri Hosten (1936): Reinforced the Calamina = Mylapore equation by arguing it was proven not through etymology alone but through the empirical continuity of pre-Portuguese pilgrimage networks converging exclusively on the Mylapore tomb.d
  • Jürgen Tubach (1997–2002): Corroborated the Cholamandalam derivation through rigorous philological analysis, arguing that 'Calamina' was a Mediterranean literary filtering of a south Indian geographic designation, the Chola coast.e
  • George Nedungatt (2008–2010): Advanced the Cholamandalam synthesis further, proposing that 'Kalamene/Calamina' is a phonetic corruption of Cholamannu ('land of the Cholas'), a precise regional identifier for the apostle's burial site on the Coromandel coast.f

A more productive approach may be to examine the toponym alongside the physical archaeological record.

Granite Liturgies

The earliest surviving physical monuments of Indian Christianity are Pahlavi stone crosses, generally dated from the 7th to 9th century CE, corresponding rougly to the period copper plates were issued as we saw in the perevious chapter. Sharing a remarkable resemblance in form, these crosses (some bearing Pahlavi inscriptions, others without inscriptions) have been discovered on both the east coast (Mylapore/Chennai) and west coast (Agassaim/Pilar, Goa), at five locations in the Malabar region, and in Sri Lanka (Anuradhapura).

Collectively, this distribution traces the contours of the Indian Ocean maritime world: the Coromandel coast, the Malabar coast, the Konkan littoral, and Sri Lanka. Two of these regions, the Konkan coast and Sri Lanka, correspond to the Persian bishoprics explicitly mentioned by Cosmas. The crosses therefore anchor the earliest surviving physical layer of Indian Christianity within the wider Persian-centered Indian Ocean network extending toward China. Even if the 'Calamina' debate remains unresolved in the manuscript tradition, the stones themselves provide physical evidence that Christian communities on both coasts participated in the same Persian maritime diaspora.

The Pahlavi Stone Crosses

These stone crosses are the earliest surviving physical artifacts of Indian Christianity.

The Paleographic Evidence

  • The Dead Script: The earliest crosses are carved in Late Pahlavi (Middle Persian). While the spoken Middle Persian language evolved, this script effectively died out for general use after the 7th-century Islamic conquests. However, it continued to be used for a few centuries by Zoroastrian priests (who used cursive Book Pahlavi for their religious texts) and by Persian Christians (who also developed a variant known as Psalter Pahlavi). Eventually, Christian communities of Fars gradually shifted their liturgical language entirely to Syriac. By the 9th and 10th centuries, Pahlavi was completely replaced in wider society by New Persian written in the Perso-Arabic alphabet (read "Sasanian Interlude" infocard in Chapter 2).10
  • The Inscription: The inscription has proven difficult to decipher, with multiple interpretations having been given over the decades. A recent and widely supported scholarly reading translates it as: "Our Lord Christ, have pity on Sabrišōʿ, (son) of Čahārbōxt, (son) of Sūray, who bore [brought/made] this (cross)."11
  • The Copper Connection: Because this specific form of Late Pahlavi fossilized early and matches the script used in the 9th-century Tharisappally copper plates, epigraphists generally date the earlier crosses to the 7th or 8th centuries.

The Crosses

  • The Type: All of these are bas-relief crosses carved into stone panels, unlike later free-standing open-air crosses.
  • The Locations: They are found in Mylapore, Goa, and Sri Lanka (Anuradhapura), as well as within the Malabar region at Kottayam, Kadamattom, Muttuchira, Kothanalloor and Alangad. The Mylapore cross and one of the Kottayam crosses are considered the earliest; the other Malabar crosses are considered to be later copies. The Sri Lanka and Kothanalloor crosses does not have a readable inscription like the others.12
  • The Estampage: The remarkable degree of resemblance in inscriptions has led to a general consensus that the crosses were made using a copying method called estampage (lifting a copy of the original by pressing paper onto its inked surface).13 Furthermore, several of these later crosses (like the smaller Kottayam cross) add a second inscription in Estrangelo Syriac (Galatians 6:14), capturing the shift in community's working liturgical language from Persian (Pahlavi) to Syriac.

Pilgrims of the Pax

The Pax Mongolica produced the most remarkable cluster of independent witnesses to the Indian coast.

Marco Polo (c. 1254–1324), the Venetian merchant, had spent nearly two decades at the court of Kublai Khan in China. Sailing westward on his return journey in 1292, his fleet touched at several Indian ports. At Mylapore he described the tomb of St. Thomas, its Christian and Muslim guardians, and the miraculous earth that cured fevers. His account in the Divisament dou Monde (dictated to Rustichello da Pisa in a Genoese prison) became the most widely read European description of Asia, shaping the geographic imagination of every subsequent generation — including, directly, Christopher Columbus, who owned and annotated a copy.

John of Montecorvino (1247–1328), a Franciscan friar from southern Italy, was travelling in the opposite direction. In 1289, Pope Nicholas IV (the same pope who had received Rabban Bar Sauma's embassy) dispatched Montecorvino as a papal legate to the Mongol court. Sailing eastward via the Persian Gulf and Indian Ocean, Montecorvino stopped at Mylapore in 1291–1292 and spent thirteen months at the shrine. He buried his travelling companion, the Dominican Nicholas of Pistoia, at the tomb site. He baptised around a hundred converts. He then continued to China, where he eventually became the first Archbishop of Khanbaliq (Beijing), a Latin Catholic see in the heart of the Mongol Empire.

Independently, around 1321–1324, another Franciscan, Odoric of Pordenone, also visited the Mylapore shrine and corroborated Polo's account of the shared reverence for the site.

A fourth witness, the Dominican Jordanus Catalani of Séverac (c. 1321), took a different path. He visited Christian communities on the western and Konkan coast (at Thana, Salsette, Sopara, Broach, and Quilon) but did not go to Mylapore. His report to Pope John XXII led to the establishment of the Diocese of Quilon in 1329, the first papally recognised Latin Catholic diocese in India.14

Apostolic in Ink

In 1301 CE (between Polo's visit and Jordanus's report) a fourteen-year-old deacon named Zakarya bar Joseph, working at Kodungallur on the Malabar coast, copied a Pauline Lectionary in Syriac Estrangelo script. His colophon, preserved in what is now Vatican Syriac MS 22, contains three clauses of extraordinary significance. He dated his work to 1612 of the Seleucid Era. He identified his distant Patriarch as Mar Yahballaha, the Mongol-born patriarch whose extraordinary career we have just traced, who was at that moment administering the Church of the East from Maragha in the Ilkhanate. And he recorded that his bishop presided over the 'Apostolic See of St. Thomas.'

The young scribe made one telling error: he numbered Yahballaha as 'the Fifth' rather than the Third. The mistake is itself evidence of the community's isolation from its institutional centre. A scribe in Baghdad would have known the patriarch's correct ordinal number. A fourteen-year-old deacon in Kodungallur, separated from the patriarchate by thousands of miles of ocean and the upheavals of the Mongol world, knew the patriarch's name and his authority but not the precise sequence of his predecessors. The error confirms the connection and the distance simultaneously.

In three sentences, this colophon establishes: that a functioning episcopal see at Kodungallur in 1301 understood itself as the Apostolic See of St. Thomas; that this see was in communion with the Church of the East Patriarch operating under Mongol-Ilkhanate conditions in Azerbaijan; and that the community was sufficiently literate, organised, and self-aware to produce a formally dated liturgical manuscript. The community was naming itself — and the name it chose was apostolic.15

Saved from the Flames

The manuscript's survival is its own story. It was brought to Rome in the 1560s by a Chaldean Catholic Metropolitan of India who was deported by the Portuguese Inquisition and sent to prove his orthodoxy. He brought ancient manuscripts as evidence that his community was not heretical but apostolic. Vatican Syriac MS 22 survived in the Vatican Library through the very period (after the Synod of Diamper in 1599) when the Portuguese were burning Syriac texts across Kerala. It was saved from the Portuguese by the Portuguese.

MS Vatican Syriac 22

This 1301 CE Pauline Lectionary is the oldest surviving dated manuscript of the St. Thomas Christians.

The Artifact (Text & Time)

  • The Seleucid Date: The 14-year-old scribe, Zakarya bar Joseph, dated his work to 1612 of the Seleucid Era (equivalent to 1301 CE), demonstrating the community's deep integration into the Middle Eastern chronological system.
  • The Transitional Script: The text is written in a fascinating transitional phase of Syriac calligraphy. It bridges the older, rigid, and monumental Estrangelo script and the later, more fluid and rounded Madnhaya (Eastern) script.
  • The Apostolic Claim: Within the colophon, the scribe explicitly identifies his local bishop as presiding over the "Apostolic See of St. Thomas," reflecting the internal self-awareness centuries before European arrival.

The Historiographical Rescue

  • The Assemanis (18th c.): Brought to Rome from Kerala, the manuscript was preserved and first cataloged by the great Maronite scholars, Giuseppe Simone and Stefano Evodio Assemani, introducing it to European Orientalists.
  • J.P.M. van der Ploeg (1983): The Dutch Syriac scholar systematically surveyed the surviving texts of Kerala in his landmark work. He firmly anchored MS 22 to the Malabar coast, establishing its geographical reality against skeptical European narratives.
  • A.M. Mundadan: The founding father of modern Indian Christian historiography utilized this text to permanently shift the historical lens from "what the Portuguese did" to "how the Indian Christians understood themselves."

Silt and Sovereignty

Back on the Malabar coast, the political ground had already shifted. Following the disintegration of the overarching Cēra Perumal empire in the early twelfth century, the political geography of the Malabar coast had fractured into two primary domains: Kolathunadu in the north, Calicut under the Zamorin, and the Venad Swaroopam (Quilon) in the south, and several vassal states.

A Port Erased

In 1341 CE, an exceptional flooding of the Periyar river silted up or destroyed the ancient harbour at Kodungallur. The port through which Roman amphorae and Sangam-era pepper consignments had once moved was eliminated by a single geological event. The Thomas Christian community, centred on Kodungallur since the tradition's earliest documented layer, shifted southward. The community consolidated at Quilon, sharpening the structural division in Kerala's port economy: Calicut under the Zamorin oriented toward the Arab Muslim-dominated Red Sea trade; Quilon under the Venad Swaroopam maintaining its connections with Persian Christian and Chinese merchant networks.16

The Dragon’s Wake

This consolidation on the Malabar coast cannot be understood without looking east to China. The thirteenth and fourteenth centuries opened a unique geographic window: the Pax Mongolica, combined with the naval supremacy of the Yuan Dynasty, produced an unprecedented maritime superhighway stretching from the Persian Gulf to the South China Sea. Quilon was a vital terminus for massive Chinese trade junks, and the Thomas Christians were the primary local brokers for the pepper these fleets demanded.17

This deep integration with the trans-Asian maritime network, peaking in the mid-fourteenth century, finally explains the profound logistical pattern underlying the European witnesses we encountered earlier. Looking at the complete span of papal envoys and merchants who reached India during the Pax Mongolica, a stark geographic reality emerges. Their presence at Mylapore was not a tradition-sponsored documentary insertion; it was dictated by the monsoon winds and the nautical requirements of reaching the Yuan Dynasty. The east coast was known to Arab geographers as Ma'bar, an Arabic term meaning "ferry" or "crossing place," first recorded by Yāqūt al-Ḥamawī (1179–1229) and still current when Marco Polo described the entire eastern seaboard as "Maabar." The name was functional: Arab vessels habitually called at its ports before making the crossing to Ceylon and points east. Europeans would later know it as the Coromandel Coast, a Portuguese rendering of the Tamil Choḻamaṇḍalam ("Realm of the Cholas").18

This matrix of itineraries reveals a distinct demographic and geographical reality by the fourteenth and fifteenth centuries. The Malabar coast possessed the strong, entrenched, and commercially dominant Christian community. Mylapore, conversely, remained the paramount apostolic shrine and a vital pilgrimage node for both Christians and Muslims, but its resident Christian demographic was likely smaller and maintained primarily to service the shrine and the trans-Asian maritime transit.19

Masters of the Pepper Gardens

It was John of Marignolli, resting at Quilon for sixteen months after his four-day transit through Mylapore, who left the clearest documentary statement of the community's economic position. He described the Thomas Christians of the Malabar coast as 'masters of the pepper gardens,' the dominant commercial class controlling the spice trade of the Venad hinterland. This was not a small-scale agricultural enterprise. Decades later, between 1405 and 1433, the Ming Dynasty dispatched Admiral Zheng He's massive treasure fleets (armadas of unprecedented scale, with ships exponentially larger than anything later sailed by Vasco da Gama) to ports like Cochin and Calicut. The Thomas Christians were the deeply entrenched mercantile syndicates filling the holds of these Chinese leviathans. A community that controlled the pepper gardens at this trans-Asian macroeconomic scale was not a tolerated minority. It was a structurally essential component of the Indian Ocean's most commercially significant economy.20

A Venetian in Mylapore

The expedition of Niccolò de' Conti (c. 1395–1469), a Venetian merchant who spent twenty-five years traversing Western, Southern, and Southeast Asia, was remarkably distinct from earlier travellers and the later Portuguese voyages, and even from many of his medieval European contemporaries, because he utilized the ancient overland and maritime arteries of the Silk Road. Departing Venice around 1419, Conti did not attempt to circumnavigate a continent. He travelled through Damascus, down the Euphrates to Baghdad, and on to Basra, operating in the exact geographic footprint of the ancient port of Charax Spasinu, the classical Parthian-era gateway to the Persian Gulf. From there, he entered the Indian Ocean trade network via Hormuz.

After reaching the western coast of India at Cambay (modern Khambhat in Gujarat, situated in the very same gulf as ancient Barygaza, the premier Roman-era trading hub), Conti travelled inland to the Vijayanagara Empire before making his way across the peninsula to the Coromandel coast. He visited Mylapore, observing the tomb of St. Thomas and the local Christian community. From the Coromandel coast, he continued his journey eastward to Southeast Asia and China.

On his return journey back to Venice, he utilized the maritime routes that necessitated a transit along the western Malabar coast, as the Mediterranean-bound shipping networks traditionally plied between the Red Sea/Persian Gulf and Malabar. It was during this return leg that he deeply explored the Malabar coast (Quilon, Cochin, Calicut). Here, he provided a crucial sociological observation: he noted that the local Christians were scattered throughout the region "like the Jews in Europe".21

Taken together, the testimonies of John of Marignolli and Niccolò de' Conti provide the ultimate pre-Portuguese synthesis of the Thomas Christian reality. They reveal a community that had achieved a status nearly incomprehensible to the medieval Latin mind: profound socio-economic dominance without independent political sovereignty. Dispersed yet deeply integrated, religiously distinct yet commercially indispensable, the Thomas Christians functioned as a high-ranking merchant and martial caste within the Hindu political frameworks of the Malabar coast, possessing hereditary aristocratic privileges, such as the right to bear arms, travel on elephants, and manage local revenues, secured centuries earlier through copper plate grants from local Cēra and Venad rulers.22

The Cartographer’s Frame

The Venetian Witness

By the mid-fifteenth century, the Thomas Christian community was sufficiently known in Mediterranean geographic consciousness to appear on the most authoritative world map of the pre-Portuguese era. Fra Mauro's Mappa Mundi (c. 1450), the great Venetian cartographic synthesis, explicitly labels 'Christiani di San Thomaxo' on the Malabar coast. Crucially, Fra Mauro's map depended heavily on the fresh, empirical intelligence brought back to Venice by Niccolò de' Conti. By the time the Ming Dynasty abruptly ended its naval expeditions and created a massive power vacuum in the Indian Ocean, Conti's observations confirmed the community's continued autonomy and commercial standing on the eve of the Portuguese arrival.23

Beyond the Apostolic Question

Before the next stage of the story unfolds, the question that has shadowed all of Part I deserves a final word. In 1984, two monumental, multi-volume histories of Christianity in India published their inaugural volumes. From outside the tradition, the Anglican bishop and Cambridge scholar Stephen Neill brought the rigorous, inherently sceptical apparatus of Western historical-critical methodology to bear on the apostolic claim and concluded:

The historian cannot prove to them that they are mistaken in their belief. He may feel it right to warn them that historical research cannot pronounce on the matter with a confidence equal to that which they entertain by faith.

— Stephen Neill, A History of Christianity in India

From within it, the Catholic CMI priest A. Mathias Mundadan, spearheading the indigenous Church History Association of India project and evaluating the same body of evidence, concluded:

The central content stands out in clear relief, namely St. Thomas the apostle preached, died, and was buried in South India. None of the arguments so far advanced seem to be strong enough to erode the validity of this central content.

— A. Mathias Mundadan, History of Christianity in India

At first glance the two appear opposed. On closer examination they are not so much in opposition as in an epistemological stalemate: one requires positive evidence for belief, the other requires decisive grounds for rejection. The difference is not one of evidence but of epistemic posture, and the deadlock is unlikely to be resolved on its own terms. Scottish theologian and evangelical missionary Claudius Buchanan, publishing his Christian Researches in Asia in 1811, observed that there is as much evidence that St. Thomas went to India as there is that St. Peter went to Rome. For Buchanan, there is no documentary evidence for these traditions apart from the living traditions themselves. Buchanan is right. If the continuity of tradition is sufficient for Rome to belong to Peter, then — for the Church — India belongs to Thomas.24

A different question can be asked, however, and the evidence does answer it. What Part I has established is the existence of a Christian community in India whose origins are attested in external sources from at least the third century, whose institutional presence on the Indian coast is documented from the sixth, and which, by the time it first names itself in a surviving document, described its own seat as the "Apostolic See of St. Thomas." The community operated within the commercial infrastructure of the Indian Ocean, was governed by the Church of the East, and was embedded, from the ninth century at the latest, in the political structure of the south Indian polity through the grant system. It is not a hypothesis. It is a documented historical reality: a commonwealth of Christians whose settlements, layered over different periods and bound by shared liturgy, ecclesiastical heritage, and Indian Ocean commerce, extended from the Persian Gulf through coastal India to Sri Lanka and Southeast Asia.25

Within this commonwealth, the Thomas tradition functioned as constitutive identity, not a decorative overlay on a community that would exist independently of it, but the thread connecting Ephrem's veneration of relics in Edessa and Isidore's martyrology in Seville to the shrine at Mylapore, to the Pahlavi crosses, to the copper plates, to the merchant networks of the Malabar coast, and to a fourteen-year-old scribe at Kodungallur in 1301 who recorded his bishop's seat as apostolic. Whether or not Thomas personally set foot on Indian soil, by the time the documentary record becomes visible, the tradition was already the shared founding narrative of a transregional Christian network, sufficiently old, widespread, and deeply embedded that no rival apostolic attribution achieved comparable acceptance within it.

Two Maps

Part I opened with a map. The Periplus of the Erythraean Sea, compiled by a Greek-speaking merchant of the first century, was the earliest Mediterranean document to set down what could be reached by sailing into the Indian Ocean. Among the great emporia it recorded was Muziris on the Malabar coast: the terminus of the pepper run, where Roman coin flowed in and Indian black gold flowed out.

Part I closes with another map. Fra Mauro's Mappa Mundi, compiled in Venice fourteen centuries later, was the most authoritative cartographic synthesis Europe had yet produced. Where the Periplus had written Muziris, the Mappa Mundi writes something else: Christiani di San Thomaxo, the Christians of St. Thomas. The port itself was gone (Muziris had been buried under the silt of the 1341 Periyar flood), but the coast remained, and where the Greek text had named a place of commerce, the Venetian cartographer names a people.

The two maps bracket the whole of the story we have told. The merchants of the Periplus, sailing on the monsoon winds, had brought home cargoes of pepper and the rumour of an apostle's tomb. The cartographer of Venice, working from the eyewitness testimony of Niccolò de' Conti, set down the descendants of the converts whom those merchants had first encountered: still there, still recognised, still labelled by the apostle's name. Between the two cartouches lie fourteen hundred years of an Indian Ocean Christian commonwealth.

What no Mediterranean map could yet record was the actual texture of the world it was naming. The Mappa Mundi could place the Thomas Christians on the Malabar coast, but it could not show that they were the dominant pepper cultivators of the Venad hinterland, the mercantile syndicates whose holds had filled Zheng He's treasure fleets, the panikkars whose kalaris trained the soldiers of the rajas who patronised them. It could not show that they understood themselves as the Apostolic See of St. Thomas, governed in the long absences of their Mesopotamian bishops by an archdeacon whose office combined sacral, judicial, military, and administrative authority in a configuration for which no European category yet existed.

That texture would have to be encountered, not mapped.

The cartographic century was drawing to a close. What the Portuguese were carrying with them when they finally rounded the Cape, and what would happen when their expectations met the complex reality of the Malabar coast, belongs to the next part.

How the Nazrani past was written, 1694–1909

The War for the Past

"Those opinions are put forward at the present day, not only in academic controversy but also as the basis of litigation for the possession of Church property and of Trust funds."

— G. T. Mackenzie, Christianity in Travancore (1901)

The nineteenth century had fought over who should govern the Serra. The twentieth century fought over who had the right to remember it. The lull with which our last chapter closed did not last a decade: songs became sources, parish legends became evidence, family traditions became genealogy, and sacred geography became historical argument. The churches did not merely inherit a past; they learned to print it, defend it, and litigate it. But before we can watch the war, we must see how the weapon reached the field and who had held it first; and that story begins, improbably, in the smallest of all the European lodgements in India, a Danish fort on the Coromandel sand. 1

Tharangambadi and the Printed Word

In 1616, King Christian IV of Denmark chartered the Danish East India Company, launching it into the Indian Ocean with ambitions that mirrored the simultaneous rise of the Dutch VOC and the English merchants. Yet the Danes arrived to find the crowded spaces of the South Indian coast already littered with rival European trade factories, a contested network stretching from the ports of Surat in Gujarat to the inland depots of Hooghly in Bengal.

Leasing the Waves

Rebuffed in Ceylon, where the Portuguese controlled the coastal approaches and the King of Kandy, Senarat, repudiated the treaty of the deceased Dutch merchant-adventurer Boschouwer, the Danish expedition was forced to seek a marginal alternative. In 1620, their admiral Ove Gjedde bargained with Raghunatha Nayak, the ruler of the Hindu kingdom of Thanjavur (Tanjore), securing a lease on a windswept fishing village called Tharangambadi (Tranquebar), or the "place of singing waves." Here, on the Coromandel beach, they built the whitewashed walls of Fort Dansborg, a lonely outpost that for nearly a century served only the trade in pepper and textiles.

Faith under Another Flag

Tharangambadi's true significance, however, would be spiritual, not mercantile. In 1705, King Frederick IV of Denmark sponsored the first Protestant mission to India, selecting two young German pietists, Bartholomaeus Ziegenbalg and Heinrich Pluetschau, to carry the Lutheran Evangelical faith to the East. When they landed at Tharangambadi in July 1706, they found themselves in a unique, isolated enclave. Squeezed between the hostile local Danish governors and the surrounding Hindu kingdom, Ziegenbalg had to operate in the teeth of official resistance.

For the Danish company officials, like their Dutch and English rivals, positioned themselves strictly as secular, neutral merchants who feared that evangelical zeal would alienate local religious sensibilities and disrupt the flow of commerce. While the English Company actively barred Protestant missionaries from landing in Madras or Calcutta to protect their trade licenses, the Danish Crown's direct patronage of Frederick IV's Royal Mission turned Tharangambadi into a rare sanctuary. 2

Types and Testaments

In this narrow space, Ziegenbalg turned his attention to the language of the soil, learning Tamil with an intensity that surpassed even the Jesuits. Recognizing that oral preaching was not enough, a printing press was brought from Europe in 1712, Tamil types were cut, and by 1715 the first Protestant translation of the New Testament stood printed in an Indian tongue.

Printing itself was older in India, for the Jesuits had set up the first press at Goa as early as 1556, and had cast Tamil types there for the missionary world of Henrique Henriques (chapter 9). It was later improved at Kollam, where the Doctrina Christam of 1578 became the first book printed in an Indian script on Indian soil; but that precocious beginning had guttered out, and between the Jesuit Tamil experiments and the Danish press stretched a gap of a hundred and thirty years. What Tharangambadi founded was the press as a sustained engine: of vernacular scripture, of schooling, of controversy, and, not least, of reports sent home to Europe, where they were read by men who would never see India and would nonetheless write its history. 3

Ink and Empire

Yet the engine would not remain confined to the Coromandel shore or to the altar. Its expansion would repeat the pattern of the south, relying once again on the sanctuary of a Danish charter. In 1793 the English Baptist missionary William Carey landed in Bengal without the licence the English East India Company required, and for six years survived on sufferance in the interior, the Company fearing any evangelical disturbance to its commerce. When his reinforcements were refused landing altogether in 1799, the mission withdrew under another flag, to the Danish settlement of Serampore (Frederiksnagore) on the Hooghly; and there, under the governor's protection, Carey, Joshua Marshman, and William Ward established the Serampore Mission Press in 1800.

What followed was a revolution in print. Serampore became a massive multilingual type-foundry, a specialized workshop where molten metal was cast into individual letter-moulds, cutting characters for over forty Indian and East Asian languages. Yet this missionary catalyst soon merged with English administrative expansion. The EIC, realizing the power of print for governance, began utilizing these very type-foundries and presses to publish secular government regulations, land surveys, and botanical reports. The tool of the evangelist became the tool of the state and the street alike, giving birth to the first great tide of vernacular journalism in Tamil, Bengali, and Hindi, and transforming print into the very nervous system of modern Indian public life. 4

Pith and Pulpits

From Tharangambadi, the printed word worked its way slowly round to the Serra, and by curious roads. The first printed Malayalam book, the Samkshepavedartham (Compendium of Christian Doctrine) of 1772, compiled by the Carmelite Clement Pianius, was printed not in Kerala at all but at Rome, on the press of the Sacred Congregation of Propaganda Fide itself.

Decades later, the technology arrived in the hills. Benjamin Bailey, the CMS missionary built a press at Kottayam with his own hands about 1821. Guided only by the illustrations in the Encyclopaedia Britannica, Bailey carved his own wooden types and designed his own press to print his landmark Malayalam translation of the Bible and the Anglican prayer books.

Then came the Catholic response. In 1846, Kuriakose Elias Chavara, whom the reader knows as the saviour of the Roman allegiance in the Rokos affair, founded at Mannanam the Syro-Malabar church's first printing press, a wooden machine of his own devising, its prototype painstakingly modelled in plantain pith and built out by local carpenters and ironsmiths; its first printed book, in 1847, was the Malayalam prayer book Jnanapiyusham (Nectar of Knowledge). Meanwhile, the presses of Kunnamkulam and Pampakuda armed the Puthankūr with their own Syriac and Malayalam liturgies. By the century's end, every church in the Serra could print; and when Father Nidhiry's Nasrani Deepika appeared in 1887, the voice of the community in the new public world, it was printed on Chavara's own Mannanam wooden press. The instrument that had arrived as an auxiliary of prayer stood ready for other work. A press, the churches would discover, was a pulpit that reached one's antagonists. 5

The French Obsession

Before the Danish connection furnished Europe with a wider history of Indian Christianity, the Malabar Church had already entered the English Protestant imagination through an Anglican door. Michael Geddes, a Church of England divine, published his History of the Church of Malabar in 1694 as a great anti-Diamper brief: a history less of the whole Nazrani past than of Portuguese violence, Jesuit ambition, the Synod of Diamper, and the reduction of an ancient Eastern church to Rome. Geddes fixed one enduring Protestant use of the Serra: the Syrians could be presented as an old non-Roman Christian people, oppressed by Roman coercion and therefore useful to the Anglican case against Rome. 6

Lamp of the Enlightenment

The first wider European history of Indian Christianity to draw deeply on the Danish connection was Mathurin Veyssière de La Croze's Histoire du christianisme des Indes (1724). Written not in India but at a Berlin desk, it was built from Portuguese chronicles, Catholic missionary material, and, crucially, the fresh reports and manuscripts sent home through the Danish Pietist mission at Tharangambadi. Operating as a Protestant convert and anti-Jesuit controversialist, La Croze treated the St Thomas tradition as a layered confusion to be stripped apart. The apostolic journey itself he traced to apocryphal and Manichaean fables; the Malabar claim, more specifically, he suspected had been strengthened by confusion between the Apostle and Thomas Gana (Thomas of Cana), or Mar Thomas, the Armenian merchant remembered in the community's own traditions. His Syrians mattered chiefly as witnesses against Rome: an ancient Eastern people whose sufferings under the Portuguese could be made to serve the Protestant indictment of Catholic tyranny, even while their East Syrian inheritance was read through the language of corruption. 7

Bard of Romanticism

Abraham-Hyacinthe Anquetil-Duperron, by contrast, came not as a reforming desk-polemicist but as an orientalist pilgrim of the manuscript age. Formed in Jansenist austerity and drawn by the hope of recovering ancient sacred languages, he landed at French Pondicherry in 1755, amid the wider Anglo-French struggle for India. From there he made his difficult overland way toward Surat, where the Parsi priestly tradition and its manuscripts could be reached. Passing through Cochin at the close of 1757, he listened to local Christian informants, took note of their traditions, and accepted the apostolic landing without subjecting it to La Croze's destructive suspicion. This was not because he possessed better proof. It was because he looked eastward for survivals: old rites, old books, old priestly memories, fragments of a sacred antiquity still living in communities that Europe had not yet fully classified. The same appetite would carry him to the Parsi community of Surat, where he gathered the materials for the first European translation of the Zoroastrian scriptures, published in French as the Zend-Avesta in 1771; and later to the Upanishads, which he rendered from their Persian version into Latin as the Oupnek'hat. More than almost any other single figure of his generation, he opened the archive of Asian sacred literature to Europe, an archive that would feed later Romantic, idealist, perennialist and occultist searches for primordial wisdom beyond the empirical rationality of the Enlightenment. 8

Arguments and Archives

The contrast between the two Frenchmen is instructive. Both were formed within Catholic worlds, both had theological training behind them, and both were philologists of uncommon appetite, La Croze in Syriac, Coptic, Armenian, and the learned languages of the library, Anquetil-Duperron in Persian, Pahlavi, Avestan, and the living languages of the road. Yet their differing temperaments and ideological commitments yielded opposite uses of the Nazrani past. If Geddes had made the Malabar church an argument against Rome, La Croze made it a system to be dissected, and Anquetil-Duperron approached it as a living remnant of older sacred worlds. In this they prefigured a generational shift: La Croze stood as the cold, analytical lamp of the Enlightenment, dissecting dogma to purge tradition; Anquetil-Duperron emerged as a warm, immersive herald, a bard of Romanticism, searching out secret wisdom to preserve a primordial, perennial memory.

The Carmelite Counter-Brief

The Carmelite Catholic answer came in 1794. Paulinus of St Bartholomew, who had worked the Serra for a dozen years and knew its churches at first hand, published his India Orientalis Christiana at Rome: a vigorous defence of the apostolate and of the Mylapore tomb, a learned and even affectionate catalogue of the Syrian parishes and their high standing, and, for all that, a brief. His regard ran to the Pazhayakūr; the Puthankūr were 'schismatics' fallen from an ancient obedience; and the history served, as history in the Serra has served at every turn of this book, a claim of jurisdiction, this time the Propaganda's. Against La Croze above all, with his attempt to dissolve the apostolic memory into Manichaean apocrypha and Thomas-of-Cana confusion, Paulinus supplied the Carmelite counter-record. These were histories, but histories sharpened into weapons: the Anglican's, the Protestant convert's, the orientalist's, and the Carmelite's. What no one had yet written was the Nazranis' own. 9

The English Century

Anglicans and Apologists

The nineteenth century belonged, on the printed page, to the Englishmen. Claudius Buchanan opened it with the Christian Researches in Asia (1811), and with a naming decision whose consequences would outlive his own evangelical purpose. In his pages, "the Syrians" meant the non-Roman body gathered around the Malankara Metropolitan: an ancient apostolic church, wounded by Rome, preserving a Syriac Bible, and awaiting Anglican friendship, correction, and scriptural renewal. The Pazhayakūr, by contrast, appeared under the diminished category of "Romish Christians," not as the continuing East-Syrian body of Kodungallur-Angamaly, but as the Romanized remainder left after the mutilation of the ancient Indian church. The effect was enormous. By awarding the name "Syrian Church" to the Malankara side, Buchanan quietly excluded the Malabar Catholics from the full inheritance of ancient Syrian memory. What had begun as Anglican anti-Roman classification would later become Malankara apologetic argument: the claim that antiquity, apostolicity, and Syrian lineage belonged properly to the non-Roman church, and that the Catholics were merely the Latinized fragment left behind.

James Hough's massive History of Christianity in India (from 1839) followed, with the CMS temper hardened into method: the South Indian mission of St Thomas dismissed as a fable, and the Syrian church valued chiefly insofar as it could be detached from Roman and Eastern accretions. George Broadley Howard, a High-Church Anglican, made the exception that proves the rule. His instinct for tradition and liturgy led him to value the very anaphoras his Evangelical brethren deplored; but Richard Collins's Missionary Enterprise in the East (1873) restored the CMS line, treating the Syrians once again as a fallen ancient church whose true future lay in Protestant reform.

Lingerings of Light

Thomas Whitehouse, British chaplain at Cochin from 1852 to 1864, gathered in his Lingerings of Light in a Dark Land (1873) the most detailed survey of the Syrian churches yet made, and his title is his thesis in miniature: an ancient light, a present darkness. He wrote with passion against Menezes and the burning of the Syriac books, with care about the old churches and their traditions, and with disdain for the living rite; and it is one of the war's better ironies that the Catholic apologists of the next generation would quarry this Protestant chaplain for their best ammunition against the Portuguese.

The Scholar's Challenge

Last came the scholar's challenge: George Milne Rae of the Madras Christian College, whose The Syrian Church in India (1892) denied the apostle to the south altogether and derived the Malabar church from East Syrian merchant colonists centuries later. Rae was no neutral antiquarian; he wrote as a Protestant missionary-scholar and openly differed from the Syrians' own account of their origin. Yet his case was not merely the old Anglican romance in another form. It drew much of its force from the new historical evidence for Gondophares: coins and inscriptional data that placed the king of the Acts of Thomas not at Mylapore or in the pepper country, but in the Indo-Parthian north-west, around Afghanistan, Gandhara, and the Punjab. Earlier syntheses of St Thomas Christian tradition, such as Roz's, had been able to absorb the Acts of Thomas into South Indian sacred geography, not by first proving a north-western Gondophares, but by placing the apostle among the Chera and Chola Perumals: the Malabar ruler and noble houses at Kodungallur on one side, the Chola ruler and the martyrdom at Mylapore on the other. Rae's reconstruction did not deny every Indian mission of Thomas; it removed the Acts from the Serra. The Malabar claim to apostolic origin itself had now met a formal historical-critical challenge. 10

The Partisan Shelf

Set the shelf in order, from La Croze to Rae, and one fact stands out, and the reader should hold it through everything that follows: until Mackenzie sat down to his chapter, there existed no neutral record of the Nazrani past. Every history yet written was a partisan's: Portuguese and Carmelite briefs for jurisdiction, Protestant briefs for reform, and the Orientalists' briefs for their own quarrels with Religion and Rome. In the decades just before the Manual, the field was held overwhelmingly by the Anglican and Protestant pen; it was Hough, Whitehouse, and Rae who stood on the library shelves of the presidency, and their lens, sympathetic to the Syrians' sufferings and contemptuous of the Syrians' apostolic claim and tradition, that passed for the learned view. This is the shelf Mackenzie inherited when the Darbar asked him for neutrality; and it is why his attempt at it satisfied no one. 11

The Resident's Chapter

Neutrality as Provocation

Mackenzie had gone about his commission as carefully as an official could. He was not Rae in government dress. On the apostolic tradition itself, he allowed more than the hard sceptics had allowed: the tradition was old, widely held, accepted by writers of repute, and in itself not improbable. The evidence was not enough, in his judgment, for the Travancore State to pronounce the matter settled; but neither was the sceptical case strong enough to sweep the tradition away. His difficulty lay elsewhere. The pre-Portuguese record was meagre, and upon those meagre materials the living churches had built claims "wide as the poles asunder."

Bernard's Battle

How the chapter reached its printed shape we know from a witness inside the campaign against it: Bernard of St Thomas, the Carmelite who became the first considerable historian of the Catholic Syrian community from within its own ranks. The quarrel he records was not over the apostle. It was over the other question, the one the Portuguese books had settled to their own satisfaction: whether the East Syrians had been Nestorian heretics down to the close of the sixteenth century, converted to Catholicism only by Menezes, or whether, as the Catholic Syrians claimed, their forefathers had kept an uninterrupted orthodoxy and loyalty to the Holy See, and had needed no Portuguese conversion at all. A generation later Bernard set down in five sentences what that quarrel did to Mackenzie's chapter: 'The compilation of the Travancore State Manual gave them an opportunity. The first draft of the chapter on Christianity by G. T. Mackenzie completely ignored the Syrian tradition and exposed the Portuguese view. The Syrians at once protested. But they had nothing except their tradition to rely on. Hence the leading Syrian priests devoted themselves to finding out and collecting old documents to support their tradition.' The protest reached beyond Malabar: the procurator of the Chaldean patriarch at Rome searched the Vatican archives and published what favoured the Syrian claim; and a claim that had been kept for three centuries as memory began, in the very years the Manual was compiling, its transformation into documented history. 12

The Four Histories

Mackenzie's printed chapter shows what he did with the storm. He sorted the competing histories into four classes. First came the Reformed Syrians and many Protestant writers, for whom the ancient church had been a primitive and comparatively pure body, forced by the Portuguese into temporary submission to Rome and thereafter struggling back toward its older simplicity. Secondly came the Latin Roman Catholic view, according to which the old church had preserved the apostolic faith in origin, fallen into Nestorian and other errors through distance from Rome, and then returned to communion when the Portuguese opened the way. Thirdly came the Syrian Catholic view: the church of St Thomas, apostolic in origin and Syriac in speech, had never truly been Nestorian, but had always kept the Catholic faith while awaiting restored contact with the Holy See. Fourthly came the Jacobite claim, that the Patriarch of Antioch had from early times included the Malabar coast within his patriarchal jurisdiction. These were not harmless antiquarian opinions. They were being put forward in court over church property and trust funds; and so, Mackenzie concluded, with the weariness of a man who had asked for the facts and been handed four gospels, an official publication could only set out the facts with little or no comment. There could be no history of the Malabar church in the state's record; there could only be four histories, catalogued.

The Manual in Court

He published the chapter separately in 1901 as Christianity in Travancore, by leave of the Darbar and ahead of the Manual. It could satisfy no one. The Syrian Catholics thought their tradition had been placed inside categories still shaped by Portuguese and Latin controversy; the Malankara side saw Protestant, Roman, and official labels being hardened into government record; and every faction understood that a paragraph in the Manual could become evidence in court. Mackenzie had attempted neutrality, but official neutrality was not a view from nowhere. It was still a history written through official eyes, and in the Serra even an official classification could wound. The Travancore State Manual appeared in 1906 with substantially the same framework. The protest did not keep Mackenzie out of the State's record. It taught the churches that the State's record had to be answered. 13

The Counter-Histories

Once Mackenzie's official neutrality had entered print, the churches could no longer leave their past in the form of inherited memory. The first major Catholic answer came from A. E. Medlycott, former vicar apostolic of Trichur, whose India and the Apostle Thomas appeared in 1905. Medlycott wrote for a world divided between believers who wanted the tradition placed on a firmer historical foundation and sceptics who dismissed it as legend. His answer to Rae was not to deny the new Gondophares evidence. He accepted the Indo-Parthian setting of the king in the Acts of Thomas, but refused the inference that this abolished the southern tradition. Thomas, in his reconstruction, had two fields of labour: an earlier mission in the Parthian and north-western Indian world of Gondophares, and a later southern field preserved in the Malabar-Mylapore memory. 14

For the Malankara church, E. M. Philip of Edavazhikal answered with The Indian Church of St Thomas, published posthumously in 1908 but already rehearsed in court, pamphlet, and press controversy. Philip wrote from within his church and for its cause, to vindicate the Malankara side from the apostle to the patriarch. His method differed from Medlycott's: he challenged Rae's use of the coins and inscriptions more sharply, and he was willing to preserve older southern identifications that Catholic scholarship did not need. Yet on the central logical point he and Medlycott converged. A north-western Thomas did not, by itself, abolish a southern Thomas. 15

Possibility Returns

That correction slowly entered the wider scholarly field. Vincent Arthur Smith, the prominent Anglo-Irish historian and retired Indian Civil Service officer, who had earlier treated the South Indian tradition with marked caution, retreated from Rae's hard exclusion. By the third edition of his book The Early History of India (1914), and in later discussion, he allowed that the antiquity of the South Indian church was much greater than Rae had admitted, even if the apostolic foundation could not be proved with certainty. John Nicol Farquhar, who had also begun as a skeptic, later gave the same opening its most careful Protestant form in his twin studies, The Apostle Thomas in North India (1926) and The Apostle Thomas in South India (1927), treating the north Indian and south Indian traditions not as mutually destructive but as historically compatible. The argument had shifted. The question was no longer simply whether Gondophares disproved Malabar, but whether the old routes traced in Part One, Jerusalem, Edessa, the Persian Gulf, Socotra, Muziris, and Mylapore, allowed a life of Thomas large enough to contain both memories. 16

By 1908 the printed field had changed. Mackenzie had made the rival histories official; Medlycott and Philip had answered Rae from opposite ecclesiastical camps; and the old question of the apostle had been reopened rather than closed. But Mackenzie's warning concerning use of history in litigation would prove prophetic. These opinions, he warned, were no longer merely academic. They were being used for the possession of church property and trust funds. Within a few years that possibility would explode within the Puthankūr world, once again.

In 1909 Mar Ignatius Abdullah II, the Patriarch of Antioch came to Malabar.

How the Nazrani past was written, 1706–1936

The War for the Past

"Those opinions are put forward at the present day, not only in academic controversy but also as the basis of litigation for the possession of Church property and of Trust funds."

— G. T. Mackenzie, Christianity in Travancore (1901)

The nineteenth century had fought over who should govern the Serra. The twentieth century fought over who had the right to remember it. The lull with which our last chapter closed did not last a decade: songs became sources, parish legends became evidence, family traditions became genealogy, and sacred geography became historical argument. The churches did not merely inherit a past; they learned to print it, defend it, and litigate it. But before we can watch the war, we must see how the weapon reached the field and who had held it first; and that story begins, improbably, in the smallest of all the European lodgements in India, a Danish fort on the Coromandel sand. 1

Tharangambadi and the Printed Word

In 1616, King Christian IV of Denmark chartered the Danish East India Company, launching it into the Indian Ocean with ambitions that mirrored the simultaneous rise of the Dutch VOC and the English merchants. Yet the Danes arrived to find the crowded spaces of the South Indian coast already littered with rival European trade factories, a contested network stretching from the ports of Surat (where the English and the Dutch had already secured Mughal patents, and Portuguese fleets dominated the sea lanes) to the inland depots of Hubli.

Leasing the Waves

Rebuffed in Ceylon, where the Portuguese controlled the coastal approaches and the King of Kandy, Senarat, repudiated the treaty of the deceased Dutch merchant-adventurer Boschouwer, the Danish expedition was forced to seek a marginal alternative. In 1620, their admiral Ove Gjedde bargained with Raghunatha Nayak, the ruler of the Hindu kingdom of Thanjavur (Tanjore), securing a lease on a windswept fishing village called Tharangambadi (Tranquebar), or the "place of singing waves." Here, on the Coromandel beach, they built the whitewashed walls of Fort Dansborg, a lonely outpost that for nearly a century served only the trade in pepper and textiles.

Faith under Another Flag

Tharangambadi's true significance, however, would be spiritual, not mercantile. In 1705, King Frederick IV of Denmark sponsored the first Protestant mission to India, selecting two young German pietists, Bartholomaeus Ziegenbalg and Heinrich Pluetschau, to carry the Lutheran Evangelical faith to the East. When they landed at Tharangambadi in July 1706, they found themselves in a unique, isolated enclave. Squeezed between the hostile local Danish governors and the surrounding Hindu kingdom, Ziegenbalg had to operate in the teeth of official resistance.

For the Danish company officials, like their Dutch and English rivals, positioned themselves strictly as secular, neutral merchants who feared that evangelical zeal would alienate local religious sensibilities and disrupt the flow of commerce. While the English Company actively barred Protestant missionaries from landing in Madras or Calcutta to protect their trade licenses, the Danish Crown's direct patronage of Frederick IV's Royal Mission turned Tharangambadi into a rare sanctuary. 16

Types and Testaments

In this narrow space, Ziegenbalg turned his attention to the language of the soil, learning Tamil with an intensity no European had yet attempted. Recognizing that oral preaching was not enough, a printing press was brought from Europe in 1712, Tamil types were cut, and by 1715 the first Protestant translation of the New Testament stood printed in an Indian tongue.

Printing itself was older in India, for the Jesuits had set up the first press at Goa as early as 1556, and had cast Tamil types there in 1577, later improved at Kollam, where the Doctrina Christam of 1578 became the first book printed in an Indian script on Indian soil; but that precocious beginning had guttered out, and between the Jesuit Tamil experiments and the Danish press stretched a gap of a hundred and thirty years. What Tharangambadi founded was the press as a sustained engine: of vernacular scripture, of schooling, of controversy, and, not least, of reports sent home to Europe, where they were read by men who would never see India and would nonetheless write its history. 2

Ink and Empire

Yet the engine would not remain confined to the Coromandel shore or to the altar. Its expansion would repeat the pattern of the south, relying once again on the sanctuary of a Danish charter. In 1793, when the English Baptist missionary William Carey landed in Calcutta, the English East India Company strictly refused him permission to operate within their territories, fearing any evangelical disturbance to their commerce. Carey was forced to seek refuge under another flag, moving to the Danish settlement of Serampore (Frederiksnagore) on the Hooghly River. There, under the protection of the governor, Carey, Joshua Marshman, and William Ward established the Serampore Mission Press in 1800.

What followed was a revolution in print. Serampore became a massive multilingual type-foundry—a specialized workshop where molten metal alloys were cast into individual letter-molds for printing—cutting characters for over forty Indian and East Asian languages. Yet this missionary catalyst soon merged with English administrative expansion. The EIC, realizing the power of print for governance, began utilizing these very type-foundries and presses to publish secular government regulations, land surveys, and botanical reports. The tool of the evangelist became the tool of the state and the street alike, giving birth to the first great tide of vernacular journalism in Tamil, Bengali, and Hindi, and transforming print into the very nervous system of modern Indian public life. 17

Pith and Pulpits

From Tharangambadi, the printed word worked its way slowly round to the Serra, and by curious roads. The first printed Malayalam book, the Samkshepavedartham (Compendium of Christian Doctrine) of 1772, compiled by the Carmelite Clement Pianius, was printed not in Kerala at all but at Rome, on the press of the Sacred Congregation of Propaganda Fide itself.

Decades later, the technology arrived in the hills. Benjamin Bailey, the CMS missionary whom the reader knows from the hopeful early years of the Kottayam "Mission of Help," built a press at Kottayam with his own hands about 1821. Guided only by the illustrations in the Encyclopaedia Britannica, Bailey carved his own wooden types and designed his own press to print his landmark Malayalam translation of the Bible and the Anglican prayer books.

Then came the Catholic response. In 1846, Kuriakose Elias Chavara, whom the reader knows as the saviour of the Roman allegiance in the Rokos affair, founded at Mannanam the Syro-Malabar church's first printing press—a wooden machine of his own devising, its prototype painstakingly modelled in plantain pith and cast in lead by local carpenters and ironsmiths; its first printed book, in 1847, was the Malayalam prayer book Jnanapiyusham (Nectar of Knowledge). Meanwhile, the presses of Kunnamkulam and Pampakuda armed the Puthankūr with their own Syriac and Malayalam liturgies. By the century’s end, every church in the Serra could print; and when Father Nidhiry’s Nasrani Deepika appeared in 1887, the voice of the community in the new public world, it was printed on Chavara’s own Mannanam wooden press. The instrument that had arrived as an auxiliary of prayer stood ready for other work. A press, the churches would discover, was a pulpit that reached one’s antagonists. 3

Lamp of the Enlightenment and Bard of Romanticism

Before the Danish connection furnished Europe with a wider history of Indian Christianity, the Malabar Church had already entered the English Protestant imagination through an Anglican door. Michael Geddes, a Church of England divine, published his History of the Church of Malabar in 1694 as a great anti-Diamper brief: a history less of the whole Nazrani past than of Portuguese violence, Jesuit ambition, the Synod of Diamper, and the reduction of an ancient Eastern church to Rome. Geddes fixed one enduring Protestant use of the Serra: the Syrians could be presented as an old non-Roman Christian people, oppressed by Catholic tyranny and therefore useful to the Anglican case against Rome.

The Enlightenment Indictment

The first wider European history of Indian Christianity to draw deeply on the Danish connection was Mathurin Veyssière de La Croze's Histoire du christianisme des Indes (1724). Written not in India but at a Berlin desk, it was built from Portuguese chronicles, Catholic missionary material, and, crucially, the fresh reports and manuscripts sent home through the Danish Pietist mission at Tharangambadi. Operating as a Protestant convert and anti-Jesuit controversialist, La Croze treated the St Thomas tradition as a layered confusion to be stripped apart. The apostolic journey itself he traced to apocryphal and Manichaean fables; the Malabar claim, more specifically, he suspected had been strengthened by confusion between the Apostle and Thomas Gana, or Mar Thomas, the Armenian merchant remembered in the community's own traditions. His Syrians mattered chiefly as witnesses against Rome: an ancient Eastern people whose sufferings under the Portuguese could be made to serve the Protestant indictment of Catholic tyranny, even while their East Syrian inheritance was read through the language of corruption.

The Jansenist Pilgrim

Abraham-Hyacinthe Anquetil-Duperron, by contrast, came not as a reforming desk-polemicist but as an orientalist pilgrim of the manuscript age. Formed in Jansenist austerity and drawn by the hope of recovering ancient sacred languages, he passed through Cochin at the close of 1757, listened to local Christian informants, took note of their traditions, and accepted the apostolic landing without subjecting it to La Croze's destructive suspicion. This was not because he possessed better proof. It was because he looked eastward for survivals: old rites, old books, old priestly memories, fragments of a sacred antiquity still living in communities that Europe had not yet fully classified. The same appetite would later carry him to the Zend-Avesta and the Oupnek'hat, helping open the archive of Asian sacred literature to Europe. 18

Arguments and Archives

The contrast between the two Frenchmen is instructive. Both were formed within Catholic worlds, both had theological training behind them, and both were philologists of uncommon appetite—La Croze in Syriac, Coptic, Armenian, and the learned languages of the library; Anquetil-Duperron in Persian, Pahlavi, Avestan, and the living languages of the road. Yet their differing temperaments and ideological commitments yielded two opposite uses of the Nazrani past. Between Geddes, La Croze, and Anquetil-Duperron, three early European readings of the Nazranis had already appeared: the Anglican brief, the Enlightenment indictment, and the oriental archive. If Geddes made the Malabar Church an argument against Rome, La Croze made it a system to be dissected, and Anquetil-Duperron approached it as a living remnant of older sacred worlds. In this, they prefigured a generational shift: La Croze stood as the cold, analytical lamp of the Enlightenment, dissecting dogma to purge tradition; Anquetil-Duperron emerged as a warm, immersive herald—a bard of Romanticism, searching out secret wisdom to preserve a primordial, perennial memory. 4

The Carmelite Catholic answer came in 1794. Paulinus of St Bartholomew, who had worked the Serra for a dozen years and knew its churches at first hand, published his India Orientalis Christiana at Rome: a vigorous defence of the apostolate and of the Mylapore tomb, a learned and even affectionate catalogue of the Syrian parishes and their high standing, and, for all that, a brief. His regard ran to the Pazhayakūr; the Puthankūr were 'schismatics' fallen from an ancient obedience; and the history served, as history in the Serra has served at every turn of this book, a claim of jurisdiction, this time the Propaganda's. Against La Croze above all, with his attempt to dissolve the apostolic memory into Manichaean apocrypha and Thomas-of-Cana confusion, Paulinus supplied the Carmelite counter-record. These were histories, but histories sharpened into weapons: the Anglican's, the Protestant convert's, the orientalist's, and the Carmelite's. What no one had yet written was the Nazranis' own. 5

The Protestant Century

The nineteenth century belonged, on the printed page, to the Englishmen. Buchanan opened it with the Christian Researches (1811), the Syrians drawn in romantic sepia, an ancient apostolic remnant awaiting Anglican purification.

Anglicans and Apologists

Hough's massive History of Christianity in India (from 1839) followed, with the CMS temper hardened into method: the South Indian mission of St Thomas dismissed as a fable. Howard, a High Churchman, made the exception that proves the rule, valuing the very liturgies his brethren deplored; Collins of the CMS restored the rule.

Lingerings of Light

Whitehouse, British chaplain at Cochin from 1852 to 1864, gathered in his Lingerings of Light in a Dark Land (1873) the most detailed survey of the Syrian churches yet made, and his title is his thesis in miniature: an ancient light, a present darkness. He wrote with passion against Menezes and the burning of the Syriac books, with care about the old churches and their traditions, and with disdain for the living rite; and it is one of the war's better ironies that the Catholic apologists of the next generation would quarry this Protestant chaplain for their best ammunition against the Portuguese.

Last came the scholar's blow: Milne Rae of the Madras Christian College, whose The Syrian Church in India (1892) denied the apostle to the south altogether and derived the church from East Syrian merchant colonists centuries later. The Serra's antiquity itself was now under formal attack. 6

The Partisan Shelf

Set the shelf in order, from La Croze to Rae, and one fact stands out, and the reader should hold it through everything that follows: until Mackenzie sat down to his chapter, there existed no neutral record of the Nazrani past. Every history yet written was a partisan's: Portuguese and Carmelite briefs for jurisdiction, Protestant briefs for reform, the philosophers' briefs for their own quarrels with Rome, and the community's own manuscripts, from the apologetic histories of the Mar Gabriel era to Paremmakkal's Varthamanapusthakam, briefs for its factions.

And in the decades just before the Manual, the field was held overwhelmingly by the Anglican and Protestant pen; it was Hough, Whitehouse, and Rae who stood on the library shelves of the presidency, and their lens, sympathetic to the Syrians' sufferings and contemptuous of the Syrians' religion, that passed for the learned view. This is the shelf Mackenzie inherited when the Darbar asked him for neutrality; and it is why his attempt at it satisfied no one. 7

The Resident's Chapter

Mackenzie had gone about his commission honestly. He gathered what the churches told him, sorted their accounts of their own past into four irreconcilable classes, and set them down side by side, resolving that an official publication must be neutral and that all he could attempt was 'to set out the facts with little or no comment'.

Neutrality as Provocation

He published his chapter separately in 1901, by leave of the Darbar and ahead of the Manual, precisely so that it might be perused and corrected before it hardened into the state's own record. He got his corrections. What he had not reckoned with was what correction meant in the Serra: not an erratum slip, but a campaign. 8

Bernard's Battle

Of what the Resident's chapter set off we possess a witness from inside the war. Writing in 1924, the Carmelite Bernard of St Thomas, the first considerable historian the Syro-Malabar church produced from its own ranks, looked back on three centuries of what he took to be a settled injustice.

The Portuguese, he wrote, had published books in Europe asserting that all the East Syrians had been Nestorians down to the close of the sixteenth century and that the Malabar Syrians had been converted to Catholicism by Menezes; these books, 'long left unrefuted by those whose interests they prejudiced', had bred 'a wrong and highly injurious, but sincere belief' in the missionaries, who 'with a good conscience' had 'shaped and followed their anti-Syrian policy for three long centuries, to the great resentment and ruin of the Syrian Church, Liturgy and people'.

Then, at the century's turn, two things changed at once: the Pazhayakūr received bishops of their own rite and country; and the Travancore Darbar commissioned its Manual. Bernard's account of what followed deserves to be quoted whole, for it is the mechanics of the entire war in five sentences: 'The compilation of the Travancore State Manual gave them an opportunity. The first draft of the chapter on Christianity by G. T. Mackenzie completely ignored the Syrian tradition and exposed the Portuguese view. The Syrians at once protested. But they had nothing except their tradition to rely on. Hence the leading Syrian priests devoted themselves to finding out and collecting old documents to support their tradition.' 9

The Hunt for the Archive

There is the whole pattern: provocation, protest, and then the arsenal. The leading priests went hunting for documents; the procurator of the Chaldean patriarch at Rome searched the Vatican archives and published what favoured the Syrian view; and the tradition that had been 'sedulously kept' as memory for three centuries began its transformation into footnoted history.

Nor was the other side idle. Bernard complains of 'certain modern writers' who 'have taken the liberty to condemn the contention of Syrian orthodoxy as a novel claim brought forward by the younger generation among the Syrians': the sceptics' counter-thesis, that the apologetic history was itself the novelty, was in the field from the first. Both charges, it will be seen, were true in part; and between them they define the war this chapter must follow. 10

When Song Became Evidence

To understand what the priests went hunting for, one must understand what the community already possessed; and what it possessed was not history in the European sense but memory in three registers. There was the sung register: the wedding and festival songs of the parishes, the Margamkalippattu danced in a ring, the Ramban Pattu chanted as the deeds of the apostle, songs which Jesuit observers, Correia in 1564, Dionisio in 1578, Gouvea at the century's end, had already noticed and mined for the community's account of itself.

Registers of Memory

There was the written register, older than any press: Syriac service books, Malayalam and Garshuni Malayalam manuscripts, padiyolas, the palm leaves through which the songs themselves passed. And there was the register of place and family: the seven churches of the apostle, the pilgrimage to Mylapore that the old people called going to Beth-Thomas, the houses that traced themselves to apostolic baptism or to Knai Thoma's ships. None of this had been assembled as evidence, because none of it had ever been required to prove anything. It was the furniture of an inherited identity. 11

From Song to Source

Print changed the function of all of it. In 1910 P. U. Lukas gathered the ancient songs into the first printed folk-song collection in Kerala's history, and what had lived in performance became a text that could be cited. The Jesuit Henry Hosten, the most indefatigable collector the field ever had, gathered, translated, annotated, and in 1928 published the Song of Thomas Ramban itself, debating its worth with the critics as he did so.

And once the songs were on the page, they were in the war. The apologists read in them the confirmation of the tradition: the landing, the seven churches, the Brahmin converts, the martyrdom at Mylapore. The sceptics read in them the tradition's manufacture: Mingana ruled that the Ramban song had no historical value at all. Between those verdicts, as a later chapter will show, lies something more interesting than either; here it is enough to mark the transformation itself. A song is sung to a community that already believes; a source is quoted against an opponent who does not. In one generation, the entire memory of the Nazranis crossed from the first condition to the second. 12

The Apologists and the Sceptics

The combatants took the field with remarkable speed, and each carried the colours of his church. For the Catholics, the vicar apostolic of Trichur, A. E. Medlycott, opened the scholarly front with India and the Apostle Thomas (1905), a learned assembling of the ancient witnesses, the Acts, the fathers, the liturgies, the tomb, in defence of the apostolate.

For the Malankara church, E. M. Philip of Edavazhikal, veteran of the Seminary-case world, answered with The Indian Church of St Thomas (1908): church history written, as he did not conceal, to vindicate the Malankara side of every question from the apostle to the patriarch.

For the Syro-Malabar church came Bernard of St Thomas, whose Malayalam Marthoma Christianikal (1916–21) and English Brief Sketch (1924) met the doubters point by point: against Fortescue's verdict that the Indian apostolate was 'a very doubtful legend, unfounded', Bernard marshalled the etymologies of Sandrok-Mahosa and Caliana, the Viradian song that Nair singers still carried from Syrian house to Syrian house for an inam, the seven churches, and the Beth-Thomas pilgrimage remembered even in King Alfred's England.

The Critics' Arena

Around and against them worked the critics: T. K. Joseph, the most formidable of the native sceptics, whose Malabar Christian Copper Plates (1925) turned modern epigraphy on the community's most cherished documents, K. M. Daniel and their fellows, with the Kerala Society Papers as their arena and Hosten as gatherer, goad, and correspondent to all parties. The war was not between Europeans and Indians. It was a war of Indians over their own past, with European scholarship supplying weapons to every side. 13

The Grammar of Antiquity

Two things must be said of this literature, and they pull against each other. The first is that it was not an invention. Perczel has edited apologetic church histories written by Thomas Christians in Syriac and Garshuni long before print, seventeenth- and eighteenth-century texts already arguing the community's past against its rivals; the genre the twentieth century perfected was as old as the community's quarrels, and the memories the apologists organized, the songs, the plates, the family claims, the sacred places, were genuinely inherited, not fabricated for the occasion.

The second is that the organizing itself was new. Under the triple pressure of litigation, missionary criticism, and the rising grammar of Indian nationalism, in which antiquity, indigeneity, and self-rule were the claims that counted, the inherited memories were gathered, systematized, footnoted, and deployed. To say that Thomas had landed at Kodungallur, founded the seven churches, converted the high-born, and died at Mylapore was no longer only to say who one's fathers were. It was to say that the Christians of Kerala were not colonial dependants but an ancient Indian people, entitled to their property, their rank, and their self-government. The apologetic history of the churches borrowed the nationalist grammar because it was fighting the same kind of war. 14

The Fronts of the War

By the time Bernard wrote, the war had settled into its fronts, and they organize the chapters that follow. There was the constitutional front, where the question was not the apostle but the present: who owned the churches, who held the vattipanam, whether the Malankara Metropolitan was head or servant, and how far Rome would carry the restoration of the Pazhayakūr; there the quarrels of the nineteenth century came to their violent maturity, in the rupture of metran and bawa and in the completion of the Syro-Malabar edifice, and to that front the next chapter belongs.

The Battle Lines

And there was the front of origins, where song, plate, and family tradition were cross-examined like witnesses: the apostle's landing and the rival dates; Knai Thoma and his seventy-two, families or privileges; Southist against Northist; the seven churches; the Ramban's song against the Acts of Thomas. To that front, where the scholars still stand, our twentieth chapter will go. The reader who has travelled from Part One will recognize the exhibits. They are the same ones we handled at the beginning of this book, before anyone had learned to shout over them. 15

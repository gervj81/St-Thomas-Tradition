The Nazranis and their historians, 1901–1936

The War for the Past

"Those opinions are put forward at the present day, not only in academic controversy but also as the basis of litigation for the possession of Church property and of Trust funds."

— G. T. Mackenzie, Christianity in Travancore (1901)

The nineteenth century had fought over who should govern the Serra. The twentieth century fought over who had the right to remember it. The lull with which our last chapter closed did not last a decade. Mackenzie's chapter for the Travancore State Manual, written to be neutral, to set out the facts 'with little or no comment', instead handed every faction of the divided church a mirror, and every faction disliked what it saw there. What followed was a war of the presses: songs became sources, parish legends became evidence, family traditions became genealogy, and sacred geography became historical argument. The churches did not merely inherit a past; they learned to print it, defend it, and litigate it. This chapter follows that war, and in following it returns, by a long road, to the questions with which this book began: for the dossier over which the twentieth century fought was the same dossier we weighed in Part One, the apostle, the tomb, the plates, and the silence. 1

The Resident's Chapter

Mackenzie had gone about his commission honestly. He gathered what the churches told him, sorted their accounts of their own past into four irreconcilable classes, and set them down side by side, resolving that an official publication must be neutral and that all he could attempt was 'to set out the facts with little or no comment'. He published his chapter separately in 1901, by leave of the Darbar and ahead of the Manual, precisely so that it might be perused and corrected before it hardened into the state's own record. He got his corrections. What he had not reckoned with was what correction meant in the Serra: not an erratum slip, but a campaign. 2

Of what the Resident's chapter set off we possess a witness from inside the war. Writing in 1924, the Carmelite Bernard of St Thomas, the first considerable historian the Syro-Malabar church produced from its own ranks, looked back on three centuries of what he took to be a settled injustice. The Portuguese, he wrote, had published books in Europe asserting that all the East Syrians had been Nestorians down to the close of the sixteenth century and that the Malabar Syrians had been converted to Catholicism by Menezes; these books, 'long left unrefuted by those whose interests they prejudiced', had bred 'a wrong and highly injurious, but sincere belief' in the missionaries, who 'with a good conscience' had 'shaped and followed their anti-Syrian policy for three long centuries, to the great resentment and ruin of the Syrian Church, Liturgy and people'. For all that time, 'for want of opportunity and of knowledge of misrepresentation abroad, the Syrians made no attempt in refuting the calumnies against their Church'. Then, at the century's turn, two things changed at once. The Pazhayakūr received bishops of their own rite and country; and the Travancore Darbar commissioned its Manual. Bernard's account of what followed deserves to be quoted whole, for it is the mechanics of the entire war in five sentences: 'The compilation of the Travancore State Manual gave them an opportunity. The first draft of the chapter on Christianity by G. T. Mackenzie completely ignored the Syrian tradition and exposed the Portuguese view. The Syrians at once protested. But they had nothing except their tradition to rely on. Hence the leading Syrian priests devoted themselves to finding out and collecting old documents to support their tradition.' 3

There is the whole pattern: provocation, protest, and then the arsenal. The leading priests went hunting for documents; the procurator of the Chaldean patriarch at Rome searched the Vatican archives and published what favoured the Syrian view; and the tradition that had been 'sedulously kept' as memory for three centuries began its transformation into footnoted history. Nor was the other side idle. Bernard complains of 'certain modern writers' who 'have taken the liberty to condemn the contention of Syrian orthodoxy as a novel claim brought forward by the younger generation among the Syrians': the sceptics' counter-thesis, that the apologetic history was itself the novelty, was in the field from the first. Both charges, it will be seen, were true in part; and between them they define the war this chapter must follow. 4

When Song Became Evidence

The transition of the Syrian historical memory from an oral and manuscript culture to the print medium was a long process that began under European observation but ended as an indigenous weapon. As early as 1578, the Jesuit Francisco Dionisio had observed that the Christians of the Serra preserved their apostolic traditions not only in written manuscripts but also in historical songs that they chanted during weddings, festivals, and public gatherings. These songs, preserved on fragile palm-leaves and transcribed repeatedly across generations, formed a parallel liturgy of memory. For centuries, they remained local, regional, and fluid. But in the early twentieth century, the demands of state documentation and sectarian litigation forced these songs to harden into print. The first major milestone in this transition was the publication in 1910 of Purathana Pattukal (Ancient Songs) by P. U. Lukas. For the first time, the wedding chants, historical ballads, and family legends of the Knanaya and Syrian Christians were gathered from palm-leaf manuscripts and presented to a reading public as printed, standardized evidence. 5

No song attracted more interest or generated more controversy than the Ramban Pattu (the Song of Thomas Ramban). In 1928, the Jesuit scholar Father Henry Hosten published an English translation of this song, catapulting it from local church recitations into the international scholarly debate. The song claimed to have been originally composed in 1601 by Thomas Ramban, a cattanar of the Pakalomattom family, who supposedly condensed an older, detailed narrative written by his ancestor, the first disciple of the Apostle. Hosten gathered and collated nine distinct manuscripts of the song, analyzing its textual variations and its claims. The song laid out a precise timeline: St. Thomas arrived in Malabar in AD 50, performed numerous miracles, established several congregations, and was martyred at Mylapore on 3 July AD 72. Yet, as critical readers noted, this printed symmetry masked a complex of plural chronologies. While the printed version settled on the AD 52 arrival and AD 72 martyrdom, older manuscript strands preserved variant traditions, such as an arrival in AD 52, a Coromandel mission beginning around AD 67, and liturgical commemorations split between 21 Dhanu (December) and 3 Karkadakam (July). In the printed age, these diverse memories crystallized into a single, unyielding historical formula. 6

The Negotiated Map

The regional traditions did not preserve a single, uniform itinerary of the Apostle, but rather a series of conflicting sacred geographies that had to be reconciled. The Margamkalippattu, the ancient dance-song of the Northist Syrians, focused heavily on a Chola-palace narrative, where a merchant named Havan was sent by King Choza to the west to find a skilled carpenter who could build a palace matching the splendor of Solomon's temple. Thomas returned with him, built the palace in a spiritual sense by distributing the king's treasure to the poor, and subsequently met his end on the Coromandel coast (Chinnamala). In contrast, the Ramban Pattu preserved a Malabar-centric itinerary, where the Apostle landed first at Malyamkara (Kodungallur), preached immediately, and established the famous seven churches of Malabar before turning to his final Coromandel mission. Over time, these disparate regional narratives were synthesized into a single, standardized geography: an arrival at Kodungallur in AD 52, the founding of the seven churches (Kodungallur, Palayoor, Kottakkavu, Kokkamangalam, Niranam, Nilackal, and Kollam), and a martyrdom at Mylapore in AD 72. This synthetic geography functioned as a negotiated map, resolving local claims of antiquity and legitimacy while providing a unified origin story for the entire Syrian community. 7

This printed map stood in stark contrast to the historical counter-map reconstructed by the Jesuit Archbishop Francisco Ros in the early seventeenth century. As discussed in Chapter 11, Ros had argued in his 1604 treatise that the St. Thomas Christians were not native to Malabar from the first century, but had fled west from Mylapore on the Coromandel coast to escape war, famine, and persecution. For Ros, the true historical heir to the ancient see of Mylapore was Angamaly, which justified the extension of Padroado jurisdiction over the entire Serra. In the twentieth-century debates, both sides mobilized manuscript evidence to defend their respective maps. The Coromandel connection was kept alive not only by the archaeological presence of the Persian cross of Mylapore with its Pahlavi inscription, but also by European scholarship publishing Eastern Syrian manuscript colophons, such as those found in J. P. N. Land's Anecdota Syriaca, and old Tamil legendary accounts first translated into English in 1837 and later republished by Hosten. The past was not a stable deposit; it was a contested landscape where every boundary, church site, and migration route was subject to revision. 8

The Plates and the Past

If the songs were the fluid currency of the war, the ancient copper-plate grants were its gold standard. The St. Thomas Christians had long pointed to these physical charters as proof of their noble status, caste privileges, and royal protection. However, the physical disappearance of the original Thomas of Kana plates, last seen by Portuguese officials in the sixteenth century, turned their textual memory into a major battleground. The traditional history asserted that Thomas of Kana, a wealthy Syrian merchant, arrived in Malabar in AD 345 with a group of seventy-two families, obtaining grand privileges from the local ruler Cheraman Perumal. In the early twentieth century, this narrative was subjected to rigorous textual criticism by scholars like T. K. Joseph. Joseph demonstrated that the famous phrase "seventy-two families," long held to be the demographic foundation of the Knanaya community, was a mistranslation of the Malayalam phrase elupattirandu vituperu, which meant seventy-two royal privileges or honors. This philological solvent dissolved a key pillar of the traditional history, transforming a census into a charter of privileges. 9

Further disruption came from archival discoveries that challenged the traditional AD 345 date of the Knanaya migration. Seventeenth-century Malayalam accounts, preserved in the archives of Rome and Paris, dated Thomas of Kana's arrival to the ninth century, placing it after the collapse of the Coromandel settlement at Mylapore. Drawing on these accounts and the indirect Garshuni Malayalam tradition documented by Anquetil du Perron, the modern scholar Istvan Perczel formulated a radical hypothesis. He argued that the details of the lost Thomas of Kana grant did not originate in the fourth century, but were copied and preserved within the famous Tarisappalli copper plates of Kollam, dating to AD 825. This hypothesis collapsed the centuries separating the legendary AD 345 migration from the historical founding of the port of Kollam in 825, suggesting that the migration was contemporaneous with the post-iconoclastic East Syrian trade expansion. By shifting the migration to the ninth century, this scholarship threatened the traditional ethno-histories of both the Southist (Knanaya) and Northist factions, showing how the early modern manuscript ecology—including documents like the Leiden manuscript analyzed by Emy Joy—had constructed a deep antiquity to safeguard local status against colonial and domestic rivals. 10

The Apologists and the Sceptics

The stakes of this historical war were high, involving not only spiritual prestige but also the physical possession of churches, trust funds, and legal recognition. The combatants divided along denominational and methodological lines. On the Malankara Syrian side, E. M. Philip published The Indian Church of St. Thomas in 1908. Writing in the shadow of the bitter Seminary Case litigation that had fractured the Jacobite community, Philip sought to defend the independent, non-Roman antiquity of the Malankara church against both Catholic claims and Anglican-backed reformers. On the Catholic side, Bishop A. E. Medlycott published his landmark India and the Apostle Thomas in 1905, systematically gathering early Roman, Greek, and apocryphal testimonies to build an unassailable dossier for the Apostle's Indian mission. These works were not disinterested academic exercises; they were briefs prepared for the courtroom of public opinion and the British raj. 11

No writer entered this arena with more polemical energy than the Carmelite Father Bernard of St. Thomas. In his Malayalam work Marthoma Christianikal (1916–1921) and his English summary A Brief Sketch of the History of the St. Thomas Christians (1924), Bernard launched a fierce counter-attack against Western skeptics like Adrian Fortescue, who had dismissed the St. Thomas tradition as a "very doubtful legend, unfounded." To refute Fortescue, Bernard deployed an elaborate apparatus of philological and local evidence. He defended the historical names in the apocryphal Acts of Thomas by arguing that "Andropolis" (the port of landing) was a corruption of the Syriac Sandrok-Mahosa, which he broke down as Sandrok (a corruption of the local name Sandrok-Shiva, linked to Kodungallur) and Mahosa (the Syriac word for city or town). Similarly, he argued that "Calamina" (the site of martyrdom) was not a fictitious location but a corruption of the local name of Mylapore. He marshaled the "Viradian song"—a traditional ballad chanted by Caliculam Nairs at Christian homes—alongside the seven churches and the medieval "Beth-Thomas" pilgrimage records (including King Alfred the Great's reported ninth-century embassy to the tomb) to prove that the memory was ancient, domestic, and unbroken. 12

Yet, even as Bernard and Hosten gathered documents to defend the tradition, a group of internal critics began to dissolve the apologetic consensus. T. K. Joseph and K. M. Daniel acted as a critical solvent, applying modern historical methods to separate the layers of memory, manuscript, and print. They pointed out that while the songs and copper plates were invaluable records of medieval social status, they could not be used as eyewitness accounts of first-century events. As Istvan Perczel has recently demonstrated, the apologetic church histories were not a twentieth-century invention; pre-modern Syrian cattanars had written similar defenses in the seventeenth and eighteenth centuries to navigate their position between the Portuguese and the Dutch. What the twentieth century added was the speed, reach, and permanence of the printing press. In the end, the war for the past returned to the very dossier we weighed in Part One of this book: the apostle, the tomb, the plates, and the long silence. The modern Syrian church did not find a new past; it constructed a fortress out of its old memories, and inside that fortress, it continues to dwell. 13

# Chapter 3: THE TOMB, THE TRADE, AND THE LONG SILENCE

## c. 550 CE – 883 CE

> \*"In Male, where pepper grows, there is a church of Christians and clergy and faithful, and likewise at Calliana there is a bishop, appointed from Persia."\*
>
> \*\*— Cosmas Indicopleustes, \*Christian Topography\*, c. 525–550 CE\*\*

\---

The documentary tradition examined in Chapter 2 belongs to the late-antique Christian Mediterranean — the world of Origen, Ephrem, the patristic chain, the contested elements that entered the record around 500 CE. What follows belongs to a different era: one shaped by the rise of Islam, the collapse of the Sasanian Empire, the emergence of a new Latin Christendom in western Europe, and — in India — a political transformation that would redraw the map of where Christian communities were documented and where they were not.

Two threads persist through this transformation. The tomb tradition at Calamina continues to be referenced by writers who had no contact with one another and no common institutional motive. And the community on the south Indian coast, after centuries of documentary silence, re-enters the record through royally issued charters in the ninth century. The question this chapter traces is why the documentary record takes the shape it does. The answer is shaped by a fact that must be stated at the outset: information flows and political structures determine what gets documented and what does not. The absence of a community from the record of a given period tells us that no political or literary system recorded it. It does not tell us the community was not there.

## 3.1 — THE LAST WITNESS AND THE COLLAPSE OF THE NETWORKS

### Cosmas: The Final Snapshot

The epigraph to this chapter is also its starting point. Cosmas Indicopleustes, an Alexandrian merchant turned monk, composed his *Christian Topography* around 525–550 CE. His testimony is the last report on Indian Christianity to reach the Mediterranean world through the Alexandrian maritime route — the Red Sea corridor that had connected Roman Egypt to the Indian Ocean since the Ptolemaic period and that had produced the *Periplus*, the Sangam-era Roman coin hoards, and the commercial world described in Chapter 1.

What Cosmas actually says deserves precise attention, because later tradition has often inflated it. He records Christians and clergy at 'Male, where pepper grows' — a reference to the pepper coast. He records a bishop at Calliana, identified with Kalyan on the Konkan coast near modern Mumbai. He records a church in Taprobane (Sri Lanka) with a priest and deacon ordained in Persia, serving visiting Persian merchants. The bishop, Cosmas specifies, was 'appointed from Persia' — almost certainly meaning consecrated by the metropolitan of Fars at Rew Ardashir, whose jurisdiction encompassed the Persian Gulf, the Indian Ocean rim, and India.\[EN1]

What Cosmas does *not* say is as important as what he does. He does not place a bishop on the Muziris port area itself. The Christians at 'Male' have a church, clergy, and faithful — but their nearest episcopal authority is at Kalyan, up the Konkan coast, or in Persia. He says nothing about the Tomb or the Coromandel coast. And he says nothing about the antiquity or institutional depth of the Malabar community. What he describes is a commercial-ecclesiastical network of Persian Christians operating across the Indian Ocean rim — at Male, at Kalyan, at Taprobane — under the distant authority of the Persian church. This is a snapshot, not a survey.\[EN2]

Gregory of Tours, writing around 590 CE, reports a pilgrimage to the tomb of the apostle Thomas in India — a reference pointing to the Coromandel coast. And Isidore of Seville, recording Thomas's martyrdom in India before his own death in 636 CE, represents the last synthesis of the Thomas tradition by a Latin author operating within the old Mediterranean information system.\[EN3]

These are the final dispatches from a world that is about to disappear.

### The Double Collapse

At the Battle of Yarmouk in 636 CE, the Arab armies destroyed Byzantine military power in Syria. Within a generation, the same force had swallowed the Sasanian Empire whole — the last Sasanian shah, Yazdegerd III, was murdered by a local miller at Merv in 651 CE. The two powers that had framed the world described in Chapters 1 and 2 — the rivals across whose territories the trade routes ran — were, within fifteen years of each other, either destroyed or permanently diminished.

The consequences for information about Indian Christianity were immediate and devastating — not because the communities ceased to exist, but because the systems that had carried information about them were broken.

**The Alexandrian vector** — the Roman Egyptian maritime route through the Red Sea — had already contracted during the late third and fourth centuries. It had revived under Aksumite stimulus in the sixth century, producing Cosmas as its final witness. The Islamic conquest of Egypt (639–642 CE) placed this route permanently under Arab control. It should be noted that the Arab takeover of Egypt was not a simple seizure by an alien force. The Coptic Christian population of Egypt, which had endured decades of persecution under the Byzantine Chalcedonian authorities, initially regarded the Arab conquerors as liberators from Byzantine theological coercion. The Fatimid caliphate that later governed Egypt (969–1171 CE) would prove relatively tolerant toward its Christian subjects; it was the earlier Umayyad period and then the disruption of the Fatimid order by the Seljuks and later the Ayyubids that progressively closed the Alexandrian corridor as a channel for Christian communication. The Indian Ocean trade itself did not stop — Arab and Persian merchants inherited and expanded it — but the route was no longer a channel through which *Christian documentary information* travelled westward to Mediterranean authors. After Cosmas, no writer working within the Alexandrian tradition would ever again report on Indian Christians. The vector was dead.\[EN4]

**The Persian Gulf vector** — the Syriac corridor from Edessa through Mesopotamia to the Gulf ports — underwent a different transformation. The Sasanian political framework was destroyed, but the Church of the East survived. The Arab conquerors, following the Quranic provisions for 'People of the Book,' granted Christians *dhimmi* status — a subordinate but legally protected position within the Islamic polity. The Patriarch continued in office, and under the Abbasid Caliphate (750–1258 CE), headquartered at Baghdad from 762, operated from the caliphal capital itself, maintaining correspondence with bishoprics across the eastern world. Patriarch Timothy I (780–823), one of the most remarkable administrators in the history of the Church of the East, negotiated directly with the caliphs, translated Aristotle's *Topics* into Arabic at the caliph al-Mahdi's request, and oversaw a missionary expansion that stretched from Mongolia to Tang Dynasty China. This vector continued to function — but it operated *within* the caliphate. Its information circulated in Syriac and Arabic, not in Latin or Greek. It did not reach the emerging Latin Christendom of western Europe.\[EN5]

**The overland corridors** through Central Asia — already weakened, as Chapter 2 noted — were further disrupted by the succession of Muslim kingdoms and Turkic powers that would eventually produce the Seljuk consolidation in the eleventh century. What had once been a web of interconnected routes became a patchwork of competing jurisdictions through which Christian communication passed with increasing difficulty.

### What Survives from India

Between Cosmas (c. 550) and the ninth century, the inventory of external evidence for Indian Christianity is brief and geographically revealing. Jesuab of Nisibis, writing in the seventh century, uses the toponym 'Calamina' for the site of Thomas's martyrdom — a name that points to the Coromandel coast. This is the only source during the entire gap period that names a specific Indian location, and it names the eastern coast, not the western. Patriarch Timothy I (780–823), who administered the most geographically extended church in Christendom from his seat at Baghdad, lists 'India and China' among his active territories — the Nestorian Stele at Xi'an (781 CE) confirms the extraordinary geographic reach. But Timothy names no specific Indian location. He confirms that the Church of the East maintained its Indian province. He does not tell us where in India the community was concentrated.\[EN6]

The communities that Cosmas had attested on the western coast — at Male, under the bishop at Kalyan — are not mentioned again by any external source for approximately three hundred years. Not by any Syriac author. Not by any Arabic geographer — the earliest Arab account of the Indian Ocean dates to c. 851, contemporaneous with the first surviving local documentation, not before it. Not by any Latin writer. The Malabar-specific silence is complete.

### The Emerging Latin West

Meanwhile, on the other side of the collapsed networks, a new Christian civilisation was taking shape — one that would eventually produce the force that arrived on the Indian coast in 1498.

The western Roman Empire had fallen in 476 CE. What emerged from its ruins was a constellation of Germanic kingdoms gradually Christianised under papal authority and Frankish military power. The coronation of Charlemagne as Holy Roman Emperor by Pope Leo III in 800 CE — within two decades of Timothy I in Baghdad listing India among his active territories — marks the symbolic consolidation of Latin Christendom as a political entity distinct from both Byzantium and the caliphate. This new civilisation was Latin-speaking, papally centred, and geographically confined to western and central Europe. Its knowledge of the world beyond its frontiers was severely limited.

Charlemagne maintained diplomatic contact with the Abbasid court — the famous exchange of gifts with Caliph Harun al-Rashid included an elephant named Abul-Abbas and a water clock — but these contacts did not carry information about Indian Christians. The Latin church's awareness of eastern Christianity was confined to its encounters with Byzantine Greeks (whom it increasingly regarded as schismatic) and to fragmentary reports from pilgrims to the Holy Land. Of the Church of the East's vast eastern network — stretching from Baghdad through Persia to India, Central Asia, and China — Latin Christendom knew almost nothing. The information systems that might have connected them were either dead (the Alexandrian vector), inaccessible (the Syriac-language Persian Gulf vector, operating within the caliphate), or not yet disrupted enough to produce the refugee intelligence that would later generate legend.

The result was a Latin Christian world simultaneously expanding in political power and contracting in geographic knowledge. It knew that Christians existed somewhere in the East — the apostolic traditions remembered Thomas in India, Bartholomew in Armenia, the Magi in Persia. But it had no current, reliable intelligence about what those communities looked like, how they were governed, or whether they still existed. This ignorance would persist for centuries, deepening with each successive disruption of the remaining channels, until it crystallised into the legend of Prester John — and legend, eventually, into the strategic vision that sent a Portuguese fleet around the Cape of Good Hope.\[EN7]

## 3.2 — THE DARK AGE: WHY THE SILENCE?

### The Sangam World in Brief

To understand the silence from the Malabar coast, the political history of south India must be briefly traced. Chapter 1 described the commercial infrastructure of the Sangam world — the ports, the pepper trade, the Roman connection. The political framework was governed by three paramount ruling families: the Cēras on the western coast and the adjoining Koṅgu country, the Cōḻas in the Kaveri delta and the Coromandel coast, the Pāṇḍyas in the Vaigai basin and the far south. Below these *vēntar* stood numerous chieftains (*vēḷir*) controlling smaller territories. The economy combined wetland rice agriculture, pastoral activity, and maritime trade. The *Periplus* and Ptolemy confirm the Cēra ruler at Muziris and the Pāṇḍya king at Madurai as known entities in the Roman commercial world.\[EN8]

Two features of this world matter for what follows. The Sangam polity had not yet adopted the *varṇāśramadharma* ideology — the Brahmanical caste hierarchy — that would later define south Indian political structure. And while the Sangam kings supervised ports and taxed trade, as Chapter 1 established, they had not yet developed the Brahmanical grant system — royally issued charters conferring formal privileges within the *varṇāśramadharma* framework — that would later become the standard instrument for incorporating merchant communities into the polity. The commercial world existed; the political technology that would later formalise merchant incorporation did not.

### A General Phenomenon with Uneven Geography

The silence from the Malabar coast between Cosmas and the ninth century is not a peculiarity of Christian documentation. It is part of a larger transformation in south Indian political history — a period conventionally called the 'dark age,' in which the Sangam-era political order dissolved and new ruling lineages, new religious dispensations, and new political technologies emerged across Tamilakam.

The term is misleading if taken to mean a general collapse. What actually occurred was a geographically uneven transformation — and the unevenness is the key to understanding why the Christian documentary record takes the shape it does.

The central event was the rise of successor states across the Deccan and the Tamil country. In the Krishna-Godavari delta, the Ikṣvākus replaced the Satavahanas. In the Kannada country, the Kadambas at Banavasi and the Gangas at Talakad established independent kingdoms. In the Tamil deep south, the Kalabhras — a warlike clan that migrated from the northern borders — established dominance over the Kaveri delta by the fourth century. Later Pāṇḍya accounts branded them oppressors of Brahmanism, but recent inscriptions at Pulangurichi show Kalabhra rulers patronising Jain and other religious institutions, complicating the received narrative. What is not in dispute is that the old Sangam-era ruling families lost their grip on large parts of their former territories.\[EN9]

Across this zone, a common process was underway: the increasing penetration of north Indian Brahmanical culture — Vedic sacrifices, Sanskrit literary traditions, the *varṇāśrama* ideology — into the south Indian political order. Ruling families claimed Kṣatriya status, performed Vedic sacrifices, and made land grants to Brahmins. This was the seedbed of the political system that would, by the ninth century, produce the formalised grant structures within which the Thomas Christians would be documented.

But the transition did not happen at the same pace everywhere.

### The Coromandel: No Dark Age

**The Coromandel coast experienced no dark age at all.** The Pallavas emerged by the third century and established themselves at Kāñcīpuram. From the third century through the ninth, the Pallava territories produced a continuous inscriptional and architectural record: charters in Prakrit, Sanskrit, and Tamil; rock-cut and structural temples; the great port of Mamallapuram. When the Chinese pilgrim Xuanzang visited Kāñcīpuram in the 640s, he described over a hundred Buddhist and eighty Hindu temples. The Pallava state was literate, inscribing, building, and administering continuously throughout the period when Kerala produced nothing.

By the sixth century, the Pāṇḍyas had also revived. Kadungon (590–620) was celebrated in a later inscription as the 'destroyer of the Kalabhra kings.' Within a generation, the Pāṇḍyas were fighting the Pallavas, expanding into the Cēra country, and generating their own inscriptional record. Between the Pallavas in the north and the Pāṇḍyas in the south, the Coromandel and the deep south had continuous, documented political life from the sixth century onward.\[EN10]

### Kerala: Five Centuries of Silence

**Kerala, by contrast, went entirely silent.** The Sangam Cēras — the dynasty that had governed the port economy described in Chapter 1 — disappeared from the record after the third century. No inscriptions, no dateable literary production, no identifiable political authority survives for approximately five hundred years. Historians state the matter plainly: the history of Kerala during this period remains unclear.\[EN11]

The site where ancient Muziris had stood presumably continued to function as a harbour — its geographic advantages did not change — and external witnesses like Cosmas describe commercial and Christian activity on the western coast. But there is no political authority producing grants, inscriptions, or dated texts that would place any community in a formal institutional context. The Malabar silence is not a silence about Christians specifically. It is a silence about everything.

### The Bhakti Transformation

From the sixth century onward, a devotional revolution swept across the Tamil country and transformed its political structure. The Pallava king Mahendravarman I (580–630) abandoned Jainism for Śaivism under the influence of the poet-saint Appar. The Pāṇḍya king Nedumaran underwent a similar conversion under the poet-saint Sambandar. The Nāyanārs (Śaiva saints) and Āḻvārs (Vaiṣṇava saints) composed hymns in Tamil that merged northern devotional theology with southern poetic tradition, creating a popular religious force of extraordinary power. The hymns of the first three Nāyanārs — Appar, Sambandar, and Sundarar, collectively called the *mūvar* — were later compiled into the *Tēvāram*, which became the liturgical core of Tamil Śaivism. Māṇikka-vāchagar, a minister of the Pāṇḍya king Varaguna II (862–885), composed the *Tiruvāsagam*, regarded as the peak of bhakti mystical poetry. Temples multiplied across the landscape. The brahmadēya system expanded massively: kings made large-scale land grants to Brahmin settlements, creating a network that managed agrarian production, administered local affairs, and provided the ideological framework of *varṇāśramadharma* that the new political structure required.\[EN12]

The primary institutional targets of this movement were the religions that the Brahmanical tradition classified as heterodox — Buddhism and Jainism — whose commercial wealth and institutional infrastructure sustained their ideological power. The hagiographic literature is explicit: saints debate and defeat Buddhist and Jain opponents, kings abandon non-Brahmanical faiths, rival institutions lose patronage. The *Periyapurāṇam*, a twelfth-century hagiography of the sixty-three Nāyanārs, preserves the narrative of this campaign in devotional form, recording how saints challenged Jain and Buddhist teachers and converted their royal patrons. By the tenth century, Buddhism had effectively disappeared from the Tamil country, and Jainism survived with strength only in Karnataka.

No Pallava or Pāṇḍya inscription mentions Christians — neither as a community to be patronised nor as one to be pressured. The silence is total. But total silence from polities that documented Brahmin settlements, Jain temples, Buddhist monasteries, merchant guilds, military campaigns, and irrigation works is itself a datum. What it establishes is that no Pallava or Pāṇḍya authority incorporated a Christian community into its political structure through the grant system — while the Cēra state, when it finally emerged, did exactly that. The implications of this contrast, and the community traditions that may bear on it, will be examined in a later chapter.\[EN13]

### The Cēra Perumal State: A New Framework

The political structure within which the Indian Christian community first appears in dated, physically surviving documentation was itself a new creation of the ninth century.

The Cēra Perumal state, with its capital at Mahōdayapuram (Kodungallur), was built upon and around the Nambudiri Brahmin settlements that had migrated to Kerala from the north in the preceding centuries. Historians characterise it as a Brahman oligarchy in the guise of a monarchy: the king's power was largely ritual, exercised under the supervision of the *Nālu Tali*, a council of celibate Brahmins drawn from four major settlements near the capital. Below the Perumal, hereditary chiefs (*nāṭuvāḻi*) governed territorial divisions — Venad in the south, Kolattunad in the north, Eranadu and other nāṭus between them — exercising political and military power while acknowledging royal suzerainty. The Brahmins controlled the agrarian economy through extensive landholdings (*brahmasvam*) and temple estates (*dēvasvam*), and provided the ideological framework of *varṇāśramadharma* that regulated social life. The king was, in effect, the ritual head of a political system whose real power lay with the Brahmin assemblies and the hereditary chiefs.\[EN14]

The instrument through which this state incorporated communities into the polity was the royally issued charter — a grant conferring specific privileges upon a named community or institution. The political grammar of early medieval south India, as analysed by historians of the period, identifies four types of corporate institution that formed the building blocks of the state: the *ūr* (peasant locality), the *nāṭu* (supralocal territorial unit), the *brahmadēya* (Brahmin settlement), and the *nagaram* (merchant town). The brahmadēyas managed agrarian production and ideological legitimation through the temple-centred *sabhā*. The nagarams managed trade and artisanal production through the *nagarattār* corporate body. Both could receive *taniyūr* status — autonomous self-governance within the territorial unit of the nāṭu. This was not a Kerala peculiarity but the common political grammar of Tamilakam, operating across the Cōḻa, Pāṇḍya, and Cēra territories with regional variations.\[EN15]

Critically, the Cēra polity practised religious pluralism in its grant-giving. Buddhist monasteries, Jain temples, and merchant communities of various backgrounds all received comparable recognition through royally issued charters. This contrasts sharply with the Pāṇḍya territories, where the bhakti movement was restructuring religious life along Brahmanical lines, and with the Pallava state, where no parallel grant to a Christian community is known.

The emergence of this state coincided with two other developments that converged on the same historical moment. The Abbasid shift of the caliphal capital to Baghdad (762 CE) reoriented Persian Gulf trade and pushed Christian merchants from former Sasanian territories toward safer destinations, including the western Indian coast, where they had prior commercial contacts. And the intensifying Cēra-Pāṇḍya military conflict — the Pāṇḍyas had captured the port of Vizhinjam from the Āy kingdom — prompted the Cēra rulers to develop Quilon as a rival port, designed to attract the foreign merchants whose commercial activity the state depended on. The founding of Quilon in 825 CE, the beginning of the Malayalam era, was an act of political strategy — a new port city, not (as later tradition held) the personal achievement of a single merchant.\[EN16]

The bhakti pressure on communities classified as heterodox in the Pallava and Pāṇḍya territories; the migration of Persian Christian merchants westward under Abbasid commercial expansion; the Cēra polity's emergence with its pluralist grant technology and its strategic need for merchant communities — all of these historically align in the ninth century. The documented Christian presence on the western coast begins at exactly this moment.

\[BOX: 'MALABAR' — THE GEOGRAPHY OF A NAME]

The toponym appears in many forms — *Mali*, *Male*, *Malibar*, *Malibarat*, *Malabar* — and its geographic reference shifts depending on who is using it and when. The root is the Malayalam *malavāram*, meaning 'hill-produce' — that is, the pepper and spice country of the Western Ghats. Cosmas uses *Male* ('in Male, where pepper grows'); the Cairo Geniza documents use *Malibarat*; Arab geographers use *Malibar* to denote the entire pepper-producing coast from Cannanore in the north to Cape Comorin in the south — over 500 kilometres encompassing multiple kingdoms, port cities, and ecological zones. The word was used by the Jewish, Christian, and Arab trading communities themselves, from whom the Portuguese later adopted it.\[EN17]

Within this broad region, the specific ports where documentation concentrates changed over time. The ancient toponym 'Muziris' (*Muchiri* in Sangam Tamil) belongs to the Roman-era commercial world: it appears in the *Periplus*, in Pliny, in Ptolemy, in the Peutinger Table, and in the Sangam poetry. After the second or third century CE, the name drops out of the documentary record entirely. When documents resume in the ninth century, the documented port is **Quilon (Kollam)** — the new harbour city founded in 825 CE — not Muziris. The Cēra Perumal capital at the same general location is called **Mahōdayapuram** (identified with Kodungallur/Cranganore), not Muziris. The identification of Kodungallur with ancient Muziris is a modern scholarly reconstruction, confirmed by the Pattanam excavations, not a continuous naming tradition. No medieval source calls the place Muziris. 'Muziris' and 'Kodungallur' are two different historical identities for the same general area, separated by a five-century gap during which neither name appears in any document.

When this study uses 'Malabar coast' or 'western coast,' it refers to the entire region unless a specific port is named.

## 3.3 — THE COPPER PLATES AND THE PORT (825–851 CE)

### Mar Sapor, Mar Prodh, and the Tharisapalli Foundation

Community tradition records that in 823 CE, two Persian Christian merchant-leaders — Mar Sapor (Maruvan Sapir Iso) and Mar Prodh (Aphraat) — arrived at Quilon from the Persian Gulf, accompanied by a figure known as the 'glorious Sabrisho.' They established a sanctuary and settlement at Quilon, called Tharisapalli, which besides being a place of worship became the centre of economic life of the port town.

The tradition of this migration is preserved in multiple, not entirely concordant, forms — and the discordances themselves are analytically revealing. The earliest surviving account is found in the Syriac apologetic church history preserved in a manuscript at the Thrissur Metropolitan's House. This text presents the arrival as a Catholicos-sent mission: Mar Sapor, having first made a voyage to India and found the Christian community there in spiritual distress, returned to Mesopotamia to report to the Patriarch. The Patriarch then dispatched him again, this time with Mar Prodh and a retinue including bishops, priests, deacons, and settler families from Jerusalem, Baghdad, and Nineveh. According to this account, the initiative came from the Church of the East's central administration — a deliberate mission to rescue and reorganise a distant community.

A second stream of tradition, preserved in Portuguese-era transcriptions of documents now lost — most importantly, a text attributed to Archbishop Francisco Roz (1603–1604), the first European bishop of the Syrian Christians, now in the British Library — connects the settlement more specifically to the founding of a church and a trading quarter at Quilon, with royal patronage from the Venad ruler. A third stream, found in the community's own oral traditions and folk songs recorded in the early modern period, links Mar Sapor to the broader Thomas of Kana migration tradition, in which a wealthy Persian merchant led a group of settler families to Kerala and was granted land and privileges by the local king.

The relationship between these traditions is debated, and the dates they assign range from the fourth century to the ninth. What they share is a common narrative template: a merchant-leader from Persia arrives by sea, establishes a church and a commercial settlement, and receives formal recognition from the local ruler. This is the same apostle-king-patronage pattern identified in Chapter 2 as the standard template for Christian foundation narratives across the Eastern Mediterranean and beyond. The critical question is not whether the template is conventional — it is — but whether the historical kernel within it can be dated. The most secure date is the one inscribed on the plates themselves: 849 CE, when the privileges were formally conferred.\[EN18]

The migration itself is best understood in the context of the Abbasid commercial expansion. The shift of the caliphal capital to Baghdad (762 CE) had intensified Persian Gulf trade, and Christian merchants who had operated in Sasanian territories moved toward the western Indian coast, where earlier commercial contacts provided a foundation. They carried with them extensive mercantile networks that had developed over centuries, and they deployed these connections to activate trade through the Tharisapalli at Kollam.

The date of 823 for the initial arrival has been subject to scholarly revision. The Malayalam era beginning in 825 commemorates the founding of the harbour city of Quilon as an act of Cēra political strategy, not the personal achievement of Mar Sapor. One reading of the seventeenth-century Portuguese accounts suggests that the founding and building of the Tharisapalli is posterior to 825 and should be dated to the 840s. The privileges themselves were conferred in 849 — some twenty-six years after the reported arrival — suggesting that the local ruler granted them not at first sight but having tested the worth and utility of the recipients in the process of wealth mobilisation.\[EN19]

### The *Paḷḷi*: An Institutional Category

The name Tharisapalli itself reveals how the community was classified within the south Indian political system. The word *paḷḷi* in Malayalam does not mean 'church.' It means 'settlement' or 'non-Brahmanical sanctuary' — the institutional category that the Brahmanical order used for all religious establishments outside its own framework. Buddhist sites were *paḷḷi*. Jain sites were *paḷḷi*. Christian sites were *paḷḷi*. The term is distinct from *kōvil* or *ambalam*, which denote Hindu temples and shrines. To call a place of worship a *paḷḷi* was to mark it as institutionally recognised but categorically non-Hindu — belonging to the same broad class as every other non-Brahmanical religious foundation in the Kerala polity.

Tharisapalli is a composite: *tarsā*, the Early New Persian word meaning 'God-fearer' (the standard Persian designation for a Christian), combined with *paḷḷi*. It means, literally, 'Christian sanctuary-settlement.' The grant recorded on the plates is not merely for a place of worship — it is for the foundation of a settlement, or emporium (*nagaram*), situated in the seaport, *around* the sanctuary called Tharisapalli. The *paḷḷi* is the institutional anchor; the merchant community organised through the guilds is the commercial body attached to it. The merchant guilds were incorporated *through* the *paḷḷi*, not alongside it — just as, in the Brahmanical system, the *sabhā* (the corporate body of Brahmin landholders) was organised around and through the temple of the *brahmadēya*. The *paḷḷi*-nagaram structure was the non-Brahmanical equivalent of the *kōvil*-brahmadēya structure: the same political technology of incorporation, applied to a different religious category.\[EN20]

### The Tharisapalli Plates (849 CE)

The grant itself — issued by Ayyan Atikal Tiruvatikal, the sub-ruler of the Venad region under the Cēra king Sthanu Ravi Varma — is the earliest extant, securely dated, physically surviving documentary evidence for the Indian Christian community with royal recognition. It is inscribed in Tamil-Grantha script, the standard official script of the Kerala polity, and witnessed in four scripts and four languages: Tamil-Grantha, Hebrew, Pahlavi (Middle Persian), and Kufic Arabic. These four scripts represent four distinct merchant communities present in Quilon in 849 CE. A forged document cannot produce four independently authenticated scripts from four communities with no common interest in fabrication. The cross-cultural witness signatures are the plates' own authentication.\[EN21]

The privileges conferred are extensive and specific. The church was given custody of the weights and measures of the city of Kollam — the *parakkoṭi*, *pañcakaṇṭi*, and *kappan* — which the Christians held until 1503, when the Portuguese took them away. This custodianship was not a religious honour; it was an economic function: a corporate body of traders entrusted with ensuring the standardisation of commercial instruments across the port. The community was also given the right to collect tolls on carts carrying merchandise into the market (*vayiṉam*) and on boats carrying cargo to the port (*veṭiyilum*), and received the assignment of several service families together with rights to collect specified taxes previously levied by the ruler himself. The community received, in addition, social status markers — the right to use palanquins, drums, parasols — and judicial autonomy to adjudicate internal disputes. These were not acts of exceptional generosity toward a favoured group. They were the standard instrument through which the polity managed its relationship with every commercially significant community.\[EN22]

### The Merchant Guilds

The plates name three merchant guilds as guarantors and protectors of the community's privileges: the **Manigramam**, the **Añcuvaṇṇam**, and the **Aruṉūṟṟuvar**. These were not local curiosities. They were pan-Tamilakam commercial organisations that operated across south India, Sri Lanka, and Southeast Asia.

The Manigramam appears in inscriptions from across the Cōḻa territories, where its members were predominantly Hindu. In Kerala, the guild's ethnic composition was different: by the ninth century, the Kerala Manigramam had evolved into a powerful commercial institution operating among Christian merchants for long-distance Indian Ocean trade, particularly between Kerala and the economic zones of the Persian Gulf, the Red Sea, and the Levant. The same guild name carried different communal content in different regions — a fact that underscores the system's character as a political and commercial framework rather than a confessional one.

The Añcuvaṇṇam was organised by Jewish, Christian, and Islamic merchants from West Asian countries. The Aruṉūṟṟuvar ('the Five Hundred,' later absorbed into the Ainūrruvar umbrella organisation from the twelfth century) was a mobile merchant body with an even wider geographic range, appearing in inscriptions from Karnataka to Sri Lanka to Southeast Asia. Statistical analysis of merchant guild inscriptions shows their distribution across the entire south Indian world from the ninth through the sixteenth century, peaking in the twelfth and thirteenth centuries. The Thomas Christian community's commercial life was embedded in this pan-Tamilakam network — not in a local arrangement peculiar to the western coast.\[EN23]

The Manigramam and Añcuvaṇṇam jointly held the right to impose customs at Quilon and were entrusted with the judicial authority to adjudicate disputes and protect the community's privileges from encroachment. The merchant guilds functioned as agents of governance, not merely as trade associations. The political logic behind the grants was fiscal: the ruler depended on maritime trade for wealth, the merchants conducted the trade, and the grants were the contractual instrument through which the ruler secured their activity by offering legal security, social status, and institutional autonomy in exchange.

### Sulaiman al-Tajir (c. 851 CE)

Two years after the plates were issued, an independent external witness confirmed the world they describe. The Persian merchant-geographer Sulaiman al-Tajir records in his *Silsilat al-Tawarikh* that Quilon was the sole Indian port used by large Chinese ships as their transshipment hub for the Persian Gulf trade. His account — the earliest surviving Arab description of the Indian Ocean commercial world — establishes Quilon as a cosmopolitan port at exactly the moment the grants were formalising the Christian community's institutional position within it. Sulaiman is not describing Christians specifically; he is describing commercial geography. But the port economy he witnesses is the same one that the Tharisapalli plates are embedding the community within.\[EN24]

## 3.4 — THE TOMB AT THE EDGE OF THE WORLD (883 CE)

In 883 CE, the Anglo-Saxon Chronicles record that King Alfred the Great dispatched envoys with votive offerings to the tomb of St. Thomas in 'India.'

Alfred's kingdom of Wessex had barely survived the Viking invasions. The Treaty of Wedmore (878) had secured a fragile peace with the Danes only five years earlier. England was partitioned, its monasteries sacked, its libraries burned. The great centres of Anglo-Saxon learning — Lindisfarne, Jarrow, Iona — had been destroyed or abandoned. And in this setting — a kingdom at the edge of the known world, recovering from catastrophe — a ruling king considered it meaningful to send material offerings to an apostolic shrine understood as being somewhere in the distant East.

Where Alfred's information came from, we cannot determine. The Alexandrian vector that had once carried reports from merchants like Cosmas was dead — killed by the Islamic conquest of Egypt two and a half centuries earlier. The Latin literary tradition preserved the memory of Thomas in India, but as hagiographic tradition, not current intelligence. Charlemagne's diplomatic contacts with the Abbasid court had not, as far as we know, carried information about Indian Christians to western Europe. Alfred himself was a noted scholar who sought out knowledge from every available source, commissioning translations of Orosius's world history, Boethius's *Consolation of Philosophy*, and portions of Augustine's *Soliloquies*. His court attracted scholars from across the surviving Christian world — the Welshman Asser, the Frankish Grimbald, the Old Saxon John. Whether the information about the Indian tomb reached him through a surviving thread of the old pilgrimage networks, through contact with eastern Christians in the Holy Land, or through some channel we cannot trace, the entry testifies to something that the documentary silence might otherwise obscure: the tradition of the apostle Thomas in India had not died. It had reached the farthest surviving outpost of the old Roman Christian world — a kingdom that had itself nearly perished.

Whether Alfred's envoys reached the destination, or some intermediate Christian community, or were turned back by the immense distances and the hostile geography, the Chronicles do not say. What we know is that they certainly could not have made such a journey if they had waited much longer. In 1037, the Seljuk Turks under Tughril Beg consolidated power in Persia and Mesopotamia, severing what remained of the overland and maritime channels that connected western Christendom to the eastern Christian world. The already weakened routes through which Patriarch Timothy I had administered his Indian diocese, through which the Tharisapalli plates' Persian and Arabic witnesses had arrived in Quilon, through which a thread of information about the Indian tomb had somehow reached Anglo-Saxon England — all of these would be cut or attenuated within a century and a half of Alfred's embassy.

The window was closing. Alfred's envoys, if they passed through it, were among the last who could.

\---

The tradition of the apostle Thomas in India had now traversed the full arc of the old Christian world — from Edessa to Alexandria, from Alexandria to Rome, from Rome to the courts of Merovingian Gaul and Visigothic Spain, and finally to a Saxon kingdom recovering from Viking devastation at the western edge of Europe. It had been carried, at each stage, by information systems that were collapsing behind it: the Alexandrian maritime route, the Persian Gulf corridor, the Latin hagiographic tradition, the fragmentary channels through which a king in Wessex could learn that an apostle's tomb existed somewhere in the East. What persisted in India itself — the shrine at Calamina, the community on the western coast now formally embedded in the Cēra polity through the merchant guild system — had entered a new documentary phase: locally attested, commercially significant, institutionally recognised. But the connection between this Indian reality and the Latin Christian world that dimly remembered it was about to be severed more completely than ever before.

What happened next — the Seljuk disruption, the Crusades, the legend of Prester John, the Mongol catastrophe and its paradoxical reopening of routes, the witnesses who converged on the Indian coast, and the Portuguese voyage that would transform everything — is the subject of the next chapter.

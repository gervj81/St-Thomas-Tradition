THE NEW INSTRUMENT

Jesuits, Schism, and the Bishop Hunt — 1542–1597

"There is a bishop from Armenia+ named Abuna Jacob, who for forty-five years has been serving God and your highness in these parts, a very old man, virtuous and holy and at the same time disfavoured by your highness and almost by all here in India."

— Francis Xavier to King João III, 26 January 1549

9.1 — THE JESUIT ARRIVAL

Francis Xavier stepped off the ship at Goa on 6 May 1542. He was thirty-six years old, a Navarrese nobleman, one of the original companions of Ignatius of Loyola, and the first Jesuit to set foot in Asia. He carried with him the mandate of the Society of Jesus and the personal backing of King João III, who had requested missionaries capable of disciplining an empire marked by deep internal disorder.

What Xavier found in Goa was not an imperial capital in command of its domains but a city in moral chaos. The casados kept concubines by the dozen. The secular clergy were few and frequently corrupt. The Confraternity of the Holy Faith had been founded in 1541 to promote conversions, and its seminary, the College of St. Paul, was training a handful of Indian boys for ordination, funded in part by the revenues of recently demolished Hindu temples. The college would become the intellectual nerve centre of the Jesuit enterprise in Asia, and Xavier saw its potential immediately. He wrote to his brethren that within six years, thirty students of various languages, races, and nations would go out from it qualified to increase the number of Christians greatly. 1

But Xavier had not come to be the parish priest of Goa. Reports had been reaching the capital from the extreme south of India, from the Fishery Coast, where an entire community of pearl-fishing Paravas had accepted mass baptism in the 1530s in exchange for Portuguese naval protection against Muslim competitors. The priests sent to tend them had found the climate unbearable and left. When Xavier arrived among them in October 1542, the Paravas knew they had ceased to be Hindus and bore Portuguese Christian names and little more. Very few could recite even a single Christian prayer. No churches had been built. Children born since the mass baptism had not been baptised. 2

Xavier threw himself into the work with a physical intensity that would kill him within a decade. He went from village to village, baptising, catechising, and organising. His method was blunt: he translated prayers into Tamil phonetically (he never truly learned the language), appointed local caste headmen (pattangattis) as moral police, and threatened them with chains and imprisonment in Cochin if they failed to stamp out idol worship. Catholic theology permits the legitimate use of limited corrective force over baptised members of the community, analogous to a parent's authority over a child. But conversion at this stage was primarily political allegiance enforced by Portuguese naval power, not interior transformation. Xavier faced an uphill task compounded by the fact that he was an outsider, ignorant of the Paravas' customs, language, and social world. 3

The limitations were obvious. Without books, without priests who spoke the language, without any engagement with the intellectual world of the converts, the Parava Christians were Christians in name and Portuguese political clients in practice. Xavier recognised this. In a letter to Ignatius from Cochin in 1549, he described the College of Santiago at Kodungallur, where the Franciscan Vicente maintained about a hundred students, 'sons of those, that descend from the Christians, whom St. Thomas converted.' Xavier noted that these Thomas Christians had 'very good churches' in some sixty places around Kodungallur, and that Vicente was 'a very great friend of mine.' The Jesuits and the Franciscans were still cooperating. The inter-order rivalry had not yet begun. 4

The Linguistic Turn

The man who began to change the Jesuit method was Henrique Henriques, a Portuguese Jesuit who arrived on the Fishery Coast in 1549 to continue Xavier's work. Henriques did what Xavier had not: he learned Tamil. He wrote the first European grammar of the language, compiled a dictionary, and translated a catechism, a confession manual, and a compendium of Christian doctrine into Tamil for use in the coastal churches. By 1552, most of the thirty churches along the Fishery Coast had copies of his Short Compendium in Malabar Language concerning the Creation of the World, Angels, Men, Hell, Heaven, Sin, and Grace. In 1567, Henriques founded a Tamil college at Punnaikkayal where music, Portuguese, Tamil, and Latin were taught to Parava youths. It was a long way from Xavier's reliance on rote memorisation. 5

The shift was profound. Henriques demonstrated that effective conversion required entering the linguistic and conceptual world of the people being converted, engaging the free will of the individual as it was moved by divine grace rather than by the threat of the Portuguese fleet. Prayers memorised in a language the converts did not understand produced compliance; doctrine explained in the mother tongue produced comprehension. This insight, born on the Fishery Coast, would travel north to the Serra and reshape the Jesuit approach to the Thomas Christians. When the seminary at Vaipicota was founded in 1568, and when Francis Roz added Syriac to its curriculum in 1584, they were applying Henriques's principle to a far more complex situation: a community that already possessed a twelve-century liturgical tradition in a language the Jesuits needed to learn, not teach.

9.2 — THE OLD BISHOP AND THE NEW APOSTLE

In January 1549, Xavier visited Cochin. There, in the Franciscan monastery of St. Antony, he met the man who had been governing the Thomas Christians for nearly half a century.

Mar Jacob was very old. He had arrived from Gazarta in 1503, full of optimism about 'our brethren the Franks,' and had spent forty-five years navigating the impossible space between cooperation and submission. Now he was living as a permanent guest of the friars who had cared for him when no one else would. 6

Xavier's letter to King João III, written from Cochin on 26 January 1549, is one of the most revealing documents of the period. He describes Mar Jacob not as a heretic or a rival but as 'a very old man, virtuous and holy and at the same time disfavoured by your highness and almost by all here in India.' He asks the king to write Mar Jacob a letter full of love, and to recommend him to the governor and captains of Cochin, 'that they give him the honour and reception which he deserves.' The charity of the Franciscans, Xavier adds, will supply abundantly whatever the bishop wants. 'But your highness needs more to be favoured by the bishop in his prayers than the bishop needs the temporal favour of your highness.' 7

The letter is remarkable for what it does not say. Xavier does not accuse Mar Jacob of heresy. He does not demand his replacement. He does not invoke the Padroado. He treats the old Chaldean bishop with a respect that the Portuguese secular officials had long since ceased to show. This was a moment of genuine cross-tradition courtesy between the institutional Church of the West and the Thomas Christian leadership.

Mar Jacob died around 1550-52. On his deathbed, he asked his friend Pero Sequeira to redeem a copper-plate grant recording the privileges of the Thomas Christians, which he had pawned to a man in the interior for twenty cruzados. Before he died, he had the happiness of knowing the plates had been recovered. 8

Xavier died on 3 December 1552, on the island of Shangchuan off the coast of China, waiting for a ship that would never come. He was forty-six years old. He had worn out his body crossing the ocean three times, baptising tens of thousands, and dreaming of the closed empire of China that lay just beyond the horizon.

The two deaths, around the same period, closed an era.

9.3 — THE SCHISM

In 1552, the year Xavier died, the Church of the East fractured.

For centuries, the patriarchate had maintained a hereditary succession system called Natar Kursya (Guardian of the Throne), introduced during the Timurid devastation to ensure institutional survival (Chapter 5). By the mid-sixteenth century, the system had produced a succession of patriarchs from a single family, concentrating power in ways that alienated significant portions of the clergy and laity. In 1552, a faction of discontented bishops elected Yohannan Sulaqa as a rival patriarch. Sulaqa travelled to Rome, professed obedience to the Pope, and was confirmed by Julius III as Patriarch of the Chaldeans in 1553. He returned to Mesopotamia and was murdered by agents of the rival patriarch in 1555. 9

The schism created two patriarchal lines: one in communion with Rome (the Chaldean Catholic Church, the Sulaqa line), and one maintaining the ancient independence of the Church of the East. The theological differences between the two lines were minimal. The political consequences for the Thomas Christians were catastrophic.

Every bishop sent to India now arrived with contested credentials. A bishop consecrated by the independent patriarch was, in Portuguese eyes, a 'Nestorian heretic' who must be arrested and deported. A bishop consecrated by the pro-Roman patriarch was, in theory, a legitimate Catholic prelate, but one whose very legitimacy threatened the Padroado's jurisdictional monopoly. If the Thomas Christians possessed a canonically valid bishop appointed by a patriarch in communion with Rome, then the archbishop of Goa had no grounds to claim authority over them. The Portuguese position effectively needed the Nazranis to be either heretics (who could be disciplined) or orphans (whose care devolved upon the nearest Latin bishop). A legitimate Chaldean Catholic bishop fit neither category and thus undermined the entire framework. The issue was less theology than jurisdiction. 10

9.4 — THE BISHOP HUNT

Mar Jacob was barely dead when the community moved. An uncle of the man who would become Archdeacon George of Christ immediately petitioned the Chaldean Patriarch for a new bishop. (How and when George of Christ became archdeacon remains unclear. He appears only in scattered references in Portuguese records. The archdeacon's connection to the Pakalomattam lineage is attested in later sources but cannot be independently verified for this early period; what is reasonably clear is that the office tended to be hereditary and was exercised within a dominant family lineage.) Both patriarchs responded. The community had asked for one bishop. It was about to receive two. 11

Two Bishops, One Serra

In late 1556, the pro-Roman patriarch Ebed Jesu's delegation arrived in Goa: Mar Joseph (brother of the martyred Sulaqa), Mar Elias (archbishop of Diarbekir), and the Maltese Dominican Ambrose Buttigeg, who served as papal nuncio. Their credentials were perfectly in order. It made no difference. The Portuguese detained all three at a Franciscan monastery in Bassein for eighteen months, the ecclesiastical authorities having already determined that no Chaldean bishops, whatever their professions, were to be admitted to the Serra. 12

Meanwhile, the independent patriarch had sent his own man. Mar Abraham arrived in Malabar in 1557, disguised as a beggar, slipping past the Portuguese blockade. He was received with joy by the Thomas Christians and settled in the Serra. 13

When Mar Joseph and Mar Elias were finally released in 1558, the situation became extraordinary. Buttigeg died in Cochin shortly after arrival. Mar Elias returned to his archbishopric in Mesopotamia. Joseph reached the Serra. Now two rival bishops were governing the same community simultaneously, each claiming legitimate authority, each attempting to evade the Portuguese, and each representing a different patriarchal line of a church that had split only five years earlier.

The community fractured geographically. Mar Abraham, fearing capture by the Portuguese, abandoned the northern strongholds and retreated deep into the southern territories, settling first at Kaduthuruthy and then at Kottayam, placing himself under the protection of the Hindu king of Thekkumkur. Mar Joseph probably remained in the northern regions, working alongside the traditional archdeacon, George of Christ. This geographical division may have mapped onto the pre-existing Vadakkumbhagakar and Thekkumbhagakar community structure (this structure will be discussed in detail in a later chapter), though the evidence for this alignment is indirect. 14

This fracture is theorised to have extended to the archdeaconate itself. Chaldean canon law required each bishop to have his own archdeacon. Abraham, no longer able to rely on George of Christ in the north, might have ordained a new archdeacon in the south to govern his loyalists in the Kaduthuruthy–Kottayam region. Sources suggest that at least two archdeaconates existed simultaneously during this period, though precise numbers remain uncertain. The single hereditary office that had governed the community's internal affairs for centuries had briefly fragmented into several jurisdictions. 15

The Arrests and the Escape

In 1558, the Portuguese arrested Mar Abraham and placed him on a ship bound for Lisbon. But en route, at Mozambique, Abraham escaped. He made his way to Mesopotamia, presented himself to the pro-Roman patriarch Ebed Jesu, was re-consecrated as metropolitan, and then proceeded to Rome. Pius IV, noting that Abraham's previous orders had been conferred by the independent patriarch and were therefore canonically doubtful, arranged for all his orders to be quietly re-conferred. Mar Abraham was now, for the third time, a mitred bishop, this time with papal approval. In 1565, the Pope confirmed him as Archbishop of Angamaly and ordered that the Malabar diocese be divided between Abraham and Joseph. 16

Back in the Serra, Mar Joseph had been introducing auricular confession, confirmation, and extreme unction among the Thomas Christians: the very sacramental practices whose absence Alvaro Penteado had complained of forty years earlier. In 1562, the Bishop of Cochin arrested him on charges of 'Nestorian' heresy and deported him to Lisbon. Joseph proved his orthodoxy to the queen and the Inquisitor General, received a papal brief from Pius IV, and returned to Malabar in 1565. His freedom lasted barely two years. The First Provincial Council of Goa (1567) took up his case, concluded that all charges were proved, and deported him for the third and final time. 17

In 1568, Mar Abraham arrived back in Goa, armed with Pope Pius IV's letter to the archbishop of Goa and to the bishop of Cochin recommending his appointment as archbishop of Angamaly. But the Viceroy and the Archbishop of Goa detained him in a Dominican monastery. Abraham escaped again, this time on the night of Holy Thursday, fleeing overland to the safety of the Serra in disguise. The Portuguese bitterly accused him of obtaining his credentials through 'dissimulating ways and dissembling humility.' 18

While Abraham reached Serra, Mar Joseph was shipped to Rome for the last time. He proved his innocence yet again to the satisfaction of the Roman authorities. He was about to return to India when death relieved the Padroado of his unwelcome presence. He died in Rome in 1569. His eighteen Syriac manuscripts, which he had carried with him across three continents, were incorporated into the Vatican Library. One of them would be later identified as Vatican Syriac MS 22 (Chapter 4).19

The pattern was now established: arrive, minister, be arrested, be deported, appeal, be vindicated, attempt to return, be blocked. The Thomas Christians were learning that the Portuguese naval monopoly over the Indian Ocean was simultaneously a blockade against incoming East Syrian bishops. The same cartaz system that controlled the pepper trade controlled the supply of prelates.

The Inquisition Arrives in Goa

In 1546, writing from the distant island of Amboina in the Moluccas—an Indonesian archipelago known historically as the “Spice Islands”—Xavier addressed King João III with a request for the establishment of the Holy Office of the Inquisition in Goa. He was motivated primarily by theological concerns, appalled by the profound religious ignorance in which the Portuguese casados left their Indian families. Furthermore, he observed that many baptized people in the Portuguese fortresses were openly living "according to the Jewish law, and according to the Mahomedan sect, without any fear of God or shame of the world." He sought an internal disciplinary tribunal to police his own countrymen. He died in 1552 without seeing what the Portuguese state would ultimately build from that request. 20

The Goa Inquisition was formally established in 1560, in the midst of the bishop hunt. While the institution of the Inquisition itself was originally established in the thirteenth century as a strictly theological tribunal for the correction of the baptized, its scope and nature had mutated significantly by the sixteenth century.

In the post-Reconquista Iberian world, the demographic and political landscape had been transformed. Jews and Muslims who chose to remain found conversion to Christianity preferable to being subjected to severe political pressure or fleeing to another land. Consequently, Spain and Portugal developed significant populations of conversos (Jewish converts) and moriscos (Muslim converts), many of whom secretly retained their former religious beliefs. By the middle of the fifteenth century, driven by complex political and economic anxieties, these converts were not merely suspected of private heresy; they were legally and socially categorized as "New Christians." Under this framework, they were strictly segregated from "Old Christians"—individuals who claimed lineages entirely free of Jewish or Muslim blood.

The formal categorization of this "New Christian" class was cemented by state mandates, beginning with the Spanish Alhambra Decree of 1492 and followed by King Manuel I’s 1497 edicts in Portugal—issued barely a year before Vasco da Gama landed in Malabar. These mandates forced the mass conversion of the Jewish population, a policy driven largely by the Crown's desire to retain their vital economic capital and mercantile expertise within the kingdom. This systemic division into cristão-novo (New Christian) and cristão-velho (Old Christian) reflected the obsession with religious purity that had driven the Iberian Inquisitions since the late fifteenth century.

Goa and Cochin hosted significant communities of Jewish merchants, possibly including families who had fled the Iberian Inquisitions. The Portuguese utilized the Holy Office of Inquisition against cristão-novo Jewish merchants and wealthy casado households whose Indian wives, baptised upon marriage, and children were cristãos da terra (Christians of the land). Conviction of secret heterodox practice and confiscation of properties, in some cases, required no more than the testimony of two witnesses.

Limpeza de sangue

The Iberian concept of "purity of blood" (limpeza de sangue in Portuguese, limpieza de sangre in Spanish) sparked a profound conflict between the universalizing theology of Catholic baptism and the racialized, exclusionary statecraft of the Spanish and Portuguese empires.

Christian Doctrine & Critics

  • The Power of Baptism: The statutes of blood purity were based on the belief that the body's fluids, and especially blood, transmitted a certain number of moral qualities from parents to children and that Jews, as a people, were incapable of change despite their baptism. In the view of critics, including Ignatius of Loyola and Pope Nicholas V, this bordered on heresy, since it appeared to deny the sacramental efficacy of baptism. According to orthodox doctrine, the waters of baptism washed away all past sins and identities, making all converts spiritually equal members of the Body of Christ.
  • Ignatius of Loyola: The founder of the Jesuits fiercely opposed purity statutes, insisting on inclusivity and unity. Ignatius famously declared that he would consider it a "special grace of Our Lord" to have Jewish ancestry, as it would mean sharing the same bloodline as Jesus Christ and the Virgin Mary. 20b
  • Papal Condemnation: The concept faced initial pushback from the highest levels of the Church. Almost immediately following the anti-converso rebellions in Toledo, Pope Nicholas V issued Humani generis inimicus in 1449 strictly condemning the statute and threatening excommunication against anyone who discriminated against baptized converts. The failure of Nicholas V’s bull was not an isolated incident, but part of a deeply entrenched Iberian legal tradition: Obedezco pero no cumplo ("I obey but I do not comply"), as seen previously in Chapter 7 regarding the fate of the anti-chattel slavery bulls Sublimis Deus (1537) and Commissum Nobis (1639).20c

Iberian Institutionalization

  • The Statutes of Purity: Originating in Spain's Toledo in 1449, the Estatutos de Limpieza de Sangre were legal codes demanding proof of "Old Christian" ancestry—meaning a lineage entirely free of Jewish (converso) or Muslim (morisco) blood—to access universities, guilds, military orders, and public offices.
  • The State Inquisition: The Spanish and Portuguese Inquisitions used these statutes, shifting their focus from purely policing theological heresy to enforcing ethnic identity. They utilized rigorous genealogical investigations to ensure individuals with "impure" blood were structurally marginalized.
  • The Renaissance Popes' Compromise: Lacking moral authority and deeply dependent on Iberian political and military support, some of the subsequent popes after Nicholas V, ratified the exclusionary statutes.
  • The Capitulation of the Religious Orders: Despite intense internal theological criticism, the major religious orders eventually bowed to state political pressure and incorporated purity requirements into their Iberian branches. The Franciscans and Dominicans adopted exclusionary rules after a period of resistance. Even the Jesuits—whose founder had fiercely defended inclusivity—finally buckled under the threats of the united Iberian Crown under King Philip II (1593). 20d
  • The Portuguese India: The Portuguese actively transported this obsession with blood purity into their overseas empire. Very few indigenous Indians or mestiços (people of mixed descent) were permitted to join Catholic religious orders before the eighteenth century.

The institution rapidly expanded its jurisdiction to native Goan converts suspected of secretly maintaining Hindu practices. Traditional food, dress, language, and song were codified as evidence of apostasy. By the time the Council of Trent (1545–1563) promulgated its final documents, the original instrument of theological order had come to function as an instrument of cultural suppression in Portuguese Goa.

The pressures generated by inquisitorial procedures, including property confiscations and the criminalisation of indigenous customs, produced two parallel effects: an increase in the number of new converts and, more significantly, a mass exodus of merchants and working people from Goan territories. This outmigration placed a severe financial strain on the Estado da Índia that its own officials had foreseen but were unable to prevent. 21

The Goa Inquisition, much like its larger Iberian predecessors, left a trail of trauma that was rapidly weaponized and polemically exaggerated by Protestant opponents. The Dutch, and later the British, would strategically position themselves as pragmatic merchants operating for mutual benefit, framing their own imperial expansions as the liberation of the Indian people from Portuguese religious tyranny.21a

▣ THE EVOLUTION OF THE INQUISITION: FROM PAPAL TRIBUNAL TO STATE APPARATUS

The Papal and Roman Models: The original Papal Inquisition, instituted around 1231 by Pope Gregory IX, functioned as a strictly theological tribunal administered directly by the Church. It was initially established to combat the Cathar (Albigensian) movement in southern France. Rather than a centralized bureaucracy, it operated on a distributed model: the Pope dispatched itinerant inquisitors—drawn almost exclusively from the newly founded Dominican and Franciscan orders due to their rigorous theological training—to root out local heresies while remaining ultimately answerable to Rome.

This medieval structure was formally centralized in 1542 when Pope Paul III established the Supreme Sacred Congregation of the Roman and Universal Inquisition (often called the Holy Office) to combat the Protestant Reformation. While this Roman Inquisition was highly structured, it remained under direct papal control and its actual enforcement jurisdiction was largely restricted to the Italian peninsula. Unlike the state-controlled Iberian tribunals, this Roman institution survived into the modern era through a series of reforms: Pope Paul VI reorganized it in 1965 as the Congregation for the Doctrine of the Faith (CDF), and Pope Francis most recently renamed it the Dicastery for the Doctrine of the Faith (DDF) in 2022.

The Iberian State Model: The Spanish Inquisition was formally established on November 1, 1478, when Pope Sixtus IV issued the papal bull Exigit Sincerae Devotionis Affectus. This granted the Catholic Monarchs, Ferdinand II of Aragon and Isabella I of Castile, the extraordinary authority to appoint and dismiss inquisitors in their domains. After years of intense diplomatic lobbying and pushback between King João III and the Vatican, Pope Paul III formally established the Portuguese Inquisition on May 23, 1536. These papal concessions fundamentally altered the traditional inquisitorial framework. The Iberian Inquisitions operated autonomously from Rome, transforming the tribunals into administrative instruments of the state. Furthermore, rather than confronting theological heresies, their primary focus was the eradication of crypto-Judaism among conversos—a calculated effort to enforce socio-political uniformity within a uniquely multi-ethnic Iberian peninsula, shaped by centuries of Islamic rule and the long Reconquista.

Global Expansion and Regional Variations: The scope and mechanics of inquisitions varied wildly across Europe and its empires. The Iberian powers actively exported their state-controlled inquisitions overseas. Spain established permanent tribunals in the Americas (Lima, Mexico City, Cartagena), while Portugal utilized periodic, wide-ranging inquisitorial "visitations" in territories like Brazil.

The Reflection in Goa: The Goa Inquisition represented the ultimate overseas extension of this Portuguese state model. It expanded its scope to aggressively police the social customs of native Indian converts. Utilizing the auto-da-fé—highly choreographed public ceremonies of sentencing—as a mechanism to project state power, it enforced cultural and administrative standardization across the colonial territory, maintaining a reputation for severity until its suppression and eventual abolition between 1774 and 1812.

The Thomas Christians watched from across the Western Ghats. They probably knew what was happening in Goa. And when, in the 1560s, the first Jesuit attempted to penetrate the interior of the Serra, the community's response demonstrated an understanding of what was at stake.

Carneiro's Expedition

Fr. Melchior Carneiro was in Cochin when he received intelligence that a 'heretical bishop' was at work in the remoter parts of the Serra. He set out to find him, travelling through the 'pepper kingdom' of Vadakkenkur and into the neighbourhood of Angamaly. He was welcomed by one cattanar who had adhered to Roman ways and was received eagerly by the common people, who had never before seen a missionary in their midst. But he was unsuccessful in locating the bishop. In one church he tried to enter, the cattanar in charge barred his way. Before reaching Angamaly, he was warned not to proceed further: two thousand Christians had declared themselves amoces of the bishop, desperadoes prepared to sell their own lives in his defence. 22

The amoces were the Portuguese rendering of the chaverpada, the suicide squads that the archdeacon could mobilise from the kalari-trained Christian warriors. Their appearance in the 1560s, decades before the Coonan Cross, confirms what the institutional analysis of earlier chapters suggested: the community's capacity for armed resistance was not improvised in extremis. It was a standing capability, activated whenever the archdeaconate perceived a threat to its autonomy.

Carneiro's expedition failed as a bishop hunt. It succeeded as an intelligence operation. He brought back to Goa the first systematic report on the interior of the Serra: its geography, its parish structure, its martial capabilities, and its fierce attachment to the Chaldean hierarchy. The lesson was clear. The Serra could not be taken by force or by freelance priests. It required an institution capable of sustained, patient, intellectual engagement. It required a seminary.

Abraham Alone

With Joseph dead and his manuscripts in the Vatican Library, Mar Abraham emerged as the sole metropolitan. He established his permanent headquarters at Angamaly, deep in the interior, where the Thomas Christian population was densest and the protection of local rulers and the archdeacon's chaverpada could be relied upon. The move from Kodungallur to the interior was itself a declaration: the community's centre of gravity was retreating beyond Portuguese reach. The fragmented archdeaconate reunified under George of Christ as vicar general. 23

9.5 — VAIPICOTA: THE LONG GAME

While the Portuguese Crown and the Padroado pursued forced conformity to their political and economic advantage, the Jesuits were beginning to recognise its futility for the conversion of souls. They began to privilege 'interior conversion': the exercise of free will moved directly by divine grace, not by the threat of the secular arm.

To achieve this without relying on state coercion, the Jesuits developed a strategy of aesthetic persuasion. In Goa, they staged spectacular, awe-inspiring ceremonies: solemn mass baptisms, elaborate religious processions, and liturgical celebrations of unprecedented magnificence. By deploying Baroque theatricality complete with music, pageantry, and opulent church architecture, they sought to captivate the imagination and emotions of the local population. The goal was to make Christianity socially and emotionally compelling, prompting individuals and groups to seek instruction and baptism voluntarily after witnessing these displays. 24

But spectacle alone could not reach the interior of minds formed in Tamil, Malayalam, or Syriac. As we saw with Henriques on the Fishery Coast, the most crucial step in the transition from external conformity to genuine persuasion occurred when the Jesuits recognised that preaching in Portuguese or Latin was ineffective for deep theological engagement. The linguistic enterprise meant the Jesuits were no longer forcing converts to 'act Portuguese'; they were entering the Indian epistemic world, providing the intellectual tools necessary for genuine spiritual comprehension. By the time the Goa Inquisition was established in 1560, the Jesuits on the frontier missions had already transitioned toward a strategy of accommodation.

In 1568, the Jesuit seminary at Vaipicota was founded. Its location was strategic: the village of Vaipicota (near Chennamangalam) sat on the boundary between the Portuguese-controlled coast and the Thomas Christian interior, close enough to Kodungallur for Portuguese supply lines, but close enough to the Serra and the metropolitan seat at Angamaly to attract Thomas Christian students. It replaced the Franciscan College of Santiago, which had languished after Vicente de Lagos's death in 1552 without anyone of equal zeal to carry it on. The Jesuits had learned from Vicente's failure. His college had produced Latinised graduates whom the Thomas Christian parishes unanimously rejected. The problem was not the quality of instruction but its orientation: graduates trained exclusively in Latin theology and Latin liturgy were aliens in their own community's churches. 25

Among the teachers at the seminary was Fr. Peter Luis, a Brahmin convert from Quilon who became the first Indian member of the Society of Jesus. His presence was a quiet revolution: a Brahmin, converted and ordained, teaching in a Jesuit seminary alongside Europeans. It was exactly the kind of bridge figure the accommodation strategy required. 26

Peter Luis's Brahmin origins connected to a tradition that the Jesuits were now, for the first time, systematically recording. The Thomas Christians claim that the Apostle Thomas converted high-caste Brahmins during his mission in Kerala, and that the community's elevated social status derived from these Brahminical origins. Until the 1570s, this tradition existed almost entirely in oral form and in the community's devotional songs (pallipattu), sung in Malayalam at festivals and church services, celebrating the life, miracles, and martyrdom of the Apostle.

In 1578, the Jesuit Francisco Dionysio produced the first systematic Portuguese documentation of these traditions. He recorded what the Thomas Christians themselves told him:

What is written below is known from the information supplied by old people; it is the common and unanimous belief of all; they hold it as a well handed-down tradition; they have put these things in their books and their songs.

Dionysio's account included the specific oral traditions regarding the Apostle's founding of the ancient churches, the conversion of the Nambuthiri Brahmins, and the community's social and ecclesial structure as transmitted across generations. The following year, 1579, Fr. Antonio de Monserrate produced a parallel account, his Informacion de los Christianos de S. Thomé, drawing on the same oral and textual sources. Together, Dionysio and Monserrate represent the earliest written record of the Thomas Christians' own foundational narrative as captured by European observers. 27

These accounts would prove to be both a gift and a weapon. They preserved traditions that might otherwise have been lost in the turmoil that followed. But they also provided the Portuguese with an ethnographic map of the community's self-understanding, one that the Padroado would later use to identify precisely which customs, beliefs, and claims needed to be suppressed at the Synod of Diamper.

▣ EARLY PORTUGUESE/EUROPEAN DOCUMENTS RECORD

What the Early Portuguese/European Documents DID Record:

  • The Kodungallur/Maliankara Landing
    Documented in: Da Ásia (Decadas) by Diogo de Couto (written c. 1603–1611). Couto recorded the local tradition that St. Thomas first landed at a port called "Megodor Patana" (Maliankara/Cranganore), noting that the Apostle arrived at this Malabar port and immediately began preaching in the region.
  • Brahmin Conversions
    Documented in: Informação da Christandade de São Thomé by Francisco Dionysio, S.J. (1578) and Informacion de los Christianos de S. Thomé by Antonio de Monserrate, S.J. (1579). Both of these Jesuit scholars recorded the deep-rooted local assertion that the St. Thomas Christians were the direct descendants of high-caste nobles and Brahmins baptized by the Apostle himself.
  • Building of Several Churches
    Documented in: Informação da Christandade de São Thomé by Francisco Dionysio, S.J. (1578). Dionysio noted that the local Christians held a tradition—preserved in their old songs and books—that the Apostle established "several" churches across the coast to serve the newly converted communities.
  • The Mylapore Tomb
    Documented in: Livro de Duarte Barbosa (Book of Duarte Barbosa) by Duarte Barbosa (c. 1516). Barbosa provided one of the earliest Portuguese descriptions of the ruined church at Mylapore where the Apostle was believed to be buried, detailing the deep reverence both Christians and non-Christians held for the site.
  • Martyrdom by Brahmins/Priests
    Documented in: Lendas da Índia by Gaspar Correia (written c. 1550s). Correia recounted the widely held local belief surrounding the Apostle's death, writing that St. Thomas was pierced with a lance by hostile "Pagan priests" (Brahmins by inference) whose religious establishment in Mylapore was threatened by his evangelization.

What the Early Portuguese/European Documents DID NOT Record:

  • No AD 50/52 Landing Date: Early European chroniclers did not record the highly specific arrival dates of AD 50 or AD 52. These exact dates belong to post-Diamper writings of Francisco Roz and later formalisation and documentation of internal oral traditions (such as the Ramban Pattu), rather than early Portuguese observations.
  • No Concrete List of the "Seven Churches": While they noted the building of "several" churches, they failed to document the rigid, specific list of the Seven Churches (Ezharappallikal) at Kodungallur, Quilon, Niranam, etc., which is a hallmark of later documentation.
  • No Specific Elite Family Names: Although the conversion of Brahmins was widely documented, these early Portuguese ethnographers did not record the specific identifying titles of those elite families (such as the Pakalomattam, Sankarapuri, Kalli, or Kaliyankal).

The seminary attracted students by the excellence of its discipline and instruction. But for its first fifteen years, the curriculum remained essentially Latin, and the results were predictable. Mar Abraham refused to ordain the Vaipicota students, presumably because they had been too deeply Latinised to be acceptable in the churches of the Serra. The seminary was reproducing Vicente's error with greater institutional sophistication.

The man who broke the cycle was Francis Roz. A Catalan Jesuit who arrived in India in late 1584, Roz did what no Portuguese missionary before him had done: he went to Mar Abraham himself to learn Syriac. He mastered Malayalam. And in 1584, against fierce opposition from the hardline Latinisers within his own order, he added a course in Syriac to the Vaipicota curriculum. The Pope himself reportedly defended the teaching of Syriac on the ground that 'the variety of rites is one of the ornaments of the church, who is like a Queen, clad in varied garments.' The future students would not be wholly alienated from the traditions of their fathers. 28

The trajectory of accommodation Roz had taken would be stretched to its most radical extreme a generation later, when Roberto de Nobili in Madurai adopted the dress, diet, and Sanskrit learning of a Brahmin sannyasi, pushing Catholic accommodation to limits that scandalised his own order. The paradox noted in Chapter 7 was already visible at Vaipicota: the same Society of Jesus that produced the rigid standardisers of Goa also produced the men who learned Syriac in Kerala and Sanskrit in Tamil Nadu. The institution oscillated between the extremes because its Tridentine mandate contained both impulses — uniformity and persuasion — without resolving the tension between them. 29

9.6 — THE THIRD BISHOP AND THE LAST OPENING

The Second Provincial Council of Goa (1575) was convened by Archbishop Gaspar de Leão Pereira to consolidate Portuguese ecclesiastical control, formalize the Inquisition, and expand the Archdiocese of Goa's jurisdiction, which included suffragan dioceses in Cochin, Malacca, and the newly created Diocese of Macau. Mar Abraham refused to attend, having discovered that the Portuguese intended to seize him. The Council decreed that future bishops for the Thomas Christians must be appointed by the king of Portugal, not the Chaldean Patriarch. Abraham ignored it. 30

In 1576, a new threat emerged from an unexpected direction. Yet another bishop, Mar Simon, claiming to be sent by the independent Patriarch Elias VII, established himself in Malabar. Mar Simon drew away a faction of the Thomas Christians, and was notably supported by some of the Franciscan missionaries. The Mendicant-Jesuit inter-order rivalry was beginning to drag itself into the open. It would end catastrophically for the Jesuits nearly two centuries later. They would be arrested, their schools closed, and their missions handed over to the Franciscans. But that is a story for a later chapter.31

Facing this new competitor in Serra, Abraham made a tactical alliance with the Jesuits. He wrote to Pope Gregory XIII in January 1578, pledging obedience and formally accepting Jesuit missionaries into his diocese. The Jesuits, for their part, saw Mar Simon as a worse alternative: a prelate from the independent patriarchate who would undo whatever progress they had made. The enemy of their enemy became their friend.

The Iberian Union and the 'worthy' Archdeacon

In 1580, Philip II of Spain absorbed the Portuguese crown after King Sebastian's death at Alcácer Quibir in Morocco (1578) and the brief reign of Cardinal Henry. The Iberian Union did not merge the two empires administratively, but it brought the Estado da Índia under the shadow of the most powerful Catholic monarchy in Europe. For Philip, religious uniformity was a condition of state security. An ancient Eastern church operating outside Padroado jurisdiction in the pepper-rich Malabar region was no longer merely a canonical anomaly. It was a security concern. 32

Meanwhile, Abraham was attempting to secure an indigenous succession. He recommended Archdeacon George of Christ to the Pope for episcopal consecration, intending him to serve as suffragan and eventual successor. The Cardinal of Como, Peter Wenzel, wrote separately to Rome advocating for the archdeacon, describing him as 'a worthy person, and very well suited for the administration of this diocese.' Gregory XIII responded: on 3 January 1579, he issued a brief appointing the archdeacon to administer the diocese in the event of Abraham's death; on 4 March 1580, he authorised his consecration as Bishop of Palayur, addressing him as episcopo seu electo Palurensi. 33

The consecration was never carried out. The reason is debated and George of Christ died before any consecration could take place. He was succeeded as archdeacon by his brother, John (known in Portuguese records as "Lona Casanar Archdiago"). The papal initiative lapsed. It was, in retrospect, the last realistic opportunity to establish an indigenous Eastern-rite episcopacy in India before Diamper foreclosed the possibility entirely. 34

In 1583, with Jesuit assistance, Abraham held the First Diocesan Synod of Angamaly, adopting some Latin traditions while preserving the community's essential Chaldean character. In the same year, the Portuguese captured Mar Simon and deported him to Goa, then to Lisbon, and finally to Rome, where investigations by the Curia in 1585 concluded that he was 'neither a bishop, nor even a priest.' He was banished to a Franciscan convent in Lisbon, where he died in 1599. But before his capture, Mar Simon had appointed a local priest named Jacob as his archdeacon (vicar-general), and Jacob fiercely opposed Latinisation for another decade, ensuring that factional trouble persisted in the Serra long after its author had departed. 35

9.7 — THE DOUBLE EDGE

At the Third Provincial Council of Goa in 1585, Mar Abraham submitted to a fresh humiliation. The Padroado authorities declared that all ordinations he had performed were invalid, on the ground that at the porrectio instrumentorum there had been no wine in the chalice. The objection was wholly irrelevant to ordinations carried out in an Eastern church, but Abraham found it advisable to submit. He signed the Council's decrees and returned home to re-ordain all his priests for the third time. 36

It was at this council that Francisco Roz first interacted directly with Abraham, read his Syriac texts, and began formulating the arguments that would change the course of the Thomas Christian story. Roz was appointed as auxiliary and adviser to Mar Abraham, placing him inside the Chaldean administration itself. 37

What followed was the most consequential intellectual operation the Jesuits had yet mounted in India. The same Roz who learned Syriac from Abraham and fought to include it in the Vaipicota curriculum now wrote two treatises cataloguing what he considered the theological errors of the East Syrian tradition. The first, De Syrorum Orientalium Erroribus ('On the Errors of the East Syrians'), was dispatched to Rome in 1586. The second, De Erroribus Nestorianorum qui in hac India Orientali Versantur ('On the Errors of the Nestorians who dwell in this Oriental India'), was completed in haste in 1587 and sent to Europe in January 1588. 38

To add fuel to fire, in 1590, Mar Abraham refused to ordain candidates from the Vaipicota seminary according to the Latin pontifical, blocking the Jesuits' effort to produce graduates acceptable to both Latin and Eastern standards. The seminary's accommodation experiment had hit the wall it was always going to hit: Abraham would accept Syriac instruction for his students but not Latin ordination for his priests.39

Roz's treatises drew a sharp distinction between Mar Abraham and his flock. Abraham was portrayed as a knowing deceiver: a highly educated prelate fully acquainted with both Catholic and East Syrian doctrines who deliberately maintained heretical positions while feigning submission. The Jesuits classified him as a wolf. In sixteenth-century Portuguese India, this charge normally carried the death penalty at the stake. The Nazrani faithful, by contrast, were treated as innocent sheep led astray, ignorant of the heresies embedded in their liturgical books, capable of being rescued through education rather than punishment.40

The Jesuit actions must be read within the context. With the Councils of Ephesus and Chalcedon in the background, and the Tridentine drive toward doctrinal standardisation immediately behind them, the Jesuits interpreted what they encountered through the categories available to them—pertinacity in error, and therefore heresy. The understandings that would later revise the label "Nestorian" lay far in the future. At this point in history, the reports the Jesuits wrote functioned as a framework that justified removing the resisting Chaldean bishop while preserving the Nazrani flock.

The Jesuit Paradox

In 1594, George of the Cross—a nephew of George of Christ—was appointed as the new archdeacon. While the exact tenure of the intervening Archdeacon John remains unclear, historical records confirm that George's appointment was initially made against Jesuit wishes. Paradoxically, from 1594 onward, Mar Abraham and the new archdeacon found themselves in deepening harmony with the Jesuit mission, especially with Francis Roz. Later that same year, Abraham solemnly entrusted the Malabar Church to the Pope and the Jesuits, while simultaneously insisting that the Chaldean Patriarch's jurisdiction over Angamaly be respected and the local archdeacon be named as his successor. Roz himself, in a letter to his superiors, recommended George of the Cross as a suitable candidate for coadjutor bishop. As a result of this improved relationship, Mar Abraham even agreed to ordain the seminarians from the Jesuit seminary at Vaipicota, a vital concession he had previously refused to make.

That exact same year, a dossier known as the Enformação do Prelado da Serra (Information about the Bishop of the Serra), generally attributed to Francis Roz, was officially dispatched to Europe by the Jesuit Provincial of India, Francisco Cabral. The document framed Mar Abraham as a 'negative heretic' and essentially recommended him to the Inquisition. This profound paradox—a public blossoming of diplomatic cooperation paired with the covert dispatch of an inquisitorial death warrant—remains a historical puzzle that would serve as the central catalyst for the jurisdictional wars to come.41

In 1595, two orders arrived from Europe in close succession. Pope Clement VIII issued a brief directing the Archbishop of Goa to investigate Abraham's faith and, upon his death, to appoint a Latin vicar apostolic to govern the Serra, effectively excluding future bishops sent from Babylon. Cardinal Alberto, the Inquisitor General in Lisbon, separately ordered the Inquisition of Goa to conduct a formal inquiry into Abraham's life and doctrine. And in the same year, Aleixo de Menezes was appointed Archbishop of Goa by the Iberian Crown. The man who would act upon the Jesuit intelligence reports was not a Jesuit, a Franciscan, or a Dominican. He was a high-born aristocrat of pure blood and an Augustinian friar of 35 years of age, whose loyalty belonged to the Iberian Crown, making him an unbending, uncompromising architect of state-sponsored orthodoxy.42

The secret judicial inquiry was held in April 1596, at Vaipicota itself. Three Jesuit witnesses testified: António Toscano, Francisco Roz, and Jorge de Castro. The Thomas Christians had no opportunity to voice their cause. But in a detail that captures the genuine complexity of the Jesuit position, Cristóvão de Castro, the Jesuit rector in Cochin who oversaw the inquiry, advised the inquisitors not to act on the report, stating that everything was fine in the Malabar Church. Roz himself tried to prevent Menezes from overshooting the mark. 43

Kept in the dark of the undercurrents against him, Mar Abraham fell ill in late 1596. He asked the Jesuits for last sacraments. Whether he received them is disputed: Toscano later testified that Abraham refused confession, though the Jesuit Du Jarric maintained that he died reconciled to the Church. He made a testament naming Archdeacon George of the Cross as administrator of the diocese. In January 1597, Mar Abraham died. Menezes promptly informed the inquisitors that Abraham had died 'a heretic and schismatic,' and sent the inquiry report to Europe. 44

Stephen Neill would later judge more sympathetically that Abraham's conduct 'may seem less odious if his difficulties are fairly considered.' He was a foreigner in India, trying to strike a balance between his responsibility for protecting the Thomas Christians, who ardently wanted to safeguard their ancient traditions, and his need to ensure the support of the Portuguese for his hierarchical jurisdiction. Whenever he gave the impression of yielding to the missionaries, he lost followers to the rival Mar Simon. Whenever he resisted, the Jesuits added another charge to the dossier. He was caught between two loyalties and survived by ambiguity. The Portuguese called it dissimulation. The Thomas Christians called it governance.

For the first time since the death of Mar Jacob nearly half a century earlier, there was no bishop in the Serra. But this time it was different. No delegation or request was en route to Mesopotamia nor was any possibility of a replacement on the horizon. The Portuguese were watching every port. The cartaz controlled the sea lanes. The Inquisition report was in the hands of the Padroado and Rome.

The paradox of Roz's position was about to be exposed. He had entered the Nazrani community's world deeply enough to understand it and empathise with it, while the very framework his understanding had produced was about to be wielded by a man who had never learned a word of Syriac.

The story of what he did in the Serra is the subject of the next chapter.

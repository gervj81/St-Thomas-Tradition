Part I Synthesis

Tradition, History, and Identity

Beyond the Apostolic Question

"The historian cannot prove to them that they are mistaken in their belief. He may feel it right to warn them that historical research cannot pronounce on the matter with a confidence equal to that which they entertain by faith."

— Stephen Neill, A History of Christianity in India (1984)

"The central content stands out in clear relief, namely St. Thomas the apostle preached, died, and was buried in South India. None of the arguments so far advanced seem to be strong enough to erode the validity of this central content."

— A. Mathias Mundadan, History of Christianity in India (1984)

In 1984, two monumental, multi-volume histories of Christianity in India published their inaugural volumes. Stephen Neill, an Anglican bishop and scholar writing for Cambridge University Press, brought the rigorous, inherently sceptical apparatus of Western historical-critical methodology to bear on the tradition. A. Mathias Mundadan, a Catholic CMI priest spearheading the indigenous Church History Association of India project, evaluated the same body of evidence.

At first glance the two appear to have reached opposing conclusions. On closer examination they are not so much in opposition as in an epistemological stalemate: one evaluating the tradition from outside, the other from within.

Neill, working from outside, concludes that the available evidence does not permit the historian to pronounce on the apostolic claim with certainty — nor to disprove it conclusively. Mundadan, working from within, concludes that while the apostolic claim is not historically proven, no argument advanced to date has succeeded in eroding the central content of the tradition. The difference is not one of evidence but of epistemic posture: one requires positive evidence for belief, the other requires decisive grounds for rejection. This is the deadlock the Preface identified as the first of two narrow, polarising questions, and which the present study likewise finds at the heart of the apostolic debate. Neill and Mundadan represent two epistemic orientations operating with equal academic rigour, and their parallel conclusions illustrate why the debate is unlikely to be resolved on its own terms.

The central claim of the tradition is that the apostle Thomas — one of the twelve disciples of Jesus of Nazareth — travelled to India, preached the gospel, was martyred there, and that his tomb remained in India while his relics were later translated. This claim cannot be verified with certainty by the available evidence. Nor can it be dismissed as a later fabrication. Efforts to prove or disprove it are therefore likely to continue. Some have sought to discredit the tradition wholesale, arguing that the absence of demonstrable historical proof for the apostolic claim constitutes sufficient grounds for rejecting the tradition itself. Others have asserted the opposite: that the tradition, by virtue of its antiquity and continuity, constitutes sufficient historical proof of the apostolic claim. Both positions are methodologically insufficient. This is not a debate about a tradition that lacks evidence; the evidence has long been known. What scholars disagree on is the standard of proof that ought to be applied to a tradition that is circumstantial in its evidentiary basis yet substantial in its antiquity, continuity, and communal depth. The issue is not whether an early, coherent, and consistent tradition exists, but how far the available evidence permits us to move from that tradition to an apostolic event.

Scottish theologian and evangelical missionary Claudius Buchanan, publishing his Christian Researches in Asia in 1811, observed that there is as much evidence that St. Thomas went to India as there is that St. Peter went to Rome. For Buchanan, the “evidence” in both cases is not documentary, but the living tradition itself. Buchanan is right. If the continuity of tradition is sufficient for Rome to belong to Peter, then — for the Church — India belongs to Thomas.1

Beyond the Apostolic Event

If the question "Did Thomas come to India?" cannot be settled by the available evidence, a different question can be asked — one that the evidence does answer.

What Part I has established is the existence of a Christian community in India whose origins are attested in external sources from at least the third century, whose institutional presence on the Indian coast is documented from the sixth, and which — by the time it first names itself in a surviving document — described its own seat as the "Apostolic See of St. Thomas." The community operated within the commercial infrastructure of the Indian Ocean, was institutionally governed by the Church of the East, and was embedded, from the ninth century at the latest, in the political structure of the south Indian polity through the grant system. It is not a hypothesis. It is a documented historical reality.

The most productive framework for understanding this community is not as a single entity that emerged at a particular moment in a single location, but as a commonwealth of Christians — a network of settlements layered over different periods, extending from the Persian Gulf through coastal India to Sri Lanka and Southeast Asia, bound together by shared liturgical traditions, the common ecclesiastical heritage of the Church of the East, the spiritual legacy of St. Thomas, and, above all, by the commercial networks of the Indian Ocean trade.2

Within this commonwealth, the Thomas tradition functioned as a unifying principle of identity. This framework does not require a definitive verdict on the apostolic question. The persistence of the tradition does not depend on the demonstrability of a first-century event but on its function as the organising narrative through which the community understood its origin, unity, and place within the wider Christian world. Whether Thomas personally came to India in the first century, or whether the tradition represents a later appropriation by a community shaped through Indo-Persian Christian networks, the functional reality is the same: by the time the documentary record becomes visible, the Thomas tradition was already the shared founding narrative of a transregional Christian network, sufficiently old, widespread, and deeply embedded that no alternative apostolic attribution achieved comparable acceptance within it.

What this study traces, then, is the history of this community — not the biography of an apostle. Whether or not Thomas personally set foot on Indian soil, the community that bears his name is real, its tomb tradition is without a sustained rival, and its presence in the Indian subcontinent is firmly established. The tradition that binds the community together — the tradition of apostolic origin — is not a decorative overlay on a community that would exist independently of it. It is the community's constitutive identity: the thread that connects Ephrem's veneration of relics in Edessa and Isidore's martyrology in Seville to the shrine at Mylapore; to the Pahlavi crosses; to the copper plates; to the merchant networks of the Malabar coast; to a fourteen-year-old scribe in Kodungallur in 1301; and — through the transformations of the Prester John tradition — to the Portuguese fleet that arrived at Calicut in 1498 seeking "Christians and spices."

That identity, the encounter between it and the Latin Christian world that misunderstood it, and the transformations that followed, are the subject of Part II.

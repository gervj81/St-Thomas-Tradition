PART I SYNTHESIS: THE EVIDENCE AND THE COMMONWEALTH

Tradition, History, and Identity

"The historian cannot prove to them that they are mistaken in their belief. He may feel it right to warn them that historical research cannot pronounce on the matter with a confidence equal to that which they entertain by faith."

— Stephen Neill, A History of Christianity in India (1984)

"The central content stands out in clear relief, namely St. Thomas the apostle preached, died, and was buried in South India. None of the arguments so far advanced seem to be strong enough to erode the validity of this central content."

— A. Mathias Mundadan, History of Christianity in India (1984)

In 1984, two monumental, multi-volume histories of Christianity in India published their inaugural volumes. Stephen Neill, an Anglican bishop and scholar writing for Cambridge University Press, brought the rigorous, inherently skeptical apparatus of Western historical-critical methodology to bear on the tradition. A. Mathias Mundadan, a Catholic CMI priest spearheading the indigenous Church History Association of India project, evaluated the exact same body of evidence and arrived at the opposite conclusion. They were not deliberately attempting to prove each other wrong — the volumes were researched and published concurrently — but their simultaneous appearance perfectly captures the permanent historiographical deadlock surrounding the St. Thomas tradition.

The central claim of that tradition is that the apostle Thomas — one of the twelve disciples of Jesus of Nazareth — personally travelled to India, preached the gospel, established Christian communities, and was martyred on the Coromandel coast, where his tomb remains. This claim cannot be verified with certainty by the available evidence. It cannot be dismissed as a later fabrication either.

A broader comparison with other apostolic foundation traditions reinforces both of these statements simultaneously. The Thomas tradition belongs to the same category as the traditions of Peter at Rome and Mark at Alexandria — circumstantial in evidentiary basis, ancient in attestation, and sustained by continuous communities. It is neither history nor fiction but tradition — a category that historians must take seriously without either accepting it uncritically or dismissing it as worthless.

Neill and Mundadan represent the two poles of a scholarly spectrum that, accumulated over more than two centuries, illustrates why the debate is unlikely to be resolved. Scholars have ranged from categorical denial to confident affirmation, with the majority occupying intermediate positions that acknowledge the tradition’s antiquity while declining to pronounce it historically certain. This is not a debate about a tradition that lacks evidence. The evidence has long been known. What scholars disagree on is the standard of proof that ought to be applied to a tradition that is circumstantial in its evidentiary basis but substantial in its antiquity, continuity, and communal depth.1

If the question "Did Thomas come to India?" cannot be settled by the available evidence, a different question can be asked — one that the evidence does answer.

What Part I of this study has established is the existence of a Christian community in India whose origins are attested in external sources from at least the third century, whose institutional presence on the Indian coast is documented from the sixth, and which — by the time it first names itself in a surviving document — described its own seat as the "Apostolic See of St. Thomas." This community operated within the commercial infrastructure of the Indian Ocean, was institutionally governed by the Church of the East, and was embedded — from the ninth century at the latest — in the political structure of the south Indian polity through the grant system. It is not a hypothesis. It is a documented historical reality.

The most historically productive framework for understanding this community is not as a single entity that suddenly emerged at a particular moment in a single location, but as a commonwealth of Christians — a network of settlements layered over different periods, extending from the Persian Gulf through coastal India to Sri Lanka and Southeast Asia, bound together by shared liturgical traditions, the common ecclesiastical heritage of the Church of the East, the spiritual legacy of St. Thomas, and, above all, by the commercial networks of the Indian Ocean trade.2

Within this commonwealth, the Thomas tradition functioned as a unifying identity. This framework does not require a definitive verdict on the apostolic question. Whether Thomas personally came to India in the first century, or whether the tradition represents a later appropriation by a community shaped through Indo-Persian Christian networks, the functional reality remains the same: by the time the documentary record becomes visible, the Thomas tradition was already the shared identity of a transregional Christian network. The tradition was sufficiently old, widespread, and deeply embedded that no alternative apostolic attribution achieved comparable acceptance within this network.

What this study traces is the history of a community, not the biography of an apostle. Whether or not Thomas personally set foot on Indian soil, the community that bears his name is historically real, its antiquity is well attested, its institutional history is documented, its tomb tradition is without a sustained rival, and its commercial and political presence in the Indian subcontinent is firmly established. The tradition that binds this community together — the tradition of apostolic origin — is not a decorative overlay on a community that would exist independently of it. It is the community’s constitutive identity: the thread that connects Ephrem’s veneration of relics in Edessa and Isidore’s martyrology in Seville to the shrine at Mylapore; to the Pahlavi crosses; to the copper plates; to the merchant networks of the Malabar coast; to a fourteen-year-old scribe in Kodungallur in 1301; and — through the transformations of the Prester John tradition — to the Portuguese fleet that arrived at Calicut in 1498 seeking "Christians and spices."

That identity — and the encounter between it and the Latin Christian world that misunderstood it, and the transformations that followed — is the subject of Part II.

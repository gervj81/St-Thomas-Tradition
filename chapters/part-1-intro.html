Reasoning Under Scarcity

The first fifteen centuries of the Thomas Christian community present a unique historiographical challenge: genuine documentary scarcity. The primary evidence is sparse, scattered, and often temporally remote from the events it purports to describe. How does a historian reason honestly when the evidentiary ground is thin and the gap between tradition and attestation is measured in centuries?

Part I approaches this problem through disciplined, context-driven analysis, distinguishing between inherited tradition, historical plausibility, and verifiable evidence. The chronological trajectory spans a millennium and a half, beginning by mapping the commercial and cultural cosmopolitan matrix of the first century to establish the practical means by which early Christianity could have reached the Malabar coast. From there, the narrative navigates the boundary between theological memory and geography, tracing how the apostolic imprint became a foundational identity. We follow the community’s deepening integration into South Indian mercantile and political structures—evidenced through Pahlavi-inscribed crosses, copper plate grants, and robust oceanic trade networks—right up to the eve of the Portuguese arrival, when the Thomas Christians first entered the global Christian imagination of European travellers.

Rather than prematurely demanding absolute proof or disproof of the apostolic foundation, the following chapters aim to understand what the Thomas Christian community was doing, how it sustained itself, and what the available evidence permits us, and does not permit us, to conclude about its shape and origins.

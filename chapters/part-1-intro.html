PART I: ORIGINS AND THE COMMONWEALTH

The First Fifteen Centuries, 1st c. BCE – 1498 CE

A Note on Method: The Three-Tier Framework

The first fifteen centuries of the Thomas Christian community present a unique historiographical challenge: genuine documentary scarcity. The primary evidence is sparse, scattered, and often temporally remote from the events it purports to describe. How does a historian reason honestly when the evidentiary ground is thin and the gap between tradition and attestation is measured in centuries? To navigate this landscape, Part I employs a three-tier analytical framework: the Legend, the Plausible, and the Historical.

This section of the study is divided into four chapters, chronologically tracing the community from its origins within the broader interconnected world of late antiquity to the eve of the Portuguese arrival. The trajectory spans an entire millennium and a half of history, examining how a continuous tradition of apostolic origin intersected with the shifting dynamics of global trade and regional ecclesiastical politics.

Chapter 1: The Cosmopolitan Matrix (1st Century BCE – 1st Century CE)

Before tracing the apostle, we must trace the trade routes. The Indian subcontinent was not an isolated peninsula, but a vital node in an extensive and intricate cosmopolitan matrix. By mapping the commercial, maritime, and cultural exchanges between the Roman, Parthian, and Indian worlds, this chapter establishes the physical plausibility and the practical means by which early Christianity could have reached the Malabar coast.

Chapter 2: The Apostolic Imprint: Text, Memory, and Geography (1st Century CE – 6th Century CE)

This chapter directly addresses the core tradition of the community: the mission, martyrdom, and legacy of St. Thomas. Analyzing texts such as the Acts of Judas Thomas alongside patristic memory, the Edessan relic cult, and geographical evidence, this section navigates the delicate boundary between theological memory and verifiable history, illustrating how the apostolic imprint became the foundational identity of the Indian Church.

Chapter 3: The Tomb, The Trade, and The Long Silence (c. 550 CE – 883 CE)

From the mid-sixth to the late-ninth century, the narrative encounters what is often perceived as a documentary silence. Yet, as this chapter demonstrates, this silence was not an absence. This era saw the deepening integration of the Thomas Christians within the mercantile and political structures of South India — evidenced through the Pahlavi inscribed crosses, the Tharisapalli copper plates, and their enduring connection to the Church of the East through robust oceanic trade channels.

Chapter 4: The Legend, The Witnesses, and The Collision (1037 CE – 1498 CE)

The final chapter of Part I covers the centuries leading up to the Portuguese arrival. During this period, the Thomas Christians became an acknowledged feature of the global Christian imagination, appearing in the accounts of European and West Asian travelers, and inextricably entwined with the legend of Prester John. This visibility set the stage for the dramatic collision that would follow the arrival of Vasco da Gama's fleet in 1498, concluding the community's era of autonomy and ushering in a long disruption.

What This Section Achieves

Rather than prematurely demanding absolute proof or disproof of the apostolic foundation, the following four chapters aim to understand what the Thomas Christian community was doing, how it survived, and the ways in which its foundational narratives shaped its enduring historical identity. The goal is to illuminate the long arc of a continuous, living communion that thrived in the unique cultural ecosystem of the Indian Ocean for more than a millennium before the arrival of European colonial powers.

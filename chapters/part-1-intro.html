The First Fifteen Centuries, 1st c. BCE – 1498 CE

Part I: Origins and the Commonwealth

A Note on Method: Reasoning Under Scarcity

The first fifteen centuries of the Thomas Christian community present a unique historiographical challenge: genuine documentary scarcity. The primary evidence is sparse, scattered, and often temporally remote from the events it purports to describe. How does a historian reason honestly when the evidentiary ground is thin and the gap between tradition and attestation is measured in centuries?

Part I approaches this problem through disciplined, context-driven analysis. Rather than forcing premature conclusions, it works by distinguishing between inherited tradition, historical plausibility, and what can be directly supported by surviving evidence. These are not treated as rigid categories, but as shifting levels of historical confidence, calibrated to the nature and limits of the sources.

The chronological tracing of the community—from its origins within the broader interconnected world of late antiquity to the eve of the Portuguese arrival—is synthesised across four chapters. This trajectory spans a millennium and a half, examining how a tradition of apostolic origin intersected with the shifting dynamics of global trade and regional ecclesiastical politics.

Chapter 1: The Cosmopolitan Matrix (1st Century BCE – 1st Century CE)

Before tracing the apostle, we must trace the trade routes. The Indian subcontinent was not an isolated peninsula, but a vital node in an extensive and intricate cosmopolitan matrix. By mapping the commercial, maritime, and cultural exchanges between the Roman, Parthian, and Indian worlds, this chapter establishes the physical plausibility and the practical means by which early Christianity could have reached the Malabar coast.

Chapter 2: The Apostolic Imprint: Text, Memory, and Geography (1st Century CE – 6th Century CE)

This chapter directly addresses the core tradition of the community: the mission, martyrdom, and legacy of St. Thomas. Analysing texts such as the Acts of Judas Thomas alongside patristic memory, the Edessan relic cult, and geographical evidence, it navigates the boundary between theological memory and verifiable history, illustrating how the apostolic imprint became the foundational identity of the Indian Church.

Chapter 3: The Tomb, The Trade, and The Long Silence (c. 550 CE – 883 CE)

From the mid-sixth to the late-ninth century, the narrative encounters what is often perceived as a documentary silence. Yet, as this chapter demonstrates, this silence was not an absence. This period saw the deepening integration of the Thomas Christians within the mercantile and political structures of South India—evidenced through the Pahlavi-inscribed crosses, the Tharisapalli copper plates, and their enduring connection to the Church of the East through robust oceanic trade networks.

Chapter 4: The Legend, The Witnesses, and The Collision (1037 CE – 1498 CE)

The final chapter of Part I covers the centuries leading up to the Portuguese arrival. During this period, the Thomas Christians became an acknowledged feature of the global Christian imagination, appearing in the accounts of European and West Asian travellers and becoming entwined with the legend of Prester John. This visibility set the stage for the dramatic collision that followed the arrival of Vasco da Gama’s fleet in 1498, concluding the community’s era of relative autonomy and ushering in a prolonged disruption.

What This Section Achieves

Rather than prematurely demanding absolute proof or disproof of the apostolic foundation, the following chapters aim to understand what the Thomas Christian community was doing, how it sustained itself, and what the available evidence permits us, and does not permit us, to conclude about its origins.

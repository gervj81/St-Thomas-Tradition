The Global War and the Fall of Cochin, 1640–1700

The Trade Companies of War

"The Honourable Company had waxed great through fighting the Portuguese."

— The Heeren XVII to the States-General, 1641

Chapter 12 closed in a harbour under guns. Joseph Sebastiani, ordered out of Malabar by a Dutch commander who had just taken Cochin, used the few days he had left to consecrate Mar Alexander de Campo, so that the reconciled Thomas Christians would not be left without a bishop of their own before he sailed. The scene belongs as much to the history of European empire as to the history of the Serra, and it leaves a question the last chapter set aside. How had a Dutch fleet come to command the Portuguese harbour on the Malabar coast, with the power to expel a Roman bishop at ten days' notice? To answer it the narrative must rewind a little, and widen a great deal. 1

The guns in Cochin harbour were the local end of a war that had been running, on and off, for sixty years and across four continents. It had been fought in the North Sea and the Baltic, off Brazil and Angola, in the Spice Islands, the Persian Gulf, and the approaches to Goa. In its scale and its simultaneity it was something close to the first global war, waged less by a kingdom than by a chartered company that kept its own fleets, fortresses, and treaties, and that had grown rich by fighting the very empire whose richest possessions it now meant to inherit.

This chapter retells the fall of Portuguese Malabar. It is the story of how a Calvinist trading corporation converged on a single pepper port in the first weeks of 1663, and of what their convergence meant for a divided church that had no part in the quarrel and no power to escape its outcome.

A War Without Borders

The Dutch East India Company, the Vereenigde Oostindische Compagnie (VOC), was not a merchant house that occasionally fought. It was an instrument of war that happened to trade. In Chapter 10, we saw Charles V partitioning his massive domains in 1556, bequeathing the Netherlands, along with Spain, the Italian possessions, and the Indies, to his son, the Habsburg king Philip II. Philip II's sustained military repression of the Dutch Protestants ignited a long rebellion against Habsburg rule, a struggle remembered as the Eighty Years' War. Forged in the middle of this revolt, VOC was chartered in 1602 by the States-General (the sovereign federal assembly of the Dutch Republic) and operated under the strategic umbrella of the Stadtholder (the republic's chief executive and military commander). It carried the Dutch revolt into Asian waters as a matter of deliberate policy. Since the crowns of Spain and Portugal had been joined under a single Habsburg sovereign from the Iberian Union of 1580, the VOC's Calvinist directors, the Heeren XVII (or the "Lords Seventeen"), viewed every Portuguese fort, carrack, and spice contract east of the Cape as a legitimate target in a global war that was at once holy and profitable. 2

The Heeren understood the fusion of faith and profit without embarrassment. They shared with their Catholic Portuguese rivals the conviction that European Christians might dispose of the trade and the territory of every people beyond the pale of Christendom; they differed only in holding that the warrant lay with the Reformed religion rather than with Rome. Nor were they under any illusion about how their rule was received. The Company's most formidable commander in these waters would acknowledge, as early as 1655, that the Dutch were detested by every nation in Asia, an admission that did nothing whatever to slow the conquests. 3

The Long Road East

The Company had not arrived on the Malabar coast in a single stride. Its first object was the spice of the Indonesian archipelago, the cloves, nutmeg, and mace of the Moluccas and the pepper of the islands, and it took that spice in large part by prising the Portuguese loose from the trade they had dominated for a century. 4

From 1619 the Company had a capital in Asia and a will to match. In that year Jan Pieterszoon Coen established its permanent Asian headquarters at Batavia, in western Java, and set about building the inter-Asian trading system that would become the engine of Dutch wealth in the East: Indian cloth from the Coromandel coast exchanged for island spices, Persian silk, Japanese silver, Chinese porcelain, the whole woven into a network whose profits could pay for the cargoes sent home to Amsterdam. To build it the Company fought continuously, and chiefly against the Portuguese, whose forts strung along the sea-roads from Mozambique to Macao stood athwart every route it wished to control. 5

In 1648, the geopolitical landscape of Europe transformed with the Peace of Westphalia. This monumental series of treaties brought a definitive end to the Eighty Years' War, finally forcing the Spanish Habsburgs to recognise a fully independent and sovereign Dutch Republic. More broadly, Westphalia drew a line under the continent's exhausting wars of religious denomination, decentralising the power of the Holy Roman Emperor in favour of a new order of independent sovereign states. When this general peace in Europe freed thousands of battle-hardened soldiers for mercenary service, the Company had both the men and the forward base its Asian ambition required.6

The Peace on Paper

Even before Westphalia, the Company had been offered a truce with the Portuguese. In December 1640 the Duke of Braganza was proclaimed King John IV of Portugal, and sixty years of union with the Spanish crown were over. Overnight Portugal ceased to be an appendage of the Dutch republic's chief enemy and became instead a possible ally against it. The logic was plain enough: a fresh front against the Habsburgs in the Iberian peninsula was worth a truce with Lisbon, and the States-General and the Stadtholder were willing to make one. 7

The Company drew the opposite conclusion. Its directors, the Heeren XVII, told the States-General without ceremony that the corporation had grown great by fighting the Portuguese and had won its Asian monopoly by the same means; to stop now would be to let a beaten rival recover its wind and compete again. They asked that any truce concluded with the restored Portuguese crown in Europe leave the East Indies exempt, exactly as an earlier truce had been exempted a generation before. The war in Asia, in their reading, was not the republic's to end. 8

A ten-year truce was negotiated at The Hague in 1641 regardless. In Asia it was, for years, simply not obeyed. The Company's government at Batavia evaded its implementation on one pretext after another, and its officers were candid about admitting that they served the Company and not the Prince of Orange or the States, and would take orders from no one else. The standing instruction from Batavia was the reverse of restraint: seize as much Portuguese territory as possible before any peace could take effect, and so fix the map before the diplomats could. 9

The Other Company

The Dutch were not alone on the water. Two years before the VOC received its charter, the English had founded their own East India Company, in 1600, on the same model of a royal monopoly over a nation's whole Asian trade. Through the first half of the seventeenth century the English company was the lesser power in the East, edged out of the spice islands by Dutch force and content, for the most part, to build its trade in Indian cloth and, increasingly, in pepper. It was a rival the VOC could afford to disdain, and largely did. 10

That would change within a generation of Cochin's fall. When England and France fell upon the Dutch republic together in 1672, the war in Europe cut into the VOC's shipping and gave the English company its opening in Asia; its share of the European pepper trade climbed steeply through the 1670s. The point is worth marking here only because the two companies' fortunes would, in the next century, decide the fate of the coast this chapter is about: the same English company that fought for pepper in the 1670s would, by the later eighteenth century, be the paramount European power in India, and the Dutch hold on Malabar would dwindle to a memory. For the moment, though, in the first weeks of 1663, it was the Dutch who held the guns, and the Portuguese who were about to lose everything. 11

Up the Pepper Coast

The road that brought a Dutch fleet to Cochin ran through Ceylon. A Dutch squadron had appeared off Malabar as early as 1604 and made common cause with the Zamorin of Calicut, the oldest enemy of the Portuguese on the coast; but the Company had chosen to secure the Indonesian archipelago and Ceylon first, and only when the conquest of coastal Ceylon was complete in 1658 did it turn the full weight of its arms northward. 12

From Ceylon the blow fell fast. Under Rijkloff van Goens, the hard and capable commander who would govern the conquered coast, the Dutch came up Malabar fortress by fortress. Quilon fell in December 1661. Kodungallur, the Portuguese Cranganore, the seat of the archbishop whose quarrel with the Archdeacon had broken the church a decade earlier, fell in January 1662. The campaign that would end the Padroado's hold on the Thomas Christians was, from the Dutch side, simply the closing of an account in pepper. 13

The Fall of Cochin

Cochin was the prize, the chief Portuguese stronghold on the coast and the seat of the Padroado bishop, and it did not fall at the first attempt. An initial Dutch descent in early 1662 ended in retreat; Van Goens re-embarked in March, leaving garrisons behind at Kodungallur and Quilon, and the Portuguese, supposing the danger past, neglected their defences. They were wrong. A second and far larger expedition appeared before Cochin on 25 October 1662, eleven ships from Batavia reinforced by van Goens himself with native Ceylonese troops brought across from the island he had just conquered. The Dutch entrenched on the approaches, cut the fort off from its allies, and settled in to take it. 14

The siege was hard and close. The Dutch raised batteries behind barricades of palm-trunks, some twenty-eight guns in all, sited by the churches of St John, St Thomas, and Calvetty (now Calvathy Road), and opened on the half-ruined pepper warehouse from the sea on the last day of 1662. The Portuguese commander, Ignatio Sarmento, had refused the terms offered him, freedom of religion and trade in exchange for the fort, and his garrison fought stubbornly; the Raja of Porakkad sent Nair troops who, by one account, displayed exceptional heroism at the landing place before fresh Dutch companies broke them. The final assault came on the Calvetty side on 6 January 1663. When it was plainly lost, Sarmento sent two commissaries out under a white flag, and the next morning he delivered the keys of the fort into the hands of the Dutch general. The siege had cost the attackers some three hundred and sixty dead, with hundreds more in the hospital or unfit for duty. The terms were signed the following day. 15

The capitulation was the formal end of a hundred and sixty years of Portuguese Malabar. By its terms the garrison marched out with flying colours, drums beating and fuses lit, to lay down its arms beyond the walls; the Portuguese were shipped to Europe or Goa. Kannur, the last Portuguese hold on the coast, surrendered to Jacob Hustaart on 13 February. The Portuguese flag had ceased to fly over Malabar, and the Dutch were left the masters of the country (the Portuguese were now confined to Goa, which the Dutch had repeatedly tried and failed to take between 1638 and 1663). 16

The New Masters and the Old Church

The new masters were Calvinists, and that single fact reshaped the position of the Thomas Christians at a stroke. The Dutch had no interest in the Padroado, no use for the religious orders that had administered the Serra, and a settled hostility toward Rome and, above all, toward Portuguese power. They expelled the Catholic European clergy from the territory they held, the port of Cochin and the captured forts, ordering out the Carmelite vicar apostolic and the Jesuits of Cochin and Kannur. But the Dutch held only a coastal sliver. The Serra itself lay inland and to the south, scattered across a patchwork of independent native kingdoms where the Company's writ did not run, and there, beyond the reach of any expulsion order, a thin Catholic presence could survive. The standing Dutch rule, held to for decades, was that no European prelate, least of all a Portuguese one, would be allowed to exercise authority within their territory. 17

It was that expulsion order, and no design of Rome's, that produced the scene with which the previous chapter ended. To see what Sebastiani did in his last days on the coast, and why it cut so deep, the two men at its centre must be named properly. The Archdeacon who had been raised up at the Coonan Cross was Thomas, proclaimed Mar Thomas I; the bishop Sebastiani now made was his own first cousin, the cattanar whom the Carmelite knew as Parambil Chandy of Kuravilangad and whom the Latin records latinise as Alexander de Campo. The two were of one family, and not by accident: the office of archdeacon of the Thomas Christians had long been hereditary, descending within a single lineage, so that the leader of the schism and the man Rome would set against him were kinsmen of the same archidiaconal house. 18

The division between these two kinsmen had in fact been some years in the making, and Sebastiani had worked at it deliberately across two Carmelite missions. Parambil Chandy was among the four senior cattanars whom the Archdeacon had appointed his councillors after the oath of 1653, the inner circle of the revolt. But Chandy was also the first of them to judge the Archdeacon's position untenable, once it was plain that the consecration by twelve priests could not stand and that Rome would never recognise it. He switched sides and threw his weight behind the papal commissaries, and laboured to bring as many cattanars and parishes back to obedience as he could, suffering persecution from the schismatic party for his refusal to support the Archdeacon. By the time Sebastiani returned for his second mission, Chandy was the natural indigenous candidate the Carmelite had been cultivating: a man of the archidiaconal house, of unimpeachable descent, who had chosen union with Rome. 19

The struggle for the cattanars had been a close and sometimes farcical thing. The Archdeacon's party had at one point produced a merchant, recently arrived at Cochin to buy pepper, who was paid to pose as the nephew of a newly elected pope and to present a forged brief confirming Thomas as archbishop and patriarch of the Thomas Christians; the fictitious document was paraded at meetings held safely inside kingdoms then at war with Cochin and the Portuguese, where the rival commissary could not follow to expose it. An attempt by Sebastiani on 9 October 1661 to seize the Archdeacon and his chief adviser, the cattanar Anjilimoottil Ittithommen, and so end the schism by force, failed when the two slipped away disguised as Hindu soldiers. It is worth pausing on this Ittithommen, for he is among the figures the two traditions see most differently. To the Catholic sources he is the priest who administered the irregular hand-laying of 1653 and was excommunicated with the Archdeacon, an agent of schism; to the Puthankūr tradition he is the faithful and sagacious counsellor who held the community to the faith of its fathers, the author of the broken-stick prophecy at the Coonan Cross and the writer of a letter, much cited in that tradition, asserting that the Patriarch of Antioch had always been the lord of Malankara and that its bishops had always come from him. The same act, the same man, read as either the steadying of an ancient loyalty or the engineering of a revolt. 20 It was against this background of forged briefs and narrow escapes that the Dutch conquest abruptly closed the contest. Ordered out, Sebastiani conferred episcopal ordination on Parambil Chandy on 31 January 1663 and appointed him Vicar Apostolic of Malabar, then on the following day, 1 February, solemnly excommunicated the Archdeacon and Ittithommen. The title mattered, and would matter more in the decades to come. A vicar apostolic was not a diocesan bishop but the personal delegate of the pope, governing through the Roman Congregation of Propaganda Fide rather than through the Portuguese Padroado; and the ancient see of the Thomas Christians, the archbishopric of Kodungallur, was a Padroado see still, vacant in fact but unsuppressed in law, to which Lisbon retained the right to nominate. In raising up a Roman vicar apostolic over a flock that on paper still belonged to a Portuguese archbishop, Sebastiani had planted, almost in passing, a jurisdictional conflict that would not be resolved for two centuries. For the moment it lay dormant, since no Padroado archbishop could set foot in Dutch territory; but the day a Portuguese prelate again reached Kodungallur, the two claims would collide. Before he sailed Sebastiani secured from van Goens an assurance that the Dutch would favour Bishop Chandy over his cousin. Then he went, and with him went the last European prelate to govern the Serra, though, as will be seen, a handful of friars clung on in the native interior. 21

The Two Bishops

The Dutch had promised Sebastiani they would favour Bishop Chandy over the Archdeacon, and for a time they kept their word, despite the Archdeacon's efforts to turn them against his cousin. The advantage that told, however, was not Dutch favour but the plain fact of orders. Chandy had been consecrated by a bishop whose own succession no one doubted; Mar Thomas I had been raised up by the hands of twelve priests at Alangad, a consecration that no apostolic church, Latin or Eastern, could recognise and one that a growing number of his own people had begun to question. In the first years of Chandy's episcopate the balance tilted steadily toward him: cattanars and parishes made their submission, and the man with valid orders gathered to himself those who wanted a bishop they could be sure was a bishop. 22

It was this, the doubt about his own consecration, that drove Mar Thomas I towards the end of 1665 to open secret negotiations with the very Jesuits he had sworn at the Coonan Cross never to obey, an episode that comes to us mostly through Carmelite and Jesuit archives but occupies a much smaller place in later Malankara narratives. Discouraged by the drift of parishes to his cousin and by the silence of the Eastern patriarchs he had requested for bishops, he sent a cattanar to the rector of the Jesuits (now withdrawn to Ambazhakkad under Zamorin after the fall of Cochin) with word that he wished to submit to the Catholic Church, provided it could be done without humiliation. The talks went far enough that an end to the schism seemed within reach, and the reunion of the Thomas Christians under Rome was, in those weeks, a real possibility. 23

The Antiochian Connection

What ended that possibility was a ship. In 1665 a West Syrian prelate, Mar Gregorios Abdal Jaleel, identified in the sources as the Metropolitan of Jerusalem, reached Malabar, an Antiochian bishop answering, it seems, the appeals Mar Thomas had been sending east for a bishop of his own. He may have travelled with Dutch complicity, much as Ahatallah had slipped onto a Dutch ship a decade before, and the new Dutch masters, hostile to Rome, had no reason to stop an anti-Roman Syrian. The moment news of his landing arrived, Mar Thomas broke off the negotiation with the Jesuits and turned to receive him. He wrote to the northern churches to welcome the newcomer, met him at Idappally with a great gathering, and presented him to the people as a patriarch sent with apostolic authority; old Ittithommen, the Archdeacon's adviser, produced once more a brief, real or forged, read out from the pulpit, and a collection of some five thousand panams was taken up for the visitor. It is from the coming of Mar Gregorios, more than from the oath of 1653, that the permanent division of the community into Puthankūr and Pazhayakūr can be dated. 24

Gregorios moved with care, for many still believed he had been sent by the Pope. When his manner of saying Mass gave offence, Mar Thomas persuaded him to use the local rite, which he did, and then set about restoring customs that the Synod of Diamper had abolished: the fermented host, the traditional vestments, the old calendar, observances common to West Syrians and Chaldeans alike. To his followers he appeared less as an innovator than as the restorer of their ancient ways, and this was the source of his success. In a letter of 1668 to the priests of Mulanthuruthy, Kandanad, and Parur he denounced those who had left the orthodox canons of the Syrian Church to join the idolatrous Romans, made heretics, as he put it, by the undue influence of the kings and queens of Portugal, and he attacked the compulsory celibacy the Latins had imposed. His aim was not, in the first place, to make the people Antiochian; it was to make them not Latin, to teach them to give up whatever the latinisers had brought in. In that he largely succeeded, and with his coming the breach took on a permanence the oath of 1653 had not given it. What hardened, it should be said precisely, was the institutional fact of two churches rather than the temper between their people: a reunion of the Pazhayakūr (the Old Allegiance, the party that held to Rome, called hereafter the Malabar Church) and the Puthankūr (the New Allegiance, the party that turned to Antioch, called hereafter the Malankara Church) was henceforth out of reach, and yet, as will be seen, the two communities went on living side by side with a familiarity that makes the word schism almost too cold for it. 25

A Contested Succession

Yet the one thing Mar Thomas most needed, Gregorios may not have given him. Whether the Jerusalem prelate ever conferred valid episcopal consecration on Mar Thomas I is precisely the point on which the sources divide. Later Malankara Church historiography would assert that he did, while contemporary Jesuit and Carmelite archives, which cannot be considered as absolutely impartial, maintain that no such consecration took place, and that Thomas, who had wanted nothing so much as undoubted orders, continued to the end of his life in the false position his cousin's secure consecration had made so painful. Mar Gregorios died in 1671, at North Parur, and Mar Thomas I followed him to the grave on 22 April 1673. 26

Gregorios's Arrival: Revival or New Allegiance? Two Historiographies

What the events of 1665 meant is told in two incompatible ways, and the difference is not merely one of detail but of the whole frame. The divergence is worth setting out once here, because it will shadow the rest of this history.

The current academic consensus and later source critical Catholic historians followed in this synthesis, Mundadan (Vol. I, 1984), Thekkedath (Vol. II, 1982), and the Anglican Stephen Neill (1984), treat the Antiochian, West Syrian connection as something substantially new in Malabar. On their account the Thomas Christians had been an East Syrian, Chaldean-rite church under the Church of the East; the arrival of a bishop sent in the name of the Jacobite Patriarch of Antioch began a fresh allegiance and a gradual West Syriac reorientation that would take generations to complete.

The historiography of the Puthankūr tradition itself reverses the picture. Its classic statement, E. M. Philip's The Indian Church of St. Thomas (1908), takes its cue from the Anglican chaplain Claudius Buchanan, who had reported after his 1806 visit that "the Syrian Christians are not Nestorians," that though they had formerly had bishops of that communion their present liturgy derived from the early church of Antioch. 27 The remark does more than it states. In calling the Malankara Church simply "the Syrian Christians," and their body "the Syrian Church of Malayala," Buchanan tacitly awards that party the whole ancient inheritance: it is the Syrian Church, the surviving core, and is moreover, so far as he could judge, no Nestorian remnant but a church of Antiochene rite. The decisive work is done not by argument but by the name. For to let the Antiochian body alone wear the title "Syrian Church" is to write the Pazhayakūr, the party reconciled with Rome, out of that lineage altogether, although it descended from the very same undivided Church as its rival. The irony is exact: the faction whose own name means the Old Allegiance is quietly excluded from antiquity, while the faction named the New Allegiance, which becomes the Malankara Church, takes the ancient identity along with the name. Philip inherits both the claim and the nomenclature, and presses the point much further: the Syrians of the Serra were never Nestorian in the first place, the connection with the Church of the East having been an incidental overlay and not a constitutional fact. Hence the title of his relevant chapter, "Revival of Jacobitism": on this account Mar Gregorios came not to introduce a new obedience but to revive and validate the ancient core of the Indian Church that the Church of the East earlier, and Rome later, had merely overlaid. 28

What followed exposed how fragile the new line really was. On Mar Thomas I's death his brother took his place and buried him in pontifical vestments, only to die himself within a week. A nephew then succeeded as Mar Thomas II and ruled until 1686, and the succession passed on through a third Mar Thomas and beyond. The validity of these orders is contested, and the two traditions simply divide: the Pazhayakūr and Catholic sources, with Neill, regard the early Mar Thomases as probably never validly consecrated, lacking the bishops a true succession required, while the Malankara tradition, given fullest voice by Philip, holds their consecration and succession to have been valid and regular, secured by Antiochian bishops in the Serra, even where contemporary documentation is wanting. To this the Malankara historiography adds a second claim of continuity: that the Mar Thomases sprang from the Pakalomattam family, the ancient hereditary house of the archdeacons of the Thomas Christians, so that the line embodied not only valid orders but the unbroken native succession of the old archidiaconate. This Pakalomattam descent is, like the antiquity argument as a whole, essentially internal to the tradition; it is not pressed by the Anglican writers Philip elsewhere invokes, and rests on the same body of family and traditional testimony examined earlier. 29

Around 1680 Mar Thomas II, like his predecessor, sounded out the Carmelites about reunion and a Roman bishopric, and the negotiation failed; on the Catholic side Bishop Chandy, whose own dealings with the archdeaconate at this moment were bound up with his hope for just such a return, was among those who let it come to nothing, for reasons the next section will make clear. So the separated line continued, its orders disputed, even as Syrian prelates from abroad came, from time to time, to make the long voyage to Malabar. 30

The Coadjutor Rome Did Not Want

The Catholic side had a succession trouble of its own, and it ran the opposite way: not a bishop the apostolic world would not recognise, but a bishop nobody on the ground had asked for. Feeling his years, Parambil Chandy applied to Rome in 1674 for a coadjutor with right of succession. Propaganda agreed at once, and its instruction was emphatic on the one point that mattered: the four Carmelites it sent out in 1675 were to appoint as coadjutor a man who was, in the words of their orders, a native of the place and not a European. It was the obvious choice and the just one, the Serra had a capable indigenous clergy and a bishop of its own at last, and Rome meant the line to continue in native hands. 31

The men on the spot did the opposite. Only two of the four reached Malabar, and on 3 March 1676 they passed over every native candidate and designated instead Raphael de Figueredo Salgado, the Vicar General of the Latin diocese of Cochin, a man of Portuguese descent. Rome had said not a European; the Carmelites chose a Luso-Indian Latin. Tisserant, who is no critic of the missionaries, calls it a choice than which one could imagine none less happy, and asks whether the spirit of the Roman instruction had been followed at all; even the Bishop of Cochin and neutral observers thought the election precipitous and imprudent. Figueredo was not consecrated and enthroned, at Edappally, until 1683, and he proved a disaster, disobeying Chandy, alienating the very Carmelites who had imposed him, and governing so badly that from 1684 those same Carmelites were writing to Rome to be rid of him. 32

It was the foreign coadjutor, more than anything, that finally moved Chandy to raise up his own nephew, and here the puzzle of the previous section resolves itself. Chandy had not been hoarding the archdeaconate for Mathew; quite the reverse. For years he had deliberately left the office vacant, holding it open for his separated cousins, in the hope that Mar Thomas I, or after him Mar Thomas II, might return to Rome and resume the place that was theirs by the tradition of the direct line, a tradition Chandy was unwilling lightly to overturn. That hope, and not any dynastic design, is why for so long he had no archdeacon at all. What changed his mind was the Figueredo affair itself: the discontent that followed the Portuguese coadjutor's imposition left the Catholic community so unsettled that Chandy judged it could no longer be without an indigenous head, and in 1678 he at last appointed his nephew Mathew Pallil archdeacon, writing into the letters-patent the careful apology that he did so only for want of a subject in the direct line. When, a year or two later, reunion talks opened around 1680 with Mar Thomas II, the Carmelites offered to restore the separated prelate to the archdeaconate of the entire community. Although Mar Thomas II was not unwilling to consider this, the negotiation fell apart because Bishop Chandy intervened to protect his nephew's position, insisting on maintaining Mathew in the office. The irony was complete, and double-edged. Rome had desired a native bishop but got a Portuguese coadjutor because the Carmelites, fearing a hereditary succession, had passed over Chandy’s nephew in favour of a Latin Luso-Indian; Chandy, in turn, had spent years holding the archdeaconate open for his separated cousin on traditional hereditary grounds, only to block that cousin’s return in order to maintain his own nephew in the office. The question of who would give the Thomas Christians an indigenous leader of their own, which had once split the community, had collapsed into a messy, blocked transition. The government of the Nazranis of the Serra had become a tangle that no party could now cleanly resolve. 33

Parambil Chandy died in 1687, having ruled some twenty-four years and held the larger part of the old community to Rome under a bishop of its own blood; the sources give the year but neither the day nor any cause beyond age. By the unhappy logic of the 1676 appointment the discredited Figueredo succeeded him as vicar apostolic, and the complaints to Rome only multiplied. At length Propaganda sent Bishop Custodio de Pinho, vicar apostolic of the Great Mughal, to investigate, and on his report, which found Figueredo guilty of simony and of neglecting his flock, Rome suspended him in January 1694. He died the next year, in October 1695. The first experiment in giving the Catholic Thomas Christians a settled succession had ended in a foreigner suspended for simony, and the see passed, for the time being, into the hands of Chandy's nephew the archdeacon as administrator, until the Carmelites should return in force. 34

The Separated Sides

The separation of Serra was hardening into permanent institutional breach but it would be quite wrong to imagine the years after 1663 as decades of settled enmity between the two bodies of people. A surprising number of churches were simply shared between them, and individuals and whole families crossed from one side to the other as it suited them; the Carmelites' own day-book, kept at the mission from 1653 onward, records the traffic both ways. 35

The two Indian heads were, if anything, on cordial terms. A Carmelite complaint of 1683 accuses the Catholic archdeacon, Mathew Pallil, of kissing the hand of Mar Thomas II at Palai and otherwise fraternising with the schismatics; ten years later the Carmelites reported, with evident alarm, that the Puthankūr actually wished to make the Catholic archdeacon Mathew their own bishop, and that he might have accepted had they not talked him out of it. Bishop Chandy, for his part, thought well enough of his cousin's successor Mar Thomas II to name him among the men he judged worthy to succeed himself, in preference to the Latin Figueredo who had been forced on him. The cousins' war was real, but it was a family's war, and the family kept reaching across it. 36

The hostility often appears in the sources to have been directed toward the foreign ecclesiastics who stood behind each side. The Catholics despised the anti-Roman polemics of the Antiochian prelates, who, right up to the moment they reached the safety of Mar Thomas's people, had to travel through Malabar in disguise for fear of the Catholic party; the followers of Mar Thomas, for their part, are described as hatching constant plots against the Carmelites, who viewed them as schismatic subjects to be disciplined. Each side could live with its kinsmen on the other side; what neither could abide was the stranger, the European friar or the Syrian bishop, who had come to take charge of them. It is a revealing pattern, and it says much about what the whole long struggle had really been about: not the faith the two groups shared, but the question of who from outside would be allowed to rule a church that wished to be ruled by its own. 37

By the end of the century the rough proportions had settled. A report two Carmelite missionaries sent to Rome in 1698 counted the Thomas Christians at one hundred and eleven churches in all: sixty-seven held by the Catholics, thirty-two by the Orthodox, and twelve still shared, partly Catholic and partly Orthodox, between the two. Roughly two in three of the old community had stayed with Rome under their indigenous bishop; one in three had gone with Mar Thomas; and a stubborn remainder worshipped, impossibly, in both camps at once. 38

The Propaganda Returns

Through all these years the Catholics had never been wholly without European priests, but they held them on Dutch sufferance, one or two at a time. The Carmelite Matthew of St Joseph, whom Sebastiani had once left as his delegate, had been swept out with the rest in 1663 and was allowed back only in 1668, when the French consul at Aleppo commended him to the Dutch commandant of Cochin; he became Bishop Chandy's adviser, and in 1673 built a church and a small residence at Chathiath near Ernakulam. The chief Carmelite house came in time to be Verapoly, where in 1682 the fathers opened a little seminary for Syrian Catholic youths, though their pupils still went in greater numbers to the better Jesuit college at Ambazhakkad. But these were a handful of tolerated individuals in Dutch Malabar, not a mission with a head. The man who had founded it never returned. Sebastiani had reached Rome in 1665 bearing his report on the Indian Church; he hoped, when a truce was signed, that he might be sent back to the south, but he was refused, raised instead to a bishopric in Italy, and he died there in 1689, never having seen Malabar again. 39

What returned, was the mission rather than the man, and it returned only on the conquerors' terms. The Padroado bishops being barred from Dutch territory, Pope Innocent XII asked the Emperor Leopold I to treat with the government of the Netherlands, and in April 1698 an agreement was reached: the Holy See might send a Carmelite bishop and missionaries to Malabar, provided they were Belgians, Germans, or Italians, never Portuguese. It was the same exclusion that had governed the coast since 1663, now written into a treaty, the Dutch would suffer a Catholic mission, but not a Portuguese one. On that condition the Carmelites came back as a hierarchy: in February 1700 Rome named one of the Verapoly fathers, Angelo Francis of St Teresa, vicar apostolic of Malabar, and with him began the long line of Carmelite vicars apostolic who would govern the Catholic Thomas Christians for the next two centuries. 40

The Dutch conquest had broken Portugal's monopoly without resolving anything the monopoly had been quarrelling over. The question that had split the Coonan Cross, who might validly give the Thomas Christians a bishop of their own, stood exactly where it had. It would be pursued across the long Dutch peace by both churches, and by the competing Roman and Portuguese jurisdictions, in a rivalry that began with Angelo Francis's contested elevation at Alangad and would eventually drive the native Catholic clergy, decades later, to rise in open revolt against their Carmelite rulers at Angamaly. Into that inferno the narrative now moves, as the next chapter opens.

— End of Chapter 13: The Trade Companies of War —

This chapter deliberately rewinds behind the close of Chapter 12 to recover the wider context of the Dutch conquest, narrated there only from the vantage of the Serra. For the events of 1663 as they bore on the Thomas Christians, see Chapter 12 above and the sources gathered at its note 29.

On the Vereenigde Oostindische Compagnie as an armed trading corporation chartered in 1602, the Calvinist and anti-Spanish character of the Dutch revolt, and the way the Iberian Union of the crowns of Spain and Portugal (1580–1640) made Portuguese Asia a target in the Eighty Years' War: Boxer, The Dutch Seaborne Empire, 1600–1800, 1–7 (the Eighty Years' War and the Calvinist oligarchies who launched the war of trade), 84 (the Company's principle that there could be neither trade without war nor war without trade).

On the shared assumption of Calvinist Dutch and Catholic Portuguese alike that European Christians might dispose of the trade and territory of peoples beyond Christendom, the religion supplying the warrant, and on Rijkloff van Goens's admission of 1655 that the Dutch were hated throughout Asia: Boxer, 233–34 (paraphrased; van Goens at 84, 234).

On the chartering of the VOC in 1602, the monopoly and sovereign powers granted it by the States-General (to wage war, build forts, conclude treaties, and govern in Asia), and its early concentration on the spices of the Moluccas and the islands, taken in large part at Portuguese expense: Koot, The VOC, the Dutch East India Company, 1602–1799, 5–7; Boxer, The Dutch Seaborne Empire, 1–7. Koot is a concise teaching synthesis and is used here only for the Company's general structure and trajectory; the narrative weight rests on Boxer and on Alexander (note 14 below).

On Jan Pieterszoon Coen's establishment of the permanent Asian headquarters at Batavia in 1619 and the building of the VOC's inter-Asian trading system (Coromandel cloth, Persian silk, Japanese silver, Chinese porcelain, island spices), sustained by continuous war against the Portuguese: Koot, 9–11; Boxer, 96.

On the Dutch campaign for Ceylon and its cinnamon from the 1630s, the taking of Colombo and Jaffna, the completion of the conquest of coastal Ceylon by 1658, and the hiring of soldiers discharged after the general European peace of 1648 for the Asian wars: Boxer, Appendices, 297–98 (chronology, conquest of coastal Ceylon 1654–8); Koot, 11. That the Malabar expedition was launched from Ceylon and reinforced with Ceylonese troops under van Goens: Alexander, The Dutch in Malabar, 35 (the second siege of Cochin, van Goens arriving "from Ceylon with a body of native Ceylon troops").

On the proclamation of the Duke of Braganza as King John IV of Portugal in December 1640, the end of the sixty-year union with the Spanish crown, and the readiness of the States-General and the Stadtholder to treat the restored kingdom as a useful diversion against Spain in the Low Countries: Boxer, 84–85.

On the memorial of the Heeren XVII to the States-General of May 1641, arguing that the Company had grown great by fighting the Portuguese and won its Asian monopoly thereby, that a beaten Portugal would soon revive as a competitor, and that any truce concluded in Europe should leave the East Indies exempt as the Twelve Years' Truce of 1609–21 had been: Boxer, 86. The chapter epigraph is drawn from this memorial as reproduced there.

On the ten-year truce negotiated at The Hague in June 1641, the prolonged evasion of its implementation in Asia by Governor-General van Diemen and the council at Batavia, the policy of seizing Portuguese territory before any peace could take effect, and the Dutch general's reported refusal at Galle to surrender a fort on the ground that he served the Company and not the Prince or the States: Boxer, 85–88.

On the founding of the English East India Company in 1600, two years before the VOC, on the same model of a royal monopoly over a nation's Asian trade, and on its subordinate position in the East through the first half of the seventeenth century: Koot, 5, 12–13; Boxer, 337.

On the combined Anglo-French attack on the Dutch republic in 1672, its damage to VOC shipping, and the consequent rise of the English company's share of the European pepper trade through the 1670s, anticipating the later eighteenth-century reversal in which the English became the paramount European power in India: Koot, 12–13. The full working-out of that reversal on the Malabar coast (the decline of Dutch influence after the Treaty of Mavelikkara of 1753) is treated by Alexander, The Dutch in Malabar, and lies beyond the present chapter.

On Malabar as the chief pepper-producing region and the object of a long-sought Dutch monopoly, the Dutch squadron of 1604 and its alliance with the Zamorin of Calicut against the Portuguese, and the decision to consolidate the Indonesian archipelago and Ceylon (conquest of coastal Ceylon completed 1658) before turning on the Kerala coast: Thekkedath, History of Christianity in India, vol. II, 20; on the pepper trade and the limits of the Dutch monopoly, Boxer, 198.

On Rijkloff van Goens as commander of the Malabar campaign and governor of the conquered coast, the fall of Quilon in December 1661 and of Kodungallur (Cranganore) in January 1662: Thekkedath, vol. II, 20; Tisserant, Eastern Christianity in India, chronology at 200–01; on van Goens, Boxer, 84, 233–34. Tisserant and Thekkedath render his name as General Rickloff and General Rijkloff respectively.

On the failed first Dutch attempt on Cochin in early 1662 and van Goens's withdrawal in March leaving garrisons at Cranganore and Quilon, the Portuguese neglect of their defences thereafter, and the second and larger expedition that appeared before Cochin on 25 October 1662 (eleven ships from Batavia, van Goens arriving from Ceylon with native Ceylonese troops): Alexander, The Dutch in Malabar, 19–20.

On the conduct of the second siege, the Dutch batteries of some twenty-eight guns sited by the churches of St John, St Thomas, and Calvetty, the opening bombardment of the pepper warehouse on 31 December 1662, the Portuguese commander Ignatio Sarmento's refusal of terms, the intervention and rout of the Raja of Porakkad's Nair troops, the final assault on the Calvetty side on 6 January 1663, Sarmento's surrender under a white flag and delivery of the keys the following morning, and the Dutch casualties (some 360 dead): Alexander, The Dutch in Malabar, 20–21. Alexander draws on Sebastiani's eyewitness account and on Tavernier, noting their divergence over the conduct of the capitulation.

On the terms of capitulation (the garrison marching out with flying colours, drums beating and fuses lit, to lay down arms beyond the walls; the unmarried Portuguese to Europe, the maimed to Goa; the clergy permitted their images and ornaments save those of gold and silver), and on the surrender of Cannanore, the last Portuguese hold on the coast, to Jacob Hustaart on 13 February 1663: Alexander, The Dutch in Malabar, 21–22. The figure of some four thousand Portuguese and Eurasians removed to Goa, with the larger topass and Indian-Christian population remaining, is given by Thekkedath, vol. II, 118.

On the Calvinism of the new masters and the scope of the expulsion: the Dutch ordered the withdrawal of the European missionary clergy from their conquered territory, the Carmelite vicar apostolic Sebastiani and the Jesuits of Cochin and Cranganore (who withdrew into the Zamorin’s dominions and founded the college of Ambazhakkad), some Franciscans remaining at churches between Quilon and Purakkad and the rest retiring to Goa: Thekkedath, vol. II, 100, 108; Tisserant, 86. That the order did not touch the indigenous Catholic bishop is shown by the Dutch conduct toward Parambil Chandy, whom van Goens undertook to favour while refusing all recognition to the Archdeacon (Tisserant, 86–87). That the expulsion was geographic rather than total: the Dutch bar ran only in “the territories which were under their direct or indirect control” (Thekkedath, vol. II, 107), a coastal sliver of Cochin and the forts, while the bulk of the Thomas Christians lived inland and southward under independent rajas (Cochin, Vadakkumkur, Thekkumkur, Purakkad and the rest), beyond Dutch reach. Hence a thin Catholic clergy survived in the interior throughout: the sidelined Figueredo, for instance, resided at the inland town of Palai (Thekkedath, vol. II, 106), and the Carmelite houses lay landward, at Chathiath near Ernakulam and then Verapoly (Thekkedath, vol. II, 108). What the Dutch barred was a European, and above all a Portuguese, bishop exercising authority in their own zone, so that the Padroado sees of Cranganore and Cochin, though never suppressed by Rome, could not be governed; this is the same national exclusion that shaped the eventual Carmelite return; see note 40 below. Neill II, 59, independently confirms the arrangement: the Portuguese went on appointing bishops of Cochin and Cranganore, but since the Dutch would permit no Portuguese bishop to reside in the territory they controlled the appointments were “little more than nominal,” while the Carmelites won the tolerance of the Dutch and kept up a continuous line of vicars apostolic, so that “effective authority in the Serra was in the hands of the Italian Carmelites.”

On the names and kinship: the Archdeacon raised up in 1653 was Thomas (Mar Thomas I), and the bishop consecrated in 1663 was his first cousin, the cattanar Parambil Chandy of Kuravilangad, latinised in the Carmelite and Padroado records as Alexander de Campo. On the hereditary character of the archdeaconate, descending within a single Kuravilangad line of which both men were members: Thekkedath, vol. II, 106 (the office of archdeacon hereditary in the direct line of Mar Thoma's family); and the contemporary Jesuit and Carmelite record, which attests the office, an uncle-to-nephew succession (George of the Cross the nephew of George of Christ; Thomas the nephew of George of the Cross), and a family it names not Pakalomattam but de Campo, that is Parambil (Sebastiani to Propaganda, 20 March 1681, APF SOCG vol. 493, ff. 61–62). The familiar identification of this line as the Pakalomattam family is a later, traditional ascription rather than a usage of the seventeenth-century European sources, which name the house Parambil/de Campo; the question is treated in full, with the Germann, padiyola, and Kollaparambil evidence, in Chapter 6 above, "The Archdeacon and the Pakalomattam Question." On Parambil Chandy as one of the four cattanars appointed councillors to the Archdeacon after the Coonan Cross oath: Thekkedath, vol. II, 94; Kollaparambil, The Archdeacon of All India, 59–62.

On Parambil Chandy's early defection from his cousin the Archdeacon, his judgement that the position of a bishop consecrated by twelve priests was untenable, his work to bring cattanars and parishes back to the obedience of the apostolic commissaries, and the persecution he suffered from the schismatic party for refusing to support the Archdeacon: Thekkedath, vol. II, 100, drawing on the Carmelite reports of the two missions (ARSI Goa 68; APF SOCG vols. 232, 234). On the slow, mission-by-mission character of the separation across Sebastiani's first (1657–58) and second (1661–63) missions, see also Tisserant, 81–86.

On the divergent portraits of Anjilimoottil Ittithommen (Itty Thoman Catanar). The Catholic account, in which he administered the laying-on of hands by which the Archdeacon was made bishop in 1653 and was excommunicated by Sebastiani in 1663, follows Thekkedath, vol. II, 92–100, and Tisserant, 82–87. The Puthankūr tradition, in which he is the loyal and resourceful counsellor of Mar Thomas, is given fullest expression in E. M. Philip, The Indian Church of St. Thomas (1908), ch. 14, which reproduces a letter of Itty Thoman to Kadavil Chandy asserting that the Patriarch of Antioch "being the Lord of Malankara" had always supplied the church's bishops, and attributes to him the broken-stick prophecy of the Coonan Cross and the stratagem by which he and Mar Thomas escaped Portuguese captivity. It must be said, however, that Philip supplies no independently verifiable source for the letter: by his own note it is a translation made from a copy in "an old vernacular manuscript of Chronicles in the possession of the author," that is, a privately held manuscript not open to third-party examination and not corroborated by any contemporary record. The document cannot therefore be weighed as the contemporary attestation it is taken for in the tradition. The letter is, for Philip, evidence that the Malabar Church always understood itself to be in communion with Antioch (with the Nestorian patriarch's authority received only as Antioch's deputy); the academic historians read the same period as one of East Syrian, not West Syrian, allegiance. On the historiographical divergence generally, see note 28 below.

On the contest for the cattanars and its incidents: the merchant (a pepper trader recently arrived at Cochin) suborned by the Archdeacon's party to pose as the nephew of a newly elected pope and to present a forged brief confirming Thomas as archbishop and patriarch, the document being exhibited at meetings held in kingdoms then at war with Cochin and the Portuguese so that the rival commissary could not expose it; the failed attempt of 9 October 1661 to seize the Archdeacon and the cattanar Anjilimoottil Ittithommen, who escaped disguised as Hindu soldiers; Sebastiani's consecration of Parambil Chandy as Vicar Apostolic of Malabar on 31 January 1663 in the brief interval before his enforced departure; the solemn excommunication of the Archdeacon and Ittithommen on 1 February 1663; and the assurance Sebastiani obtained from General Rijkloff van Goens that the Dutch would favour Bishop Chandy over the Archdeacon: Thekkedath, vol. II, 99–100; Tisserant, 86–87; and Chapter 12 above, note 29. On the significance of the title "vicar apostolic," a papal delegate governing through Propaganda Fide rather than a diocesan bishop of the Padroado, and on the archbishopric of Cranganore (Kodungallur) remaining an unsuppressed Padroado see to which Portugal retained the right of nomination even while it could not be governed under the Dutch: Thekkedath, vol. II, 107; Neill II, 59. The latent conflict between the two jurisdictions is taken up in the following chapter.

On the Dutch keeping, for a time, their promise to favour Bishop Chandy despite the Archdeacon's efforts to turn them against him; on the decisive advantage of Chandy's undoubted consecration against the invalidity of Mar Thomas I's consecration by twelve priests (a consecration that neither Latin nor Eastern canon law could admit, since in the universal discipline of the Catholic and Orthodox churches alike only a bishop can consecrate a bishop); and on the steady submission of cattanars and parishes to Chandy in the first years of his episcopate: Thekkedath, vol. II, 100–01 (citing the letter of Fr Braz de Azevedo SJ, Ambazhakkad, 28 July 1666, LDSC vol. II, 188); Tisserant, 141–42.

On Mar Thomas I's discouragement at the loss of parishes and the silence of the Eastern patriarchs, and his secret approach to the Jesuits at Ambazhakkad towards the end of 1665 offering submission to the Catholic Church provided it could be effected without humiliation, the negotiations advancing to the point where an end to the schism seemed near: Thekkedath, vol. II, 100–01 (citing APF SOCG vol. 234 and the Ambazhakkad Jesuit correspondence). The episode survives principally in Jesuit and Propaganda correspondence and receives little attention in subsequent Malankara historical traditions.

On the arrival of Mar Gregorios in 1665, the Metropolitan of Jerusalem (Tisserant, 142): Mar Thomas's abandonment of the Jesuit negotiation on news of the landing, the welcome at Idappally, the presentation of Gregorios as a patriarch with apostolic authority, Ittithommen's production of a brief read from the pulpit, and the collection of some five thousand fanams: Thekkedath, vol. II, 101–02 (citing the letter of Fr Braz de Azevedo SJ, 28 July 1666). On the appeals east, the evidence is looser than the standard account allows. Thekkedath, vol. II, 100, has Mar Thomas discouraged by the indifference of "several eastern patriarchs," but the one appeal he footnotes to a document (APF SOCG vol. 234, ff. 350, 358–9) is a Syriac letter addressed to the Catholic patriarch of Chaldea, that is, to the East Syrian, Rome-aligned side, not to the Jacobite patriarch of Antioch. That Gregorios came in response to a request to the Jacobite patriarch is Tisserant's inference (142, "tends to explain his request to the Jacobite patriarch"), not a documented fact; and the irony that a man petitioning the Catholic Chaldean patriarch, while simultaneously negotiating submission to Rome, should receive an avowedly anti-Roman Antiochian prelate is consistent with Thekkedath's picture of a Mar Thomas casting about in several directions at once. On Gregorios reaching Malabar because the Portuguese sea-watch (which had carried off Ahatallah) was broken and no guard was kept at Ormuz, Tisserant, 142. That he came with Dutch connivance is likely but not documented for 1665: the new masters were hostile to Rome and had no motive to stop an anti-Roman Syrian, Ahatallah himself had reached Mylapore from Surat on a Dutch ship in 1652 (Neill I, 316), and later Antiochian prelates would certainly come on Dutch vessels, though the sources do not record the Dutch actively assisting Gregorios's own passage. On the permanent Puthankūr/Pazhayakūr division dating effectively from this event, Thekkedath, vol. II, 102.

On Gregorios proceeding with caution because many still thought him sent by the Pope, Mar Thomas persuading him to adopt the local rite (he declined only unleavened bread), and his restoration of pre-Diamper customs (fermented host, traditional vestments, the old calendar) common to West Syrians and Chaldeans; on his letter of 5 May 1668 to the priests of Mulanthuruthy, Kandanad, and Parur denouncing union with the "idolatrous Romans" and the latinisers' compulsory celibacy; and on the assessment that his programme was less to impose Antiochian distinctives than to de-latinise the community: Thekkedath, vol. II, 102 (quoting the 1668 letter via Malancharuvil's rendering of E. M. Philip); Tisserant, 142–43; Neill I, 327–28. On the Severian Monophysite professions and the anathematisation of Nestorius imposed at this time, Tisserant, 142.

On the contested question of Mar Thomas I's orders: later Puthankūr historians and Malankara traditions (e.g., E. M. Philip, The Indian Church of St. Thomas, 1908) assert that Mar Gregorios conferred valid episcopal consecration on him in 1665. Among Catholic and academic historians, there is a divergence: Eugene Tisserant (142) and Stephen Neill (Neill I, 328) maintain that no consecration took place, drawing on contemporary Carmelite and Jesuit letters where Thomas is styled a "pseudo-bishop" (Tisserant judging that "Thomas did not receive, it seems, the episcopal character he had been desiring for so long"). Conversely, Jacob Kollaparambil (The Archdeacon of All India, 181–182) accepts as a historical fact that Mar Gregorios did lay hands on Mar Thomas I in 1665, regularizing his episcopal character, though from the Roman Catholic canonical viewpoint, this regularization was considered illicit and schismatic. However, Kollaparambil joins Tisserant (143 n. 4) and Neill in rejecting the validity of the subsequent successions (Mar Thomas II, III, etc.), viewing them as having governed without valid episcopal orders. Thekkedath, vol. II, 102–103, records the 1665 consecration as a contested claim. The present account frames the validity as historically disputed, acknowledging that almost all contemporary documents derive from partisan Latin archives. On the deaths of Mar Gregorios (1671, at North Parur) and Mar Thomas I (22 April 1673): Thekkedath, vol. II, 102; Tisserant, 143. Neill I, 328, dates Gregory's death to 1672 and notes that the date of Mar Thomas I's death is uncertain, though he records Germann's date of 1673 as probably correct.

On the Pakalomattam claim within Malankara historiography. Alongside the validity of the Mar Thomas line, the tradition holds the line to have descended from the Pakalomattam family, the hereditary house from which, on this account, the archdeacons of the Thomas Christians had been drawn since antiquity: E. M. Philip, The Indian Church of St. Thomas, makes the family a central thread, devoting a chapter (ch. 19, "The Extinction of the Pakalomattam Family") to it and reporting the tradition, given in a Mar Thoma's reply to a government enquiry, that for the 1,308 years from A.D. 345 the Pakalomattam family had supplied the archdeacons in unbroken succession; Philip himself concedes there are "historical inaccuracies" in that reply, while affirming the family's antiquity and centrality. The claim is not corroborated by the Anglican writers Philip elsewhere cites: neither Buchanan, Christian Researches in Asia, nor Hough, The History of Christianity in India, who treat the archdeacon as an office and nowhere assert a hereditary Pakalomattam house. It rests on the same family and traditional testimony, of uncertain and largely uncorroborated provenance, discussed in Chapter 6 above ("The Archdeacon and the Pakalomattam Question") and in note 20 above; the seventeenth-century European records name the archidiaconal house not Pakalomattam but Parambil (de Campo).

On the two historiographies of 1665. For the West Syrian (Antiochian) connection as substantially new, the prior allegiance of the Thomas Christians having been East Syrian: Neill I, 319–331; Tisserant, 89–96; Mundadan, vol. I; Thekkedath, vol. II, 96–110. For the contrary, "revival" reading, that Jacobite orthodoxy long predated 1665 and Gregorios restored rather than introduced it: E. M. Philip (Edavazhickal), The Indian Church of St. Thomas, first published posthumously by the author's son, Adv. Chev. E. P. Mathew (Kottayam, 1908); cited here from the second English edition, ed. K. C. Moolayil (Changanacherry: Mor Adai Study Centre, 2002), ch. 16 ("Revival of Jacobitism: Mar Gregorius and Mar Thoma I"), invoking Renaudot's date of 696 for an earlier introduction of Jacobitism and reproducing a 1668 encyclical of Mar Gregorios. On Philip's standpoint: he wrote as Secretary of the Malankara Jacobite Syrian Association and as the conductor of the Syrian Church litigation in Travancore (Philip, Author's Preface), and his history is in part an apologetic for the antiquity and continuity of the traditionalist party in a live succession dispute. For his distinctive claims he leans, by his own account (Philip, Author's Preface), on family diaries and privately held manuscripts and on local tradition, sources not open to third-party verification. On the confessional character of the Western authorities he relies upon: Philip lists Claudius Buchanan, James Hough, and John Mason Neale among his Western corroborators (Philip, Author's Preface); all were Anglicans writing in the early nineteenth century within a tradition of sympathy for the Syrians as fellow opponents of Rome, antedating the Church Missionary Society's intervention and the consequent reformed schism of the 1830s. They do not constitute the neutral counterweight to the supposedly biased Catholic sources that Philip's argument implies. Buchanan's Christian Researches in Asia (1811) is the influential early statement of that sympathy. The present narrative accordingly prefers, among Protestant historians, the later academic work of Neill.

The words quoted are Buchanan's report of the Syrian Christians, given to him on his visit of 1806 and printed in his Christian Researches in Asia (1811), in the section on the Syrian Church; Buchanan there cites his own earlier Account of the Syrian Christians (Calcutta, 1807), 3. Buchanan adds, tellingly for the later use made of him, that the Syrians are "usually denominated Jacobites" but differ in ceremonial from the church of that name in Syria and from any existing church, and that, the patriarchate of Antioch being then nearly extinct, the Malabar Church might henceforth look to Britain, a hope that prefigures the Anglican and later C.M.S. involvement noted above. Philip reproduces the passage as independent corroboration of the Syrians' anti-Nestorian antiquity; this synthesis treats Buchanan, like Hough and Neale, as a sympathetic Anglican witness rather than a neutral one.

On the succession after Mar Thomas I: his brother's burial of him in pontifical vestments and his own death within days; the accession of the nephew as Mar Thomas II (ruled to 1686), who is reported to have conferred orders and consecrated the holy oils though himself unconsecrated; the Carmelite report of 15 March 1681 of his consecration by one Mar Andreas, said by the Carmelites to be not a bishop but a priest of Aleppo; his abortive reunion overture to the Carmelites around 1680, frustrated in part by Bishop Chandy's concern to preserve the archdeaconate for his nephew Mathew (appointed 1678); and the passing of the line to Mar Thomas III, a layman until ordained: Thekkedath, vol. II, 103; Tisserant, 143 and n. 4. Neill I, 328, also notes that Thomas was succeeded by a line of "shadowy heads" who all took the name Thomas, and reports the tradition that Mar Thomas III was a layman at his accession and was consecrated by twelve cattanars. Tisserant, 143 n. 4, Neill I, 328, and Kollaparambil all reflect the Western historical view that none of these subsequent early Mar Thomases received valid consecration, whereas the Malankara historical tradition asserts the continuity and validity of their office, supported by subsequent regularizations by visiting Eastern prelates.

On Parambil Chandy's request to Rome in 1674 for a coadjutor with right of succession, Propaganda's acceptance, and the dispatch of four Carmelites in 1675 with instructions to appoint a coadjutor who was "either a secular priest or a religious who, however, is a native of the place, not a European": Tisserant, 87–88; Thekkedath, vol. II, 105. Neill I 329 also covers the coadjutor dispute, noting that Chandy wished to appoint his nephew Matthew but was blocked by the Carmelites.

On the designation, on 3 March 1676, not of a Syro-Malabar but of Raphael de Figueredo Salgado, Vicar General of the Latin diocese of Cochin and a Latin of Portuguese extraction; on Tisserant's verdict ("one cannot imagine a less happy choice") and his question whether the spirit of the Roman instructions had been followed; on the judgement of Bishop Castro and the Theatine Fr Carlo Ferrarini that the election was precipitous and imprudent; on Figueredo's enthronement at Edappally in 1683; and on the Carmelites turning against their own appointee from 1684 and petitioning Rome against him: Tisserant, 87–88; Thekkedath, vol. II, 105–06. Tisserant, 88, dates Figueredo's designation to 3 March 1676, whereas Thekkedath, vol. II, 105, dates it to 3 March 1677.

On Chandy's long delay in filling the archdeaconate (hoping for the return of his separated cousins and reluctant to overturn the hereditary tradition of Mar Thoma's family), his appointment of his nephew Mathew Pallil as archdeacon in 1678, the declaration in the letters-patent that he did so "because of the absence of a subject in the direct line," and how this appointment subsequently spoiled the 1680 reunion proposal by preventing Mar Thomas II from receiving the archdeaconate as offered by the Carmelites: Thekkedath, vol. II, 103–105. Note that Tisserant, 88, mentions only the Carmelite refusal to appoint the bishop's nephew "Matthew de Campo" as coadjutor, while Neill I 329 reports only that Chandy's wish to appoint Matthew as coadjutor was blocked by the Carmelites, with neither author mentioning the archdeaconate vacancy, Mathew's 1678 appointment as archdeacon, or his role in blocking the 1680 reunion. On the later Carmelite support for Mathew (from 1684) and the Puthankūr wish to make him their own bishop (1693), see notes 35 and 36 below.

On the death of Parambil Chandy (Alexander de Campo) in 1687, after a rule of some twenty-four years: Thekkedath, vol. II, 106; the sources give the year only, with no precise date or cause recorded (Tisserant, 87, notes a Syriac psalter once his, with marginalia for 1660–70, now Bibliothèque Nationale, Paris, syr. 25 suppl. 72). On Figueredo succeeding him as vicar apostolic; the continued complaints; the investigation by Custodio de Pinho, vicar apostolic of the Great Mughal; the finding of simony and neglect; and Figueredo's suspension and death: Thekkedath, vol. II, 106 (suspension in January 1694; Figueredo died 12 October 1695). Tisserant, 88, places de Pinho's commission and Figueredo's suspension in "January 1684," but this cannot be reconciled with Figueredo's succession to the vicariate on Chandy's death in 1687, and Thekkedath's January 1694 is to be preferred. On Archdeacon Mathew administering the archdiocese in the interval: Thekkedath, vol. II, 106.

On the division being one of persons and prestige rather than doctrine, the two sides continuing to regard themselves as one community, the sharing of churches, and the passage of individuals and families between the two: Thekkedath, vol. II, 108–09, drawing on the "Narrazione" of Fr Ildefonso, the Carmelite mission day-book kept from 1653 to 1740 (Ambrosius a S. Theresia, 202, 208–09).

On the cordiality of the two Indian heads: the Carmelite document of 1683 accusing the Catholic archdeacon Mathew Pallil of kissing the hand of Mar Thomas II at Palai (Kollaparambil, The Archdeacon of All India, 208, citing the letter of 18 April 1683 in APF SOCG vol. 496, ff. 115–16); the Carmelite report of 28 October 1693 that the Puthankūr wished to make Archdeacon Mathew their bishop, which he might have accepted but for Carmelite dissuasion (Kollaparambil, 213, citing APF SOCG vol. 520, ff. 136–7); and Bishop Chandy's inclusion of Mar Thomas II among those he thought worthy to succeed him rather than Figueredo (Kollaparambil, 198 n. 23, citing Chandy to Propaganda, 9 January 1679, APF SC[IOC] vol. 2, f. 90). All three are reported in Thekkedath, vol. II, 109.

On the hatred reserved for the foreign leaders of the opposite party: the Antiochian prelates detested by the Catholics and obliged to travel in disguise until they reached Mar Thomas's people (Malancharuvil, The Syro-Malankara Church, 29, citing E. M. Philip and G. B. Howard), and the "constant plots" of Mar Thomas's followers against the Carmelites (Ambrosius a S. Theresia, 209): Thekkedath, vol. II, 109.

On the strength of the two parties at the end of the century, from the report of two Carmelite missionaries to Propaganda in 1698: of 111 churches in all, 67 Catholic, 32 Orthodox, and 12 partly Catholic and partly Orthodox: Thekkedath, vol. II, 107–08 (APF, Congregationi Particolari, vol. 30, f. 807). On the national exclusion governing the Carmelite return, see note 40 below.

On the continued presence of a few Carmelites and Jesuits among the Syrian Catholics: Matthew of St Joseph, Sebastiani's former delegate, expelled with the other Europeans in 1663 and readmitted only in 1668 on the recommendation of the French consul at Aleppo to the Dutch commandant of Cochin, after which he served as Bishop Chandy's adviser and in 1673 built a church and residence at Chathiath near Ernakulam (Thekkedath, vol. II, 107; Tisserant, 87); the rise of Verapoly as the chief Carmelite residence and the small seminary opened there in 1682, whose pupils nonetheless mostly attended the Jesuit college at Ambazhakkad: Thekkedath, vol. II, 108. On Sebastiani himself: his arrival at Rome in 1665 with his report, his brief and disappointed hope of returning south after the Luso-Dutch truce, his elevation to an Italian bishopric, and his death in 1689 without seeing Malabar again: Tisserant, 87 and the Carmelite hierarchical sources there cited.

On the agreement of April 1698, negotiated by Pope Innocent XII through the Emperor Leopold I with the government of the Netherlands, by which the Holy See might send to Malabar a Carmelite bishop and missionaries provided they were Belgians, Germans, or Italians and not Portuguese; and on the consequent appointment of Angelo Francis of St Teresa as vicar apostolic of Malabar in February 1700, beginning the line of Carmelite vicars apostolic who governed the Catholic Thomas Christians until 1887: Thekkedath, vol. II, 107; Tisserant, chronology at 200 (Angelus Francis nominated 1700). That the Padroado sees of Cranganore and Cochin were never suppressed by Rome but could not be governed under Dutch rule, being administered in the interim by ecclesiastical governors and vicars-general (Cranganore until 1701, Cochin until 1693) rather than by resident bishops: Thekkedath, vol. II, 107; Tisserant, 88 n. The 1698 agreement therefore restored not a Catholic clergy, which had never wholly left the native interior, but a Catholic bishop able to govern. Neill I, 331, gives the vivid measure of the same fact: the Portuguese bishop of Cochin, Peter Pacheco (from 1699), resided “in the southern part of his diocese where the Dutch writ did not run,” and of the three Portuguese prelates in India at the turn of the century “none was permitted by the Dutch to reside anywhere in the Serra”; a later bishop of Cochin, the Jesuit Francis Vasconcellos, held the see for twenty years from 1722 “without ever being able to reside within the limits of his diocese” (Neill II, 59).

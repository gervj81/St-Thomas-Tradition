The Global War and the Fall of Cochin, 1640–1663

The Company's War

"The Honourable Company had waxed great through fighting the Portuguese."

— The Heeren XVII to the States-General, 1641

Chapter 12 closed in a harbour under guns. Joseph Sebastiani, ordered out of Malabar by a Dutch commander who had just taken Cochin, used the few days he had left to consecrate Mar Alexander de Campo, so that the reconciled Thomas Christians would not be left without a bishop of their own before he sailed. The scene belongs as much to the history of European empire as to the history of the Serra, and it leaves a question the last chapter set aside. How had a Dutch fleet come to command the Portuguese harbour on the Malabar coast, with the power to expel a Roman bishop at ten days' notice? To answer it the narrative must rewind a little, and widen a great deal. 1

The guns in Cochin harbour were the local end of a war that had been running, on and off, for sixty years and across four continents. It had been fought in the North Sea and the Baltic, off Brazil and Angola, in the Spice Islands, the Persian Gulf, and the approaches to Goa. In its scale and its simultaneity it was something close to the first global war, waged less by a kingdom than by a chartered company that kept its own fleets, fortresses, and treaties, and that had grown rich by fighting the very empire whose richest possessions it now meant to inherit.

This chapter tells the fall of Portuguese Malabar from the conquerors' side. It is the story of how a Calvinist trading corporation, a restored Portuguese crown, and a peace that arrived too late converged on a single pepper port in the first weeks of 1663, and of what their convergence meant for a divided church that had no part in the quarrel and no power to escape its outcome.

A War Without Borders

The Dutch East India Company, the Vereenigde Oostindische Compagnie (VOC), was not a merchant house that occasionally fought. It was an instrument of war that happened to trade. In Chapter 10, we saw Charles V partitioning his massive domains in 1556, bequeathing the Netherlands, along with Spain, the Italian possessions, and the Indies, to his son, the Habsburg king Philip II. Philip II's sustained military repression of the Dutch Protestants ignited a long rebellion against Habsburg rule, a struggle remembered as the Eighty Years' War. Forged in the middle of this revolt, VOC was chartered in 1602 by the States-General (the sovereign federal assembly of the Dutch Republic) and operated under the strategic umbrella of the Stadtholder (the republic's chief executive and military commander). It carried the Dutch revolt into Asian waters as a matter of deliberate policy. Since the crowns of Spain and Portugal had been joined under a single Habsburg sovereign from the Iberian Union of 1580, the VOC's Calvinist directors, the Heeren XVII (or the "Lords Seventeen"), viewed every Portuguese fort, carrack, and spice contract east of the Cape as a legitimate target in a global war that was at once holy and profitable. 2

The Heeren understood the fusion of faith and profit without embarrassment. They shared with their Catholic Portuguese rivals the conviction that European Christians might dispose of the trade and the territory of every people beyond the pale of Christendom; they differed only in holding that the warrant lay with the Reformed religion rather than with Rome. Nor were they under any illusion about how their rule was received. The Company's most formidable commander in these waters would acknowledge, as early as 1655, that the Dutch were detested by every nation in Asia, an admission that did nothing whatever to slow the conquests. 3

The Long Road East

The Company had not arrived on the Malabar coast in a single stride. Chartered in 1602 out of a clutch of competing Dutch ventures, it had been granted by the States-General a sweeping warrant: a monopoly of all Dutch trade east of the Cape, and with it the right to raise armies, build forts, conclude treaties with Asian rulers, and wage war in the republic's name. It was, from its founding charter, a sovereign power in commercial dress. Its first object was the spice of the Indonesian archipelago, the cloves, nutmeg, and mace of the Moluccas and the pepper of the islands, and it took that spice in large part by prising the Portuguese loose from the trade they had dominated for a century. 4

From 1619 the Company had a capital in Asia and a will to match. In that year Jan Pieterszoon Coen established its permanent Asian headquarters at Batavia, in western Java, and set about building the inter-Asian trading system that would become the engine of Dutch wealth in the East: Indian cloth from the Coromandel coast exchanged for island spices, Persian silk, Japanese silver, Chinese porcelain, the whole woven into a network whose profits could pay for the cargoes sent home to Amsterdam. To build it the Company fought continuously, and chiefly against the Portuguese, whose forts strung along the sea-roads from Mozambique to Macao stood athwart every route it wished to control. 5

The road that brought a Dutch fleet to Cochin ran through Ceylon. The island's cinnamon was a prize to rival the pepper of Malabar, and from the 1630s the Company pressed the Portuguese hard for it, taking Colombo and Jaffna and completing the conquest of the Ceylonese littoral by 1658. That conquest mattered for Malabar in the most practical way: it placed a Dutch base, with troops, ships, and a hardened command, directly across the short sea-passage from the Kerala coast. When the general peace in Europe in 1648 freed thousands of discharged soldiers for Asian service, Batavia had both the men and the forward base it needed. From Ceylon the Company could reach out and take the Portuguese pepper ports one by one, and the commander who had finished the Ceylon war was the same man it now turned loose on Malabar. 6

The Other Company

The Dutch were not alone on the water. Two years before the VOC received its charter, the English had founded their own East India Company, in 1600, on the same model of a royal monopoly over a nation's whole Asian trade. Through the first half of the seventeenth century the English company was the lesser power in the East, edged out of the spice islands by Dutch force and content, for the most part, to build its trade in Indian cloth and, increasingly, in pepper. It was a rival the VOC could afford to disdain, and largely did. 7

That would change within a generation of Cochin's fall. When England and France fell upon the Dutch republic together in 1672, the war in Europe cut into the VOC's shipping and gave the English company its opening in Asia; its share of the European pepper trade climbed steeply through the 1670s. The point is worth marking here only because the two companies' fortunes would, in the next century, decide the fate of the coast this chapter is about: the same English company that fought for pepper in the 1670s would, by the later eighteenth century, be the paramount European power in India, and the Dutch hold on Malabar would dwindle to a memory. For the moment, though, in the first weeks of 1663, it was the Dutch who held the guns, and the Portuguese who were about to lose everything. 8

The King Who Changed Sides

Into this long war came a sudden complication. In December 1640 the Duke of Braganza was proclaimed King John IV of Portugal, and sixty years of union with the Spanish crown were over. Overnight Portugal ceased to be an appendage of the Dutch republic's chief enemy and became instead a possible ally against it, a kingdom with its own pressing reasons to see Spain weakened. In Europe the logic was plain enough: a fresh front against the Habsburgs in the Iberian peninsula was worth a truce with Lisbon, and the States-General and the Stadtholder were willing to make one. 9

The Peace the Company Would Not Keep

The Company drew the opposite conclusion. Its directors, the Heeren XVII, told the States-General without ceremony that the corporation had grown great by fighting the Portuguese and had won its Asian monopoly by the same means; to stop now would be to let a beaten rival recover its wind and compete again. They asked that any truce concluded with the restored Portuguese crown in Europe leave the East Indies exempt, exactly as an earlier truce had been exempted a generation before. The war in Asia, in their reading, was not the republic's to end. 10

A ten-year truce was negotiated at The Hague in 1641 regardless. In Asia it was, for years, simply not obeyed. The Company's government at Batavia evaded its implementation on one pretext after another, and its officers were candid about where their allegiance lay. When a Portuguese envoy in Ceylon came to claim a fort that the treaty had assigned to him, the Dutch general refused to surrender it, observing that he and his men served the Company and not the Prince of Orange or the States, and would take orders from no one else. The standing instruction from Batavia was the reverse of restraint: seize as much Portuguese territory as possible before any peace could take effect, and so fix the map before the diplomats could. 11

Down the Pepper Coast

What made Malabar worth the effort was pepper. The coast between Quilon and Cannanore produced the spice that had drawn the Portuguese east in the first place, and a monopoly of it was a prize the Company had coveted since its earliest ships reached these shores. A Dutch squadron had appeared off Malabar as early as 1604 and made common cause with the Zamorin of Calicut, the oldest enemy of the Portuguese on the coast; but the Company had chosen to secure the Indonesian archipelago and Ceylon first, and only when the conquest of coastal Ceylon was complete in 1658 did it turn the full weight of its arms northward. 12

From Ceylon the blow fell fast. Under Rijkloff van Goens, the hard and capable commander who would govern the conquered coast, the Dutch came up Malabar fortress by fortress. Quilon fell in December 1661. Kodungallur, the Portuguese Cranganore, the seat of the archbishop whose quarrel with the Archdeacon had broken the church a decade earlier, fell in January 1662. The campaign that would end the Padroado's hold on the Thomas Christians was, from the Dutch side, simply the closing of an account in pepper. 13

The Fall of Cochin

Cochin was the prize, the chief Portuguese stronghold on the coast and the seat of its bishop, and it did not fall at the first attempt. An initial Dutch descent in early 1662 ended in retreat; Van Goens re-embarked in March, leaving garrisons behind at Cranganore and Quilon, and the Portuguese, supposing the danger past, neglected their defences. They were wrong. A second and far larger expedition appeared before Cochin on 25 October 1662, eleven ships from Batavia reinforced by van Goens himself with native Ceylonese troops brought across from the island he had just conquered. The Dutch entrenched on the approaches, cut the fort off from its allies, and settled in to take it. 14

The siege was hard and close. The Dutch raised batteries behind barricades of palm-trunks, some twenty-eight guns in all, sited by the churches of St John, St Thomas, and Calvetty, and opened on the half-ruined pepper warehouse from the sea on the last day of 1662. The Portuguese commander, Ignatio Sarmento, had refused the terms offered him, freedom of religion and trade in exchange for the fort, and his garrison fought stubbornly; the Raja of Porakkad sent Nair troops who, by one account, displayed exceptional heroism at the landing place before fresh Dutch companies broke them. The final assault came on the Calvetty side on 6 January 1663. When it was plainly lost, Sarmento sent two commissaries out under a white flag, and the next morning he delivered the keys of the fort into the hands of the Dutch general. The siege had cost the attackers some three hundred and sixty dead, with hundreds more in the hospital or unfit for duty. The terms were signed the following day. 15

The capitulation was the formal end of a hundred and sixty years of Portuguese Malabar. By its terms the garrison marched out with flying colours, drums beating and fuses lit, to lay down its arms beyond the walls; the unmarried Portuguese were to be shipped to Europe and the maimed to Goa; the clergy might carry off their images and church ornaments, save those of gold and silver. Cannanore, the last Portuguese hold on the coast, surrendered to Jacob Hustaart on 13 February. The Portuguese flag had ceased to fly over Malabar, and the Dutch were left the masters of the country. 16

The sharpest irony was in the timing, and the conqueror knew it. The Dutch republic and the restored Portuguese crown had in fact ratified their peace in Europe in December 1662, before Cochin fell, and one of its clauses required that places taken be restored to their former masters. Van Goens, it was charged, had deliberately withheld the news of the peace so that it should not stop his conquest; accused of the trick, he answered coolly that he was only playing the game the Portuguese themselves had played on the Dutch at the capture of Pernambuco in Brazil not many years before. The Company stood on a narrow technicality, that the treaty bound it only from its later publication, which fell some months after Cochin and Cannanore were already taken, and kept its conquests. It had secured its pepper coast and its accomplished fact together, and presented the peacemakers with a map already redrawn. 17

The New Masters and the Old Church

The new masters were Calvinists, and that single fact reshaped the position of the Thomas Christians at a stroke. The Dutch had no interest in the Padroado, no use for the religious orders that had administered the Serra, and a settled hostility toward the Catholic missions they found in place. Their first instinct on taking Cochin was to order the European clergy out. 18

It was that expulsion order, and no design of Rome's, that produced the scene with which the previous chapter ended. Joseph Sebastiani, the Carmelite whom Propaganda had sent to win the schismatics back, was given his days to leave, and he spent them as the last chapter described: summoning a synod at Kaduthuruthy and, on 1 February 1663, consecrating Mar Alexander de Campo, a cousin of Mar Thomas I, as Vicar Apostolic, so that the reconciled community would not be left without a bishop of its own race. Before he sailed he secured from van Goens an assurance that the Dutch would favour the new bishop over the Archdeacon. Then he went, and the European missionary governance that had ruled the Serra since Menezes went out with him. 19

What remained was a coast with new masters and a church with two heads. The Pazhayakūr followed Mar Alexander into communion with Rome; the Puthankūr followed Mar Thomas I; and both now had to find their way in a Malabar where the Portuguese were no longer the power and the Dutch had no wish to be a Catholic one. The Padroado's monopoly, which had shaped, and broken, the Thomas Christians across a hundred and sixty years, did not so much end as drown. What rose in the space it left, an indigenous Catholic bishop on the one side and an Eastern, soon West Syrian, succession on the other, is the matter of the chapters that follow. 20

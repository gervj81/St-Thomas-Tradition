The Global War and the Fall of Cochin, 1640–1700

Companies of War

"The Honourable Company had waxed great through fighting the Portuguese."

— The Heeren XVII to the States-General, 1641

Chapter 12 closed in a harbour under guns. Joseph Sebastiani, ordered out of Malabar by a Dutch commander who had just taken Cochin, used the few days he had left to consecrate Mar Alexander de Campo, so that the reconciled Thomas Christians would not be left without a bishop of their own before he sailed. The scene belongs as much to the history of European empire as to the history of the Serra, and it leaves a question the last chapter set aside. How had a Dutch fleet come to command the Portuguese harbour on the Malabar coast, with the power to expel a Roman bishop at ten days' notice? To answer it the narrative must rewind a little, and widen a great deal. 1

The guns in Cochin harbour were the local end of a war that had been running, on and off, for sixty years and across four continents. It had been fought in the North Sea and the Baltic, off Brazil and Angola, in the Spice Islands, the Persian Gulf, and the approaches to Goa. In its scale and its simultaneity it was something close to the first global war, waged less by a kingdom than by a chartered company that kept its own fleets, fortresses, and treaties, and that had grown rich by fighting the very empire whose richest possessions it now meant to inherit.

This chapter retells the fall of Portuguese Malabar. It is the story of how a Calvinist trading corporation converged on a single pepper port in the first weeks of 1663, and of what their convergence meant for a divided church that had no part in the quarrel and no power to escape its outcome.

A War Without Borders

The Dutch East India Company, the Vereenigde Oostindische Compagnie (VOC), was not a merchant house that occasionally fought. It was an instrument of war that happened to trade. In Chapter 10, we saw Charles V partitioning his massive domains in 1556, bequeathing the Netherlands, along with Spain, the Italian possessions, and the Indies, to his son, the Habsburg king Philip II. Philip II's sustained military repression of the Dutch Protestants ignited a long rebellion against Habsburg rule, a struggle remembered as the Eighty Years' War. Forged in the middle of this revolt, VOC was chartered in 1602 by the States-General (the sovereign federal assembly of the Dutch Republic) and operated under the strategic umbrella of the Stadtholder (the republic's chief executive and military commander). It carried the Dutch revolt into Asian waters as a matter of deliberate policy. Since the crowns of Spain and Portugal had been joined under a single Habsburg sovereign from the Iberian Union of 1580, the VOC's Calvinist directors, the Heeren XVII (or the "Lords Seventeen"), viewed every Portuguese fort, carrack, and spice contract east of the Cape as a legitimate target in a global war that was at once holy and profitable. 2

The Heeren understood the fusion of faith and profit without embarrassment. They shared with their Catholic Portuguese rivals the conviction that European Christians might dispose of the trade and the territory of every people beyond the pale of Christendom; they differed only in holding that the warrant lay with the Reformed religion rather than with Rome. Nor were they under any illusion about how their rule was received. The Company's most formidable commander in these waters would acknowledge, as early as 1655, that the Dutch were detested by every nation in Asia, an admission that did nothing whatever to slow the conquests. 3

The Long Road East

The Company had not arrived on the Malabar coast in a single stride. Its first object was the spice of the Indonesian archipelago, the cloves, nutmeg, and mace of the Moluccas and the pepper of the islands, and it took that spice in large part by prising the Portuguese loose from the trade they had dominated for a century. 4

From 1619 the Company had a capital in Asia and a will to match. In that year Jan Pieterszoon Coen established its permanent Asian headquarters at Batavia, in western Java, and set about building the inter-Asian trading system that would become the engine of Dutch wealth in the East: Indian cloth from the Coromandel coast exchanged for island spices, Persian silk, Japanese silver, Chinese porcelain, the whole woven into a network whose profits could pay for the cargoes sent home to Amsterdam. To build it the Company fought continuously, and chiefly against the Portuguese, whose forts strung along the sea-roads from Mozambique to Macao stood athwart every route it wished to control. 5

In 1648, the geopolitical landscape of Europe transformed with the Peace of Westphalia. This monumental series of treaties brought a definitive end to the Eighty Years' War, finally forcing the Spanish Habsburgs to recognise a fully independent and sovereign Dutch Republic. More broadly, Westphalia drew a line under the continent's exhausting wars of religious denomination, decentralising the power of the Holy Roman Emperor in favour of a new order of independent sovereign states. When this general peace in Europe freed thousands of battle-hardened soldiers for mercenary service, the Company had both the men and the forward base its Asian ambition required.6

The Peace on Paper

Even before Westphalia, the Company had been offered a truce with the Portuguese. In December 1640 the Duke of Braganza was proclaimed King John IV of Portugal, and sixty years of union with the Spanish crown were over. Overnight Portugal ceased to be an appendage of the Dutch republic's chief enemy and became instead a possible ally against it. The logic was plain enough: a fresh front against the Habsburgs in the Iberian peninsula was worth a truce with Lisbon, and the States-General and the Stadtholder were willing to make one. 7

The Company drew the opposite conclusion. Its directors, the Heeren XVII, told the States-General without ceremony that the corporation had grown great by fighting the Portuguese and had won its Asian monopoly by the same means; to stop now would be to let a beaten rival recover its wind and compete again. They asked that any truce concluded with the restored Portuguese crown in Europe leave the East Indies exempt, exactly as an earlier truce had been exempted a generation before. The war in Asia, in their reading, was not the republic's to end. 8

A ten-year truce was negotiated at The Hague in 1641 regardless. In Asia it was, for years, simply not obeyed. The Company's government at Batavia evaded its implementation on one pretext after another, and its officers were candid about admitting that they served the Company and not the Prince of Orange or the States, and would take orders from no one else. The standing instruction from Batavia was the reverse of restraint: seize as much Portuguese territory as possible before any peace could take effect, and so fix the map before the diplomats could. 9

The Other Company

The Dutch were not alone on the water. Two years before the VOC received its charter, the English had founded their own East India Company, in 1600, on the same model of a royal monopoly over a nation's whole Asian trade. Through the first half of the seventeenth century the English company was the lesser power in the East, edged out of the spice islands by Dutch force and content, for the most part, to build its trade in Indian cloth and, increasingly, in pepper. It was a rival the VOC could afford to disdain, and largely did. 10

That would change within a generation of Cochin's fall. When England and France fell upon the Dutch republic together in 1672, the war in Europe cut into the VOC's shipping and gave the English company its opening in Asia; its share of the European pepper trade climbed steeply through the 1670s. The point is worth marking here only because the two companies' fortunes would, in the next century, decide the fate of the coast this chapter is about: the same English company that fought for pepper in the 1670s would, by the later eighteenth century, be the paramount European power in India, and the Dutch hold on Malabar would dwindle to a memory. For the moment, though, in the first weeks of 1663, it was the Dutch who held the guns, and the Portuguese who were about to lose everything. 11

Up the Pepper Coast

The road that brought a Dutch fleet to Cochin ran through Ceylon. A Dutch squadron had appeared off Malabar as early as 1604 and made common cause with the Zamorin of Calicut, the oldest enemy of the Portuguese on the coast; but the Company had chosen to secure the Indonesian archipelago and Ceylon first, and only when the conquest of coastal Ceylon was complete in 1658 did it turn the full weight of its arms northward. 12

From Ceylon the blow fell fast. Under Rijkloff van Goens, the hard and capable commander who would govern the conquered coast, the Dutch came up Malabar fortress by fortress. Quilon fell in December 1661. Kodungallur, the Portuguese Cranganore, the seat of the archbishop whose quarrel with the Archdeacon had broken the church a decade earlier, fell in January 1662. The campaign that would end the Padroado's hold on the Thomas Christians was, from the Dutch side, simply the closing of an account in pepper. 13

The Fall of Cochin

Cochin was the prize, the chief Portuguese stronghold on the coast and the seat of the Padroado bishop, and it did not fall at the first attempt. An initial Dutch descent in early 1662 ended in retreat; Van Goens re-embarked in March, leaving garrisons behind at Kodungallur and Quilon, and the Portuguese, supposing the danger past, neglected their defences. They were wrong. A second and far larger expedition appeared before Cochin on 25 October 1662, eleven ships from Batavia reinforced by van Goens himself with native Ceylonese troops brought across from the island he had just conquered. The Dutch entrenched on the approaches, cut the fort off from its allies, and settled in to take it. 14

The siege was hard and close. The Dutch raised batteries behind barricades of palm-trunks, some twenty-eight guns in all, sited by the churches of St John, St Thomas, and Calvetty (now Calvathy Road), and opened on the half-ruined pepper warehouse from the sea on the last day of 1662. The Portuguese commander, Ignatio Sarmento, had refused the terms offered him, freedom of religion and trade in exchange for the fort, and his garrison fought stubbornly; the Raja of Porakkad sent Nair troops who, by one account, displayed exceptional heroism at the landing place before fresh Dutch companies broke them. The final assault came on the Calvetty side on 6 January 1663. When it was plainly lost, Sarmento sent two commissaries out under a white flag, and the next morning he delivered the keys of the fort into the hands of the Dutch general. The siege had cost the attackers some three hundred and sixty dead, with hundreds more in the hospital or unfit for duty. The terms were signed the following day. 15

The capitulation was the formal end of a hundred and sixty years of Portuguese Malabar. By its terms the garrison marched out with flying colours, drums beating and fuses lit, to lay down its arms beyond the walls; the Portuguese were shipped to Europe or Goa. Kannur, the last Portuguese hold on the coast, surrendered to Jacob Hustaart on 13 February. The Portuguese flag had ceased to fly over Malabar, and the Dutch were left the masters of the country (the Portuguese were now confined to Goa, which the Dutch had repeatedly tried and failed to take between 1638 and 1663). 16

The New Masters and the Old Church

The new masters were Calvinists, and that single fact reshaped the position of the Thomas Christians at a stroke. The Dutch had no interest in the Padroado, no use for the religious orders that had administered the Serra, and a settled hostility toward Rome and, above all, toward Portuguese power. They expelled the Catholic European clergy from the territory they held, the port of Cochin and the captured forts, ordering out the Carmelite vicar apostolic and the Jesuits of Cochin and Kannur. But the Dutch held only a coastal sliver. The Serra itself lay inland and to the south, scattered across a patchwork of independent native kingdoms where the Company's writ did not run, and there, beyond the reach of any expulsion order, a thin Catholic presence could survive. The standing Dutch rule, held to for decades, was that no European prelate, least of all a Portuguese one, would be allowed to exercise authority within their territory. 17

It was that expulsion order, and no design of Rome's, that produced the scene with which the previous chapter ended. To see what Sebastiani did in his last days on the coast, and why it cut so deep, the two men at its centre must be named properly. The Archdeacon who had been raised up at Alangad was George of the Cross's nephew Parambil Thomas, as Mar Thomas I. The man Sebastiani now raised to be Vicar Apostolic, with the administration of the Kodungallur-Angamaly see, was the Archdeacon's own first cousin: the cattanar whom the Carmelites knew as Parambil Chandy of Kuravilangad and whom the Latin records latinise as Alexander de Campo. The two were of one family, and not by accident: the office of archdeacon of the Thomas Christians had long been hereditary, descending within a single lineage, so that the leader of the schism and the man Rome would set against him were kinsmen of the same archidiaconal house. 18

The Family Feud

The division between these two kinsmen had in fact been some years in the making, and Sebastiani had worked at it deliberately across two Carmelite missions. Parambil Chandy was among the four senior cattanars whom the Archdeacon had appointed his councillors after the oath of 1653, the inner circle of the revolt. But Chandy was the earliest and the weightiest of the commissaries' gains from that inner circle. From the outset of Sebastiani's first mission in 1657 he gave the papal commissary his full support, sharing the judgement on which, in time, the general abandonment of the Archdeacon would turn: that the position of a metropolitan consecrated by twelve priests could not stand. He threw his weight behind the commissaries and laboured to bring as many cattanars and parishes back to Roman obedience as he could. Nor was he the only councillor to cross. Kadavil Chandy of Kaduthuruthy, the celebrated Syriac scholar among the four, likewise made his peace with Rome, and the sources of both traditions agree that he ended his days assisting Bishop Chandy. Of the four, only Ittithommen appears in the record rallying the Archdeacon's cause. The defection of the two Chandys did more than thin the council; it opened the war of memory. The earliest polemic the separated tradition preserves is aimed not at Rome in the abstract but at a defector by name, a letter of reproach addressed to Kadavil Chandy himself, and the kinsmen's quarrel over allegiance became, in the same motion, a quarrel over who had kept the faith of the fathers. By the time Sebastiani returned for his second mission, Parambil Chandy was the natural indigenous candidate the Carmelite had been cultivating: a man of the archidiaconal house, of unimpeachable descent, who had chosen union with Rome. 19

The struggle for the cattanars had been a close and sometimes farcical thing. The Archdeacon's party had at one point produced a merchant, recently arrived at Cochin to buy pepper, who was paid to pose as the nephew of a newly elected pope and to present a forged brief confirming Thomas as archbishop and patriarch of the Thomas Christians; the fictitious document was paraded at meetings held safely inside kingdoms then at war with Cochin and the Portuguese, where the rival commissary could not follow to expose it. An attempt by Sebastiani on 9 October 1661 to seize the Archdeacon and his chief adviser, the cattanar Anjilimoottil Ittithommen, and so end the schism by force, failed when the two slipped away disguised as Hindu soldiers. 20

Anjilimoottil Ittithommen

It is worth pausing on this Ittithommen, the one councillor who held unmoved. The contemporary record, all of it from the Catholic side, draws him in dark lines: a senior cattanar of Kallisseri, about sixty-seven in 1653, old enough to remember the days of Mar Abraham and exceptionally skilled in Syriac, the skill on which, in that record's firm judgement, the forged Ahatallah letters and the twelve-priest consecration turned. Sebastiani excommunicated him by name beside the Archdeacon. The Puthankūr tradition remembers the same man in reverse: the faithful and sagacious counsellor who held the community to the faith of its fathers, the prophet of the broken stick at the Mattancherry oath, the author of the letter reproaching Kadavil Chandy with the claim that Antioch had always been the lord of Malankara. But that memory speaks from two and a half centuries after the events, through E. M. Philip, and rests on documents no contemporary record corroborates; it belongs with the two historiographies of the split set out later in this chapter, and is weighed there and in the note below. What matters at this point in the story is the shape of things: from the moment the council split, each party possessed its loyalist and its apostates, and the materials of two irreconcilable memories of the same events were already in hand. 21

The See in the Shadows

It was against this background of forged briefs, narrow escapes, and a deepening factional split that the Dutch conquest abruptly closed the contest. Ordered out, Sebastiani summoned the priests in union with Rome to Kaduthuruthy and there, on 1 February 1663, conferred episcopal ordination on Parambil Chandy, and at the same gathering solemnly excommunicated the Archdeacon and Ittithommen. The form of the appointment was chosen with care. Chandy was consecrated not as archbishop of the vacant see but as titular bishop of Megara, Vicar Apostolic and administrator of the vacant see of Kodungallur-Angamaly. The title mattered, and would matter more in the decades to come. A vicar apostolic was not a diocesan bishop but the personal delegate of the pope, governing through the Roman Congregation of Propaganda Fide rather than through the Portuguese Padroado; and the ancient see of the Thomas Christians, the archbishopric of Kodungallur-Angamaly, was a Padroado see still, vacant in fact but unsuppressed in law, to which Lisbon retained the right to nominate. Neill reads the chosen form both ways at once: a deference to the Portuguese claim under the Padroado, which Sebastiani would not formally violate, and an assertion that the church of the Serra stood in complete subordination to the See of Rome. In raising up a Roman vicar apostolic over a flock that on paper still belonged to a Portuguese archbishop, Sebastiani had planted, almost in passing, a jurisdictional conflict that would not be resolved for two centuries. For the moment it lay dormant, since no Padroado archbishop could set foot in Dutch territory; but the day a Portuguese prelate again reached Kodungallur, the two claims would collide. In 1663 Sebastiani had no time to think past the immediate fact of the expulsion order, let alone to play the visionary about a collision two centuries off. Before he sailed he secured from van Goens an assurance that the Dutch would favour Bishop Chandy over his cousin. Then he went, and with him went the last European prelate to govern the Serra, though, as will be seen, a handful of friars clung on in the native interior. 22

The Two Bishops

The Dutch had promised Sebastiani they would favour Bishop Chandy over Mar Thomas I, and for a time they kept their word. The advantage for Chandy however, was not Dutch favour but the plain fact of orders. He had been properly consecrated as a bishop whose succession no one doubted; Mar Thomas I had been raised up by the hands of twelve priests at Alangad, a consecration that no apostolic church, Latin or Eastern, could recognise as valid and one that a growing number of his own people had begun to question. In the first years of Chandy's episcopate the balance tilted steadily toward him: cattanars and parishes made their submission, and the man with valid orders gathered to himself those who wanted a bishop they could be sure was a bishop. 23

It was this, the doubt about his own consecration, that drove Mar Thomas I towards the end of 1665 to open secret negotiations with the very Jesuits he had sworn at the Coonan Cross never to obey, an episode that comes to us mostly through Carmelite and Jesuit archives. Discouraged by the drift of parishes to his cousin and by the silence of the Eastern patriarchs he had requested for bishops, he sent a cattanar to the rector of the Jesuits (now withdrawn to Ambazhakkad under the Zamorin after the fall of Cochin) with word that he wished to submit to the Catholic Church, provided it could be done without humiliation. The talks went far enough that an end to the schism seemed within reach, and the reunion of the Thomas Christians under Rome was, in those weeks, a real possibility. 24

The Antiochian Connection

What ended that possibility was a ship. In 1665 a West Syrian prelate, Mar Gregorios Abdal Jaleel, identified in the sources as the Metropolitan of Jerusalem, reached Malabar, an Antiochian bishop answering, it seems, the appeals Mar Thomas had been sending east for a bishop of his own. He may have travelled with Dutch complicity, much as Ahatallah had slipped onto a Dutch ship a decade before, and the new Dutch masters, hostile to Rome, had no reason to stop an anti-Roman Syrian. The moment news of his landing arrived, Mar Thomas broke off the negotiation with the Jesuits and turned to receive him. He wrote to the northern churches to welcome the newcomer, met him at Idappally with a great gathering, and presented him to the people as a patriarch sent with apostolic authority; old Ittithommen, the Archdeacon's adviser, produced once more a brief, real or forged, read out from the pulpit, and a collection of some five thousand fanams was taken up for the visitor. It is from the coming of Mar Gregorios, more than from the oath of 1653, that the permanent division of the community into Puthankūr and Pazhayakūr can be dated. 25

Gregorios moved with care, for many still believed he had been sent by the Pope. When his manner of saying Mass gave offence, Mar Thomas persuaded him to use the local rite, which he did, and then set about restoring customs that the Synod of Diamper had abolished: the fermented host, the traditional vestments, the old calendar, observances common to West Syrians and Chaldeans alike. To his followers he appeared less as an innovator than as the restorer of their ancient ways, and this was the source of his success. In a letter of 1668 to the priests of Mulanthuruthy, Kandanad, and Parur he denounced those who had left the orthodox canons of “the Syrian Church” to join the idolatrous Romans, made heretics, as he put it, by the undue influence of the kings and queens of Portugal, and he attacked the compulsory celibacy the Latins had imposed. With his coming the breach took on a permanence the oath of 1653 had not given it. What hardened, it should be said precisely, was the institutional fact of two churches rather than the temper between their people: a reunion of the Pazhayakūr (the Old Allegiance, the party that held to Rome, called hereafter the Malabar Church) and the Puthankūr (the New Allegiance, the party that turned to Antioch, called hereafter the Malankara Church) was henceforth out of reach, and yet, as will be seen, the two communities went on living side by side with a familiarity that makes the word schism almost too cold for it. 26

A Contested Succession

Yet the one thing Mar Thomas most needed, Gregorios may not have given him. Whether the Jerusalem prelate ever conferred valid episcopal consecration on Mar Thomas I is precisely the point on which later Indian Church history would divide and the division is not merely between two readings but also between two kinds of evidence. On the one side stand the contemporary yet not impartial Jesuit and Carmelite letters, which maintain that no true consecration took place and style Thomas a pseudo-bishop. On the other side stands the later Malankara historiography, which affirms that Mar Gregorios, acting with a mandate from the Patriarch of Antioch, did properly consecrate him. The assertion rests on no contemporary document of its own, but on later documents in private collections and on oral tradition.27

Neill states the difficulty plainly. Doubt exists, he writes, as to whether Gregorios conferred consecration at all; local tradition affirms that he did, but if any such consecration took place it must have been done in secret, since Mar Thomas could not afford an open ceremony that would have conceded his earlier orders to be wanting. There is the knot: a secret regularisation, if it happened, would itself suggest that something was felt to need regularising. And the community's own later conduct can be read the same way. The doubt did not end with Mar Thomas I; it troubled his successors down the line, until in the eighteenth century Mar Thomas V, troubled, as Neill records, by the same uncertainty about his status that had dogged his predecessors, sent abroad for Antiochian bishops to settle the question once for all by an undoubted consecration. For Neill it is hard to read a century of repeated effort to import bishops as anything other than the conduct of a church not at rest about the validity of its own bishops. For Malankara historiography, however, the same recourse reads as the community strengthening itself against both Roman allegations of invalid ordination and the proselytising of a Chaldean bishop in the Serra. Whatever the case, the reconsecration that came in 1772 would itself occasion a split in the Malankara Church, the first of several that followed; that story belongs to a later chapter. Mar Gregorios died in 1671, at North Parur; Mar Thomas I outlived him by two years, dying on 22 April 1673.28

The Syrians of Malabar

What the events of 1665 meant is told in three incompatible ways, and the difference is not merely one of detail but of the whole frame. The divergence is worth setting out once here, because it will shadow the rest of this history:

  • The Malabar Catholic historiography and the source-critical academic writers followed in this synthesis, Mundadan (vol. I, 1984), Thekkedath (vol. II, 1982), and the Anglican Stephen Neill (1984), treat the Antiochian, West Syrian connection as something substantially new in Malabar. On their account the Thomas Christians had been an East Syrian, Chaldean-rite church under the Church of the East. One part of it chose union with Rome, and came in time to tell its own story as the cleansing of that Chaldean inheritance from its latinisation; for the other, the arrival of a bishop sent in the name of the Jacobite Patriarch of Antioch began a counter-allegiance and a gradual West Syriac reorientation that would take generations to complete.
  • The Malankara Jacobite historiography reverses the picture. Its classic statement, E. M. Philip's The Indian Church of St. Thomas, takes its cue from the Anglican chaplain Claudius Buchanan, who had reported after his 1806 visit that "the Syrian Christians are not Nestorians." Philip argues that the Syrians of the Serra were never properly Nestorian in the first place, the connection with the Church of the East a deputed arrangement rather than a constitutional fact, the rule of Babylon exercised, as the community had always understood it, on behalf of Antioch. Hence the title of his relevant chapter, "Revival of Jacobitism": Mar Gregorios came not to introduce a new obedience but to revive and validate the ancient Jacobite core of the Indian Church.29
  • The Malankara Orthodox historiography reads the same events through a third frame, one that borrows from each of the other two and refuses the conclusion of both. The official history of the Malankara Orthodox Syrian Church accepts that the ecclesiastical identity of the Thomas Christians down to the sixteenth century was East Syrian in doctrine, liturgy, and bishops, and states flatly that in the sixteenth and seventeenth centuries the community had no connection with the Antiochene Syrians at all. But it declines to read that bond as constitutive: the Indian church was connected with the Church of the East precisely as an independent church, served by visiting bishops yet constituted in itself, administered by its native archdeacon and palliyogams, its touchstone its fidelity to the Marthoma margam, the Law of Thomas. On this account the Syrians of the Serra were neither Chaldean nor Jacobite but autocephalous, and the archdeacons, and the Mar Thoma metropolitans after them, were defending that independence against the disruptions of Roman and Antiochian alike, one long war of an Indian church against successive foreign masters, whose vindication the tradition locates in the Catholicate of 1912. 30
  • The name as apologetic history. Much of this contest is carried by names rather than arguments, and a name, asserting nothing, can never be refuted. Buchanan gave the process one of its most influential modern formulations. By referring to the Antiochian party simply as "the Syrian Christians" and to their body as "the Syrian Church of Malayala," he quietly awarded that party the whole ancient inheritance in the act of naming it, without ever having to argue the point. The effect was an inversion. The Pazhayakūr, whose very name means the Old Allegiance, appeared in this scheme merely as "Romo-Syrians," a title that subtly cast them as a later Roman deviation standing outside the antiquity, while the faction named the New Allegiance carried the ancient identity along with the title Malankara Church. The Orthodox heir then re-inverted the trick against Antioch: its official history discards "Jacobite Syrians" as one more appellation imposed by opponents, and its chosen style, the Malankara Orthodox Syrian Church of the 1934 constitution, does with "Orthodox" what Buchanan's usage had done with "Syrian," writing the claim to be the original and authentic continuation of the Indian Syrian Christians into the title itself. The faction that emerged from the Anglican-influenced reform of the nineteenth century identified itself as the original Marthoma Margam stripped of every later accretion—Chaldean, Latin, or Antiochian. By taking the founder's own name, the Mar Thoma Syrian Church likewise compressed a historical argument into a title, presenting itself as a recovery of the apostolic path before later developments obscured it. Each party's whole history is thus compressed into what it calls itself.31

On Mar Thomas I's death his brother took his place and buried him in pontifical vestments, only to die himself within a week. A nephew then succeeded as Mar Thomas II and ruled until 1686, and the succession passed on through a third Mar Thomas and beyond. Catholic sources, with Neill, regard the early Mar Thomases as probably never validly consecrated, lacking the bishops and mandate a true succession required, while the Malankara history holds the succession to have been regular throughout: Mar Thomas II consecrated by Mar Gregorios in his uncle’s lifetime, and his successors by Hidayat Allah Mar Ivanios, one of the two Antiochian bishops who arrived in 1685. 32

Around 1680 Mar Thomas II, like his predecessor, sounded out the Carmelites about reunion and a Roman bishopric, and the negotiation failed; on the Catholic side Bishop Chandy, whose own dealings with the archdeaconate at this moment were bound up with his hope for just such a return, was among those who let it come to nothing, for reasons the next section will make clear. So the separated line continued, its orders disputed, even as Syrian prelates from abroad came, from time to time, to make the long voyage to Malabar. 33

The Coadjutor Rome Did Not Want

The Catholic side had a succession trouble of its own, and it ran the opposite way: not a bishop the apostolic world would not recognise, but a bishop nobody on the ground had asked for. Feeling his years, Parambil Chandy applied to Rome in 1674 for a coadjutor with right of succession. Propaganda agreed at once, and its instruction was emphatic on the one point that mattered: the four Carmelites it sent out in 1675 were to appoint as coadjutor a man who was, in the words of their orders, a native of the place and not a European. It was the obvious choice and the just one, the Serra had a capable indigenous clergy and a bishop of its own at last, and Rome meant the line to continue in native hands. 34

The men on the spot did the opposite. Only two of the four reached Malabar, and on 3 March 1677 they passed over every native candidate and designated instead Raphael de Figueredo Salgado, the Vicar General of the Latin diocese of Cochin, a man of Portuguese descent. Rome had said not a European; the Carmelites chose a Luso-Indian Latin. Chandy at first refused to consecrate the coadjutor who had been forced on him, so Figueredo received episcopal ordination at Calicut, some two months after his designation, from the vicar apostolic of Kanara, and was solemnly enthroned at Edappally only in 1683. He proved a disaster, disobeying Chandy, alienating the very Carmelites who had imposed him, and governing so badly that from 1684 those same Carmelites were writing to Rome to be rid of him. 35

It was the foreign coadjutor, more than anything, that finally moved Chandy to raise up his own nephew, and here the puzzle of the previous section resolves itself. Chandy had not been hoarding the archdeaconate for Mathew; quite the reverse. For years he had deliberately left the office vacant, holding it open for his separated cousins, in the hope that Mar Thomas I, or after him Mar Thomas II, might return to Rome and resume the place that was theirs by the tradition of the direct line, a tradition Chandy was unwilling lightly to overturn. That hope, and not any dynastic design, is why for so long he had no archdeacon at all. What changed his mind was the Figueredo affair itself: the discontent that followed the Portuguese coadjutor's imposition left the Catholic community so unsettled that Chandy judged it could no longer be without an indigenous head, and in 1678 he at last appointed his nephew Mathew Pallil archdeacon, writing into the letters-patent the careful apology that he did so only for want of a subject in the direct line. When, a year or two later, reunion talks opened around 1680 with Mar Thomas II, the Carmelites offered to restore the separated prelate to the archdeaconate of the entire community. Although Mar Thomas II was not unwilling to consider this, the negotiation fell apart because Bishop Chandy intervened to protect his nephew's position, insisting on maintaining Mathew in the office. The irony was complete, and double-edged. Rome had desired a native bishop but got a Portuguese coadjutor because the Carmelites, fearing a hereditary succession, had passed over Chandy’s nephew in favour of a Latin Luso-Indian; Chandy, in turn, had spent years holding the archdeaconate open for his separated cousin on traditional hereditary grounds, only to block that cousin’s return in order to maintain his own nephew in the office. The question of who would give the Thomas Christians an indigenous leader of their own, which had once split the community, had collapsed into a jurisdictional quagmire and a hardening of factions. The government of the Nazranis of the Serra had become a tangle that no party could now cleanly resolve. 36

Parambil Chandy died in 1687, having ruled some twenty-four years and held the larger part of the old community to Rome under a bishop of its own blood; the sources give the year but neither the day nor any cause beyond age. By the unhappy logic of the 1676 appointment the discredited Figueredo succeeded him as vicar apostolic, and the complaints to Rome only multiplied. At length Propaganda sent Bishop Custodio de Pinho, vicar apostolic of the Great Mughal, to investigate, and on his report, which found Figueredo guilty of simony and of neglecting his flock, Rome suspended him in January 1694. He died the next year, in October 1695. The first experiment in giving the Catholic Thomas Christians a settled succession had ended in a foreigner suspended for simony, and the see passed, for the time being, into the hands of Chandy's nephew the archdeacon as administrator, until the Carmelites should return in force. 37

The Separated Sides

The separation of the Serra was hardening into a permanent institutional breach, but it would be quite wrong to imagine the years after 1663 as decades of settled enmity between the two bodies of people. A surprising number of churches were simply shared between them, and individuals and whole families crossed from one side to the other as convenience served; the Carmelites' own day-book, kept at the mission from 1653 onward, records the traffic both ways. 38

The two Indian heads were, if anything, on cordial terms. A Carmelite complaint of 1683 accuses the Catholic archdeacon, Mathew Pallil, of kissing the hand of Mar Thomas II at Palai and otherwise fraternising with the 'schismatics'; ten years later the Carmelites reported, with evident alarm, that the Malankara Church actually wished to make the Catholic archdeacon Mathew their own bishop, and that he might have accepted had they not talked him out of it. Bishop Chandy, for his part, thought well enough of his cousin's successor Mar Thomas II to name him among the men he judged worthy to succeed himself, in preference to the Latin Figueredo who had been forced on him. The cousins' war was real, but it was a family's war, and the family kept reaching across it. 39

The hostility often appears in the sources to have been directed toward the foreign ecclesiastics who stood behind each side. The Catholics despised the anti-Roman polemics of the Antiochian prelates, who, right up to the moment they reached the safety of Mar Thomas's people, had to travel through Malabar in disguise for fear of the Catholic party; the followers of Mar Thomas, for their part, are described as hatching constant plots against the Carmelites, who viewed them as schismatic subjects to be disciplined. Each side could live with its kinsmen on the other side; what neither could abide was the stranger, the European friar or the Syrian bishop, who had come to take charge of them. It is a revealing pattern, and it says much about what the whole long struggle had really been about: not the faith the two groups shared, but the question of who from outside would be allowed to rule a church that wished to be ruled by its own. 40

By the end of the century the rough proportions had settled. A report two Carmelite missionaries sent to Rome in 1698 counted the Thomas Christians at one hundred and eleven churches in all: sixty-seven held by the Catholics, thirty-two by the Orthodox, and twelve still shared, partly Catholic and partly Orthodox, between the two. Roughly two in three of the old community had stayed with Rome under their indigenous bishop; one in three had gone with Mar Thomas; and a stubborn remainder of shared parishes served both groups, with families and entire churches moving fluidly between the two allegiances as local circumstances dictated. 41

The Propaganda Returns

Through all these years the Catholics had never been wholly without European priests, but they held them on Dutch sufferance, one or two at a time. The Carmelite Matthew of St Joseph, whom Sebastiani had once left as his delegate, had been swept out with the rest in 1663 and was allowed back only in 1668, when the French consul at Aleppo commended him to the Dutch commandant of Cochin; he became Bishop Chandy's adviser, and in 1673 built a church and a small residence at Chathiath near Ernakulam. The chief Carmelite house came in time to be Verapoly, where in 1682 the fathers opened a little seminary for Syrian Catholic youths, though their pupils still went in greater numbers to the better Jesuit college at Ambazhakkad. But these were a handful of tolerated individuals in Dutch Malabar, not a mission with a head. The man who had founded it never returned. Sebastiani had reached Rome in 1665 bearing his report on the Indian Church; he hoped, when a truce was signed, that he might be sent back to the south, but he was refused, raised instead to a bishopric in Italy, and he died there in 1689, never having seen Malabar again. 42

What returned was the mission rather than the man, and it returned only on the conquerors' terms. The Padroado bishops being barred from Dutch territory, Pope Innocent XII asked the Emperor Leopold I to treat with the government of the Netherlands, and in April 1698 an agreement was reached: the Holy See might send a Carmelite bishop and missionaries to Malabar, provided they were Belgians, Germans, or Italians, never Portuguese. It was the same exclusion that had governed the coast since 1663, now written into a treaty, the Dutch would suffer a Catholic mission, but not a Portuguese one. On that condition the Carmelites came back as a hierarchy: in February 1700 Rome named one of the Verapoly fathers, Angelo Francis of St Teresa, vicar apostolic of Malabar, and with him began the long line of Carmelite vicars apostolic who would govern the Catholic Thomas Christians for the next two centuries. 43

The Dutch conquest had broken Portugal's monopoly without resolving anything the monopoly had been quarrelling over. The question that had split the Coonan Cross, who might validly give the Thomas Christians a bishop of their own, stood exactly where it had. It would be pursued across the long Dutch peace by both churches, and by the competing Roman and Portuguese jurisdictions, in a rivalry that began with Angelo Francis's contested elevation at Alangad and would eventually drive the native Catholic clergy, decades later, to rise in open revolt against their Carmelite rulers at Angamaly. Into that inferno the narrative now moves, as the next chapter opens.

The letter's fitness to later controversy counsels further caution: the two councillors it presupposes as fallen away are Kadavil Chandy, its addressee, and Parambil Chandy, the future Mar Alexander de Campo and first indigenous bishop of the Catholic party — so that, as deployed in the tradition, the document at once attests the Antioch thesis and casts the rival church's founding generation as oath-breakers; and Philip, who published it, was conducting the litigation in which the Antioch connection was the matter at issue (see note 31 below). Three considerations weigh on the other side. The letter's tone is intimate rather than doctrinal, dense with remembered detail and a rueful warmth toward its "dear brother" that a fabricator had no need to invent; "apostate" is Philip's editorial frame, not the letter's own language; and even on the hypothesis of authenticity the letter would attest, at most, what the community had begun to say of Antioch in the decade of Ahatallah and on the eve of Mar Gregorios, when an Antiochian pedigree had become urgently useful — not what had been true of the centuries before. Authenticity and probative force are separate questions, and the second does not follow from the first. Notably, Philip's own gloss on the letter concedes that the church at Menezes's arrival was under the direct supremacy of the Patriarch of Babylon, and holds that the community understood that rule as a deputyship of Antioch. First, the letter as transmitted denounces those who left "the true and orthodox canons of the Syrian Church", calling them men who were once true Syrians; this is the earliest point in the present narrative at which the title Syrian Church is used as the exclusive possession of the non-Roman party, the naming usage the historiographical discussion below traces through Buchanan to Philip, and it cannot now be determined whether the term belongs to Gregorios or to the chain of transmission. What can be said without strain is what the body text retains: that to his followers Gregorios appeared as a restorer of ancient ways, and that this appearance was the source of his success.

On the contested question of Mar Thomas I's orders. The Puthankūr tradition (e.g. Philip) holds that Mar Gregorios conferred valid episcopal consecration in 1665. Among Catholic and academic historians the question is itself disputed: Tisserant (142) and Neill I, 328, hold that no true consecration took place, citing contemporary Carmelite and Jesuit letters that style Thomas a "pseudo-bishop" (Tisserant: "Thomas did not receive, it seems, the episcopal character he had been desiring"); others allow that Gregorios did lay hands on him in 1665, regularising his character, though on the Roman view illicitly. The point remains, in Podipara's words, one that "some affirm, others deny." What the Catholic and academic writers agree on is the rejection of the later successions (Mar Thomas II, III, and so on) as lacking valid orders (Tisserant, 143 n. 4; Neill). The contemporary documents derive almost wholly from partisan Latin archives. On the deaths: Mar Gregorios on 24 April 1671 (Thekkedath, vol. II, 102, following Kollaparambil, 147, and the contemporary Carmelite letters cited there; Tisserant, 143, and Neill I, 328, give 1672), buried at North Parur; Mar Thomas I on 22 April 1673 (Thekkedath, vol. II, 102; Tisserant, 143).

On the doubt whether Mar Gregorios conferred consecration at all: Neill I, 328, who records that local tradition affirms the consecration but that it must be regarded as doubtful, and that if it took place it must have been done in secret, since Mar Thomas I would not act in a way that cast doubt on the validity of his earlier consecration. On the recurrence of the same anxiety in the line, Mar Thomas V being “plagued by the same uncertainty as to his status which had troubled his predecessors” and seeking an Eastern bishop to settle the question by an undoubtedly valid consecration: Neill II, 65–66. The reading of the community’s repeated efforts to secure valid orders, culminating in the reconsecration of 1772, as evidence of its own unresolved doubt is the author’s, offered as interpretation, not as a claim the sources make in these terms. The evidentiary asymmetry, contemporary but partisan Latin records against later but tradition-internal Malankara historiography, is set out so that the reader may weigh it; this work does not adjudicate the question of the 1665 orders. For the act in which the long uncertainty finally ended: Neill II, 68. Mar Thoma VI's own account, there quoted, concedes that in 1772, at Niranam, he received every order afresh, beginning from the tonsure itself, at the hands of the Jacobite metropolitan Mar Gregory, taking the name Dionysius; Neill observes that no one was thereafter likely to doubt the validity of this consecration, which is to say that on his reading the doubt had run unbroken from 1653 to 1772. How that act is remembered depends on which heir of the Puthankūr is remembering, for the party itself divided in 1912 into a faction holding to the Patriarch of Antioch and an autocephalous faction, the Malankara Orthodox Syrian Church, and the two need the native consecrations to be different things. For the Jacobite allegiance, validity flows from Antioch: the defect of 1653 was cured in 1665 and cured definitively in 1772. The Orthodox faction's official history concedes the anxiety while reversing its conclusion, stating that Mar Thomas I, against constant allegations that he was a false bishop, sought remedy by inviting the West Syrian prelate, and in the tradition's account thereby placed the legitimacy of his office beyond his followers' doubt ("After 1653 as an Independent Church", mosc.in, accessed June 2026). Yet the same faction's position since 1912, repudiating Antiochene jurisdiction as the community had once repudiated Rome's, requires the delegation-less ordinations to stand on their own, and in its devotional memory they do: the consecrations of the Mar Thomas line are commemorated as performed "without the presence of any foreign bishops", Antiochene attempts at re-ordination are recalled as bids to establish supremacy, rightly refused, and the community of 1727 is already called the Malankara Orthodox Church, a retrojection of exactly the kind discussed at note 31 below (see, e.g., the feast-day commemoration of Mar Thoma V at oneinchrist.in, accessed June 2026). What Neill weighs as a century of unresolved doubt about orders, one heir of the community remembers as anxiety duly remedied from Antioch, and the other as the title-deeds of autocephaly.

The words quoted are Buchanan's report of the Syrian Christians, made after his visit to Kerala and his contact with the Malankara Church in 1806, and printed in his Christian Researches in Asia (1811) in the section on the Syrian Church; Buchanan there cites his own earlier Account of the Syrian Christians (Calcutta, 1807), 3. Buchanan adds, tellingly, that the Syrians are "usually denominated Jacobites" but differ in ceremonial from the church of that name in Syria and from any existing church, and that, the patriarchate of Antioch being then nearly extinct, the Malabar Church might henceforth look to Britain, a hope that prefigures the Anglican and later C.M.S. involvement noted above. Philip reproduces the passage as independent corroboration of the Syrians' anti-Nestorian antiquity; this synthesis treats Buchanan, like Hough and Neale, as a sympathetic Anglican witness rather than a neutral one.

On the Malankara Orthodox frame: the official history published by the Malankara Orthodox Syrian Church at mosc.in ("History"; "Before 1653"; "After 1653 as an Independent Church", accessed June 2026). The pages affirm that the ecclesiastical identity of the Thomas Christians until the end of the sixteenth century was East Syrian in doctrine, liturgy, and government, with the Catholicos-Patriarch of the East as head and the East Syrian bishops as occasional visitors over an indigenous body administered by its archdeacon and palliyogams; state that during the sixteenth and seventeenth centuries the community had no connection whatever with the Antiochene Syrians; present the East Syrian bond as the relationship of an independent church rather than a subjection; describe the West Syrian bishops from 1665 as at first helpful in preserving the church’s Syrian identity and internal freedom against Rome and later as agents of a West-Syrianisation that damaged its East Syrian inheritance; and treat names such as "Jacobite Syrians" as appellations imposed by opponents, the church’s present name dating from the constitution of 1934. These pages are official church history, apologetic in genre and published by a party to the division of 1912; they are cited as evidence of how the tradition frames the period, not as evidence for the seventeenth century itself, and the provenance caution entered at note 28 above and note 31 below applies here with equal force. It is worth noticing that on the substance of the pre-1653 allegiance this frame stands closer to Mundadan, Thekkedath, and Neill than to Philip: it parts from the academic consensus over the constitutional reading of the East Syrian bond, not over the bond’s existence, which the Jacobite frame must instead contest.

On the two historiographies of 1665. For the West Syrian (Antiochian) connection as substantially new, the earlier allegiance having been East Syrian: Neill I, 319–331; Tisserant, 89–96; Thekkedath, vol. II, 96–110; and Mundadan, vol. I, 80–86, who names the pre-Portuguese mother church East Syrian "as against West-Syrian or Antiochene," treats the synod of Markabta (424) as ending whatever dependence on the Antiochene Church may have existed, and notes that recent scholarship seriously questions whether Persian Christianity ever depended on Antioch at all. For the contrary "revival" reading, that Jacobite orthodoxy long predated 1665 and Gregorios restored rather than introduced it: E. M. Philip, The Indian Church of St. Thomas (written early in the twentieth century and published posthumously; Mundadan, vol. I, 6, lists it under 1929, the year of Philip's death; cited from the 2nd English ed., ed. K. C. Moolayil, Mor Adai Study Centre, 2002), ch. 16, invoking Renaudot's date of 696 and reproducing a 1668 encyclical of Gregorios. On the reform behind the fourth name: it began within the Malankara Church under CMS influence with Abraham Malpan (1796–1845), who held that the doctrines and practice of the church must be tested by the Scriptures (Neill II, 251–53); the reformed party, separately constituted after the Royal Court judgement of 1889, became the Mar Thoma Syrian Church. These nineteenth-century developments lie far beyond this chapter and are noticed here only for the naming pattern. Philip himself concedes that at Menezes's arrival the church stood under the direct supremacy of the Patriarch of Babylon, holding that the community understood that rule as a deputyship exercised on behalf of Antioch (see also note 21 above). On Philip's standpoint: he wrote as Secretary of the Malankara Jacobite Syrian Association and conductor of the Travancore Syrian Church litigation (Author's Preface), and for his distinctive claims he leans, by his own account, on family diaries, privately held manuscripts, and local tradition, not open to third-party verification. The Western authorities he invokes were not neutral: Buchanan, Hough, and John Mason Neale were early-nineteenth-century Anglicans sympathetic to the Syrians as fellow opponents of Rome, before the C.M.S. intervention and the reformed schism of the 1830s. This narrative accordingly prefers, among Protestant historians, the later academic work of Neill. Mundadan's survey classes Philip, with Z. M. Parett, as representing the views of the Syrian Orthodox (Jacobite) community, as Bernard of St Thomas and Podipara reflect those of the Syrians in communion with Rome (vol. I, 6–7); and his methodological censure is even-handed, finding writers of both camps — E. M. Philip and K. N. Daniel no less than the Catholic Bernard — "not quite free from facile explanations and generalizations" (vol. I, 64 n. 91). The present synthesis follows Mundadan in that even-handedness.

On the succession in the tradition’s own telling: E. M. Philip, The Indian Church of St. Thomas, ch. 17, where Mar Thoma II, nephew of Mar Thoma I, is consecrated by Mar Gregorios together with Mar Thoma I, dies in April 1686, and is buried at Niranam; two Jacobite prelates, Mor Baselios and Mor Ivanios (Hidayat Allah), deputed by the Patriarch of Antioch, land at Tellicherry in 1685 and travel in disguise to Kothamangalam; Mor Ivanios consecrates Mar Thoma III as successor, and on the latter’s death in April 1688 consecrates Mar Thoma IV, before dying in August 1693 and being buried at Mulanthuruthy. The body text’s summary of the Malankara position follows this account. For the Catholic and academic record of the same succession, and its rejection of the early consecrations, see the following note. On the later Malankara identification of this episcopal family as the Pakalomattam house: Philip devotes a chapter to its extinction (ch. 19) and reports the tradition, from a Mar Thoma’s reply to a government enquiry, that for the 1,308 years from A.D. 345 the family had supplied the archdeacons in unbroken succession, while himself conceding "historical inaccuracies" in that reply; the claim is not corroborated by the Anglican writers he cites, neither Buchanan nor Hough treating the archdeaconate as the property of a hereditary house, and the seventeenth-century European records name the house not Pakalomattam but Parambil (de Campo). What is contested is the name and the antiquity, not the legitimacy of the archdeacon: both factions agree he was the rightful hereditary head, and the first native bishops of both parties were kinsmen of the one house. See note 18 above and Chapter 6, "The Archdeacon and the Pakalomattam Question."

On the succession after Mar Thomas I: his brother's burial of him in pontifical vestments and his own death within days; the accession of the nephew as Mar Thomas II (ruled to 1686), who is reported to have conferred orders and consecrated the holy oils though himself unconsecrated; the Carmelite report of 15 March 1681 of his consecration by one Mar Andreas, said by the Carmelites to be not a bishop but a priest of Aleppo; his abortive reunion overture to the Carmelites around 1680, frustrated in part by Bishop Chandy's concern to preserve the archdeaconate for his nephew Mathew (appointed 1678); and the passing of the line to Mar Thomas III, a layman until ordained: Thekkedath, vol. II, 103; Tisserant, 143 and n. 4. Neill I, 328, also notes that Thomas was succeeded by a line of "shadowy heads" who all took the name Thomas, and reports the tradition that Mar Thomas III was a layman at his accession and was consecrated by twelve cattanars. Tisserant, 143 n. 4, Neill I, 328, and Kollaparambil all reflect the Western historical view that none of these subsequent early Mar Thomases received valid consecration, whereas the Malankara historical tradition asserts the continuity and validity of their office, supported by subsequent regularizations by visiting Eastern prelates.

On Parambil Chandy's request to Rome in 1674 for a coadjutor with right of succession, Propaganda's acceptance, and the dispatch of four Carmelites in 1675 with instructions to appoint a coadjutor who was "either a secular priest or a religious who, however, is a native of the place, not a European": Tisserant, 87–88; Thekkedath, vol. II, 105. Neill I 329 also covers the coadjutor dispute, noting that Chandy wished to appoint his nephew Matthew but was blocked by the Carmelites.

On the designation, on 3 March 1677, not of a Syro-Malabar but of Raphael de Figueredo Salgado, Vicar General of the Latin diocese of Cochin, born at Cochin of Portuguese parents; on Tisserant’s verdict ("one cannot imagine a less happy choice") and his question whether the spirit of the Roman instructions had been followed; on the judgement of Bishop Castro and the Theatine Fr Carlo Ferrarini that the election was precipitous and imprudent; and on the Carmelites turning against their own appointee from 1684 and petitioning Rome against him: Tisserant, 87–88; Thekkedath, vol. II, 105–06. On the date: Tisserant, 88, gives the designation as 3 March 1676, Thekkedath, vol. II, 105, as 3 March 1677; Thekkedath is followed here, the more readily as Tisserant’s own list of the vicars apostolic (at 200) assigns Figueredo the years 1677–94. On the orders and the throne, which the sources are careful to distinguish: Bishop Chandy at first refused to consecrate the coadjutor imposed on him (Tisserant, 88), and Figueredo accordingly received episcopal ordination at Calicut, about two months after his election, from Bishop Thomas de Castro, vicar apostolic of Kanara, the same Castro who had judged the election imprudent (Thekkedath, vol. II, 105); his solemn enthronement at Edappally followed only in 1683, after Chandy at last accepted him (Tisserant, 88). The discontent that followed the election was such that Figueredo withdrew to Goa for about a year to let it subside (Thekkedath, vol. II, 106).

On Chandy's long delay in filling the archdeaconate (hoping for the return of his separated cousins and reluctant to overturn the hereditary tradition of Mar Thoma's family), his appointment of his nephew Mathew Pallil as archdeacon in 1678, the declaration in the letters-patent that he did so "because of the absence of a subject in the direct line," and how this appointment subsequently spoiled the 1680 reunion proposal by preventing Mar Thomas II from receiving the archdeaconate as offered by the Carmelites: Thekkedath, vol. II, 103–105. Note that Tisserant, 88, mentions only the Carmelite refusal to appoint the bishop's nephew "Matthew de Campo" as coadjutor, while Neill I 329 reports only that Chandy's wish to appoint Matthew as coadjutor was blocked by the Carmelites, with neither author mentioning the archdeaconate vacancy, Mathew's 1678 appointment as archdeacon, or his role in blocking the 1680 reunion. On the later Carmelite support for Mathew (from 1684) and the Puthankūr wish to make him their own bishop (1693), see notes 38 and 39 below.

On the death of Parambil Chandy (Alexander de Campo) in 1687, after a rule of some twenty-four years: Thekkedath, vol. II, 106; the sources give the year only, with no precise date or cause recorded (Tisserant, 87, notes a Syriac psalter once his, with marginalia for 1660–70, now Bibliothèque Nationale, Paris, syr. 25 suppl. 72). On Figueredo succeeding him as vicar apostolic; the continued complaints; the investigation by Custodio de Pinho, vicar apostolic of the Great Mughal; the finding of simony and neglect; and Figueredo's suspension and death: Thekkedath, vol. II, 106–07 (suspension in January 1694; Figueredo died at Pallipuram in October 1695, before the order of suspension could be executed; the day, 12 October, is Tisserant’s, at 88; de Pinho, old and sickly, never reached Malabar and died in 1697, and there was no bishop in the Serra until 1701). Tisserant, 88, places de Pinho's commission and Figueredo's suspension in "January 1684," but this cannot be reconciled with Figueredo's succession to the vicariate on Chandy's death in 1687, and Thekkedath's January 1694 is to be preferred. On Archdeacon Mathew administering the archdiocese in the interval: Thekkedath, vol. II, 106.

On the division being one of persons and prestige rather than doctrine, the two sides continuing to regard themselves as one community, the sharing of churches, and the passage of individuals and families between the two: Thekkedath, vol. II, 108–09, drawing on the "Narrazione" of Fr Ildefonso, the Carmelite mission day-book kept from 1653 to 1740 (Ambrosius a S. Theresia, 202, 208–09).

On the cordiality of the two Indian heads: the Carmelite document of 1683 accusing the Catholic archdeacon Mathew Pallil of kissing the hand of Mar Thomas II at Palai (Kollaparambil, The Archdeacon of All India, 208, citing the letter of 18 April 1683 in APF SOCG vol. 496, ff. 115–16); the Carmelite report of 28 October 1693 that the Puthankūr wished to make Archdeacon Mathew their bishop, which he might have accepted but for Carmelite dissuasion (Kollaparambil, 213, citing APF SOCG vol. 520, ff. 136–7); and Bishop Chandy's inclusion of Mar Thomas II among those he thought worthy to succeed him rather than Figueredo (Kollaparambil, 198 n. 23, citing Chandy to Propaganda, 9 January 1679, APF SC[IOC] vol. 2, f. 90). All three are reported in Thekkedath, vol. II, 109.

On the hatred reserved for the foreign leaders of the opposite party: the Antiochian prelates detested by the Catholics and obliged to travel in disguise until they reached Mar Thomas's people (Malancharuvil, The Syro-Malankara Church, 29, citing E. M. Philip and G. B. Howard), and the "constant plots" of Mar Thomas's followers against the Carmelites (Ambrosius a S. Theresia, 209): Thekkedath, vol. II, 109.

On the strength of the two parties at the end of the century, from the report of two Carmelite missionaries to Propaganda in 1698: of 111 churches in all, 67 Catholic, 32 Orthodox, and 12 partly Catholic and partly Orthodox: Thekkedath, vol. II, 107–08 (APF, Congregationi Particolari, vol. 30, f. 807). On the national exclusion governing the Carmelite return, see note 43 below.

On the continued presence of a few Carmelites and Jesuits among the Syrian Catholics: Matthew of St Joseph, Sebastiani's former delegate, expelled with the other Europeans in 1663 and readmitted only in 1668 on the recommendation of the French consul at Aleppo to the Dutch commandant of Cochin, after which he served as Bishop Chandy's adviser and in 1673 built a church and residence at Chathiath near Ernakulam (Thekkedath, vol. II, 107; Tisserant, 87); the rise of Verapoly as the chief Carmelite residence and the small seminary opened there in 1682, whose pupils nonetheless mostly attended the Jesuit college at Ambazhakkad: Thekkedath, vol. II, 108. On Sebastiani himself: his arrival at Rome in 1665 with his report, his brief and disappointed hope of returning south after the Luso-Dutch truce, his elevation to an Italian bishopric, and his death in 1689 without seeing Malabar again: Tisserant, 87 and the Carmelite hierarchical sources there cited.

On the agreement of April 1698, negotiated by Innocent XII through the Emperor Leopold I with the Netherlands government, allowing the Holy See to send a Carmelite bishop and missionaries to Malabar provided they were Belgian, German, or Italian, not Portuguese; and on the consequent appointment of Angelo Francis of St Teresa as vicar apostolic of Malabar in February 1700, beginning the line of Carmelite vicars apostolic who governed the Catholic Thomas Christians until 1887: Thekkedath, vol. II, 107; Tisserant, 200. The Padroado sees of Cranganore and Cochin were never suppressed by Rome but could not be governed under Dutch rule, being run in the interim by vicars-general rather than resident bishops; so the 1698 agreement restored not a Catholic clergy, which had never wholly left the interior, but a Catholic bishop able to govern. Neill I, 331, gives the measure: Bishop Pacheco of Cochin (from 1699) resided "where the Dutch writ did not run," and of the three Portuguese prelates in India "none was permitted by the Dutch to reside anywhere in the Serra"; Vasconcellos later held Cochin twenty years from 1722 "without ever being able to reside within the limits of his diocese" (Neill II, 59).

The Decline of the Dutch and the Rise of New Powers, c. 1740–1795

The New Sovereigns

"I would rather wish that the ocean had swallowed the whole coast of Malabar a hundred years ago."

— Jacob Mossel, Governor-General at Batavia

At the close of the last chapter, our gaze shifted from the cattanars and their bishops to the powers that ruled the ground beneath them. The view from that height is the subject of the present chapter. For the better part of two centuries, the fortunes of the Thomas Christians had been bound up with two European masters: the Padroado crown that had come with the ships of Vasco da Gama, and the Dutch Company that had supplanted it on the Malabar coast. By the middle of the eighteenth century, both were passing from the stage, and the powers that rose in their place were of a wholly different kind. To follow the Serra into the century that came, we must first watch the ground itself change, as the old European sea-power failed and new sovereigns, native and British alike, rose to inherit the coast. 1

The Kingmakers

The Mughal empire, which under Aurangzeb had stretched from Kabul to the Carnatic, did not survive his death in 1707 as a living power. Within a generation, it had dwindled to a court at Delhi that commanded little beyond its own walls. The great provinces it had once ruled—Hyderabad in the Deccan, Bengal in the east, Awadh in the north—passed to governors who paid the emperor the homage of his name and kept for themselves the substance of his power. These were the first of the successor states, sovereignties carved from the body of a dying empire. Into the vacuum rode the Marathas, the confederacy of the western Deccan, who through the middle of the century came nearer than any other to a paramount power, their armies ranging from Rajasthan to Bengal to levy tribute, until their advance was checked, in 1761, on the field of Panipat. 2

The far south, however, lay beyond the reach of Marathas and Mughals alike, and there, in the country that concerns this history, the dissolution of the old order took its own form. Upon the long-decayed inheritance of the empire of Vijayanagar, a scramble of new warrior-states arose. Raised by ambitious and self-made men, these centralized and militarized states soon entangled themselves with the European companies that clung to the coasts. Mysore in the uplands, the Carnatic on the eastern plain, Travancore in the south-west: these were the new sovereigns among whom the Thomas Christians would now have to make their way. 3

The Wadiyars

The first of the new sovereigns rose not on the coast but in the hills behind it, in the kingdom of Mysore. One of the longest-ruling Hindu dynasties of South India, the Wadiyar house was founded as a vassal of Vijayanagar in 1399. After the decline of Vijayanagar, the Wadiyars gradually asserted their independence, consolidating their power in the late seventeenth and early eighteenth centuries. Hyder Ali was a humble soldier, born into a family of soldiers who had migrated from the north and sold their swords to the Wadiyars in the south. Hyder Ali climbed through the Mysore army by sheer military gift until, by 1761, he was strong enough to put down the great officers of state and make himself master of the kingdom. He did not trouble to depose the ancient Hindu dynasty of the Wadiyars whose servant he had been; he left them upon their throne as crowned pensioners and ruled in their name, taking for himself the substance of sovereignty and leaving them its shadow.4

What Hyder built upon that borrowed throne was a thoroughly modern thing. He hired French officers to drill his soldiers, raised an artillery and a disciplined army on the European pattern, paid for it by squeezing his lands through an efficient and merciless fisc, and turned Mysore from a sleepy successor-state into the most aggressive military power in the south. His son Tipu would in time carry the usurpation to its conclusion, setting the Wadiyars aside altogether and proclaiming himself sultan in his own right. Here, in miniature, was the new age entire: a self-made soldier upon a pensioned throne, an ancient dynasty reduced to ornament, a French-drilled army financed by a centralized treasury. The sovereign state had arrived in the south, and it had arrived by the sword. 5

The Arcots

The second of the new sovereignties lay across the peninsula, on the eastern plain that the old geographers called the Carnatic. Its nawab ruled from Arcot, a deputy in name of the Nizam of Hyderabad, who was himself a deputy in name of the emperor at Delhi. The forms of Mughal authority survived here, as everywhere, emptied of their substance. What set the Carnatic apart was not its nawab but its guests. On this coast stood the chief settlements of the English and the French, Madras and Pondicherry, within a few days' march of one another. Here, near the middle of the century, the two companies first turned from trade to war. They fought not in their own names but through rival claimants to the nawab's seat. The French raised up one prince and the English another, each arming its candidate and taking in return a share of his revenues and his land. In 1751, a young Company clerk named Robert Clive seized the fortress of Arcot and held it against the French party. The balance tipped to the English. When the contest was over, their man sat at Arcot, and the company that had placed him there was the master behind his chair.

What had been worked out here was something new. The nawab kept his title, his court, and his honours, but little else. To pay for the troops that had made him and now upheld him, he borrowed heavily from the Company and from its servants in their private dealings. His debts to these men swelled into a power of their own, swaying the Company's counsels and reaching into the politics of London. He reigned; the Company governed; the creditors pressed. In 1801, the last form was set aside. The Carnatic was taken into the Company's own territory and ruled directly from Madras. The pattern was first cut here: a native prince left with the shell of his throne, a trading company grown into the sovereign behind it. In time it would be carried the length of India. Nor was this coast a stranger to the Christian story. At Mylapore, beside Madras, stood the tomb that tradition assigned to the Apostle Thomas himself, the founder from whom the Christians of the western coast traced their descent. But the new dominion would reach those western Christians by another road; and on their own coast, meanwhile, the oldest of the European masters was ebbing toward its end. 6

The Ebb of Orange

While Mysore rose in the hills, the power that had ruled the coast in the name of the Prince of Orange was beginning to fail. 7 The Dutch East India Company broke, as it happened, against the very kind of state we have just watched rise in Mysore. On the southern stretch of the coast, a small kingdom called Travancore had begun, under an ambitious raja, to swallow its neighbours one by one, and its expansion struck at the root of the Company's pepper monopoly. In February 1739, the Dutch governor of Ceylon, Van Imhoff, made the journey to the raja's court in person to threaten Travancore into docility. The raja was not impressed; he reminded the governor that the first Dutch fleet sent to take Cochin had been forced to retreat before the fleet of the Raja of Purakkad. 8

The VOC–Travancore war that followed, dragging on from 1739 to 1743, did not go as the Company intended. The raja pre-empted the Dutch design and routed their ally before the campaign could unfold. Then, more damaging than any single engagement, the Dutch ranks dissolved in mass desertion, some hundreds of the Company's own soldiers going over to the raja's service, where they were received with every kindness. At the same moment, the Company's reserves were swallowed by a rising of Chinese labourers at Batavia itself, so that the reinforcements Malabar needed never came. The clash at Colachel in 1741, where the Dutch attempt to hold the southern coast collapsed, was less a great battle than the visible sign of a Company that could no longer afford its own wars. All the same, it would be remembered as the first time an Asian power put a European one to flight on Indian soil.9

The Sovereign of the Coast

The raja who broke the Dutch was Marthanda Varma of Venad Swaroopam, and the state he was forging on the Malabar coast—Travancore—was precisely the kind of centralized power Hyder Ali was building in the hills. To finance his wars, he declared a monopoly of pepper throughout his territory and sold the spice, for cash and for arms, to the English at Anjengo and the French at Mahé. With the proceeds, he built and drilled an army on the European model, and with the army he broke the old nobility of his land and absorbed the petty kingdoms around him, gathering into one centralized hand a power his ancestors had never held. 10

What Marthanda Varma made was a small-scale version of the absolutism then current in Europe, the old web of customary obligation between a king and his nobles giving way to a single sovereign will. It was the Westphalian state in Indian dress, and it had appeared on the Malabar coast not as a European import but as a native creation, built by a Hindu king to defeat a European company. The irony was fitting to the age. The Dutch, who had taught the coast the uses of disciplined force, were undone by a pupil who had learned the lesson better than his teachers. 11

The Terms of Retreat

The terms on which the Company made its peace measured the completeness of its retreat. By the first treaty of Mavelikkara, in May 1743, the Dutch surrendered the pepper monopoly that had been the whole purpose of their presence, and gave up too the guardianship they had claimed over their client states. Ten years later, by the second treaty of 1753, they went further and abandoned their oldest ally on the coast, the Raja of Cochin, undertaking to stand aside from his quarrels with Travancore. The Raja of Cochin, who had sheltered under Dutch protection for a century, protested bitterly that the Company had sacrificed an old friend and broken every contract it had made. 12

The Company never recovered the men it lost. Among the soldiers taken in the southern fighting was a Flemish captain, Eustache de Lannoy of Arras, whom Marthanda Varma spared and took into his service. De Lannoy repaid the mercy by building the Travancore army into a force of some fifty thousand men, drilled in the European fashion, with which the raja went on to conquer the length of the central coast. He raised, too, across the kingdom's northern marches the long fortified rampart known as the Nedumkotta, the lines of Travancore, behind which the realm could shelter, and which would one day hold the armies of Mysore at bay. The Company's own officer had become the sword, and the shield, of the Company's conqueror. 13

The deepest measure of the change lay not in any treaty, but in a sentence the Dutch wrote years afterward to Hyder Ali of Mysore. War in Malabar, they confessed, did nothing but reveal how powerless the Europeans were against the people of the country once matters had to be settled by arms, while loading the Company with a burden out of all proportion to anything it might gain. It was the epitaph of the merchant-warrior. The armed company that had ruled the coast by force now owned that force was beyond its means, and chose instead the long road of disengagement and decline. 14

The Tiger's Wars

If the Dutch had learned the limits of force, the new native sovereigns had not, and the most formidable of them now turned his face toward the coast. Hyder Ali's son Tipu Sultan, who succeeded to Mysore in 1782 and soon set the Wadiyars aside to rule in his own name, was a conqueror by temperament, and he carried his father's war into Malabar with a violence that fell on the country's Christians as heavily as on all else. His armies overran the northern coast, and in 1790 his troops came into the heart of the Serra and burned the Jesuit college at Ambazhakkad. 15

The coast Tipu's armies overran had been, for centuries, the country of the Zamorin of Calicut, the old Hindu sea-king at whose port Vasco da Gama had first landed. His power did not survive the Mysorean storm. When Hyder Ali came down into Malabar in 1766, his armies fell upon the Zamorin and his nobles with a fury the old kingdom could not withstand. The Zamorin sent his women and children south to safety, set fire with his own hand to the palace of his fathers, and perished in the flames. His lords and captains fled after him, and the order of the north dissolved. Under Tipu, the Mysorean grip tightened, and wave upon wave of refugees, Hindu and Christian, took the road to Travancore, the one southern power that still stood. When the wars at last gave Malabar to the British, the Zamorin's house came back not to a throne but to a pension, its sea-kingdom gone the way of the Dutch. 16

What drew Tipu down upon Travancore was a bargain struck by the departing Dutch with the Travancore Dewan, Raja Keshavadas. As the Company gave up its place in Malabar, it sold off its last forts on the coast, Kodungallur and Ayacotta, and it sold them not to Mysore but to the Dharma Raja (Karthika Thirunal Rama Varma) of Travancore. Hyder had claimed the north Malabar coast as his by conquest, and the ports lay in the Kingdom of Cochin, by then a vassal of Mysore. To watch the Dutch deliver their strongholds to Travancore, and Travancore push its frontier north to take them in, was a trespass he would not suffer. He resolved to bring Travancore to heel, and may well have reckoned the business an easy one. But in this Tipu had miscalculated, for the coast he coveted was no longer the Dutch coast. As the old sea-power ebbed, a new European power had been rising, and it was a power of an altogether different make. The British East India Company, ruling now from Madras and from Calcutta, was no mere trading monopoly but a territorial empire in the forming, with revenues and armies that dwarfed anything the Dutch had ever fielded. Where the merchant-warrior had sought only to control a traffic, the new Company sought to govern a country, and that ambition would in the end fold the whole south into a single paramountcy. 17

When Tipu attacked Travancore in 1789, he struck at a state the British had named their ally, and so broke the peace he had concluded with them only five years before. It was the third trial of arms between Mysore and the British Company in a single generation: Hyder Ali had fought the British twice and died undefeated. The Treaty of Mangalore that closed the second war in 1784 had brought peace for the time being, and Tipu's attack on the Nedumkotta shattered it. The British Governor-General, Lord Cornwallis, judged the attack a cause of war and acted with a speed and decision that took Tipu by surprise. When the British surrounded his capital of Seringapatam, Tipu was forced to come to terms in February 1792; he lost half his dominions and paid a heavy indemnity. Seven years later, the war would end with his death, the storming of his capital, and the extinction of his line. 18

The Hoop Closes

The wars that destroyed Tipu fastened the British upon the very state that had broken the Dutch. Travancore had been saved from Mysore by British arms, and the salvation came at a price. The kingdom was bound to maintain a British force and to pay for its keep; and when its finances fell into ruin, the dalawa Velu Thampi, the kingdom's chief minister, rose in revolt against the burden and was crushed. The question of whether Travancore should keep its separate existence at all hung for a time in the balance; annexation, the Resident's own papers show, was a real possibility. 19

What preserved the kingdom was its submission. The Rani who came to the throne in those years made a careful art of helplessness. She cast herself as a daughter in want of the Company's protection, and asked who would protect her if the Company did not. The Resident, Colonel Munro, answered the appeal. In his single person, he held both the British political power and, for a time, the office of the kingdom's own chief minister. In this way, he became the real master of the state. The form of native sovereignty remained; its substance had passed to the Residency. The instrument of the new dependence was the subsidiary alliance. Under it, a native ruler kept his throne and his ceremony, but surrendered the conduct of his own defence and diplomacy. He paid for a British force he could not command, and admitted to his capital a Resident through whom the paramount power made its wishes known. The arrangement left the shell of sovereignty standing while it emptied the kernel. It would serve as the model of British dominion over the princely states for a century and more to come. 20

The Dutch, meanwhile, had made their last exit. In 1795, the British took Cochin from them, and the Company that had ruled the coast for a century and a third was gone from it. The merchant-warrior had not merely been pacified; it had been supplanted. By the turn of the century, there was no longer any question where power lay. The native states of the south survived, but as protected dependencies; the old European trading companies were finished as political forces; and over the whole country, by the judgement of the next generation, England had become the paramount power in India. 21

What this would mean for the Thomas Christians is the matter of the chapters to come, but its shape can already be seen. The Christians of the Serra now lived under a Hindu sovereign state and a Protestant paramount power, and the fate of their bishops, Pazhayakūr and Puthankūr alike, would henceforth be weighed in the durbar at Trivandrum and in the Residency beside it. It was no accident that the native revolt of 1787 had been settled, in the end, neither by Rome nor by Lisbon but by the King of Travancore. That was the new order showing its hand. In the century that followed, the British Resident would reach into the affairs of the Syrian church more deeply than any Dutch commandeur had ever cared to do, and the long contest over who should govern the Thomas Christians would be fought out, at the last, under a wholly new kind of power. 22

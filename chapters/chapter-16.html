The Decline of the Dutch and the Rise of New Powers, c. 1740–1795

The New Sovereigns

"…to reveal the impotence of the Europeans against the natives of the country."

— The Dutch governor-general and council in Batavia, on the wars of Malabar

At the close of the last chapter the eye was lifted from the cattanars and their bishops to the powers that ruled the ground beneath them, and the view from that height is the matter of the present one. For the better part of two centuries the fortunes of the Thomas Christians had been bound up with two European masters: the Padroado crown that had come with the ships of Vasco da Gama, and the Dutch Company that had supplanted it on the Malabar coast. By the middle of the eighteenth century both were passing from the stage, and the powers that rose in their place were of a wholly different kind. To follow the Serra into the century that came, we must first watch the ground itself change, as the old European sea-power failed and new sovereigns, native and British alike, rose to inherit the coast.

The change had a shape, and it was the shape this history has followed from the beginning. The order born at the Peace of Westphalia, of self-conscious sovereign states each claiming entire mastery within its own borders, had remade Europe; and now, by a curious logic, it was remaking the south of India as well. The new powers of the Malabar country, whether Hindu, Muslim, or British, were built upon a single principle: the centralized state, financed by monopoly and taxation, defended by a standing army drilled in the European manner, owing obedience to no authority above itself. The merchant-warrior, the armed trading company that had been the characteristic European instrument of the seventeenth century, was about to be pacified by the sovereign state, the characteristic instrument of the eighteenth. 1

To see why this should be so, the eye must travel wider still, past the coast to the whole of India, for the pattern that remade Malabar was a pattern of the age. The Mughal empire, which under Aurangzeb had stretched from Kabul to the Carnatic, did not survive his death in 1707 as a living power. Within a generation it had dwindled to a court at Delhi that commanded little beyond its own walls, while the great provinces it had once ruled, Hyderabad in the Deccan, Bengal in the east, Awadh in the north, passed to governors who paid the emperor the homage of his name and kept for themselves the substance of his power. These were the first of the successor states, sovereignties carved from the body of a dying empire. Into the vacuum rode the Marathas, the confederacy of the western Deccan, who through the middle of the century came nearer than any other to a paramount power, their armies ranging from Rajasthan to Bengal to levy tribute, until their advance was checked, in 1761, on the field of Panipat. 2

The far south, however, lay beyond the reach of Marathas and Mughals alike, and there, in the country that concerns this history, the dissolution of the old order took its own form. Upon the long-decayed inheritance of the empire of Vijayanagar a scramble of new warrior-states arose, raised by ambitious and self-made men, each building the same centralized and militarized kind of sovereignty, and each soon entangled with the European companies that clung to the coasts. Mysore in the uplands, the Carnatic on the eastern plain, Travancore in the south-west: these were the new sovereigns among whom the Thomas Christians would now have to make their way. 3

The Soldier on the Usurped Throne

The first of the new sovereigns rose not on the coast but in the hills behind it, in the kingdom of Mysore. Hyder Ali, born in 1722 into a family of soldiers who had hired their swords to one Deccan power after another, climbed through the Mysore army by sheer military gift until, by 1761, he was strong enough to put down the great officers of state and make himself master of the kingdom. He did not trouble to depose the ancient Hindu dynasty of the Wodeyars whose servant he had been; he left them upon their throne as crowned pensioners and ruled in their name, taking for himself the substance of sovereignty and leaving them its shadow. The manner of it is caught in a story told of him: that when an old Wodeyar died, Hyder gathered the eligible boys of the line into a hall "strewed with fruits, sweetmeats and toys" and bade them help themselves, a tableau of how lightly the old royalty now sat upon its seat. 4

What Hyder built upon that borrowed throne was a thoroughly modern thing. He hired French officers to drill his soldiers, raised an artillery and a disciplined army on the European pattern, paid for it by squeezing his lands through an efficient and merciless fisc, and turned Mysore from a sleepy successor-state into the most aggressive military power in the south. His son Tipu would in time carry the usurpation to its conclusion, setting the Wodeyars aside altogether and proclaiming himself sultan in his own right. Here, in miniature, was the new age entire: a self-made soldier upon a pensioned throne, an ancient dynasty reduced to ornament, a French-drilled army financed by a centralized treasury. The sovereign state had arrived in the south, and it had arrived by the sword. 5

The Old Sea-Power Fails

While Mysore rose in the hills, the power that had ruled the coast was beginning to fail. The Dutch East India Company had been, in its strength, the very type of the merchant-warrior: not a territorial empire but an armed trading monopoly, a company of merchants that kept fleets and forts and soldiers for the single purpose of mastering the traffic in pepper and spice. It was the Company that had driven the Portuguese from Cochin in 1663, that had barred the bishops of the Padroado from its waters, that had detained the Syrian delegates of 1751 and dictated the passage-money for which a metropolitan was thrown into a Dutch prison. Through all the quarrels of the last three chapters the Company had been the silent arbiter of the coast, the power before which Pazhayakūr and Puthankūr alike were obliged to bow. 6

That power broke, as it happened, against the very kind of state we have just watched rise in Mysore. On the southern stretch of the coast a small kingdom called Travancore had begun, under an ambitious raja, to swallow its neighbours one by one, and its expansion struck at the root of the Company's pepper monopoly. In February 1739 the Dutch governor of Ceylon, Van Imhoff, made a personal journey to the raja's court at Trivandrum to overawe him, reminding him that it was Dutch arms that had once driven the Portuguese from Malabar, and threatening, if he would not desist, to bombard his palace. The raja was not impressed. He answered that the first Dutch fleet sent against Cochin had itself been forced to retreat before an Indian fleet, and he did not desist. 7

The war that followed, dragging on from 1739 to 1743, did not go as the Company intended. The raja pre-empted the Dutch design and routed their ally before the campaign could unfold; and then, more damaging than any single engagement, the Dutch ranks dissolved in mass desertion, some hundreds of the Company's own soldiers going over to the raja's service, where they were received with every kindness. At the same moment the Company's reserves were swallowed by a rising of Chinese labourers at Batavia itself, so that the reinforcements Malabar needed never came. The clash at Colachel in 1741, where the Dutch attempt to hold the southern coast collapsed, was less a great battle than the visible sign of a Company that could no longer afford its own wars, and it has rightly been remembered all the same as the first time an Asian power put a European one to flight on Indian soil. Batavia drew the only conclusion left to it: better, the High Government decided, to make peace with Travancore. 8

The Sovereign of the Coast

The raja who broke the Dutch was Martanda Varma of Travancore, and he was raising, on the Malabar coast, precisely the kind of state that Hyder Ali was raising in the hills. To finance his wars he declared a monopoly of pepper throughout his territory and sold the spice, for cash and for arms, to the English at Anjengo and the French at Mahé, renewing and widening a bargain his predecessors had struck with the English factors as early as 1723. With the proceeds he built and drilled an army on the European model, and with the army he broke the old nobility of his land and absorbed the petty kingdoms around him, gathering into one centralized hand a power his ancestors had never held. 9

What Martanda Varma made was, in the judgement of the kingdom's modern historian, a small-scale version of the absolutism then current in Europe, the old web of customary obligation between a king and his nobles giving way to a single sovereign will. It was the Westphalian state in Indian dress, and it had appeared on the Malabar coast not as a European import but as a native creation, built by a Hindu king to defeat a European company. The irony was fitting to the age. The Dutch, who had taught the coast the uses of disciplined force, were undone by a pupil who had learned the lesson better than his teachers. 10

The Merchant-Warrior Pacified

The terms on which the Company made its peace measured the completeness of its retreat. By the first treaty of Mavelikkara, in May 1743, the Dutch surrendered the pepper monopoly that had been the whole purpose of their presence, and gave up too the guardianship they had claimed over their client states. Ten years later, by the second treaty of 1753, they went further and abandoned their oldest ally on the coast, the Raja of Cochin, undertaking to stand aside from his quarrels with Travancore; and the Raja of Cochin, who had sheltered under Dutch protection for a century, protested bitterly that the Company had sacrificed an old friend and broken every contract it had made. 11

The Company never recovered the men it lost. Among the soldiers taken in the southern fighting was a Flemish captain, Eustache de Lannoy of Arras, whom Martanda Varma spared and took into his service; and De Lannoy repaid the mercy by building the Travancore army into a force of some fifty thousand men, drilled in the European fashion, with which the raja went on to conquer the length of the central coast. He raised, too, across the kingdom's northern marches the long fortified rampart known as the Nedumkotta, the lines of Travancore, behind which the realm could shelter, and which would one day hold the armies of Mysore at bay. The Company's own officer had become the sword, and the shield, of the Company's conqueror. 12

The deepest measure of the change lay not in any treaty but in a sentence the Dutch wrote, years afterward, to Hyder Ali of Mysore. War in Malabar, they confessed, did nothing but reveal how powerless the Europeans were against the people of the country once matters had to be settled by arms, while loading the Company with a burden out of all proportion to anything it might gain. It was the epitaph of the merchant-warrior. The armed company that had ruled the coast by force now owned that force was beyond its means, and chose instead the long road of disengagement and decline. 13

The Tiger and the Wars

If the Dutch had learned the limits of European force, the new native sovereigns had not, and the most formidable of them now turned his face toward the coast. Hyder Ali's son Tipu Sultan, who succeeded to Mysore in 1782 and soon set the Wodeyars aside to rule in his own name, was a conqueror by temperament and a zealous Muslim by conviction, and he carried his father's war into Malabar with a violence that fell on the country's Christians as heavily as on all else. His armies overran the northern coast, and in 1790 his troops came into the heart of the Serra and burned the Jesuit college at Ambazhakkad, the seminary that had trained the Syrian clergy for more than a century; churches were sacked, congregations scattered, and the institutional ruin of the Catholic Syrians, begun by the suppression of the Jesuits, was finished by the sword. The violence, terrible where it fell, was nonetheless a weapon of conquest rather than a settled war upon the faith: beyond his borders Tipu wielded the language of holy war as an instrument of statecraft, but within his own dominions he kept up, as his father had, working relations with Hindus and Christians who gave him no trouble. The bitterest collisions of community against community in the south would come not from him, but later, and from the colonial peace itself. 14

But Tipu had miscalculated, for the coast he coveted was no longer the Dutch coast. As the old sea-power ebbed, a new European power had been rising, and it was a power of an altogether different make. The English East India Company, ruling now from Madras and from Calcutta, was no mere trading monopoly but a territorial empire in the forming, with revenues and armies that dwarfed anything the Dutch had ever fielded. Where the merchant-warrior had sought only to control a traffic, the new Company sought to govern a country, and that ambition would in the end fold the whole south into a single paramountcy. 15

When Tipu attacked Travancore in 1789, he struck at a state the British had named their ally, and so broke the peace he had concluded with them only five years before. It was the third trial of arms between Mysore and the Company in a single generation: Hyder Ali had fought the British twice and died undefeated, and the treaty of Mangalore that closed the second war in 1784 had settled nothing. The Governor-General, Lord Cornwallis, judged the attack a cause of war and acted with a speed and decision that took Tipu by surprise. Surrounded in his capital of Seringapatam, his resources spent, Tipu was forced in February 1792 to throw himself upon Cornwallis's mercy; he lost half his dominions and paid a heavy indemnity, and was left his throne only in the hope that he had learned to keep his word. He had not, and seven years later a final war would end with his death in the storming of his capital and the extinction of his line. 16

The Hoop Closes

The wars that destroyed Tipu fastened the British upon the very state that had broken the Dutch. Travancore had been saved from Mysore by British arms, and the salvation came at a price. The kingdom was bound to maintain a British force and to pay for its keep, and when its finances fell into ruin and a desperate minister rose in revolt against the burden and was crushed, the question of whether Travancore should keep its separate existence at all hung for a time in the balance; annexation, the Resident's own papers show, was a real possibility. 17

What preserved the kingdom was its submission. The Rani who came to the throne in those years made a careful art of helplessness, casting herself as a daughter in want of the Company's protection and asking who would protect her if the Company did not; and the Resident, Colonel Munro, who held in his single person both the British political power and, for a time, the office of the kingdom's own chief minister, answered the appeal and made himself the real master of the state. The form of native sovereignty remained; its substance had passed to the Residency. The instrument of the new dependence was the subsidiary alliance, by which a native ruler kept his throne and his ceremony but surrendered the conduct of his own defence and diplomacy, maintained at his own charge a British force he could not command, and admitted to his capital a Resident through whom the paramount power made its wishes known. It was a manner of rule that left the shell of sovereignty standing while it emptied the kernel, and it would serve as the model of British dominion over the princely states for a century and more to come. 18

The Dutch, meanwhile, had made their last exit. In 1795 the British took Cochin from them, and the Company that had ruled the coast for a century and a third was gone from it. The merchant-warrior had not merely been pacified; it had been supplanted. By the turn of the century there was no longer any question where power lay. The native states of the south survived, but as protected dependencies; the old European trading companies were finished as political forces; and over the whole country, by the judgement of the next generation, England had become the paramount power in India. 19

What this would mean for the Thomas Christians is the matter of the chapters to come, but its shape can already be seen. The powers that had framed their history for two hundred years, the Catholic Padroado and the Calvinist Company, were gone or going, and the masters who took their place owed nothing to the altar of either confession. The Christians of the Serra now lived under a Hindu sovereign state and a Protestant paramount power, and the fate of their bishops, Pazhayakūr and Puthankūr alike, would henceforth be weighed in the durbar at Trivandrum and in the Residency beside it. It was no accident that the native revolt of 1787 had been settled, in the end, neither by Rome nor by Lisbon but by the King of Travancore. That was the new order showing its hand. In the century that followed, the British Resident would reach into the affairs of the Syrian church more deeply than any Dutch commandeur had ever cared to do, and the long contest over who should govern the Thomas Christians would be fought out, at the last, under a wholly new kind of power. 20
